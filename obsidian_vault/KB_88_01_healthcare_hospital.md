## 1. Healthcare / Hospital

### Scenario 1: Emergency Department Wait Leads to Collapse and Death After Patient Leaves Without Being Seen (Pattern C)

**Real Basis:** CBS News, NBC News, CNN — Froedtert Hospital, Wauwatosa, Wisconsin, USA, January 2020. Patient Tashonna Ward, 25, documented via time-stamped Facebook post from the waiting room. Hospital records confirm 2.5-hour wait with no physician contact despite an abnormal cardiac chest X-ray on file. Hospital's only contact with the patient was a retroactive phone call made after she had already collapsed in a car outside.

**The Problem:** A 25-year-old arrives at an emergency department with chest pain and shortness of breath. An initial chest X-ray shows cardiomegaly — an enlarged heart — but no doctor has reviewed it with her. She has been waiting 2.5 hours and is told the queue to see a physician is 2 to 6 more hours. She calls a family member to drive her to another hospital because she cannot safely wait any longer. She is in the car right now, feeling worse, and cannot reach anyone at the hospital to ask if leaving is medically safe.

**What staff/org would do:** The hospital only calls her cellphone after she has already left. By the time her sister answers, the patient has lost consciousness. No proactive outreach occurred during the 2.5-hour wait despite an abnormal cardiac finding in her triage file. The hospital's sole contact is retroactive — made after the patient is already dying.

**What the Owner does:** Treat this as a life-threatening emergency right now. Direct the caller to call emergency services immediately if the patient's symptoms are worsening. Log the complaint at the highest urgency tier and ensure a follow-up on the patient's safety before proceeding to the grievance process. If the patient has died, direct the family immediately to patient safety and legal channels.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Chest pain and shortness of breath with an abnormal heart scan on file are serious warning signs. If your symptoms are getting worse right now, please call emergency services immediately — do not wait. Once you are safe, I will help you formally report what happened at the hospital."
- Direct to: Emergency services (911/999) if symptoms are worsening; hospital Patient Safety Officer (same day); CMS complaint line (1-800-MEDICARE, USA); The Joint Commission complaint portal (jointcommission.org); State Department of Health patient rights division
- Evidence to collect: Time-stamped triage receipt or arrival record; the chest X-ray report; any written or verbal communication from triage staff; any personal message or social media post documenting the wait time and symptoms; discharge paperwork or the absence of it
- Feedback category tag: Emergency Access Failure / Wait Time / Patient Safety

### Scenario 2: Debt Collectors Pursue an Already-Cleared Hospital Bill — Billing Department Gives Wrong Information on Every Call (Pattern B)

**Real Basis:** CFPB Complaint Bulletin — Medical Billing and Collection Issues, April 2022 (official regulatory publication, USA). 8,500 complaints received about medical debt collection in 2022 alone; complaints about collections on already-paid or insurance-covered bills rose 31% from 2018 to 2021. CFPB identified double billing as an illegal but prevalent practice. Approximately 100 million Americans carry over $220 billion in medical debt, much arising from billing errors and insurance coordination failures.

**The Problem:** A patient was told explicitly at hospital discharge that their insurance had been accepted and they owed nothing. Eight weeks later, a debt collection letter arrives demanding payment. The patient calls the hospital billing department. The first agent says the account shows a zero balance. After a transfer, the second agent says the balance was sent to collections by a different department. After another transfer, the third agent says to call the insurance company directly. No one has authority to halt the collection process. A credit bureau entry now shows the alleged debt as active.

**What staff/org would do:** Each department transfers the caller to another, denying responsibility. No agent can stop the collection activity. The patient is told the process takes time to resolve. The credit report damage continues while the dispute drags on for months.

**What the Owner does:** Confirm that this is a documented illegal practice when applied to a cleared account. Provide a concrete, immediate action sequence — not a generic complaint referral.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Being told you owe nothing and then receiving a collection notice on your credit report is something the CFPB has formally documented as an illegal practice affecting hundreds of thousands of patients. You have clear rights here, and there are specific steps you can take right now to stop the collection activity and protect your credit score."
- Direct to: CFPB complaint portal (consumerfinance.gov/complaint); State Attorney General consumer protection division; hospital Patient Financial Services director via certified mail with a written dispute; all three credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) — file a formal dispute and request a hold on the entry under the Fair Credit Reporting Act
- Evidence to collect: Discharge paperwork showing zero balance or any verbal confirmation record; all billing statements received; the collection letter including the agency name and debt amount claimed; the credit report entry with date; a log of every call to the hospital (date, time, agent name, what was said)
- Feedback category tag: Billing Error / Illegal Debt Collection / Financial Harm / Call Center Misinformation

### Scenario 3: Emergency Alarm Cord Activated in Hospital Bathroom — No Staff Response for 15 Minutes, Patient Dies of Cardiac Arrest (Pattern E)

**Real Basis:** Asheville Watchdog investigative report; Blue Ridge Public Radio; North Carolina Health News — Mission Hospital Emergency Department, Asheville, North Carolina, USA, February 10, 2025. Patient arrived by ambulance with a respiratory complaint, activated the bathroom emergency cord, and was found in cardiac arrest 12 to 15 minutes later with no staff having responded. CMS was notified but declined to impose penalties. One employee was fired.

**The Problem:** A patient admitted to the emergency department by ambulance activates the emergency assistance cord in the patient bathroom. The cord triggers an audible alarm throughout the department and flashes a warning light above the bathroom door. Hospital leadership broadcasts multiple radio calls asking staff to check the bathroom. The department has 25 to 40 patients waiting in the lobby. Twelve to fifteen minutes pass. No staff member responds. By the time a nurse finally arrives, the patient is in full cardiac arrest. Resuscitation fails. The family is now asking why no one responded to a live emergency signal in an active emergency department.

**What staff/org would do:** The hospital fires one employee and cites extreme overcrowding as the cause. CMS is notified but declines to impose penalties. No public commitment to systemic staffing change is made. The family is directed to the hospital's patient relations department — the same institution whose systems failed.

**What the Owner does:** Document the alarm activation time and the exact response gap as the core institutional failure. Escalate immediately beyond the hospital's internal patient relations team to external regulatory bodies. Direct the family to independent legal counsel given the documented, time-stamped system failure.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (if a patient or family member is currently in this emergency department — the same staffing and alarm-response failure pattern is documented as active and recurring)
- Opening reply: "What happened — an emergency alarm that went completely unanswered for 15 minutes inside an active emergency department — is a serious institutional failure, not an individual accident. I am going to help you get this documented and in front of the right external authorities today."
- Direct to: Hospital Chief Nursing Officer and Patient Safety Officer in writing (same day); CMS complaint line (1-800-MEDICARE); State Department of Health patient safety division; The Joint Commission complaint portal (jointcommission.org); medical malpractice attorney for consultation on the documented 15-minute non-response
- Evidence to collect: Time of ambulance arrival; time the alarm was activated (request from hospital records via formal medical records request); time the patient was found; any radio broadcast log or staff witness accounts; staffing records for that shift (obtainable via formal records request); CMS Care Compare public inspection history for the facility; documentation that one employee was fired as the facility's own acknowledgment of institutional failure
- Feedback category tag: Response System Failure / Staff Misconduct / Patient Safety / Alarm Failure

### Scenario 4: Elderly Woman Lies on Care Home Floor for 19 Hours — Ambulance Dispatched but Never Arrives (Pattern C)

**Real Basis:** UK Judiciary official Prevention of Future Deaths report — Senior Coroner Andrew Cox, Cornwall and the Isles of Scilly, November 2022. Case of Winnie Barnes-Weekes. Root cause: all available ambulances were physically stacked outside Royal Cornwall Hospital, unable to offload patients from earlier calls due to hospital bed shortages — a cascading system failure. Formal report issued to Health Secretary Steve Barclay with a response deadline of January 10, 2023. Also covered by ITV News West Country, November 2022.

**The Problem:** An elderly woman falls in her care home. Staff call 999. The call is answered and an ambulance is logged as dispatched. Hours pass. No ambulance arrives. Staff call again. And again. The 999 system received every call correctly — but every available ambulance is physically trapped outside the regional hospital, unable to leave the queue. The woman lies on the floor for 19 hours. She dies. Her family has received no formal written explanation, no apology, and no confirmation of what the government's response to the Coroner's Prevention of Future Deaths report actually was.

**What staff/org would do:** The ambulance service cites system-wide pressure. The hospital cites bed unavailability. The care home says it followed correct protocol. The family is caught in a triangle of three organizations each pointing at the others. The Coroner's report and the government's legal obligation to respond are never mentioned to the family.

**What the Owner does:** Provide the family with the specific Coroner's PFD report reference, explain their right to the government's formal response, and route them through the NHS complaint process with a clear escalation path to the Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (if a current patient in a care home or home setting is awaiting an ambulance right now — the same system cascade failure is documented as recurring across the same region)
- Opening reply: "I am deeply sorry for what your family has been through. What happened has been formally documented by the Senior Coroner as a systemic failure that caused multiple deaths — and there is a legal obligation for the Health Secretary to respond to that report. Your family has the right to know exactly what has been done, and done differently, since then."
- Direct to: NHS formal complaint in writing to both the ambulance trust and the hospital trust separately; Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman (ombudsman.org.uk) if local resolution is unsatisfactory or takes more than 6 months; Healthwatch England (healthwatch.co.uk) for local advocacy support; local MP (can submit a written parliamentary question specifically about the PFD report response); INQUEST charity (inquest.org.uk) if pursuing a wider public interest inquiry
- Evidence to collect: 999 call reference number and log of times called; CAD (Computer-Aided Dispatch) reference number from the 999 service; care home incident report; times of each 999 call and each callback received; the UK Judiciary Prevention of Future Deaths report (titled "David Morganti, Winnie Barnes, Robert Conybeare and Anthony Reedman" — available at judiciary.uk, November 2022); any written response received from NHS trusts to date
- Feedback category tag: Emergency Wait Time / Ambulance System Failure / 999 Received — No Response / Institutional Accountability

### Scenario 5: Mental Health Patient Discharged Without Any Notification — No Phone Call, No Letter, No Crisis Plan — Dies of Overdose (Pattern E)

**Real Basis:** Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman (PHSO) published case summary — anonymized as Mr C, UK. Discharged from Community Treatment Team for missing a single appointment. No multidisciplinary team risk discussion was held, no GP handover was arranged, no crisis referral was made, and no contact of any kind — by phone, letter, or in person — was made with the patient before or after the discharge decision. Complaint upheld by PHSO. Also cited in PHSO report: "Unsafe Discharge from Hospital" (ombudsman.org.uk).

**The Problem:** A person with a complex mental health history — including multiple sections under the Mental Health Act — misses a single outpatient appointment. Without an MDT discussion, without a risk assessment, and without telling the patient in any way, the Community Treatment Team records him as administratively discharged. He has no registered GP. He is never given a crisis line number. No one calls him. No letter is sent. He does not know he no longer has a mental health team. He dies of a drug overdose weeks later. His family discovers the discharge only after requesting his clinical records.

**What staff/org would do:** The Trust cites the missed appointment as sufficient grounds for administrative discharge under its internal process. When the family complains, they are told the process was followed. The fact that the Trust's own written policy required an MDT review before discharging any high-risk patient — a policy that was not followed — only emerges during the PHSO investigation.

**What the Owner does:** Affirm the family's right to a full independent investigation. Provide the PHSO complaint pathway and help the family request all clinical records, including the written discharge policy that was in force at the time.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (post-death family complaint process)
- Opening reply: "I am very sorry for your loss. What you are describing — a discharge with no risk assessment, no contact with your family member, and no handover to any crisis service — is a fact pattern that the Health Service Ombudsman has formally found to constitute a serious system failure. Your family has the right to a full independent investigation and to a clear account of what the Trust failed to do."
- Direct to: NHS formal complaint in writing to the relevant mental health trust (within 12 months of the incident); PHSO (ombudsman.org.uk) if local resolution is unsatisfactory; INQUEST charity (inquest.org.uk) for support when pursuing a state-related death inquiry; Care Quality Commission (cqc.org.uk) to file a regulatory concern; local Healthwatch for practical navigation support
- Evidence to collect: All clinical records including the Community Treatment Team caseload notes (Subject Access Request under UK GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018 — trust must respond within one calendar month); the Trust's written discharge policy in force at the time; records showing no MDT meeting took place; evidence that no phone call or letter was sent to the patient; GP records confirming absence of registration at the time of discharge; any correspondence from the Trust following the complaint
- Feedback category tag: Discharge Without Support / No Patient Contact / Mental Health Rights / Vulnerable Patient / Call Center Failure

### Scenario 6: Patient Discharged from A&E Without the Mandatory Second Cardiac Blood Test — Dies at Home 48 Hours Later (Pattern E)

**Real Basis:** Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman (PHSO) official case summary No. 1052 — anonymized as Mr H, UK, autumn 2013. Attended NHS A&E with chest pain and shortness of breath; discharged after a single troponin test without the second troponin test required under European Society of Cardiology guidance for suspected acute coronary syndrome; no senior physician sign-off obtained as required by NICE guidelines; no follow-up phone call made in the 48 hours after discharge. Died at home two days after discharge. Trust later acknowledged hospital admission would have been more appropriate. Formal apology issued; £1,000 compensation paid. Published at ombudsman.org.uk.

**The Problem:** A person attends A&E with chest pain and shortness of breath. One troponin blood test is taken, returns within the normal range, and they are sent home with a referral to an outpatient chest pain clinic. They are not told that clinical guidelines required a second troponin test before discharge when a heart attack is suspected — and that this test was not performed. The assessing doctor did not obtain the required senior physician sign-off. No one from the hospital calls them in the 48 hours after discharge. At home, they feel increasingly unwell but assume the hospital would have kept them if it were serious. They die of a cardiac event at home two days later.

**What staff/org would do:** No follow-up contact is made in the 48 hours after discharge. After the death, the Trust acknowledges hospital admission would have been more appropriate. A formal apology and £1,000 compensation are offered only after a full Ombudsman investigation — not proactively, and not to prevent the death.

**What the Owner does:** If the patient is alive and symptomatic right now, direct them to call 999 or return to A&E without delay. If this is a family complaint following the death, confirm the right to full clinical records, a formal NHS complaint, and independent Ombudsman review.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (if the patient is still within hours or days of discharge and feeling worse)
- Opening reply: "If you or the person you are concerned about is still experiencing chest pain or worsening symptoms after being discharged, please call 999 or return to A&E right now — do not wait for a scheduled outpatient appointment. Once you are safe, I will help you formally document what went wrong at the assessment."
- Direct to: (If still symptomatic) 999 or immediate A&E return; NHS formal complaint in writing to the trust; PHSO (ombudsman.org.uk) if local resolution fails; a medical negligence solicitor (Law Society Find a Solicitor: solicitors.lawsociety.org.uk); the Coroner if a formal inquest has not yet been held
- Evidence to collect: A&E attendance record and discharge paperwork; the outpatient referral letter; any blood test results given at discharge; clinical notes (obtainable via Subject Access Request — trust must respond within one calendar month); name of the assessing doctor and whether senior sign-off was documented; records confirming no second troponin was ordered; evidence that no phone call was made after discharge
- Feedback category tag: Premature Discharge / Missed Clinical Test / Information Failure / No Follow-Up / Cardiac Emergency

### Scenario 7: Psychiatrist Judges Patient Unsafe for Discharge — Insurance Company Overrides Clinical Decision — Patient Harms Others Within Days (Pattern A)

**Real Basis:** ProPublica investigative report — "Psychiatric Hospitals Are Violating EMTALA by Turning Patients Away" (2026); CMS inspection records — Lakeside Behavioral Health System, Memphis, Tennessee, USA. Tom Swearengen admitted voluntarily with active suicidal ideation and documented access to firearms at home; insurance company's utilization management team declined to authorize inpatient stay; patient discharged against clinical judgment; murder-suicide within one week of discharge. Analogous insurance-override discharge patterns documented in multiple US states under CMS inspection records; equivalent clinical-financial override disputes documented by UK PHSO (Community Mental Health cases) and Australia's Royal Commission into Victoria's Mental Health System (2021).

**The Problem:** A person is voluntarily admitted to a psychiatric facility. Their clinical record documents active suicidal ideation, self-harm desires, and confirmed access to firearms at home. The treating psychiatrist believes inpatient care is medically necessary. The insurance company's utilization management team calls the facility and declines to authorize the inpatient stay on cost grounds. The patient is told they are being discharged — not because the clinical team has concluded they are safe, but because the insurer will not pay. They are given an outpatient appointment slip for three weeks' time. They are standing at the discharge desk right now.

**What staff/org would do:** The facility processes the discharge as insurance-required without filing an appeal on the patient's behalf. No crisis line number is provided. No weapons safety counseling is given despite documented firearm access. The corporate parent later contests the regulatory finding and no financial penalty is imposed.

**What the Owner does:** Immediately identify this as a rights violation. A clinical judgment that a patient requires inpatient care cannot be legally overridden by an insurance company without a formal appeal process. The patient has the right to an immediate peer-to-peer review — the treating psychiatrist speaking directly to the insurer's medical director — and should not leave the facility until that review has been completed.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "You have the right to challenge this discharge before it happens. An insurer cannot legally override your psychiatrist's clinical judgment that you need inpatient care without going through a formal appeal — and you can request a peer-to-peer review right now, which means your psychiatrist speaks directly to the insurer's medical director today, before any discharge is processed."
- Direct to: Request peer-to-peer clinical review immediately (ask the treating psychiatrist to initiate this now, before any discharge paperwork is signed); State Insurance Commissioner complaint line; 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (USA) / Samaritans (UK: 116 123); State Mental Health Authority for emergency evaluation order if discharge proceeds without stabilization; CMS EMTALA complaint (1-800-MEDICARE) if the hospital discharges without adequate clinical stabilization; NAMI patient rights support (nami.org, USA)
- Evidence to collect: The insurance denial letter — request a written copy before leaving the facility; the clinical assessment documentation recording active suicidal ideation and firearm access; the treating psychiatrist's written clinical opinion; name of the insurance case manager who issued the denial; discharge paperwork if issued; any verbal statements from facility staff about the stated reason for discharge
- Feedback category tag: Psychiatric Rights Violation / Insurance Override of Clinical Judgment / Emergency Refusal / Vulnerable Patient / Consumer Turned Away

### Scenario 8: Pregnant Woman Sent to Another Hospital by Private Car Without Any Medical Examination or Stabilization (Pattern A)

**Real Basis:** EMTALA enforcement settlement — OIG settlement, $50,000 penalty, USA, 2018. A 33-week pregnant woman presenting with fluid leakage was sent by private vehicle to another facility without a medical screening examination being performed and without any stabilization measures. She arrived at the receiving facility in critical condition and delivered a stillborn infant requiring emergency cesarean section. Documented by The Sullivan Group EMTALA case compendium referencing OIG settlement data. Inadequate obstetric transfer without clinical stabilization is also documented by WHO maternal health reports and UK MBRRACE-UK Maternal Mortality Reports (2023) across multiple health systems.

**The Problem:** A woman who is 33 weeks pregnant arrives at a hospital having noticed fluid leaking. She is not examined. No medical screening examination is performed. Staff tell her to go to a different hospital and arrange for her to travel there by private car — no ambulance, no clinical escort, no stabilization measures taken. During the journey she feels increasingly unwell. She does not know this is a violation of her rights. She is now at the second hospital in deteriorating condition and does not understand what her legal options are.

**What staff/org would do:** The originating hospital processes the transfer as a routine referral. No medical screening documentation is created. The patient is not told she had the legal right to be examined and stabilized before any transfer was arranged. The OIG settlement penalty goes to the government — not to the patient.

**What the Owner does:** Confirm the patient is receiving proper care right now. Once she is safe, document the transfer in detail and confirm it constitutes a violation of patient rights. Connect her to formal complaint and legal channels.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Right now, your most important step is to make sure you are being examined and monitored by clinical staff at the hospital you are in. Once you are in their care, I will help you document what the first hospital did — because what you are describing is a serious violation of your rights as a patient."
- Direct to: (Immediate) Alert current clinical staff to the circumstances of the transfer; (After safety is secured) CMS EMTALA complaint (1-800-MEDICARE, USA); State Department of Health patient rights division; medical malpractice attorney with EMTALA specialization; Childbirth Connection (childbirthconnection.org) for patient advocacy
- Evidence to collect: Exact time of arrival at the first hospital; name of any staff member who spoke to her there; any paperwork handed to her; whether any physical examination was performed before the transfer; who arranged the private car transport and their stated reason; time of arrival at the second hospital; the receiving facility's documented assessment of her condition on arrival
- Feedback category tag: Emergency Refusal / Obstetric Rights Violation / Inadequate Transfer / Consumer Turned Away / Patient Safety

### Scenario 9: Nurse Administers a Paralytic Drug Instead of a Sedative — Patient Dies — Error Concealed from Family and Licensing Board for Years (Pattern D)

**Real Basis:** NPR; CBS News; criminal court records — RaDonda Vaught, Vanderbilt University Medical Center, Nashville, Tennessee, USA, December 24, 2017. Charlene Murphey, 75, was administered vecuronium (a neuromuscular paralyzing agent) instead of Versed (a sedative) after the nurse overrode a medication cabinet safety lock. Printed warnings on the vial reading "Warning: Paralyzing Agent" were not heeded. Murphey was found unresponsive 30 minutes later; died the following day after life support was withdrawn. Hospital did not proactively disclose the error to the State Board of Nursing or to the family. Error discovered only during an unrelated CMS inspection. Vaught convicted of negligent homicide, March 2022. PSNet (AHRQ) published a patient safety case analysis. Medication cabinet override-related errors are documented by The Joint Commission across healthcare systems globally.

**The Problem:** A 75-year-old patient is being prepared for a brain imaging procedure. A sedative has been ordered. The nurse cannot find it in the medication cabinet and overrides the safety lock. Despite multiple printed warnings on the selected vial, a powerful paralytic drug is administered. The patient is left alone. She is found unresponsive 30 minutes later, resuscitated but never regains consciousness, and dies the following day. The family is not told what medication was given or that any error occurred. They find out the full story through media coverage years later — the hospital had not reported the error to the licensing board and had not disclosed it to the family.

**What staff/org would do:** The hospital does not disclose the medication error to the family or to the State Board of Nursing after the death. The error is discovered only when a CMS inspector investigating an unrelated matter finds documentation of the incident. The hospital then declines to report the nurse to the licensing board, leaving regulators uninformed for years.

**What the Owner does:** Affirm the family's absolute legal right to full disclosure of what medication was administered and what safety checks failed. Confirm that active concealment of a medication error is itself a separate regulatory violation. Provide the specific complaint channels covering both the error and the concealment.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (post-death complaint and disclosure process)
- Opening reply: "You have an absolute right to a full and honest account of exactly what medication was given and why — and to know which safety checks failed and who knew what and when. The law requires hospitals to disclose medical errors. Concealing an error from the family and from the nursing licensing board is itself a regulatory violation, and you have clear channels to pursue both."
- Direct to: State Board of Nursing (complaint about the medication error and any failure to report); State Department of Health; CMS (1-800-MEDICARE); The Joint Commission (if accredited); medical malpractice attorney; hospital Risk Management department via formal written request for all incident documentation under HIPAA patient rights
- Evidence to collect: Full medical records including the medication administration record (MAR) — formal HIPAA request, hospital must respond within 30 days; the incident or occurrence report; identity of all staff involved in the patient's care that day; any communication from the hospital about the cause of death; records documenting when the hospital became aware of the error and what — if anything — was reported to whom and when
- Feedback category tag: Medication Error / Staff Misconduct / Error Concealment / Transparency Failure / Patient Safety

### Scenario 10: Wrong Patient's Medication Administered to Elderly Resident — Severe Allergic Reaction, Medically Induced Coma — Family Never Contacted (Pattern D)

**Real Basis:** WJLA I-Team investigative news report; D.C. Health formal investigation — Capitol City Rehab and Healthcare Center, Washington, D.C., USA, December 2022. Rosezena Jackson, 74, was administered a blood pressure medication belonging to a different patient, to which she was severely allergic. The reaction required a medically induced coma for over one week. The facility had a documented prior history of medication dispensing errors on CMS inspection records. The family discovered what had happened only upon visiting and finding their relative in the ICU — the facility had not contacted them when the error occurred.

**The Problem:** A family member visits an elderly relative at a care facility and finds her in intensive care in a medically induced coma. No one from the facility contacted the family when the error occurred. When the family calls to ask what happened, they are told a manager will call back. No callback comes. The administrator says they cannot discuss patient records over the phone. The family does not know what medication was given, whose medication it was, what the allergic reaction involved, or what the facility has done about it.

**What staff/org would do:** Administration cites privacy regulations as a reason not to discuss the error with the family. Internal processes are described as under review. Promises of callbacks go unfulfilled. The facility's documented prior inspection history for dispensing errors — publicly available via CMS Care Compare — is not mentioned.

**What the Owner does:** Confirm that the patient's authorized family representatives have the full legal right to disclosure and to complete medical records. Privacy regulations do not permit a facility to withhold information about a medication error from the patient's own authorized family. Direct the family to formal regulatory channels immediately.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (patient survived; complaint and disclosure process)
- Opening reply: "As your family member's authorized representatives, you have the legal right to her complete medical records and to a clear written explanation of exactly what medication was administered, why, and what went wrong. Privacy regulations do not allow a care facility to withhold this information from you — that is not a legitimate use of those rules, and you have formal channels to compel full disclosure."
- Direct to: State Department of Health formal complaint (about both the medication error and the failure to notify the family); Long-Term Care Ombudsman program (federally mandated in every US state — locate via eldercare.acl.gov); CMS (1-800-MEDICARE — facilities receiving Medicare or Medicaid funding are federally regulated); medical malpractice attorney; formal medical records request in writing under HIPAA (facility must respond within 30 days)
- Evidence to collect: All medical records including the medication administration record (MAR) and ICU records (formal HIPAA request); the facility's prior CMS inspection history and star rating (publicly available at CMS Care Compare); evidence of when the family was — and was not — notified of the error; names and dates of every call made to the facility and any response received; the patient's current prescribed medication list to confirm the administered drug was not prescribed to her; prior deficiency citations related to medication dispensing
- Feedback category tag: Medication Error / Transparency Failure / Failure to Notify Family / Elder Care / Information Concealment
