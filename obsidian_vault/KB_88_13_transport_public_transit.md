### Scenario 13-1: Flight Cancellation Crisis — Call Center Inaccessible for Days (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** US Department of Transportation enforcement order, December 2023 — Southwest Airlines $140M civil penalty for 2022 holiday meltdown; DOT named call center failure as a standalone consumer protection violation affecting hundreds of thousands of passengers; NPR; CNN Business.
**The Problem:** The passenger's flight was cancelled during a mass operational meltdown. They have been on hold for over 4 hours trying to rebook through the airline's call center. When a call finally connects, the agent cannot access the rebooking system and delivers a scripted apology with no actionable options. The passenger is stranded at the airport with no confirmed route home, no hotel voucher, and no information about when any onward flight exists.
**What staff/org would do:** Transfer the call to another queue, offer a future travel voucher, and tell the passenger to check the airline app — which is also non-functional due to the volume of simultaneous requests across hundreds of thousands of affected passengers.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the stranding situation as urgent. Confirms the passenger's rights under DOT rules: full refund or rebooking at no charge on the next available flight on any carrier; reimbursement of reasonable meals and accommodation if the cancellation was within airline control. Advises documenting all out-of-pocket expenses and directs to the DOT complaint portal if the airline fails to provide options.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a serious situation and I want to help you get through it. Being stranded with no rebooking path is not acceptable — here is exactly what you are owed right now and how to get it, even if the call center remains unreachable."
- Who to direct to: US DOT Aviation Consumer Protection (airconsumer.dot.gov); airline's airport duty manager in person rather than the call center queue; DOT complaint form at transportation.gov/airconsumer; DOT consumer hotline 202-366-2220
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot of the cancellation notice with timestamp; receipts for all expenses incurred (hotel, meals, ground transport); record of all call attempts with start and end times; any agent names or reference numbers received; note if the airline's app or website was inaccessible and when
- Feedback category tag: Emergency / Call Center Failure / Flight Cancellation / Consumer Rights

### Scenario 13-2: Wheelchair Destroyed by Airline — 72 Hours, No Loaner, No Contact (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** US DOT $50M landmark penalty against American Airlines, October 2024, for mishandling 10,760+ wheelchairs and mobility scooters between 2019 and 2023; DOT enforcement order; NPR; Paralyzed Veterans of America formal complaints; The Century Foundation "Trips Not Taken" report documenting 227-day replacement waits.
**The Problem:** A passenger with a physical disability arrived at baggage claim to find their power wheelchair returned with a buckled frame and broken joystick — completely unusable. The baggage agent photographed it, issued a damage report reference number, and said someone would "be in touch." It has now been 72 hours. No contact has been made. The passenger has no mobility device and cannot perform daily activities. Calls to the general baggage line result in transfers between baggage, disability services, and customer relations, with each department having no record of the other's involvement.
**What staff/org would do:** Direct the passenger to wait for the claims department to assess the wheelchair's monetary value before making any offer; provide no loaner device; transfer calls between three departments, none of which can authorise a replacement or rental reimbursement.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as a disability rights emergency, not a standard baggage claim. Confirms rights under the Air Carrier Access Act: the airline must repair or replace the wheelchair promptly and must provide a loaner equivalent device during any repair or replacement period. Escalates directly to DOT's aviation disability hotline and advises the passenger not to accept a monetary settlement without knowing the full replacement cost.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Losing access to your wheelchair is not a baggage inconvenience — it is a mobility emergency and you have specific legal rights that require immediate action from the airline. I am going to help you use those rights right now."
- Who to direct to: DOT Aviation Disability Hotline: 1-800-778-4838; file disability complaint at transportation.gov/airconsumer; Paralyzed Veterans of America (pva.org) for advocacy; ACAA rights at transportation.gov/airconsumer/passengers-disabilities
- Evidence to collect: Damage report reference number and issuing agent's name; photographs of all damage before anything is moved or repaired; written confirmation of when and where the damage was discovered and reported; receipts for any mobility equipment rental, accessible transport, or care costs incurred; documentation of all contact attempts with dates and outcomes
- Feedback category tag: Disability Rights / Accessibility Emergency / Baggage Mishandling / Vulnerability

### Scenario 13-3: Passenger Forcibly Removed from Overbooked Flight — Police Called Instead of Supervisor (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** United Airlines / Dr David Dao incident, 9 April 2017; Chicago O'Hare airport; Wikipedia (2017 United Express passenger removal); NBC News (confidential settlement); Fortune (DOT decision not to issue civil penalty); CNBC; The Points Guy (policy changes enacted post-incident).
**The Problem:** A passenger holding a confirmed paid ticket has been selected for involuntary bumping on an overbooked flight. They cannot give up their seat — they have a medical commitment the following morning that cannot be postponed. Gate agents offered $800 in travel vouchers to volunteers; no one accepted. Rather than increasing the offer or consulting a supervisor with authority to raise the compensation, staff have called airport police and told the passenger they must leave the aircraft or face arrest. No written statement of their DOT rights has been provided.
**What staff/org would do:** Frame the situation as a law enforcement matter and have the passenger physically removed; the initial statement later blames the passenger for being "disruptive," deflecting from the airline's failure to follow its own overbooking compensation procedures or seek a higher volunteer offer.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies this as an involuntary bumping situation governed by DOT rules. Confirms that airlines must seek volunteers before any involuntary removal; that the mandatory compensation for involuntary bumping is up to 400% of the one-way fare (capped at $1,550 under DOT rules); that the passenger must receive a written statement of their rights at the gate. Advises the passenger not to physically resist but to document everything and pursue rights after the fact, where legal standing is strong.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is happening right now and your safety matters most — do not physically resist. But document everything around you, because your legal rights here are clear and enforceable after you are safely off the aircraft."
- Who to direct to: DOT Aviation Consumer Protection complaint portal: airconsumer.dot.gov; airline's customer relations division in writing post-incident; small claims court or civil attorney if mandatory DOT compensation is not paid; DOT consumer hotline 202-366-2220
- Evidence to collect: Boarding pass and booking confirmation; note the voluntary compensation amount offered and to whom; names and badge numbers of gate agents and any law enforcement personnel; video or audio recording of events if safely possible; written note of whether a DOT rights statement was provided; medical documentation confirming why the travel date was non-negotiable
- Feedback category tag: Involuntary Denial of Boarding / Staff Misconduct / Emergency / Consumer Rights

### Scenario 13-4: UK261 Flight Delay Compensation Rejected — Identical Claims Later Paid Under Regulator Pressure (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** UK Civil Aviation Authority enforcement action against Wizz Air, July 2023; CAA compelled re-examination of 25,486 previously rejected claims; approximately 6,000 found to be valid; £1.24M in additional refunds paid; coverage for flights to and from UK airports from 18 March 2022; Simple Flying; Travel Weekly.
**The Problem:** A passenger's flight was delayed 4 hours and 10 minutes. They submitted a claim for £350 compensation under UK261 (the UK's post-Brexit equivalent of EU261). The airline rejected the claim citing "extraordinary circumstances" — a technical fault. The passenger has called customer service twice; both times a scripted response confirmed the rejection and said further review at that level was not possible. The passenger believes the fault was within the airline's operational control, not an external event.
**What staff/org would do:** Send a template rejection letter with the phrase "extraordinary circumstances" without specifying which external event caused the delay; offer a £50 travel voucher as a goodwill gesture; confirm on any follow-up call that the decision is final.
**What the Owner does:** Explains that "extraordinary circumstances" under UK261 is a precise legal definition that excludes the vast majority of technical faults — only manufacturer defects or hidden faults that could not have been detected through scheduled maintenance typically qualify. Regulatory enforcement history, including the CAA finding that this airline wrongly rejected thousands of comparable claims, supports challenging the rejection directly.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Your frustration is completely valid. 'Extraordinary circumstances' is a specific legal term, and technical faults within the airline's maintenance control rarely qualify. Here is how to challenge this rejection — and why the odds are in your favour."
- Who to direct to: AviationADR (aviationadr.org.uk) — the UK's free alternative dispute resolution body for aviation; UK Civil Aviation Authority passenger complaint portal (caa.co.uk/passengers); Citizens Advice for UK261 rights guidance (citizensadvice.org.uk)
- Evidence to collect: Original flight booking confirmation; airline's rejection letter with the exact reason stated; flight tracking data showing actual vs scheduled departure and arrival times (flightradar24.com or similar); any airline communication identifying the specific cause of delay; dates and reference numbers for all contacts with the airline; keep the travel voucher offer in writing as evidence the airline acknowledged the delay
- Feedback category tag: Compensation Refusal / Flight Delay / UK261 Rights / Financial Harm

### Scenario 13-5: Train Delay Repay Claim — 6 Weeks, No Response, CRM System Has No Record (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Office of Rail and Road (ORR) enforcement action against West Midlands Trains, 2023; ORR finding WMT was processing only 18% of complaints within the 20-working-day legal obligation; broken CRM system forcing customers to restate cases on every contact; ORR Annual Rail Consumer Report 2023–2024; UK overall complaint handling satisfaction rate of 31% (2024–25).
**The Problem:** A rail commuter submitted a Delay Repay compensation claim for a 35-minute delay — which qualifies for 50% of the single fare under the operator's Delay Repay scheme. Six weeks have passed with no response. When they call the customer relations line, the agent has no record of the original claim and asks them to submit again. After resubmitting, a further 10 days pass with no acknowledgment. A third call produces the same request to resubmit from a different agent who also cannot find any case history.
**What staff/org would do:** Ask the customer to resubmit via the online form each time, provide a generic acknowledgment with no case reference number, and advise waiting a further 20 working days — resetting the clock with each resubmission.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that the 20-working-day legal response obligation under the National Rail Conditions of Travel has been breached multiple times. Explains the escalation path to the Rail Ombudsman, which the passenger can use after the operator fails to resolve the complaint or after 40 working days from the original submission. Advises the passenger to preserve all submission evidence as proof of the timeline.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The operator has missed its legal response deadline more than once. You do not need to keep resubmitting — you now have the right to take this to the independent Rail Ombudsman, at no cost to you."
- Who to direct to: Rail Ombudsman (railombudsman.org) — free, independent, and binding on operators; Transport Focus (transportfocus.org.uk) for passenger advocacy; ORR passenger information at orr.gov.uk/consumers
- Evidence to collect: Original claim submission — screenshot or confirmation email with the submission date; every subsequent contact with dates, agent names if given, and any reference numbers; train ticket or contactless payment record for the journey; timetabled vs actual times (available via National Rail journey history or the operator's app); any automated acknowledgment emails received at any stage
- Feedback category tag: Compensation Delay / Call Center Failure / Response Obligation Breach / Rail

### Scenario 13-6: Guide Dog User Refused by Licensed Taxi Driver — Third Refusal This Month (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** UK House of Commons Transport Committee "Access Denied: Rights versus Reality in Disabled People's Access to Transport," 2023; evidence from Stephen Anderson that guide dog user taxi refusals had doubled since the end of Covid lockdowns; Guide Dogs UK data; 70+ pieces of written evidence submitted; Committee finding that disabled access progress had gone into reverse since the pandemic.
**The Problem:** A blind passenger with a registered guide dog has just been refused by a licensed taxi driver who states he has a pet allergy. This is the third refusal the passenger has experienced in four weeks. They are now stranded and running late for a hospital appointment. The taxi company's dispatch line, when called, says they will "note it on the driver's record" and advises trying to book another cab — but makes no attempt to send an alternative vehicle and offers no acknowledgment of the legal position.
**What staff/org would do:** Log the complaint as a driver preference issue; suggest the passenger use a different service; take no immediate enforcement action and provide no written confirmation that the refusal has been recorded as a potential Equality Act breach.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an active discrimination incident and a medical appointment emergency simultaneously. Explains the legal position: refusing a guide dog user is unlawful disability discrimination under the Equality Act 2010 and a specific criminal offence under Section 168 of the Act; a driver may only be exempt if they hold a current medical exemption certificate issued by the local licensing authority — they cannot self-declare an allergy at the point of contact. Provides both immediate practical help and the reporting pathway.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is unlawful discrimination and I want to help you get to your appointment and hold this driver accountable. Here is what you are entitled to, what the driver's legal position actually is, and exactly who to call right now."
- Who to direct to: Local council taxi licensing department — report the driver and request sight of any medical exemption certificate on file; Equality Advisory and Support Service (EASS): 0808 800 0082; Guide Dogs UK advocacy team (guidedogs.org.uk/about-us/policy-campaigns); police non-emergency line 101 if the driver was abusive or threatening
- Evidence to collect: Taxi licence plate number; driver badge number if visible on the vehicle or driver; exact time, date, and location of each refusal; name or booking reference of the taxi company or app used; written record of dispatch operator's response and what action they said they would take; note whether the driver claimed to hold a medical exemption certificate
- Feedback category tag: Disability Discrimination / Service Refusal / Equality Act / Vulnerability

### Scenario 13-7: Fatal Bus Accident — Family Cannot Reach Compensation Process Across Four Departments (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Mumbai BEST (Brihanmumbai Electric Supply and Transport) bus accident, Kurla, December 2024; 7 killed, 49 injured; driver arrested for culpable homicide; BEST initial offer of ₹2 lakh per deceased family widely criticised as inadequate; Maharashtra Chief Minister separately announced ₹5 lakh from relief fund; Free Press Journal; Lok Mat Times; The News Minute.
**The Problem:** A family member of one of the victims killed in the bus accident is attempting to initiate a compensation claim. BEST's main administrative number is not being answered. When a call eventually connects after repeated attempts, the family member is transferred to the operations department, then to the legal department, then told to attend a BEST office in person. At the office, staff say the compensation scheme has not been finalised and to call back the following week. No written information about eligibility, amounts, or the process is provided.
**What staff/org would do:** Provide inconsistent information across departments, direct the family between phone lines and physical offices without resolution, and offer no written confirmation of the compensation process or timeline — leaving the family without clarity on whether to accept the ₹2 lakh administrative offer or pursue legal action.
**What the Owner does:** Provides immediate clarity on the legal landscape. Confirms that the family has two parallel paths: the BEST administrative payment (now enhanced by CM Relief Fund to ₹5 lakh) and an independent Motor Accident Claims Tribunal claim for full civil damages, which is not extinguished by accepting administrative relief. Directs to free legal aid to navigate both paths simultaneously within limitation periods.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "I am deeply sorry for what your family is going through. While no process can address your loss, you have legal rights that must be acted on now to protect your family's full entitlement. Here is how to move forward without being passed between offices."
- Who to direct to: Motor Accident Claims Tribunal (MACT) — engage a lawyer to file a claim within three years under the Motor Vehicles Act; National Legal Services Authority (NALSA) for free legal aid: nalsa.gov.in or call 15100; Maharashtra State Legal Services Authority (MSLA) for state-level representation; National Consumer Helpline: 1915 for guidance on administrative claims
- Evidence to collect: Death certificate; copy of the FIR (First Information Report) filed against the driver; all BEST correspondence with dates and reference numbers; written record of the ₹2 lakh and ₹5 lakh announcements; hospital, mortuary, and funeral expense records; documentation establishing dependents and their relationship to the deceased
- Feedback category tag: Fatal Accident / Emergency / Compensation / Vulnerability / Staff Misconduct

### Scenario 13-8: State Bus Operator Contests Child Death Award Through Successive Courts (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Supreme Court of India 2024 ruling restoring ₹8.55 lakh Motor Accident Claims Tribunal award after the Madras High Court reduced it; Tamil Nadu State Transport Corporation held liable for death of a 10-year-old child; parallel 2024 Patna High Court ruling enhancing compensation to ₹33.21 lakh with 6% interest; India Legal; Samvida Law case analysis.
**The Problem:** The parents of a child killed by a state-run bus have been awarded compensation by the Motor Accident Claims Tribunal. The state transport corporation has now appealed to the High Court, which reduced the award amount. The parents cannot afford extended litigation. When they contact the transport corporation's administrative office to ask whether any interim payment is possible while the appeal runs, they are told the matter is "before the court" and no communication is possible outside legal proceedings — effectively leaving the family without any compensation for an indeterminate period.
**What staff/org would do:** Refer all communication to the state's legal department; make no interim payment; advise the family to await the judicial process without providing any estimate of duration or any information about interim relief mechanisms available under the Motor Vehicles Act.
**What the Owner does:** Clarifies that the family is not powerless during the appellate process. The Supreme Court has consistently restored MACT awards reduced by High Courts in comparable 2024 cases. The Motor Vehicles Act provides a mechanism for interim payment pending appeal. Free legal representation is available through state legal services authorities. Accepting the administrative "wait" framing would delay legitimate relief by years unnecessarily.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The state transport corporation's position — that you must simply wait — is not your only option. The Supreme Court ruled in favour of families in exactly this situation in 2024, and you can apply for interim compensation now. Here is how."
- Who to direct to: State Legal Services Authority (SLSA) for free legal representation in High Court proceedings; District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) to apply for interim compensation under Section 168A Motor Vehicles Act; National Consumer Helpline: 1915; Supreme Court Legal Aid Committee if the matter reaches that level
- Evidence to collect: Full MACT judgment with the original award amount; High Court appeal notice and any interim orders already passed; the transport corporation's written refusal to make interim payment; all original accident documentation; school enrolment and age documentation for the child; evidence of the family's financial dependence on the deceased
- Feedback category tag: Compensation Dispute / Public Transport Liability / Escalation Refused / Transparency Failure

### Scenario 13-9: Lost Bag — Airline Offers ₹2,450, Consumer Forum Awards ₹45,000 in Equivalent Case (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Air India lost baggage consumer forum case, Mumbai District Consumer Forum, 2025; HappyFares analysis of 47 public consumer forum filings 2023–2025 finding 60% resulted in awards higher than airline's initial offer with a median escalation duration of 187 days; INGRAM / National Consumer Helpline data; pattern of airlines offering systematically below-minimum settlements at first contact.
**The Problem:** An Air India passenger's checked bag was lost on a domestic route. The airline's claims team has offered ₹2,450 as full and final settlement — approximately the cost of a single item of clothing. The bag contained electronics, professional documents, and clothing valued considerably higher. Three follow-up calls over four weeks have each produced the same scripted answer: the settlement is fixed under airline policy and cannot be changed at this level. No written explanation of how the ₹2,450 figure was calculated has been provided.
**What staff/org would do:** Maintain the ₹2,450 offer as non-negotiable, reference an internal liability cap without citing its legal basis, and advise the passenger to accept in writing or close the case — relying on the friction and unfamiliarity of escalation to prevent most passengers from pursuing the claim further.
**What the Owner does:** Explains that airline internal policy caps do not override the passenger's rights under the Consumer Protection Act 2019 or, for international flights, the Montreal Convention. The consumer forum award data shows that in 60% of equivalent baggage cases, courts awarded substantially more than the initial airline offer — the median uplift is 18 times the airline's first figure. Gives a clear, simple escalation path with realistic time expectations.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "₹2,450 is almost certainly well below your legal entitlement. Airlines rely on passengers not escalating — but consumer forums consistently award far more in identical cases, and the process is less complex than it sounds. Here is how to pursue this."
- Who to direct to: National Consumer Helpline: 1915 and INGRAM portal (consumerhelpline.gov.in) for complaint registration; District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission under the Consumer Protection Act 2019 — no lawyer required for claims under ₹50 lakh; DGCA passenger complaint portal at dgca.gov.in; for international flights, the Directorate of Air Transport
- Evidence to collect: Booking confirmation and baggage receipt/tag numbers; Property Irregularity Report (PIR) filed at the airport with date and reference number; itemised list of lost bag contents with purchase receipts, credit card statements, or estimated replacement values; all airline correspondence including the ₹2,450 offer letter; receipts for essential replacement purchases made while the bag was missing; record of all call contacts with dates and any agent reference numbers
- Feedback category tag: Baggage Loss / Financial Harm / Under-Compensation / Consumer Forum

### Scenario 13-10: Transit Card Overcharges on Public Holiday — No Proactive Refund, Form Loop Only (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Myki (Melbourne) contactless transit card system failure to apply the Public Holiday Daily Fare Cap, January 2022; passengers systematically overcharged rather than charged at the lower public holiday rate; Public Transport Victoria made no proactive remediation or outreach to affected cardholders; passengers required to identify the error individually and submit online refund forms manually; documented in Waking Up in Geelong; Wikipedia Myki criticism section; Myki history of GPS zone errors causing incorrect fare calculations.
**The Problem:** A passenger reviews their Myki account transaction history and notices they were charged the standard daily fare cap on a public holiday, when the lower public holiday cap should have applied — an overcharge of $4.60. They call the Myki contact line and are told to submit an online refund form. After submitting, they receive an automated confirmation but no further communication for three weeks. A second call produces the same instruction: submit the form and wait up to 30 days. The agent gives no information about whether this is a known system error affecting other passengers, and makes no attempt to process the refund during the call.
**What staff/org would do:** Script: redirect to the online form, quote the 30-day processing window, and provide no information about whether the overcharge was systemic or isolated — placing the entire burden of identification, documentation, and follow-up on the individual passenger.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the systemic nature of the issue. Myki has a documented history of fare cap and zone calculation errors; the public holiday cap failure in January 2022 affected multiple passengers, none of whom received proactive notification. Confirms the passenger's right to a full refund and explains that if the form process does not resolve within the stated timeframe, a formal complaint to the Public Transport Ombudsman is available at no cost.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "This is a genuine system error and you are entitled to a full refund. The form is the right first step, but here is what to do if it stalls — and why this matters beyond the $4.60 alone."
- Who to direct to: Public Transport Victoria formal complaint pathway at ptv.vic.gov.au — distinct from the refund form submission; Public Transport Ombudsman Victoria (ptovic.com.au) if PTV does not resolve within 30 days; Consumer Affairs Victoria (consumer.vic.gov.au) for systemic overcharging complaints affecting multiple passengers
- Evidence to collect: Myki account transaction history showing the public holiday date, fare charged, and the correct public holiday cap amount; screenshot of the refund form submission confirmation with submission date and time; record of all follow-up calls with dates, agent names if provided, and any reference numbers given; note any automated acknowledgment emails received at each stage and their content
- Feedback category tag: Fare Overcharge / System Error / Transparency Failure / Financial Harm
