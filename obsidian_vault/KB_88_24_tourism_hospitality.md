## 24. Tourism / Hospitality

### Scenario 24-1: Thomas Cook Collapse — Family Stranded Abroad With No Return Flights (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** CAA/UK Civil Aviation Authority ATOL refund data; National Audit Office investigation into Thomas Cook collapse; ABTA guidance (September 2019) — 150,000 UK tourists stranded across 54 airports; Operation Matterhorn repatriated ~94% of passengers over 15 days
**The Problem:** A British family of four is on day two of a two-week holiday in Majorca. They have just read on their phones that Thomas Cook has ceased trading overnight. Their return flights no longer exist. The hotel has told them they must "make their own arrangements" within 24 hours as the booking no longer has a valid payment reference. They cannot get through on Thomas Cook's defunct customer service number, and ABTA's phone line is playing an automated message. They do not know if they have ATOL protection.
**What staff/org would do:** Direct the family back to the Thomas Cook number that no longer works; advise them to "wait and see" before taking any action; fail to mention that the CAA has a dedicated repatriation operation actively running right now with free flights home.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately confirms the family should not pay the hotel again or book their own flights yet. Directs them to the UK CAA's dedicated Thomas Cook information page, explains that Operation Matterhorn is running free repatriation flights right now for package holiday customers, and tells them to check their booking confirmation for an ATOL certificate number. Advises them to document every hotel communication in writing in case ATOL claims are needed, and tells them to stay at the hotel until the CAA flight notification arrives.

### Scenario 24-2: Resort Food Poisoning Cluster — Tour Operator Dismisses Illness Complaints Before Deaths (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Times of Israel, Egyptian Streets, Hospitality Inside reporting on Steigenberger Aqua Magic Hotel, Hurghada, Egypt (August 2018); Sick Holiday / YorkTest illness-claim database documenting 380 UK tourist illness claims from Hurghada since 2017; guest Alison Cope's documented testimony that Thomas Cook "fobbed off" earlier complaints
**The Problem:** A British guest at a large all-inclusive resort in Hurghada has been violently ill for 36 hours with severe vomiting, diarrhoea, and high fever. She reported her illness to the resort medical centre yesterday; staff told her to drink more water and gave her a single oral rehydration sachet. She has since learned that three other guests on her floor have identical symptoms. Her tour operator's in-resort representative dismissed her concern by phone, saying "there's always a settling-in period in a new climate." She can no longer keep any fluids down.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue reassuring the guest that symptoms are normal; delay calling a doctor; fail to flag the illness cluster to resort management or Egyptian health authorities; keep her in the resort rather than arranging hospital transfer, citing "policy."
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as a medical emergency requiring immediate escalation. Instructs her to demand hospital transfer in writing — not just resort medical centre attention — and to contact the British Embassy emergency line if the operator representative remains unresponsive. Tells her that Egyptian regulations require hotels to report illness clusters, and she can invoke this formally. Advises her she has full rights under the UK Package Travel Regulations 2018 to demand alternative accommodation and medical cost reimbursement if the operator fails to act.

### Scenario 24-3: All-Inclusive Resort Food Poisoning — Family Refused Hospital Transfer as Child Deteriorates (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** CBC News, Lawyer Monthly, PAX News reporting on Viva Dominicus Beach by Wyndham Resort, Dominican Republic — Gougeon family deaths December 2023; Dominican Republic Coroner's Report; $10 million lawsuit filed January 2025 naming Wyndham Hotels, Air Transat Holidays, and the resort's on-site medical clinic
**The Problem:** A Canadian couple arrived at a Wyndham all-inclusive resort in the Dominican Republic with their two children. Within hours of eating at the resort buffet, all four are severely ill. Both parents and their elder child have been taken to the resort's medical clinic; the on-site doctor has told them it is "stomach upset from the flight" and given anti-nausea tablets. The younger child's condition is deteriorating rapidly. The father is demanding immediate hospital transfer; resort staff are telling him to "give the medicine time" and that leaving the resort "will void the medical coverage."

Instructs him to call an ambulance directly (Dominican Republic emergency: 911) or demand the resort call one immediately without waiting for staff agreement. Tells him to photograph the buffet and all food served, keep any food packaging visible, and send video immediately to a family member abroad for safekeeping. Advises him to contact the Canadian Embassy in Santo Domingo and his travel insurer's 24-hour emergency line simultaneously.

### Scenario 24-4: Hotel Files Criminal Defamation Charges Against Guest for Negative Online Review (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Fox News Travel, View from the Wing, Divernet reporting on Wesley Barnes arrest — Sea View Resort & Spa, Koh Chang, Thailand (2019); TripAdvisor's documented "Alarming Business Practices" warning badge created specifically in response to this case; Thailand's Computer Crimes Act (amended 2017) carrying penalties up to five years imprisonment
**The Problem:** A foreign tourist posted a one-star Google review of a Thai beach resort two weeks ago, describing rude staff and a dirty room. He has just received a formal letter from a Thai lawyer stating that the resort owner has filed a criminal defamation complaint under Thailand's Criminal Defamation Act and Computer Crimes Act. The letter demands he appear at a local police station within five days and states he faces up to two years in prison. He is still in Thailand on a tourist visa and cannot leave the country while charges are pending.
**What staff/org would do:** Advise the tourist to simply take down the review and apologise publicly to make the case go away — without explaining that a public apology may constitute an implicit admission of guilt or be weaponised against him in further proceedings, and without recommending legal counsel.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately advises the tourist to make no public statements, not delete the review, and not issue any apology without consulting a Thai criminal defence lawyer first, as doing so may seriously prejudice his legal position. Tells him to register with the US Embassy in Bangkok immediately, and to retain a lawyer before responding to any police summons. Explains that this is a documented pattern in Thailand — not an isolated event — and that TripAdvisor has a formal process for marking properties that use legal threats against reviewers with an "Alarming Business Practices" warning.

### Scenario 24-5: Phone-Spoofed Fake Airline Call Center Charges Fraudulent Rebooking Fees (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** BBB Warning press release — Flightsassist, Midtown Manhattan operation, 109 BBB complaints across 30 US states, F rating (2021–2024); documented pattern of phone number spoofing to impersonate airline reservations lines, charging consumers for non-existent "problems with their bookings"
**The Problem:** A traveller in Texas searched online for "Delta Airlines customer service" and called the top result. The agent confirmed they were "Delta reservations" and told her there was "a problem with her upcoming booking" that required immediate correction or her ticket would be cancelled. She paid $340 by credit card to "re-confirm" the booking. She has now called the real Delta Airlines and been told they have no record of the payment, her original booking is completely unaffected, and they have no authorised third-party agents who contact customers in this way. The fraudulent call center number is now unreachable.
**What staff/org would do:** Tell the consumer "there is nothing we can do since you authorised the charge"; treat it as a merchant dispute rather than fraud; fail to advise on the Section 75 or chargeback pathway; not mention reporting to the FTC, FBI IC3, or Texas AG.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately confirms this is a documented phone spoofing fraud scheme targeting airline customers and that there is a strong pathway to recovering her money. Advises her to call her credit card issuer immediately and report it as fraud — specifically as an "unauthorised transaction / fraud," not a standard merchant dispute — as this changes the processing pathway and gives her the strongest recourse. Tells her to file reports with the FTC, BBB Scam Tracker, and FBI IC3 today, and that the Texas AG's office has taken specific enforcement action against operators running this exact scheme.

- Opening reply: "What you have described is a documented travel booking fraud scheme where scammers impersonate airline call centres. You have a strong case for a full refund through your credit card issuer. Here is what to do right now."

### Scenario 24-7: Resort Pool Child Drowning — Safety Accountability and Evidence Preservation (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Hawaii News Now, Maui News, KITV4, eTurboNews reporting on Westin Maui Resort & Spa — Daelyn Ntwaagae, 4 years old, Kaanapali, Hawaii (1 May 2023); media coverage documented it was "unclear if lifeguards were on duty and what, if anything, they did to assist"; Marriott-branded property made no public statement on safety protocols
**The Problem:** A couple staying at a major branded resort in Hawaii is at the poolside after their four-year-old was found unresponsive in the resort's main swimming pool. Emergency services have been called and the child is being transported to hospital. The parents are in shock. Resort security is telling them to "move to an office" before they have had any opportunity to document anything at the scene. A resort front desk manager has told them "the resort cannot make any statements at this time."
**What staff/org would do:** Move the parents away from the pool scene, prevent any witness contact, decline to confirm whether lifeguards were on duty, and route all further communication through a legal department that will not engage for days.
**What the Owner does:** Advises the parents — or a trusted person helping them — that before moving from the poolside they need to document the scene: photograph whether lifeguard chairs are staffed, whether any "no lifeguard on duty" or supervision signs are posted, and whether any safety equipment is in place. Advises them to note the names and contact details of any witnesses who approach voluntarily, and to formally request in writing from the duty manager a copy of the pool safety logbook and lifeguard duty roster for that day. Tells them to file an incident report directly with Maui Police Department rather than through the resort, and to consult a personal injury attorney before signing any resort documents.

### Scenario 24-8: Cross-Border Hotel Cancellation — OTA Forces Voucher, Each Party Blames the Other (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** ECC-Net Annual Report 2023 — 124,119 cross-border consumer queries handled; 21% involving cancelled accommodation services; €8.8 million recovered through direct mediation in 22,500 cases; European Commission documentation of structural cross-border call center barrier where consumer in Country A, OTA in Country B, and hotel in Country C creates unresolvable transfer loop
**The Problem:** A German tourist booked three nights at a hotel in Lisbon through an international OTA registered in the Netherlands. Four days before arrival the hotel cancelled her booking, citing "renovation work." The OTA is refusing a full cash refund and offering only a voucher for a future OTA booking. She does not want a voucher; she wants her €540 back. She has called the OTA's German customer service line three times; each time the agent tells her the refund decision is "the hotel's decision, not ours," and the hotel tells her it is "the OTA's decision, not ours." She cannot absorb the loss.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue directing the consumer between the hotel and the OTA indefinitely, with each citing the other's responsibility; present the voucher as the only resolution; not mention the EU Alternative Dispute Resolution framework or her rights under EU law for cancelled services.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms clearly that under the EU Consumer Rights Directive, where a service is cancelled by the provider, the consumer is entitled to a full cash refund — not a voucher — within 14 days. Advises her to file a formal complaint with ECC Germany, which will mediate directly with the Dutch-registered OTA on her behalf under the ECC-Net cross-border framework at no cost. Advises her to also file simultaneously with the Dutch ACM as the OTA's home regulator. Explains that if mediation fails, the EU Small Claims Procedure allows cross-border claims up to €5,000.

### Scenario 24-9: Flights-Only Booking Not Covered by ATOL — Consumer Discovers Hidden Coverage Gap After Collapse (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** CAA ATOL refund data; National Audit Office investigation into Thomas Cook collapse; ABTA guidance; HuffPost UK reporting (September–December 2019) — approximately 55% of Thomas Cook passengers held flight-only bookings with no ATOL protection; the coverage distinction was not disclosed at the point of sale
**The Problem:** A couple booked flights to Cyprus directly through Thomas Cook Airlines online three months ago — not as part of a package holiday. Thomas Cook has collapsed overnight. They have seen the government announcements about ATOL protection and free repatriation flights. When they check their booking confirmation, there is no ATOL certificate attached. Their outbound flight tomorrow is cancelled. The CAA website states their booking type may not be covered. They paid £680 and had no idea there was any distinction between a package holiday and a direct flights-only booking that would affect their legal protection.
**What staff/org would do:** Direct them to the now-defunct Thomas Cook customer service line; tell them "ATOL should cover you" without checking the booking type first; fail to explain the legal distinction between package and flight-only bookings and the completely different recovery pathways that apply.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms clearly that flight-only bookings are not covered by ATOL and that this gap affected approximately 55% of Thomas Cook customers — a systemic information failure, not a mistake on their part. Tells them they have two alternative recovery pathways available right now: a Section 75 claim against their credit card issuer if they paid by credit card and the cost exceeded £100 per card, or a chargeback via their bank if they paid by debit card. Advises them to act immediately as card issuers have strict time windows, and to check their travel insurance policy for tour operator insolvency cover.

### Scenario 24-10: Vacation Rental Does Not Exist on Arrival — Wire Transfer Lost, Family Stranded Abroad (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** FTC Consumer Sentinel Network 2024 Data Book (published March 2025) — nearly 50,000 vacation rental fraud complaints filed in 2024, losses exceeding $10 million; FTC Consumer Advice on vacation rental scams documenting hijacked listings, wire transfer demands, and non-existent properties; Mastercard Economics Institute research showing fraud spikes 28% during peak travel seasons
**The Problem:** A family of five flew to Cancún for two weeks, having booked a private villa through what appeared to be a legitimate listing on a major rental platform. The listing required payment of €2,400 by wire transfer directly to the "property owner," explaining this avoided "platform fees." They have just arrived at the listed address. The property does not exist — the address is a closed commercial building. The phone number for the owner is disconnected. Their platform account now shows the listing has been removed. They have no accommodation and have lost €2,400.
**What staff/org would do:** Tell the family "we are not responsible for third-party listings"; direct them to file an online report with an expected response time of 5–7 business days; fail to explain that a police report is legally required for any compensation claim and that the wire transfer must be reported to the sending bank within 24 hours for any chance of recall.
**What the Owner does:** Tells the family that securing safe accommodation tonight is the absolute immediate priority. Then advises them to file a formal police report — a Denuncia — at the local Mexican Ministerio Público before anything else, as this is required for every insurance and platform claim to succeed. Explains that wire transfer recall is very difficult but not impossible if reported to the sending bank within 24 hours citing fraud. Advises them to contact their travel insurer's emergency line and the rental platform's trust and safety team in writing, citing that the platform hosted a fraudulent listing.
