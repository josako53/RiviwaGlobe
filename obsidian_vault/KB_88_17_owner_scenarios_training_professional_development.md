### Scenario 17-1: Worker Discovers His OSHA Safety Card Is Fraudulent — Never Received Any Training (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Manhattan DA indictment and guilty plea, Valor Security & Investigations (February 2024 / June 2025); Safety+Health Magazine; Equipment World. Valor issued approximately 20,000 fraudulent construction safety credentials without providing any training; worker Ivan A. Frias died on 28 November 2022 at 263 West End Avenue, Manhattan, allegedly having received no fall protection training from Valor.
**The Problem:** A construction worker has just been told by his site safety manager that his 40-hour Site Safety Training card — purchased through a broker for $480 and issued by Valor Security & Investigations — failed a spot-check verification scan. He has been working at height on a 20-storey Manhattan project for seven months and has never attended any fall protection training. His employment is now suspended pending investigation. He does not know whether he is a victim or whether he faces criminal exposure for using the card.
**What staff/org would do:** The construction company's HR department hands him a suspension letter and tells him to "resolve the card issue" before returning. The broker who sold him the card does not answer calls. No referral is made to any safety authority or support body.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges immediately that this is both a safety emergency and a potential injustice — the worker was likely a victim of a fraudulent certification scheme, not a perpetrator. Gives him clear, calm guidance on what to do right now.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a serious situation and your safety matters first. Based on what you have described, you are most likely a victim of a fraudulent training provider — not someone who did anything wrong. Here is what to do right now."
- Direct to: Manhattan District Attorney's Office (manhattanda.org) — report as a victim of the Valor Security fraud scheme; NYC Department of Buildings (nyc.gov/buildings) — report the fraudulent card and request guidance on your situation; OSHA (osha.gov) — report the uncertified training provider; retain an employment attorney regarding wrongful suspension if you acted in good faith as a victim
- Evidence to collect: Receipt or proof of payment to the broker; all communications (text, email) from the broker or Valor; the physical card itself; the broker's name and contact details; dates of purchase and any enrollment confirmation
- Feedback category tag: safety_urgent / fraud / worker_rights_violation

### Scenario 17-2: Military Spouse Misled by Healthcare Career Training — Diploma Withheld as Leverage (Pattern B, D)
**Real Basis:** FTC settlement with Career Step LLC, July 2024 ($43.5 million — $15.7M cash refunds plus $27.8M debt cancellation); FTC consumer alert; State AG reports. Career Step targeted military servicemembers and their spouses with false claims that 80%+ of graduates were placed with healthcare employer partners including CVS and Walgreens; actual employment rate was 8% of completers; diplomas and transcripts were withheld as leverage over students who disputed their accounts.
**The Problem:** A military spouse enrolled in Career Step's medical coding program after an admissions agent — and the company's AI chatbot — told her that 82% of graduates were placed with healthcare employer partners including CVS and Walgreens. She paid $3,200 and completed the program. No placement came. When she asked for her diploma and official transcript to apply to employers directly, she was told her records were on hold due to an "outstanding account query." There is no outstanding balance she recognises. She suspects her credentials are being withheld to pressure her into silence.
**What staff/org would do:** Customer service tells her to "wait for our accounts team to review" and places her on a callback queue that never calls back. The accounts team sends a form letter. Her diploma remains inaccessible.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises this as the specific misconduct named in the FTC enforcement action — Career Step withheld diplomas and transcripts as leverage — and tells her she has concrete legal rights and a clear enforcement pathway.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What has happened to you is not a misunderstanding — it matches a pattern of conduct that a US regulator formally sanctioned Career Step for. You have rights and a clear route to get your diploma and any refund you are owed."
- Direct to: FTC refund portal (ftc.gov) — check eligibility for the $15.7M refund fund distributed to former Career Step students; Utah Attorney General's Office (agutah.gov) — Career Step's home state; if the disputed debt was placed on your credit report, contact Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion with the FTC settlement documentation — the settlement mandated deletion of enrolled students' debts from credit reports
- Evidence to collect: All enrollment communications including chatbot transcripts, emails, and any recorded phone calls; copy of the original enrollment contract; payment receipts; any written or verbal statements about job placement rates or employer partnerships; all correspondence about the withheld diploma or transcript
- Feedback category tag: financial_fraud / withheld_credentials / military_consumer_rights

### Scenario 17-3: Vocational Training Call Centre Refused Refund and Sent Debt Collectors (Pattern B, D, F)
**Real Basis:** ACCC Federal Court proceedings against Get Qualified Australia, September–December 2016 (accc.gov.au). Call centre staff were unqualified to assess consumer eligibility, used false scarcity pressure, charged upfront fees of $700–$8,500, promised a "100% money back guarantee" that was "almost always" refused, and subsequently pursued debt collection against dissatisfied consumers who sought to enforce it.
**The Problem:** A consumer paid $4,200 upfront to Get Qualified Australia after a call centre agent told her she was "definitely eligible" for a workplace health and safety qualification and that "only three places remain this week." The qualification has not been delivered — she was told weeks later that she does not meet entry requirements that were never disclosed before payment. She called back to invoke the "100% money back guarantee" she was read during the original sales call. The agent told her the guarantee "doesn't cover this situation," put her on hold for 22 minutes, then transferred her to a second agent who repeated the same refusal. She has now received two calls from a debt recovery company claiming she owes a further $1,800 for course materials she never received.
**What staff/org would do:** The company's customer service team continues to deny the guarantee applies, offers no escalation path, and allows debt collection calls to continue. Agents provide contradictory information across different calls.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises the documented call centre failure pattern — unqualified agent, false eligibility assurance, guaranteed refund refused, debt collection threat — and gives the consumer specific regulatory and legal options.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You were given false information by an unqualified sales agent, charged upfront without proper disclosure, and your guaranteed refund was refused. This conduct was found unlawful by an Australian court. Here is what you can do to stop the debt collection and recover your money."
- Direct to: Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (accc.gov.au/consumers/complaints) — this is the exact enforcement body that took Get Qualified Australia to Federal Court; your state or territory consumer affairs office (Consumer Affairs Victoria, NSW Fair Trading, etc.) — file a formal complaint; if debt collectors continue to contact you, file a complaint with ASIC (asic.gov.au) under the National Credit Code; consider a free consultation with a community legal centre for assistance writing a formal dispute letter
- Evidence to collect: Notes from the original sales call including date, time, and agent name or ID; copy of the enrollment contract; payment receipt; any written documentation of the money back guarantee; records of all callback attempts (dates, hold times, agent names); copies of debt collection letters or notes on calls received
- Feedback category tag: call_centre_failure / refund_refused / debt_collection / financial_harm

### Scenario 17-4: Disadvantaged Student Unknowingly Enrolled in Two VET Courses — $74,000 Debt (Pattern B, C)
**Real Basis:** ACCC v Phoenix Institute of Australia and Community Training Initiatives, Federal Court ($438M penalty ordered July 2023; ACCC media releases 2021 and 2023). Phoenix and CTI enrolled over 11,000 students in VET FEE-HELP courses by falsely representing courses as "free"; students were enrolled in two programs simultaneously without understanding; the Federal Court found students were "disadvantaged individuals" with "no reasonable prospect of completing" the courses; average debt exceeded $37,000 per student; only 9 of 11,000 students completed even one course.
**The Problem:** A 34-year-old woman with limited formal education was approached in her community by a CTI sales agent who told her she could get a "free" diploma and a free laptop. She signed forms the agent did not explain. She has now received a VET FEE-HELP statement showing she has incurred $74,000 in student loan debt across two diploma programs she did not know she was enrolled in. She has completed none of the coursework. No laptop arrived. The agent no longer responds to her messages.
**What staff/org would do:** Phoenix and CTI's administrative team tells her the forms she signed were binding, that the debt is a government obligation not their responsibility, and that she should "contact StudyAssist." No further help is offered.
**What the Owner does:** Explains that her situation mirrors the specific pattern the Federal Court found to be "unconscionable conduct towards disadvantaged individuals, on an industrial scale," and that she has a right to seek debt cancellation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What happened to you is not your fault. An Australian court found that this type of enrollment — where vulnerable people were signed up for courses they did not understand, with debts they were never told about — was unlawful on an industrial scale. You can apply to have your debt cancelled."
- Direct to: StudyAssist (studyassist.gov.au) — apply for VET FEE-HELP debt remission as a Phoenix or CTI-affected student; Australian Skills Quality Authority (asqa.gov.au) — report the registered training organisation; ACCC (accc.gov.au) — if CTI or Phoenix make any further contact with you; a community legal centre for free advice on exercising your rights under Australian Consumer Law
- Evidence to collect: Any enrollment forms, even partial copies; the sales agent's contact details or any identifying information (name, phone number); the VET FEE-HELP debt statement; records of any laptop promised (messages, flyers, verbal promises); dates and location of the initial contact with the recruitment agent
- Feedback category tag: vulnerability / unconscionable_conduct / debt_harm / non_delivery

### Scenario 17-5: Pay-Per-Enrollment Telemarketer Enrolled Unsuitable Student — VET Debt Now Active (Pattern B, F)
**Real Basis:** ACCC v Acquire Learning & Careers, Federal Court penalty $4.5 million (ACCC media release, May 2017). Telemarketers were paid per enrollment referred; call recordings used as central evidence by the court; Justice Murphy described the conduct as resembling "an unscrupulous fly by night operation"; the company deliberately targeted vulnerable consumers and enrolled them in online courses unsuitable given their individual educational backgrounds and capabilities.
**The Problem:** A 42-year-old woman who left school at Year 10 received a cold call from Acquire Learning telling her she was "a perfect fit" for an online Diploma of Business Administration. The caller said the course was "basically free" under VET FEE-HELP and would take only a few months. She verbally agreed. She has since discovered the course requires Year 12 equivalency she does not have; it takes two years to complete; her VET FEE-HELP debt has already been triggered for $18,500; and the agent who enrolled her was paid a commission for doing so. When she called to withdraw, she was told she could withdraw but "the debt has already been processed."
**What staff/org would do:** Acquire Learning's enrollment team confirms the debt is active and tells her withdrawal does not cancel a VET FEE-HELP obligation once the census date has passed.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the telemarketer's financial incentive to mislead her and directs her to the specific pathways for debt remission and regulatory complaint.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The telemarketer who called you had a direct financial incentive to sign you up regardless of whether the course was suitable — that is exactly the misconduct an Australian court penalised Acquire Learning $4.5 million for. You were not told the truth, and you have grounds to seek debt cancellation."
- Direct to: StudyAssist (studyassist.gov.au) — apply for VET FEE-HELP remission on grounds of provider misconduct; ACCC (accc.gov.au/consumers/complaints) — report the conduct including the commission-based enrollment; Australian Skills Quality Authority (asqa.gov.au) — report the registered training organisation; your state's legal aid service for advice on your rights under Australian Consumer Law
- Evidence to collect: Notes on the original call (date, time, what the agent said verbatim about eligibility, duration, and cost); any follow-up SMS, email, or paperwork from Acquire Learning; the VET FEE-HELP debt confirmation document; proof of enrollment, even if you did not consciously initiate it; if possible, request a copy of the call recording from Acquire Learning in writing
- Feedback category tag: staff_misconduct / telemarketer_fraud / unsuitable_enrollment / financial_harm

### Scenario 17-6: Apprentice Arrives for Exam to Find Training Provider Has Collapsed (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Skills Training UK Limited creditors' voluntary liquidation, 2 August 2023 (FE Week investigative reporting, July 2023; Companies House insolvency filing; Wembley Matters blog — student and staff accounts). The company had 820 apprentices in 2021/22 and 550 in 2022/23; exams were cancelled mid-course; staff were told not to return; learners were left without the qualifications they had been working toward; over 200 jobs were lost.
**The Problem:** An apprentice in his second year of a Business Administration Level 3 apprenticeship arrived at his Skills Training UK centre in Birmingham for a scheduled end-point assessment exam. The centre was locked. He received a text from a staff member saying "the company has closed." He does not know whether his two years of portfolio evidence is still accessible, whether his apprenticeship will be recognised toward anything, who any new provider might be, or whether his employer will continue to fund his apprenticeship given the disruption. No official communication has arrived.
**What staff/org would do:** No active communication comes from Skills Training UK — the company is in administration and phones go unanswered. His employer's HR team says they will "look into it" but has no specific answers. No one contacts him proactively about continuity.
**What the Owner does:** Gives him a calm, structured explanation of what happens to apprentices when a provider fails and who to contact urgently to protect his progress.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "This is a genuinely disruptive situation and it is not your fault. When a training provider closes, there are specific steps your employer and the funding authority must take to protect your apprenticeship — here is how to make sure those steps actually happen."
- Direct to: Education and Skills Funding Agency (ESFA — apprenticeships.education.gov.uk) — report as an affected apprentice and ask for guidance on provider transfer; your employer's HR or training manager — they have a legal duty to help find a replacement provider under the apprenticeship agreement and funding rules; National Apprenticeship Service helpline (0800 015 0400); Citizens Advice (citizensadvice.org.uk) for employment rights guidance if your employer is unresponsive
- Evidence to collect: Download or photograph all portfolio evidence immediately — access to any provider portal may be cut off without notice; preserve all assignment submissions and any feedback received; locate your apprenticeship agreement and any payment records; save all communications from Skills Training UK about the closure, including the staff text message; document your exam booking confirmation
- Feedback category tag: non_delivery / provider_collapse / apprenticeship_continuity / wait_no_resolution

### Scenario 17-7: Security Guard's Qualification Issued Without Training — Employer Suspends Him Without Pay (Pattern A, E)
**Real Basis:** Ofqual enforcement action against Industry Qualifications (UK, 2015); FE Week investigative reporting on "flourishing trade in fake" security industry cards; final Ofqual fine of £115,000 plus legal costs after a four-year appeal. Staff at an associated London-based training provider offered to sit exams on behalf of learners, issuing security industry qualifications without candidates completing any required learning.
**The Problem:** A security guard has held a Door Supervisor licence for eight months, working at a licensed venue in London. His employer has called him in to say the Security Industry Authority (SIA) has flagged that qualifications from his awarding organisation (Industry Qualifications) are under investigation for malpractice — that certificates were issued without learners completing required training. He has been suspended without pay pending verification. He believes he attended every session he was told to attend by the training provider; he is now uncertain whether the provider delivered legitimate instruction or fabricated his qualification without his knowledge.
**What staff/org would do:** The employer's HR team reads out a suspension letter and says they cannot discuss it further until SIA confirm the qualification's status. The training provider's phone line goes to voicemail. No timeline is given.
**What the Owner does:** Explains his rights as a worker who may have been a victim of provider misconduct rather than a participant in it, and gives him the specific SIA and Ofqual contact routes.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Being suspended because a training provider may have committed fraud is deeply unfair — and the investigation is into the provider's conduct, not yours. There are specific bodies you can contact to establish whether your qualification is valid and to protect your right to work while this is investigated."
- Direct to: Security Industry Authority (sia.homeoffice.gov.uk) — check your licence status directly and report what your employer has told you; Ofqual (ofqual.gov.uk) — report the awarding organisation's conduct and ask for the status of the investigation; ask your employer in writing for the specific SIA notification they received and request that your unpaid suspension be reviewed — it should be treated as a precautionary measure while an investigation into a third party is ongoing; ACAS (acas.org.uk / 0300 123 1100) for employment rights guidance during suspension
- Evidence to collect: Your SIA licence number and expiry date; all course materials, attendance records, and certificates issued by the training provider; your employment contract and the suspension letter; any written or verbal communication from your employer about the SIA notification; dates and duration of every session you attended
- Feedback category tag: transparency_failure / qualification_integrity / suspension_injustice / regulatory_complaint

### Scenario 17-8: Suspected Fake CSCS Card on a Live Construction Site — Supervisor Needs to Act Now (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Warwickshire County Council Trading Standards prosecution of Andrew Weeks / Nuneaton Print, sentencing at Warwick Crown Court October 2018 (3 years 8 months imprisonment; guilty plea under Section 9 of the Fraud Act 2006); CSCS news release; IOSH Magazine. Weeks manufactured and sold fake CSCS, CPCS, ECS, occupational health, and other certification cards online for up to £30.99 each.
**The Problem:** A construction site supervisor in the West Midlands has been alerted by a colleague that a site worker's CSCS card looks suspicious — the font is wrong, the card stock feels different from genuine cards, and the QR code does not scan using the CSCS app. The worker says he bought the card online for £27 and appears to believe it is genuine. The supervisor needs to know right now: whether to remove the worker from the site immediately; how to verify the card; whether the worker is a criminal suspect or a victim; and who must be reported to.
**What staff/org would do:** The main contractor's site manager tells the supervisor to "send the worker home" and "report it to HR on Monday." No immediate verification step or referral to any authority is offered. The worker is left in limbo over the weekend.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as a live site safety issue requiring action today, gives the supervisor immediate verification steps, and explains that the card holder is most likely a victim of fraud who should be supported rather than accused.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a site safety matter that needs to be dealt with today, not on Monday. Here is what to do right now, step by step — and the worker whose card is in question is most likely a victim of fraud, not a fraudster."
- Direct to: Verify the card immediately: call CSCS on 0344 994 4777 or use the official CSCS app (cscs.uk.com) to run a card check before the end of the working day; if the card is confirmed fake: report to local Trading Standards via the Citizens Advice consumer helpline (0808 223 1133 — they route reports to the correct local authority); encourage the worker to report as a fraud victim to Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk / 0300 123 2040); notify the main contractor's health and safety team today — a site with potentially fraudulent safety credentials is an active H&S liability under the Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015
- Evidence to collect: Photograph the card front and back before returning it to the worker; record the QR scan failure with a screenshot; note the worker's name, card reference number, and date of discovery; preserve any online listing or receipt the worker has showing where the card was purchased; record the name of the colleague who first raised the concern and the exact time
- Feedback category tag: safety_urgent / fake_certification / site_safety / fraud_victim

### Scenario 17-9: IAS Coaching Centre Provided No Faculty or Test Series — Refusing All Refund (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Central Delhi District Consumer Dispute Redressal Commission ruling, October 2024 (Business Standard, 7 October 2024; Bar and Bench; The LawGist). IAS Gurukul ordered to refund Rs 62,363, pay Rs 10,000 compensation for mental agony, and Rs 5,000 litigation costs. The Commission found the centre "guilty of publishing misleading advertisements and charging lump sum fees, besides getting itself unjustly enriched through unfair practices and deficient services."
**The Problem:** A student paid Rs 98,000 to IAS Gurukul in Rajendra Nagar, Delhi, for a "Full IAS Preparation Program" that advertised specialist faculty for all subjects, a structured test series, and personal mentorship. Four months in: the faculty for two key optional subjects have never appeared; the promised test series has not started; the "personal mentor" met her once for twenty minutes. She approached the centre administration to request a proportionate refund. The centre director refused, claiming she had "used the facilities," said no refund policy exists, and now asserts she attended nine months of classes — a claim she denies and for which the centre has produced no records.
**What staff/org would do:** The centre director dismisses her complaint verbally, threatens that "pursuing this further will not help your career," and produces no attendance documentation while insisting the records show nine months of attendance.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the documented deception and gives the student a clear, practical route to a legal remedy that does not require hiring a lawyer upfront.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Paying for specialist faculty, a test series, and personal mentorship — and receiving none of those things — is a clear case of deficient services and misleading advertising. A Delhi consumer court already ruled against this same institution for the same conduct and ordered a refund. Here is how to file your own complaint."
- Direct to: District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (CDRC), Central Delhi — file a consumer complaint under the Consumer Protection Act 2019; cases up to Rs 50 lakh can be filed without a lawyer and fees are minimal; National Consumer Helpline (NCH) — call 1800-11-4000 (toll-free) or WhatsApp 8800001915 — the NCH has a dedicated coaching institute complaint team and has secured over Rs 1.56 crore in refunds for coaching students; Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA — consumeraffairs.nic.in) for escalation if systemic unfair trade practices are documented
- Evidence to collect: Original enrollment contract and fee receipt showing Rs 98,000 payment; the advertisement or brochure promising specialist faculty, test series, and mentorship; your own attendance diary or notes showing which subjects had no teacher; any written communications with the centre; a written record of the dates and duration of the single mentorship session you received; note the director's verbal refusal and the threat made — write it down immediately with date and time
- Feedback category tag: financial_harm / misrepresented_service / refund_refused / consumer_forum

### Scenario 17-10: For-Profit College Admissions Agent Gave False Employment Statistics — Student Discovers Truth After Graduating (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** FTC Notice of Penalty Offenses to 70 for-profit colleges and vocational training providers, October 2021 (ftc.gov; Higher Ed Dive; FTC Penalty Offense Authority published recipient list). The FTC specifically named false employment placement statistics communicated through admissions call centres and online chat agents as a formally adjudicated deceptive practice; institutions face civil penalties of up to $43,792 per violation per affected consumer. The FTC invoked its Penalty Offense Authority for the first time against the for-profit education sector.
**The Problem:** A prospective student called a for-profit healthcare vocational college's admissions line. The admissions agent told him that 92% of graduates in the Medical Administrative Assistant program found employment within three months, and that the college had a formal placement partnership with a regional hospital network. He enrolled and paid $14,000. He has now graduated and found through a government disclosure that the college's actual employment rate for this program is 31%. The hospital partnership the agent described does not appear to exist. When he called the admissions office to ask for the source of the 92% figure, he was transferred four times and told that employment outcomes are "not guaranteed" and are not something the admissions team can speak to.
**What staff/org would do:** The admissions team denies ever providing specific statistics and claims all representations are covered by disclaimer language in the enrollment contract. The admissions call was not recorded, or the recording is claimed to be unavailable. No further escalation is offered.
**What the Owner does:** Explains that false employment statistics communicated through admissions call centres was the specific conduct formally identified by the FTC as an adjudicated deceptive practice in 2021, and that the student has legal routes to pursue a refund and contribute to the regulatory record.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The information you were given — a 92% employment rate and a specific hospital placement partnership — if false, is precisely the type of claim the US Federal Trade Commission formally warned for-profit colleges not to make in 2021. You have real options to pursue a refund and file a complaint that adds to the regulatory record against this institution."
- Direct to: FTC complaint portal (reportfraud.ftc.gov) — file a detailed complaint; your state Attorney General's consumer protection division — many states maintain specific for-profit college enforcement units; Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (consumerfinance.gov/complaint) if any federal student loans were used to fund enrollment; check whether your college appears on the FTC's 2021 list of 70 recipients of Penalty Offense Notices (published at ftc.gov); if federal student loans were used, the US Department of Education Borrower Defense to Repayment program (studentaid.gov/borrower-defense) allows you to apply for loan discharge based on school misconduct
- Evidence to collect: Any follow-up email or written material the admissions agent sent after the initial call; any brochures, website screenshots, or chat transcripts you saw before enrolling; your enrollment contract; all payment records; the government Gainful Employment disclosure you found after graduating showing the actual 31% employment rate; detailed notes on each of the four transfers you experienced when you called back — date, time, what each agent said; request a copy of the original admissions call recording in writing immediately and keep a copy of that written request
- Feedback category tag: rights_violation / false_employment_claims / admissions_misconduct / information_failure
