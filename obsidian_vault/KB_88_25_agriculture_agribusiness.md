### Scenario 25-1: Rajasthan Farmer Receives Fake DAP Fertilizer, Crop Dying, Helpline Sends Him to District Office (Pattern A + E)
**Real Basis:** Global-Agriculture.com investigation into Rajasthan fake fertilizer factory network; 30+ factories raided manufacturing fake DAP/SSP using marble slurry and carcinogenic dyes; Agriculture Department helpline directed farmers to file written complaints at district offices causing multi-week investigation delays (2024–2025)
**The Problem:** Rajit Kumar is a wheat farmer in Sriganganagar district, Rajasthan. He purchased DAP fertilizer from a cooperative three weeks ago and applied it to his standing wheat crop. The crop is now yellowing and stunted. His neighbor, who bought from the same batch, has the same symptoms. He has seen news reports about fake fertilizer factories being raided across the region. He called the Agriculture Department Kisan Call Centre and was told to submit a written complaint at the District Agriculture Office 47 km away. He has no transport today and cannot afford to lose another day watching his crop deteriorate.
**What staff/org would do:** The helpline operator repeats the procedure: submit a written complaint at the district office, bring the fertilizer bag with the batch number, and wait for an inspector to be assigned. No timeline is given. The call ends.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an urgent crop-safety emergency. Acknowledges the fear and financial risk Rajit is facing. Helps him preserve evidence immediately — photographs, purchase receipt, bag label and batch number — and informs him of multiple simultaneous escalation routes: the state agriculture commissioner's complaint portal, the national fertilizer quality complaint mechanism, and the IFFCO Kisan helpline, which has local reach. Documents the complaint through Riviwa as a timestamped record of what was reported, when, and to whom.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — yellowing crop after applying new fertilizer — is serious and time-sensitive. I want to help you act right now, not just file a form."
- Direct to: Rajasthan State Agriculture Department online complaint portal; national Kisan Call Centre (1800-180-1551, toll-free, 24/7); IFFCO Kisan helpline (1800-103-1967); district police station if the purchased product is suspected counterfeit — selling substandard fertilizer is a criminal offence under the Fertiliser (Control) Order 1985
- Evidence to collect: photograph the fertilizer bag showing brand, batch number, and MRP; photograph the yellowing crop in the field; retain the original purchase receipt from the cooperative; ask the cooperative for their batch dispatch records; note the date of purchase and date of application in writing
- Feedback category tag: Input Quality Fraud / Emergency Crop Loss

### Scenario 25-2: Kenyan Smallholder Farmer Sold Substandard Subsidized Fertilizer, Turned Away from Compensation Depot (Pattern A + B)
**Real Basis:** Daily Nation Kenya — KEL Chemicals fake fertilizer scandal under President Ruto's subsidized fertilizer programme; 13,633 farmers received phosphorus-deficient product (24% instead of declared 26%); NCPB compensation process inconsistent with farmers turned away without reference numbers; 8 KEBS officials suspended (2024)
**The Problem:** Wanjiku Mwangi is a smallholder maize farmer in Nakuru County. She purchased Kelphos Plus fertilizer under the government subsidized programme through the National Cereals and Produce Board. Her 1-acre plot produced significantly below expected yield. She has now heard on the radio that the government is offering replacement bags to affected farmers, but when she visited her local NCPB depot the officer told her her name was not on the list and she should come back. She was given no written record of the rejection and no explanation of how to appeal. She spent her savings on this season's inputs.
**What staff/org would do:** The NCPB depot officer tells her to try again next week when "the lists are updated." No complaint reference number is issued. No appeals process is explained. She is turned away without any record of the interaction.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the financial harm and the confusion caused by inconsistent treatment at the depot. Immediately helps her document what happened — date, officer name if known, what was said — creating a verifiable Riviwa record. Informs her of her formal escalation path as an enrolled programme purchaser and explains she does not need to be on a list to assert her entitlement if she has proof of purchase.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "You are entitled to compensation as a programme participant, and being turned away without a reference number or any explanation is not an acceptable response. Let me help you document what happened and find a formal escalation path."
- Direct to: NCPB Head Office complaints desk (Nairobi, 020-2246062); KEBS consumer complaints line (020 6948000); Kenya National Farmers' Federation (KENAFF) advocacy channel for collective farmer grievances; Office of the Cabinet Secretary for Agriculture (public petition mechanism)
- Evidence to collect: original NCPB receipt from the subsidized fertilizer purchase; M-Pesa or bank payment record from the transaction; photograph of any remaining product and its packaging; written or Riviwa-recorded account of the depot interaction with date and officer name; documentation of yield shortfall if available (estimated versus actual harvest weight)
- Feedback category tag: Subsidy Programme Fraud / Compensation Denial

### Scenario 25-3: Ilocos Filipino Farmer Never Received Government Fertilizer Inputs He Was Enrolled For, Office Provides No Information (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Sandiganbayan conviction of two former Department of Agriculture Region 1 officials, Philippines, March 2023; liquid fertilizer purchased without public bidding and deliberately not distributed to enrolled farmer beneficiaries in Ilocos region; farmers who enquired at DA offices received no information (Philstar, 2023)
**The Problem:** Renato Delos Reyes is a rice farmer in Ilocos Norte enrolled in a Department of Agriculture Region 1 agricultural input assistance program two seasons ago. He was told he would receive liquid fertilizer inputs as part of the program. He never received them. When he visited the DA regional office to enquire, he was told his enrollment records needed to be "verified" and to come back. He has visited three times in six months and received no answer each time. He has since learned through news reports that DA officials were convicted of fraud related to this very program. He does not know whether he is entitled to the fertilizer, any cash equivalent, or how to register a complaint now that a conviction has been handed down.
**What staff/org would do:** The DA regional office clerk tells him the matter is "handled by the courts" and there is nothing the office can do. No complaint form is offered, no referral to a legal aid or ombudsman service is given, no documentation is provided. He leaves with nothing.
**What the Owner does:** Validates his entitlement as a registered program beneficiary and explains that the Sandiganbayan conviction legally establishes the fraud — which means remediation obligations may exist for enrolled farmers who received nothing. Guides him to the Office of the Ombudsman, the DA Secretary's public complaint office, and the Public Attorney's Office for free legal guidance on his options. Documents his situation with a timestamped Riviwa record.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "Your situation is a documented rights violation. Officials were convicted precisely because enrolled farmers like you did not receive inputs they were entitled to. A conviction is an important foundation — you may have grounds for redress. Let me walk you through how."
- Direct to: Office of the Ombudsman Philippines — public complaint and request for enforcement of remediation from the convicted case (8-479-7300); DA Secretary's Public Assistance and Complaints Center (PACC, 8-844-0206); Public Attorney's Office (PAO) — free legal counsel on whether a civil damages claim can accompany the criminal case; Commission on Audit regional office — to request audit of program disbursements in Region 1
- Evidence to collect: any document proving enrollment in the DA Region 1 program; barangay certification of residence and agricultural land ownership or tenancy; any written communications ever received from DA; names and dates of each visit to the regional office; reference to the Sandiganbayan ruling of March 2023 convicting former DA Region 1 officials for this scheme
- Feedback category tag: Government Program Rights Violation / Input Entitlement Denied

### Scenario 25-4: Mindanao Filipino Farmer Discovers Fertilizer Funds Meant for Her Were Funneled Through a Politician's NGO (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Sandiganbayan conviction of former Cagayan de Oro representative Constantino Jaraula and three DA Region 10 officials; public fertilizer program funds funneled through an unqualified NGO; conviction upheld on appeal November 2024 (Rappler, Philstar, 2024)
**The Problem:** Lourdes Abad is a corn farmer in Bukidnon province, Mindanao, who was a registered beneficiary of a DA Region 10 fertilizer assistance program. She received nothing from the program. She has now learned through news reports that a former city representative and three DA officials were convicted by the Sandiganbayan for funneling the fertilizer funds meant for farmers like her through an NGO that was not qualified to receive government money. She wants to know whether she is entitled to any compensation, who is responsible for recovering the loss on behalf of farmers, and whether there is still time to pursue a civil claim.
**What staff/org would do:** She calls the DA Region 10 office and is told the matter is "handled by the courts" and there is nothing the office can do. No referral to a legal aid service, the Ombudsman, or any restitution process is given.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that a criminal conviction has been obtained and explains that this creates the evidentiary foundation for a civil recovery claim. Directs her to the Public Attorney's Office for free legal advice and explains the role of the Commission on Audit in recovering misused government funds. Documents her case as an identified beneficiary who suffered provable program loss.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "The people responsible were convicted by the court — that is important for your situation. A criminal conviction means the wrong is legally established. You may have options for civil recovery on top of that. Let me direct you to the right offices."
- Direct to: Public Attorney's Office (PAO) — free counsel on civil damages claim arising from the convicted scheme; Office of the Ombudsman — to monitor restitution enforcement from the Jaraula conviction; Commission on Audit Region 10 — to request information on fund recovery proceedings; Integrated Bar of the Philippines (IBP) if private counsel is needed for a damages suit
- Evidence to collect: any document proving enrollment in the DA Region 10 fertilizer program (enrollment slip, barangay certification); any amounts the farmer was told she would receive and in what form; written record of any communications with DA Region 10; official court case reference — Sandiganbayan, conviction of Constantino Jaraula et al., upheld November 2024
- Feedback category tag: Corruption / Fertilizer Program Funds Embezzlement

### Scenario 25-5: US Corn Farmer Paid $128,000 Extra Per Year Due to Alleged Fertilizer Price Fixing by Six Major Producers (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Buckman Farms v. Canpotex Ltd. et al., US District Court Missouri, Case No. 2:26-cv-04063, filed March 23, 2026; six major fertilizer producers accused of fixing NPK prices since 2020; US farmers paid more than 60% more for fertilizer between 2021 and 2022, adding an estimated $128,000 in costs per farm in 2022; FTC investigation opened May 2026 (classaction.org, DiCello Levitt, RFDTV, 2026)
**The Problem:** Dale Hutchinson operates a 2,000-acre corn and soybean farm in Iowa. Between 2021 and 2022, his nitrogen, phosphate, and potash fertilizer costs increased by more than 60%. His records show he paid approximately $127,000 more in 2022 than in 2019 for the same volume of inputs. He has heard about the antitrust class action accusing Canpotex, CF Industries, Koch Agronomic Solutions, Nutrien, Mosaic, and Yara of coordinating supply restrictions to maintain inflated prices. He wants to know whether he can join the class action, whether he should be reporting his pricing data to the FTC, and whether the federal investigation can accelerate compensation.
**What staff/org would do:** His local co-op tells him there is nothing that can be done about commodity prices and to contact his state farm bureau if he has concerns. No mention of the class action, no guidance on how to participate or preserve records.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms the active federal class action and explains the process for participating as a class member — individual filing is not required if the class is certified, but preserving detailed purchase records now is critical. Explains how to submit information to the FTC's active investigation and connects him to class-action counsel and the American Farm Bureau Federation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "What you experienced was not just market volatility. It is the subject of an active federal antitrust lawsuit and an FTC investigation. Your purchase records are evidence. Here is what you should do now to protect your position in the class."
- Direct to: DiCello Levitt (lead counsel in Buckman Farms v. Canpotex) — class member registration; FTC complaint portal (reportfraud.ftc.gov) — to submit pricing data to the open investigation; American Farm Bureau Federation legal assistance line; state attorney general's consumer protection division — parallel state antitrust claim may be available
- Evidence to collect: all fertilizer purchase invoices from 2019 to present showing price per unit, supplier name, and date; year-by-year volume records to isolate per-unit price increase from volume changes; any supplier communications about pricing or supply availability; crop input cost records for each planting season from 2019 onward
- Feedback category tag: Price Fixing / Antitrust Violation / Financial Overcharge

### Scenario 25-6: Madhya Pradesh Farmer Paid PMFBY Premium, 75% Crop Loss, Zero Payout After Four Months, Helpline Cannot Give Status (Pattern C + B)
**Real Basis:** Groundreport.in ground investigation, Kharif 2024; named farmer Shishupal Thakur of Samaswada village, Chhindwara — 75% maize loss, no damage survey conducted; PMFBY helpline and pmfby.gov.in portal unable to provide claim status to farmers; not a single Kharif 2024 claim settled as of February 2025; Parliamentary Standing Committee (Lok Sabha, August 2021) described PMFBY as "turned into a bonanza for insurance companies" (2024–2025)
**The Problem:** Shishupal Thakur farms maize in Samaswada village, Chhindwara district, Madhya Pradesh. His maize crop suffered approximately 75% damage during the Kharif 2024 season due to unseasonal rainfall. He enrolled in PMFBY and has been paying premiums annually. No crop damage survey was conducted on his land despite his notification to the local agriculture office. As of February 2025, more than four months after harvest, not a single PMFBY Kharif 2024 claim has been settled in his district. He called the PMFBY helpline and could not get any status on his claim. The portal shows nothing. He owes money to an input supplier and cannot access credit for the Rabi season without the insurance payout clearing.
**What staff/org would do:** The helpline tells him his claim is "under processing" and to wait. No timeline is provided. No explanation for the absence of a damage survey is given. He is referred to his local patwari or Krishi Vibhag office, where he is again told to wait.
**What the Owner does:** Recognizes this as a financial crisis with direct consequences for the next growing season. Helps him document the full timeline — premium payments, date of damage, date of notification, all follow-up contacts — creating a formal complaint record. Guides him to the IRDAI Bima Bharosa portal for a formal complaint against the insurer and to the state-level PMFBY grievance mechanism.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "Paying premiums and receiving no payout after a documented 75% crop loss is not acceptable — and you are not alone in your district. Thousands of farmers face the same situation this season. Let me help you build a formal complaint record that can escalate this beyond the helpline."
- Direct to: IRDAI Bima Bharosa grievance portal (bimabharosa.irdai.gov.in) — formal complaint against the insurer for non-settlement; PMFBY Grievance Redressal Committee at state level (Madhya Pradesh Agriculture Department); Chief Minister's Jan Sunwai helpline (181, Madhya Pradesh); Lok Sabha constituency MP's office — to raise the matter as a district-level issue in Parliament
- Evidence to collect: PMFBY enrollment certificate and all premium payment receipts; written notification sent to the local agriculture office about crop damage — request a date-stamped copy; photographs of the damaged crop taken at or near harvest time; land records (khasra/khatauni); written or Riviwa-recorded log of every helpline call and office visit with dates and what was said; any written response (or formal absence of response) from the insurance company
- Feedback category tag: Crop Insurance Denial / Claim Processing Failure / Helpline Information Gap

### Scenario 25-7: Uttar Pradesh Farmer Cannot File Legitimate Insurance Claim Because a Fraudulent Policy Was Already Placed on His Land Without His Knowledge (Pattern B + C)
**Real Basis:** Uttar Pradesh Farrukhabad crop insurance fraud — 9 bank managers, 3 insurance company employees, and 32 farmers booked by police; fraudulent PMFBY policies issued on barren, non-agricultural, and third-party land without the knowledge of the actual landowners; legitimate claimants found their land blocked by fraudulent prior entries (seaandjob.com, The420.in, 2024)
**The Problem:** Ramesh Yadav owns agricultural land in Farrukhabad district, Uttar Pradesh. After his wheat crop was damaged, he went to his bank to initiate a crop insurance claim under PMFBY. The bank officer told him that a crop insurance policy is already recorded on his plot — but Ramesh never enrolled it. Someone used his land records without his knowledge to take out a fraudulent policy. Because the system shows his land as already covered, he cannot file a new claim. He has been turned away at the bank, does not know who took the fraudulent policy, and has no idea how to get the false entry removed so he can file his legitimate claim.
**What staff/org would do:** The bank branch manager tells him to return with a written complaint from the district agriculture office before the bank can act. At the district agriculture office, the clerk says this is a bank matter and sends him back. The circular referral continues with no action and no record created.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately identifies this as both identity fraud and a blocked legitimate claim — two harms requiring simultaneous action. Helps Ramesh document everything: the bank visit date, what was told to him, and the fact that he never authorized any policy. Directs him to file an FIR with the local police under identity fraud provisions and a formal complaint to the insurance company's grievance officer and IRDAI.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "Your land was used without your knowledge to create a fraudulent insurance policy. This is identity fraud and it is also blocking your legitimate claim. You need to act on two fronts at once — reporting the fraud and unblocking your entitlement."
- Direct to: Local police station — file FIR for identity fraud and misuse of land records (IPC Sections 420, 465, 467); IRDAI Bima Bharosa portal — report the fraudulent policy and formally request its removal; bank's nodal grievance officer — written complaint that no policy was ever authorized by Ramesh; District Magistrate's office — request an inquiry into unauthorized use of land records for insurance purposes
- Evidence to collect: land ownership documents (khasra and khatauni showing Ramesh as rightful owner); Aadhaar and bank account records showing no enrollment was authorized; bank's written record of the unauthorized policy — request a printed statement; name of the insurance company that issued the fraudulent policy; written record of the bank's refusal to process the claim (request this in writing or log it through Riviwa)
- Feedback category tag: Insurance Identity Fraud / Claim Access Denial

### Scenario 25-8: Ugandan Smallholder Farmer Faces Total Crop Failure After Planting Counterfeit Maize Seeds, No Complaint Channel Exists (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Uganda National Bureau of Standards documented approximately 30% of seeds on the Ugandan market are counterfeit; TASAI recorded 48 actual counterfeit seed cases in Uganda in a single year versus only 3 officially reported to government, revealing a massive under-reporting gap; Longe 5 maize the most counterfeited variety; farmers reported total crop failure; no centralized complaint hotline with escalation pathways documented for Uganda (Monitor Uganda; Frontiers in Environmental Economics 50-country analysis, 2026)
**The Problem:** Grace Namukasa is a subsistence farmer in Mpigi district, Uganda. She planted maize seeds she bought from a local agro-dealer — seeds labeled as Longe 5, a certified drought-tolerant variety. Three weeks after planting, barely 15% of seedlings have emerged. The ones that did emerge are weak and off-type. This is her family's primary food source for the season. She went to the agro-dealer who denies responsibility and claims she must have planted poorly. There is no complaint hotline she knows of, no district agriculture office that formally tracks such complaints, and she has no written receipt from the purchase. If she does not act, the incident will not appear in any official count.
**What staff/org would do:** The agro-dealer denies any product problem. The district agriculture office tells her to bring seeds for testing — but she has already planted all of them and has no remaining seed. No formal intake of her complaint is made. She is effectively turned away.
**What the Owner does:** Recognizes this as a food-security emergency and treats it as urgent. Helps Grace photograph what evidence remains — the packaging she kept, the sparse seedling field — and explains that UNBS operates a product quality complaint line and that the district Crop Protection Officer can conduct a field assessment. Documents the complaint through Riviwa so the incident contributes to the enforcement data gap, not to the silent majority that goes uncounted.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "A near-total germination failure is a food security emergency. I am treating this as urgent. Even without seeds left to test, there is evidence we can preserve right now — and people who need to know about this."
- Direct to: Uganda National Bureau of Standards (UNBS) product quality complaint line (0800 133 133, toll-free); district Agriculture Office — Crop Protection Officer for field assessment even at the germination stage; National Agricultural Advisory Services (NAADS) district coordinator; Uganda Seed Trade Association (USTA) complaints channel; local police if the dealer is identifiable — selling counterfeit seeds is a criminal offence under the Agriculture (Seeds and Plants) Statute
- Evidence to collect: photograph all visible seedlings in the field showing sparse and abnormal emergence; retain any seed bag, packaging, or label even if empty — photograph both sides for brand, batch numbers, and certification markings; note the name and location of the agro-dealer; note the purchase date, quantity purchased, and price paid; speak with neighboring farmers who may have bought from the same dealer and document their experience for a collective complaint, which carries significantly more weight with regulators
- Feedback category tag: Counterfeit Input Emergency / Food Security Crisis

### Scenario 25-9: Kentucky Farmer Reports Crop Insurance Adjuster Fraud to USDA Hotline, Receives No Status Updates for Six Months (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** USDA-OIG Hotline (1-800-424-9121) is the designated channel for reporting crop insurance fraud; Michael McNew operated a scheme in Kentucky causing losses exceeding $23 million as a crop insurance adjuster and agent; farmers who reported suspected fraud to the hotline received no case status updates back — investigation timelines were opaque to the reporting party (FBI, Oversight.gov, Department of Justice, 2021–2023)
**The Problem:** Harold Bowen is a tobacco and corn farmer in Muhlenberg County, Kentucky. Over two seasons he has noticed that a crop insurance adjuster consistently over-estimates losses on a neighboring farm — losses Harold does not believe occurred based on what he has observed. His own valid loss claims have been approved at minimum amounts after extremely brief inspections by the same adjuster. He called the USDA-OIG Hotline six months ago and filed a complaint. He has heard nothing since. He does not know whether his complaint was received, whether an investigation is open, who it was assigned to, or whether he should report elsewhere. The adjuster still works in his county.
**What staff/org would do:** When Harold calls the hotline again, the operator tells him that complaint status cannot be disclosed and investigations may take an extended period. No case tracking number is offered. His concern about the adjuster's continuing activity in his county is not addressed.
**What the Owner does:** Validates that the absence of feedback from the USDA-OIG is a documented systemic issue — the hotline takes intake but does not communicate status back to complainants. Explains parallel channels that exist outside the OIG to ensure the information is tracked in multiple places. Guides Harold on how to compile a specific, dated written dossier that is more actionable than a verbal call.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "Your complaint may be active — the USDA-OIG does not communicate case status back to the person who filed, which means silence does not mean inaction. But there are steps to reinforce your report and ensure your information is not siloed in one channel."
- Direct to: USDA Risk Management Agency (RMA) Agent and Adjuster Fraud reporting line — a separate intake channel from OIG; FBI local field office — crop insurance fraud is a federal crime under 18 U.S.C. sections 1014 and 1343; your US Congressman's constituent services office — they can formally enquire with USDA-OIG on your behalf without revealing your identity; state Department of Insurance — the adjuster holds a state licence that can be suspended pending investigation
- Evidence to collect: dates and times of all calls to USDA-OIG and any intake or confirmation number received; written notes with specific dates, field locations, and your observations of the neighboring farm during the claimed loss periods versus what you saw; photographs of the neighboring farm taken near the claimed loss dates if available; your own claim records compared to your actual losses and inspection timelines; any written correspondence from the insurance company or the adjuster
- Feedback category tag: Insurance Agent Fraud / Complaint System Information Failure

### Scenario 25-10: DRC Farmer Sold Counterfeit Seeds, Crop Underperforms by 40%, No Local Complaint Mechanism Exists and Incident Will Not Be Counted (Pattern A + E)
**Real Basis:** Frontiers in Environmental Economics 50-country comparative analysis (2026) documenting 185 counterfeit seed incidents in DRC in a single reporting year; massive under-reporting gap with official government figures near zero; AGRA and TASAI data confirming counterfeit seeds cause 30–50% yield reductions pushing smallholder farmers into debt; no centralized complaint hotline or tracking system documented for DRC
**The Problem:** Celestin Muyumba is a cassava and maize farmer in Kasai province, DRC. He purchased seeds labeled as a certified drought-tolerant maize variety from a vendor in the local market. After planting, his crop showed poor and uneven germination and the plants that emerged did not match the expected variety. His harvest was approximately 40% below what the certified variety should have yielded. When he returned to the market, the vendor had moved. He has no complaint office he is aware of in his area, no quality assurance hotline, and no written receipt. If this goes unrecorded, his case joins the hundreds that do not appear in official statistics — part of the reason the problem persists and is never enforced against.
**What staff/org would do:** The local agriculture extension office, when reached, tells him that seed complaints must be made in Kinshasa — hundreds of kilometres away — and that there is no local mechanism. No record of his complaint is created. He is turned away and the incident goes uncounted.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as both an immediate financial harm and a data-collection moment that matters for the broader enforcement pattern. Helps Celestin document what he can from memory and what physical evidence remains. Explains the FAO country office as a more accessible channel than Kinshasa. Notes that collective complaints — if other farmers in his area experienced the same failure — are significantly more actionable than individual ones and more likely to trigger a field investigation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "Your case matters, even if local channels have turned you away. What happened to you is part of a documented pattern across the DRC. Recording it properly through Riviwa means your experience becomes part of an evidence base that can reach national and international authorities — not another incident that disappears."
- Direct to: FAO DRC Country Office (Kinshasa) — FAO operates seed quality programs and accepts farmer complaints through its provincial field offices, which are more accessible than Kinshasa directly; International Institute of Tropical Agriculture (IITA) DRC office — conducts seed system and quality investigations in Central Africa; World Food Programme DRC — for emergency food security assessment if the yield loss threatens household food access; Alliance of Bioversity International and CIAT — seed system oversight in the Central Africa region
- Evidence to collect: any remaining seed from the purchased batch — even a handful in a sealed bag — for laboratory testing at an IITA or FAO facility; photograph of any packaging, label, or markings on the seed bag; photographs of the field showing sparse, uneven, or off-type crop emergence; name and approximate market location of the vendor; estimated quantity of seed purchased and price paid; accounts from neighboring farmers who may have bought from the same vendor at the same time — collective testimony is significantly more effective
- Feedback category tag: Counterfeit Input Fraud / Institutional Complaint Gap / Enforcement Under-reporting
