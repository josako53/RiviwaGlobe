### Scenario 31-1: Truck Owner Trapped in Defeat-Device Recall Transfer Loop (Pattern D, E)
**Real Basis:** EPA and DOJ enforcement announcement, January 11, 2024; Manufacturing Dive and Detroit News coverage of Cummins Inc. $1.675 billion Clean Air Act settlement — largest in US history — for defeat-device software installed in 630,000+ RAM 2500/3500 diesel trucks (model years 2013–2019). Dealer recall process documented in Cummins and Stellantis recall notices.
**The Problem:** A Ram 2500 owner in Ohio received a federal recall notice about illegal Cummins defeat-device emissions software. She calls her dealership to schedule the free mandated software update. The service advisor says the update "isn't available yet" with no timeline offered. She calls Cummins customer support, which tells her to contact the dealer. She calls Stellantis/RAM's national line, which says the repair is Cummins's responsibility. Four calls over two days, zero case numbers opened, and still no repair appointment — while her truck continues producing NOx emissions far exceeding legal limits in the community around her.
**What staff/org would do:** Each call center representative correctly identifies the issue as another party's responsibility and provides a phone number to call next — but no one opens a case file, issues a confirmation number, or commits to a callback. The consumer cycles between Cummins, Stellantis/RAM, and the dealership with no one owning the resolution.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the frustration of being passed in a loop over a federally mandated recall. Opens a case record capturing every prior contact. Provides the EPA recall reference number (January 2024 enforcement case) which the consumer can cite as legal authority for the mandatory repair. Advises her to file a complaint with NHTSA's Vehicle Safety Hotline as an independent escalation route and explains that NHTSA can compel the manufacturer to act within a set timeframe.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You have been passed between three organisations over two days without a resolution on a federally mandated recall — that is not acceptable. Let me help you take control of this and get your repair on record."
- Direct the consumer to: NHTSA Vehicle Safety Hotline (1-888-327-4236); EPA online complaint portal (epa.gov/enforcement); verify recall status by VIN at nhtsa.gov/recalls; EPA enforcement case reference: United States v. Cummins Inc., January 2024
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Name and employee ID of every agent spoken to; dates, times, and substance of each call; request written email confirmation from the dealer at the time any appointment is made; keep the original recall notice received
- Feedback category tag: `recall_process_failure` / `manufacturer_transfer_loop` / `information_failure`

### Scenario 31-2: Infant Left in Recalled Fisher-Price Sleeper — Only Voucher Offered, Refund Refused (Pattern A, B, E)
**Real Basis:** CPSC formal recall re-announcement, January 2023, Fisher-Price Rock 'n Play Sleeper (4.7 million units; at least 100 confirmed infant deaths including 8 post-recall); Consumer Reports formal advocacy documenting voucher-only remedy as inadequate; U.S. House Oversight Committee investigation report, 2021.
**The Problem:** A mother of a three-month-old discovers via a news alert that the Fisher-Price Rock 'n Play Sleeper she bought in 2018 — and has been using every night for two months — is under a federal recall linked to over 100 infant deaths. The recall has been reissued because the original notice failed to reach enough parents. She immediately stops using the product. She calls Fisher-Price's recall line and is told she qualifies only for a "product voucher" for another Fisher-Price product, not a cash refund. She asks whether any replacement products have also been recalled. The agent cannot answer. She cannot afford to buy a safe alternative tonight. Her baby has nowhere safe to sleep.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center agent reads from the recall script: "You are eligible for a Fisher-Price product voucher — would you like me to process that now?" The agent does not ask about the baby's current sleeping arrangement, does not offer safe sleep guidance, and has no authority to issue a refund. The parent is left with a dismantled product, a voucher she cannot use tonight, and no safe sleep solution.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises this immediately as an infant safety emergency. Confirms recall coverage using the product's model number against the CPSC database. Tells the parent clearly that the product must not be used for infant sleep again — ever. Provides the AAP and CPSC safe sleep guidance (flat, firm, separate surface on the parent's back). Explains that the voucher-only remedy has been formally criticised as inadequate and that the parent can pursue a full cash refund by filing a complaint directly with CPSC, referencing Consumer Reports' advocacy position.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Your baby's safety comes first. Do not put your baby in that sleeper tonight or any night. I am going to help you find a safe sleep solution and also get your money back — this product should have been removed from the market years ago."
- Direct the consumer to: CPSC Consumer Hotline (1-800-638-2772); CPSC complaint portal (cpsc.gov/Recalls) to formally dispute the voucher-only remedy; American Academy of Pediatrics safe sleep guidance (healthychildren.org/SafeSleep); local health department, WIC office, or Safe Kids Worldwide for emergency safe sleep resources if cost is a barrier
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Model number and lot code from the product label; purchase receipt or bank transaction record; screenshot of the CPSC recall page confirming coverage; written record of the Fisher-Price call including the voucher offer made and agent's failure to answer safe sleep questions
- Feedback category tag: `urgent_product_safety` / `recall_remedy_refusal` / `infant_vulnerability`

### Scenario 31-3: LA Garment Worker Receives Fake Paychecks — Overtime Systematically Stolen (Pattern E, D, A, C)
**Real Basis:** U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division enforcement announcement, January 2024; Supply Chain Dive and ESG Dive coverage of Beyond Yoga / Good Cash LLC / Premium Quality Apparel LLC $1.1 million settlement — largest California garment worker wage theft settlement in DOL history. Contractors issued falsified payroll records and fake checks to conceal non-payment of overtime for 165 workers averaging 52-hour weeks.
**The Problem:** A garment worker at a Los Angeles sewing contractor supplying a premium athleisure brand has been working 52-hour weeks for six months. Her paychecks look correct on paper. A coworker has just told her that the checks were falsified — overtime hours were never calculated at the required 1.5x rate, and some checks were printed in names that are not the workers'. She cannot read English easily, has no idea how to verify whether her total pay is lawful, and is afraid her employer will fire her or report her if she complains internally. She wants to know if she has been defrauded and what she can do.
**What staff/org would do:** The contractor's internal HR does not exist for floor workers. If she raises it with her floor manager, the manager dismisses it and says her pay is correct. The payroll system has been deliberately falsified to produce records that appear compliant on inspection.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms this is a documented pattern of wage fraud, not a calculation error. Tells her she has legal protection against retaliation under the FLSA and California Labor Code Section 98.6 — she cannot be lawfully fired for reporting wage theft. Explains that DOL Wage and Hour Division complaints are confidential and that back wages plus equal liquidated damages are recoverable. Notes that DOL has Spanish-speaking investigators and that immigration status does not affect the right to claim unpaid wages.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Falsified payroll records and unpaid overtime are wage theft — and it is illegal. You have the right to report this confidentially. You cannot be legally fired for doing so, and your immigration status does not affect your right to be paid what you are owed. Here is what to do."
- Direct the consumer to: DOL Wage and Hour Division (1-866-487-9243; dol.gov/agencies/whd/contact/local-offices — Los Angeles district office); California Labor Commissioner's Office (dir.ca.gov/dlse); Garment Worker Center Los Angeles (garmentworkercenter.org) for bilingual support and advocacy

### Scenario 31-4: Rana Plaza — Worker Ordered Back Into Building After Structural Crack Discovered (Pattern C, A, E, D)
**Real Basis:** ILO documentation of the Rana Plaza disaster, Savar, Bangladesh, April 24, 2013 (1,134 killed, 2,500 injured); Clean Clothes Campaign; Ethics Unwrapped (University of Texas). The day before the collapse, visible structural cracks were discovered. Bank and ground-floor shops immediately closed. Garment factory managers on upper floors ordered 5,000 workers to return the following morning, threatening dismissal for those who refused.
**The Problem:** It is the morning of April 24, 2013. A 24-year-old garment worker arrives at Rana Plaza in Savar and can see the crack in the building's load-bearing column that was photographed and widely discussed the previous day. She and two coworkers stop at the entrance. The floor supervisor is already there telling workers that if they do not clock in, they will lose this month's wages and their positions. She can see the crack clearly. She is the sole earner for her mother and younger siblings. She does not know whether there is anyone she can report the structural danger to, or whether reporting would protect her from dismissal.
**What staff/org would do:** The floor supervisor applies direct coercive pressure: "Engineers checked — it is fine. Go upstairs now or lose your job." No independent safety channel exists. The factory has no posted emergency contact for Bangladesh's Department of Inspection for Factories and Establishments. There is no union at this facility. Workers who refused were threatened with termination without severance.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an immediate life-safety emergency requiring the clearest possible instruction. Tells the worker directly: do not enter the building — a crack in a load-bearing column is a recognised warning sign of structural failure risk. Explains that threatening workers with dismissal to force them into an unsafe structure is a violation of Bangladesh Labour Act provisions on safe workplace conditions and constitutes unlawful coercion. Provides DIFE's emergency contact number. Advises her that if she is dismissed for refusing to enter an unsafe building, this is wrongful termination with legal recourse.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Do not enter that building. A crack in a structural column is a serious warning sign of potential collapse. Your life is worth more than any shift, and the law is on your side. Here is exactly who to call and what your rights are right now."
- Direct the consumer to: Bangladesh Department of Inspection for Factories and Establishments (DIFE) emergency contact; Bangladesh Centre for Worker Solidarity; Bangladesh Garment Workers Solidarity; nearest police station to report the coercive threat; ILO Bangladesh country office
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Photograph the crack immediately with a visible timestamp; record the full name of the supervisor making the threat; note the names of any coworkers who heard the threat; preserve any written dismissal notice if one is subsequently issued
- Feedback category tag: `urgent_structural_safety` / `worker_coercion` / `workplace_rights_violation` / `staff_misconduct`

### Scenario 31-5: Takata Airbag Recall — Dealer Has No Parts, Driver Told to Limit Driving in Florida (Pattern C, D, B)
**Real Basis:** NHTSA Takata Recall Spotlight (official NHTSA page); Consumer Reports comprehensive Takata recall guide; documented consumer complaints of part unavailability and transfer loops between manufacturer call centers and dealerships. NHTSA continued issuing urgent warnings into 2023; recall completion rates in high-risk humid regions remained below 75% years after the initial recall. NHTSA's consent orders required manufacturers to provide loaner vehicles for Priority Group 1 vehicles.
**The Problem:** A driver in Tampa, Florida receives an "urgent" NHTSA recall notice for the driver-side airbag in her 2014 Honda Civic. The notice explains that the Takata inflator may rupture violently, sending metal fragments at the driver — and that Florida's heat and humidity accelerates the risk. She calls her Honda dealer. The service advisor says no replacement parts are in stock, cannot give a timeline, and suggests she "try to avoid driving in hot or humid conditions." She lives in Tampa. She drives to work every day. She calls Honda's national customer service line and is transferred three times before reaching a voicemail. She has a car NHTSA classifies as Priority Group 1 — highest lethal risk — and no repair date.
**What staff/org would do:** Each call results in being transferred or told to "check back in a few weeks." No case number is opened. The national Honda line has no visibility into individual dealer parts inventory. The consumer is left with a vehicle NHTSA has classified as a lethal risk and zero actionable next steps.
**What the Owner does:** Validates the urgency — NHTSA classified certain Takata vehicles as Priority Group 1, and Florida residents in hot, humid climates are explicitly in the highest-risk category. Explains that Honda's consent order with NHTSA requires the manufacturer to provide a loaner vehicle or rental car reimbursement for Priority Group 1 owners when parts are unavailable — the dealer is legally obligated to offer this and failed to do so. Provides the specific NHTSA Takata hotline number and the language the consumer needs to invoke her reimbursement entitlement immediately.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "NHTSA has classified your vehicle as Priority Group 1 — that means your airbag poses a real risk of death or serious injury right now. You are entitled to a loaner car while your parts are on order. The dealer failed to tell you this. Here is exactly what to demand and who to call."
- Direct the consumer to: NHTSA Vehicle Safety Hotline (1-888-327-4236); NHTSA Takata Recall Spotlight page (nhtsa.gov/vehicle-safety/takata-recall-spotlight); Honda customer relations escalation department (separate from general 1-800 line); Honda loaner/rental reimbursement programme (documented in NHTSA consent order — cite this by name to the dealer)
- Evidence to collect/preserve: The recall notice received — note the Priority Group designation; dates, times, and names from all calls to the dealer and Honda national line; written confirmation from the dealer of parts unavailability (request this by email immediately); any expense receipts if a loaner or rental is provided
- Feedback category tag: `urgent_recall` / `parts_unavailability` / `call_center_transfer_loop` / `manufacturer_remedy_failure`

### Scenario 31-6: Boohoo Leicester Supplier — Garment Worker Paid £3.50 Per Hour During COVID Lockdown (Pattern E, C, A, D)
**Real Basis:** Sunday Times undercover investigation, July 5, 2020; Clean Clothes Campaign documentation; Corporate Justice Coalition case study. Workers in Leicester factories supplying Boohoo were paid as little as £3.50 per hour — less than half the UK National Living Wage of £8.72 per hour. During COVID-19 lockdown, factories continued operating at full capacity under sustained Boohoo orders with no PPE or social distancing. Payroll records were falsified to conceal the violations.
**The Problem:** A 38-year-old garment worker in Leicester has sewn garments for a factory supplying Boohoo for three years. During the April–July 2020 lockdown she worked 60-hour weeks with no masks, no distance between workstations, and no sick pay. She has now calculated that after unexplained deductions for "machine maintenance" and "cleaning supplies" taken from her payslips, her effective hourly rate is £3.50. She assumed she was being paid minimum wage. She wants to report the underpayment and the lockdown safety violations but is worried about what happens to her if she makes a formal complaint and whether her status as a worker through a labour agency affects her rights.
**What staff/org would do:** Boohoo's consumer-facing customer service has no intake route for supplier worker grievances. The factory management would deny any underpayment if directly confronted. There is no posted notice in the factory of workers' minimum wage rights and no functioning internal grievance channel.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms the National Living Wage in the UK was £8.72/hour for workers aged 25+ during 2020, making her described pay rate clearly illegal. Explains that HMRC's minimum wage enforcement is confidential and that agency workers have the same minimum wage rights as direct employees. Provides the HMRC anonymous reporting line for minimum wage violations and the ACAS helpline. Explains the Health and Safety Executive route for reporting the COVID safety failures separately.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You are owed the difference between what you were paid and the legal National Living Wage for every hour you worked during that period. Reporting this is completely confidential. Your status as an agency worker does not take away your minimum wage rights. Here is how to act."
- Direct the consumer to: HMRC National Minimum Wage anonymous reporting line (0800 328 9100; gov.uk/report-minimum-wage-violation); ACAS helpline (0300 123 1100) for employment rights advice; Health and Safety Executive (hse.gov.uk) for the COVID workplace safety violation report; Leicester Citizens Advice Bureau; Ethical Trading Initiative for supply chain escalation to Boohoo's brand level
- Evidence to collect/preserve: All payslips — photograph every one; any written or verbal explanation of the maintenance and cleaning deductions; a written log of actual hours worked each week during the lockdown period; photograph of the workstation layout showing no social distancing; names of any colleagues who can corroborate the conditions and pay levels
- Feedback category tag: `wage_theft` / `below_minimum_wage` / `workplace_safety_covid` / `worker_rights_violation`

### Scenario 31-7: Foxconn Zhengzhou — Worker Discovers Contract Differs From What Recruiter Promised (Pattern E, C, A, B)
**Real Basis:** CNN Business, VOA News, South China Morning Post reporting on November 2022 Foxconn Zhengzhou worker protests; China Labor Watch investigation documenting systematic violations at the facility including 60–75 hour weeks, wage withholding, coerced internship arrangements, and retaliation against workers who raised complaints. Foxconn publicly apologised in November 2022 after protests over advertised 25,000 yuan bonus terms that differed from signed contracts.
**The Problem:** A 22-year-old worker from rural Henan province arrived at Foxconn's Zhengzhou campus after seeing an online recruitment posting promising a 25,000 yuan (~$3,500) bonus for two months of work on the iPhone 14 production line. After six weeks of 70-hour shifts, his team leader tells him the bonus requires three additional months at a lower base wage before it is paid — and that this condition is in the contract he signed. He does not have a copy of his contract. He does not recall reading any such condition. He wants to know if he has been deceived and what he can do without being fired or blacklisted from future factory work across the industry.
**What staff/org would do:** The factory's internal HR says the contract "clearly states" the conditions and that he signed it. The recruiter who made the original promise is a third-party labour agency, and Foxconn denies responsibility for what the agency stated. Workers who raised concerns through the factory's internal WeChat channel were monitored and warned to stop.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms this is a documented and publicly acknowledged pattern — Foxconn apologised for exactly this recruitment mismatch in November 2022 following large-scale worker protests at the same facility. Explains that under Chinese Labour Contract Law Article 8, an employer must truthfully disclose all working conditions and compensation terms before a contract is signed — misrepresentation at the recruitment stage is a legal violation. Explains that under Article 16, he is entitled to a copy of his signed contract and should request it immediately in writing.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — being recruited under one pay arrangement and then presented with a different contract — is a documented violation of Chinese Labour Contract Law, and Foxconn has already admitted publicly that this happened at your facility. You are entitled to a copy of your contract and to challenge the discrepancy. Here is how."
- Direct the consumer to: China national labour complaints hotline: 12333; local Human Resources and Social Security Bureau (劳动局) in Zhengzhou; China Labour Bulletin (clb.org.hk) for documentation support and legal referrals; if the contract copy is refused, this refusal is itself a violation of Article 16 and can be reported to 12333
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Screenshots of the original recruitment advertisement (WeChat post or online job listing) showing the 25,000 yuan bonus and two-month timeline; the recruiter's or agency's name and contact; any onboarding paperwork received; a written request for a copy of the signed contract submitted to HR (keep the message); a personal log of daily hours worked

### Scenario 31-8: DRC Child Cobalt Miner Injured in Tunnel Collapse — No Complaint Channel Exists (Pattern C, E, A, D)
**Real Basis:** Amnesty International "This Is What We Die For," January 2016; Amnesty International campaign page on child labour in cobalt supply chains; Business and Human Rights Resource Centre; 2021 US federal lawsuits by families of children killed in DRC cobalt mines against Apple, Tesla, Microsoft, and others. Approximately 40,000 children worked in artisanal cobalt mines in southern DRC for $1–$2 per day; Amnesty found none of the 16 implicated consumer brands had a mechanism through which DRC miners could raise safety or pay concerns.
**The Problem:** The father of a 12-year-old boy who has been working at an artisanal cobalt mine near Kolwezi, DRC wants to report that his son was injured last week when a tunnel section partially collapsed, breaking the boy's arm. The boy was not paid for the days he could not work and received no medical assistance from the mine operator. The father wants to know: who is legally responsible for his son's injury? Where can he report the unsafe conditions? Is there any way to reach the global brands — Apple, Samsung, others — whose products use cobalt from mines like this one, and do those brands have any obligation to help?
**What staff/org would do:** There is no formal complaint mechanism at the artisanal mining site. The site is managed by a local négociant (ore trader) with no posted safety rules and no first aid provision. The trader sold ore onward through a chain leading to global battery manufacturers. When Amnesty International contacted 16 consumer electronics brands in 2016, none had a mechanism through which DRC miners could raise safety or compensation concerns.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the severity and injustice of the situation with full empathy — a child was injured doing hazardous work that powers global consumer products, and the family has been left with no channel. Explains the documented supply chain so the father understands where accountability flows. Explains that DRC's SAEMAPE (the government body with a mandate over artisanal mining) is the primary state mechanism to report unsafe mining conditions. Provides contacts for Pact DRC, which operates community grievance programs in Kolwezi, and Amnesty International, which actively documents and pressures brands on exactly this issue.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Your son was injured doing work that feeds into products used around the world. You deserve to be heard and you deserve accountability. No easy path exists here — but there are specific organisations who will take your report seriously and act on it. Here is what I know."
- Direct the consumer to: SAEMAPE — Service d'Assistance et d'Encadrement du Small-Scale Mining (DRC government artisanal mining authority); Pact DRC (pactworld.org) — operates community grievance and mine safety programs in Kolwezi; Amnesty International DRC country desk; Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance (responsiblemining.net); local health centre or hospital for formal injury documentation
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Medical documentation of the injury — hospital or clinic record with date and diagnosis; the name of the mine site operator or négociant; the date and location of the tunnel collapse; names of any witnesses; any wage record showing what was withheld during the recovery period
- Feedback category tag: `child_labour` / `mining_safety` / `supply_chain_accountability` / `no_grievance_channel` / `vulnerability`

### Scenario 31-9: Nike Cambodia Supplier — Worker Coerced Into Signing "Voluntary Leave" Form to Strip Wages (Pattern E, C, B, A)
**Real Basis:** Worker Rights Consortium reports, 2020; Business and Human Rights Resource Centre documentation; Partners for Dignity & Rights, December 2025 — five-year campaign that forced Nike to pay compensation for wage theft and retaliation. Workers at Hong Seng Knitting (Thailand, 3,300 workers) were deceived into signing forms falsely stating they wished to take voluntary unpaid leave; workers at Violet Apparel (Cambodia, 1,280 workers) were left without legally mandated severance after abrupt factory closure. Workers who objected faced intimidation via the factory's Facebook page.
**The Problem:** A 29-year-old seamstress at a Cambodian garment factory supplying Nike is told by her supervisor in April 2020 to sign a document titled "Employee Voluntary Leave Agreement." She is told everyone must sign because of COVID and that she will receive 40% of her normal wage during a temporary production reduction. She did not volunteer for leave — she wants to work and needs her full wage to pay rent. She asks what the form says; it is in English. When three of her coworkers asked questions yesterday, their names and photos were posted on the factory's Facebook page with a caption calling them troublemakers. She wants to know whether she is legally required to sign this form and what she loses if she does not.
**What staff/org would do:** The factory HR representative tells her this is standard COVID procedure approved by the government and that if she refuses, "production may halt and everyone will go home with no pay at all." No copy of the form is offered. No union representative is present. The only complaint channel is the factory's own HR desk.
**What the Owner does:** Tells her directly and clearly: do not sign the form without independent advice first. Explains that under Cambodian Labour Law, a reduction in wages or working hours requires the worker's written, informed, and genuine consent — a form in a language she cannot read, presented under threat of collective dismissal, does not constitute valid legal consent. Explains that the public Facebook shaming of workers who asked questions is a documented form of retaliation and is a separate violation. Provides contacts for the Cambodian Ministry of Labour and the Worker Rights Consortium, which was actively investigating exactly this pattern at Nike supplier factories in 2020.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Do not sign that form yet. A document you cannot read, handed to you under threat of mass dismissal, does not count as voluntary consent under Cambodian labour law. You have rights here. Let me explain them before you do anything."
- Direct the consumer to: Cambodia Ministry of Labour and Vocational Training (molvt.gov.kh); Worker Rights Consortium (workersrights.org) — actively investigated Nike supplier factory practices in Cambodia and Thailand during 2020; CENTRAL (Cambodian Labour Rights NGO); Coalition of Cambodian Apparel Workers' Democratic Union (CCAWDU); C&A Foundation worker support network
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Do not sign the form — if pressured, ask for a copy to "review with a family member" and photograph it immediately; screenshot and preserve the Facebook posts naming coworkers as troublemakers (with timestamp); record the names and roles of supervisors who applied pressure; note names of coworkers who were publicly identified

### Scenario 31-10: Adidas India Supplier — Factory Claims False Government Order to Cut Wages, Call Refused (Pattern C, A, E, D)
**Real Basis:** Asia Floor Wage Alliance report on COVID-19 wage losses at Adidas supplier factories across Cambodia, India, Indonesia, and Pakistan; Clean Clothes Campaign study; Sourcing Journal and just-style coverage of Adidas "grappling with legacy of alleged wage theft." Eight identified Adidas supplier factories recorded $11.7 million in direct wage losses for 30,190 workers during the pandemic period; extrapolated losses across 15 factories estimated at $320 million.
**The Problem:** A 33-year-old worker at a Bengaluru garment factory supplying Adidas was placed on a 50% wage arrangement in April 2020. The factory told workers this was a government-mandated COVID measure. She called the Karnataka Labour Department to verify this and was told no such order applied to her factory's size and category. Her wage reduction was never communicated in writing — she only noticed it on her payslip. When she called the factory's HR line to ask about it, the representative said "we cannot help you with this" and ended the call. She wants to recover her missing wages and understand whether Adidas, as the brand, has any direct obligation to workers in its supply chain.
**What staff/org would do:** The factory HR line refuses to engage and ends the call. Adidas's consumer service email addresses product returns and brand inquiries — it has no intake route for supplier worker grievances. Adidas's Supplier Code of Conduct is published online but provides no direct contact mechanism through which workers can report violations.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that under Indian labour law (Payment of Wages Act 1936; Code on Wages 2019), any wage reduction without a lawful basis and without written advance notice to the worker is illegal. Explains that she has already done the key step of verifying with the Karnataka Labour Department that no applicable government order existed — that conversation is evidence the factory's stated justification was false. Explains that the Karnataka Labour Commissioner can investigate the factory directly and that the complaint can be filed without the factory's knowledge. Notes that Adidas is a Fair Labor Association member, which provides a third-party complaint mechanism for supply chain workers.

- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The factory told you there was a government order — you checked, and there was not one. That means the wage reduction had no legal basis, and you are owed the difference. The factory's call refusal is an additional violation. Here is how to recover your wages."
- Direct the consumer to: Karnataka Labour Commissioner office (labour.kar.nic.in); India national Shramik Labour Helpline: 14567; Asia Floor Wage Alliance (asia.floorwage.org) — actively pursuing Adidas supply chain accountability; Fair Labor Association third-party complaint mechanism (fairlabor.org/report-a-concern) — Adidas is a member; Clean Clothes Campaign India contacts
- Evidence to collect/preserve: All payslips from April 2020 onwards showing the reduced amounts; any written or verbal communication from the factory citing a government order; a written record of the Karnataka Labour Department response confirming no applicable order (request confirmation in writing); notes from the HR call — date, time, and the fact that the call was ended without engagement; names of coworkers who received the same reduction
