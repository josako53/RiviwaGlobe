### Scenario 19-1: Stolen Settlement Funds — Plane Crash Family Cannot Reach Their Lawyer (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** U.S. Department of Justice, USAO-CDCA press releases; ABA Journal; State Bar of California disciplinary records — Tom Girardi / Girardi Keese Law Firm, California, USA, fraud 2010–2020, conviction 2024
**The Problem:** A family in Los Angeles won a wrongful death settlement through Girardi Keese after a relative died in the Lion Air Flight 610 crash. They have been waiting years for disbursement. They learn from a news broadcast that Girardi has been convicted for stealing over $15 million from clients — including Lion Air families. They call the firm number immediately: it is disconnected. They call the State Bar's general line and wait on hold. They do not know if their settlement money still exists or whether they will ever receive it.
**What staff/org would do:** The bankruptcy trustee's voicemail says to submit a written claim with no timeline given. The State Bar hold message directs them to a website. No one confirms the settlement amount owed, whether the money was spent, or what recourse exists. The call ends with the family knowing nothing more than when they started.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the betrayal and financial urgency directly. Confirm that the State Bar of California Client Security Fund exists specifically for this — reimbursing clients defrauded by licensed California attorneys. Tell them to contact the USAO-CDCA victim notification line, file a claim with the bankruptcy court, and engage a new attorney immediately to assert their priority claim in the estate.

- Evidence to preserve: Original settlement agreement signed by Girardi, all correspondence from Girardi Keese, disbursement instructions or bank transfer records, original retainer agreement, any checks or wire transfer confirmations received from the firm

### Scenario 19-2: Entire Law Firm Collapses Overnight — £64.5 Million Gone and Completion in Four Days (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** SRA press release; Law Gazette; Serious Fraud Office criminal charges (GOV.UK); SRA Client Protection Annual Report 2022/23 — Axiom Ince / Pragnesh Modhwadia, London, England, UK, SRA intervention October 2023
**The Problem:** A first-time buyer in London has a property conveyancing matter with Axiom Ince. They have already exchanged contracts, with their £200,000 deposit held in the firm's client account. Their completion date is in four days. News breaks that the SRA has intervened — the firm has stopped trading, over 1,400 staff dismissed, and £64.5 million in client money is unaccounted for. They call the SRA's intervention team and are placed on hold for 40 minutes. When an agent answers, they are told to register on the portal and expect contact within a few weeks. Completion cannot wait weeks.
**What staff/org would do:** The SRA portal acknowledges the submission with a reference number. No one calls back. No one explains whether the exchange deposit is secure, whether the completion can be protected, or what happens if completion is missed. The client faces potential breach of contract for missing completion through no fault of their own.
**What the Owner does:** Act immediately on two fronts. The SRA Compensation Fund does protect client account balances — but the completion deadline is a separate problem that requires a new solicitor today, who can contact the other party's solicitor to agree an emergency extension and apply for any court protection needed. Do not wait for the SRA process to resolve the completion timeline.

- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Your deposit is protected under the SRA Compensation Fund — but your four-day completion deadline is a separate and urgent problem the SRA process alone cannot solve. You need a new solicitor instructed today. Here is exactly what to do."
- Who to direct to: SRA intervention team (0370 606 2555 / sra.org.uk/consumers/problems/report-a-solicitor); Law Society Find a Solicitor (solicitors.lawsociety.org.uk) for urgent new instruction; Serious Fraud Office victim support (0207 239 7272); Legal Ombudsman (0300 555 0333) to register the complaint; alert the other party's solicitor directly to avoid being placed in breach of contract
- Evidence to preserve: Exchange contract with deposit amount, completion statement, bank transfer proof of deposit, all Axiom Ince correspondence, mortgage offer documents, search results

### Scenario 19-3: Hired a Fake Lawyer — Court Hearing in Three Weeks and No Real Representation (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Los Angeles County District Attorney's Office press release; California State Bar press release — Efferin Deans / "Law Offices of Deans & Associates," Canoga Park, Los Angeles, California, USA, charged October 2021
**The Problem:** A consumer in the San Fernando Valley hired "Deans & Associates" after finding their business card. They paid a $3,500 retainer for a civil landlord-tenant dispute. The firm appeared legitimate — an office address, business cards showing bar credentials, court filings under Deans' name. They have a hearing in 21 days. A friend sends them a news link: Deans has been charged by the LA County DA with felony impersonation and has never held a California bar license. They call Deans' number: disconnected. They call the LA County Superior Court clerk to ask whether the filed documents are valid and are told "we can't give legal advice." They call the California State Bar helpline and are placed on hold for 18 minutes.
**What staff/org would do:** The State Bar helpline eventually tells them to submit a written complaint online. No one addresses the upcoming hearing. No one explains whether court filings submitted by an unlicensed person have any legal standing or whether the judge will be notified. The client is left managing an active court case with no lawyer and 21 days until the hearing.
**What the Owner does:** Make clear this is urgent. The court must be notified of fraudulent representation and can grant a continuance of the hearing. The client should contact the LA County Superior Court clerk immediately to advise of the fraud and request continuance by written motion. A licensed replacement attorney must be found before the hearing date. Fee recovery may be available through the State Bar Client Security Fund.

### Scenario 19-4: Billed for Phone Calls That Never Happened — 83 Phantom Call Entries on the Invoice (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** SRA disciplinary notices; Legal Futures ("Fraud, overbilling and more — SRA bans non-solicitors over misconduct") — National Accident Law / Chantelle Dallas, England, UK, overbilling identified 2020, SRA action 2021–2022
**The Problem:** A personal injury claimant in Manchester receives their final bill from a high-volume personal injury firm. Reviewing the itemized schedule, they spot 21 separate "telephone attendance" charges at £50 each — totalling £1,050 — on dates when they have no recollection of being contacted. They check their mobile bill: 17 of those calls simply do not appear on their records. They call the firm's billing department and are transferred to three different people. The third person states "all entries are verified in the file" and promises a callback within 48 hours. No call comes. A second attempt produces the same promise and the same silence.
**What staff/org would do:** A billing supervisor eventually emails to say the entries were "confirmed by a paralegal supervisor." No itemized justification is provided. The firm issues a final demand for the full amount and treats the matter as closed.
**What the Owner does:** Tell the client that mobile phone records showing calls never occurred are direct evidence of billing fraud — fabricating time entries, not a billing estimate dispute. The process is: first, a formal written complaint to the firm's named Complaints Partner under the SRA-mandated internal complaints procedure. If unresolved or rejected within 8 weeks, escalate to the Legal Ombudsman. In parallel, report the fabricated entries to the SRA as a conduct matter.

- Evidence to preserve: Itemized bill from the firm listing every telephone attendance charge, mobile phone bill for all dates in question (request itemized records from your provider if not held), all email and postal correspondence with the firm, a written log of every phone call you recall including dates and staff names, any text message confirmations from the firm regarding calls

### Scenario 19-5: Cannot Verify If Your Lawyer Is Enrolled — Fake Advocates Pervasive and Register Inaccessible (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Bar Council of India official statements; Bar Council of Delhi public list of 117 fake advocates (December 2022); Indian Bar Association publication — India, systemic and ongoing
**The Problem:** A family in Delhi has instructed a lawyer to handle a property inheritance dispute involving their late parent's home worth approximately ₹82 lakh. A neighbor warns them that two lawyers on the same street recently appeared on the Bar Council of Delhi's published list of fake advocates. They want to verify their own lawyer's enrollment before handing over original title documents. They call the Bar Council of Delhi's listed number — it rings with no answer. They visit the office in person and are told the verification officer is unavailable and to return with a written application on a working day. There is no public online real-time lookup tool. They leave without verification and with their concern unresolved.
**What staff/org would do:** The clerk hands them a form and says verification will take 2–4 weeks. No emergency pathway exists. No officer can confirm over the counter whether a named advocate is enrolled. The family is turned away with original property documents still at risk of being handed over.
**What the Owner does:** Validate this concern as serious and officially documented — the BCI Chairman publicly stated 35–40% of practitioners may hold fraudulent credentials, and the Bar Council of Delhi publicly named 117 fake advocates in December 2022 alone. Tell the family not to hand over any original documents until they have the lawyer's enrollment number and can cross-reference it. Any genuine enrolled advocate can produce their enrollment certificate and bar roll number immediately on request.

### Scenario 19-6: Estate Planning Done by a Fake Partner — Will May Not Be Valid (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Court records; Smithsonian Magazine — Kimberly Kitchen / McKean County, Pennsylvania, USA, fraud approximately 2003–2013, convicted
**The Problem:** An 80-year-old widower in rural Pennsylvania had their late spouse's estate and will drafted by Kitchen, who served as a partner at a local firm and as president of the county bar association. Following Kitchen's criminal conviction — she had forged her law degree, bar exam results, and registration documents, and had never attended law school or been admitted to the bar — the widower calls the county courthouse to ask whether the will is still legally valid. The clerk says they cannot give legal advice. They call the Pennsylvania Bar Association referral line and wait 22 minutes before hanging up. Their adult child calls on their behalf and also reaches voicemail. The family has no idea whether the estate documents hold legal force.
**What staff/org would do:** Callers are directed between the courthouse, the bar association, and the disciplinary board. None of them explains whether documents drafted by an unlicensed person are valid under Pennsylvania law. The 80-year-old is left with no guidance and no clear route to get estate administration back on track.
**What the Owner does:** Reassure the caller that in Pennsylvania, documents drafted by an unlicensed person may still be legally valid if they accurately reflect the testator's true intent — but this must be confirmed by a licensed attorney reviewing the specific documents. Direct them to the Pennsylvania Lawyers Fund for Client Security for fee recovery, and to a licensed estate solicitor. Recognize the vulnerability of this caller — elderly, bereaved, in a rural area — and keep the guidance simple and accessible.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "A will drafted by someone who was not a licensed attorney may still be legally valid depending on its content — but you need a licensed attorney to confirm this for your specific situation. Do not destroy or alter any documents. Here is where to go."

### Scenario 19-7: Cannot Navigate the Complaint System — No One Will Explain Who to Contact (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Legal Services Consumer Panel 2024 Tracker Survey; Legal Ombudsman 2024/25 Annual Complaints Data and Insight — England and Wales, UK, 2024 (14% of affected consumers cited provider being too hard to reach; 21% did not know how to complain; 49% of firms found to handle first-tier complaints inadequately)
**The Problem:** A homeowner in England instructed a conveyancing solicitor to purchase a property. Six months after completion, they discovered a restrictive covenant on the title — not disclosed before exchange — that prevents the extension they planned to build. They try to raise a complaint with the firm. The client care letter they received runs to nine pages with no clear complaint procedure signposted. They call the firm and are told to email the enquiries address. They send the email and receive an auto-acknowledgement. Three weeks pass with no substantive response. They search online and find five different bodies — the SRA, Legal Ombudsman, Law Society, Citizens Advice, and the Solicitors Disciplinary Tribunal — with no explanation of which one handles which type of issue or in what order they should be contacted.
**What staff/org would do:** After 15 working days the enquiries inbox sends a second auto-reply stating the matter is being reviewed internally. No written acknowledgment of the complaint is issued, violating the SRA's Transparency Rules. No one explains what reviewed means or sets a timeline for a substantive response.
**What the Owner does:** Provide the exact escalation path in order. Step one: formal written complaint to the firm's named Complaints Partner — every SRA-regulated firm is required to designate one. Step two: if no substantive response within 8 weeks, escalate to the Legal Ombudsman, who handles service quality complaints. The SRA handles conduct failures, not service quality — make the distinction clear. Note that non-disclosure of a restrictive covenant may also constitute professional negligence worth a specialist solicitor's opinion.

- Opening reply: "The complaint process for solicitors is genuinely confusing — 21% of UK consumers do not know how to complain, and nearly half of law firms handle first-tier complaints inadequately. Let me give you the exact steps in clear order."
- Who to direct to: Legal Ombudsman (0300 555 0333, legalombudsman.org.uk) — after 8 weeks of firm non-response or after rejection; SRA (0370 606 2555, sra.org.uk) — for conduct failures including not complying with complaints transparency rules; Citizens Advice (0800 144 8848) for free immediate guidance; Professional Negligence Lawyers Association (pnla.org.uk) for referral to a specialist if the covenant failure caused financial loss
- Evidence to preserve: Original client care letter, all correspondence with the solicitor, the completion statement, the title register extract showing the restrictive covenant, any pre-purchase searches or property information forms provided by the firm, all emails and letters related to the complaint with timestamps

### Scenario 19-8: Law Firm Will Not Answer the Phone — Limitation Deadline in 34 Days (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Clio Legal Trends Report 2022 (secret shopper methodology, USA and Canada) — more than 50% of law firms did not respond to client inquiries; only 41% of responding firms provided fee information; 73% of secret shoppers said they were unlikely to recommend the firm
**The Problem:** A small business owner in Toronto, Ontario, needs to file a civil claim against a former supplier for breach of contract. They found a firm advertising commercial litigation services, with four named lawyers on their website. Over three days they have called the main number twice (voicemail each time), emailed via the contact form (auto-reply only), and sent a LinkedIn message to one of the named partners. Nothing. Their statutory limitation period for filing the claim is 34 days away. They do not know whether to keep waiting, find another firm, or whether missing this deadline will permanently destroy their case.
**What staff/org would do:** The firm's voicemail says calls will be returned within one business day. No human calls back. The contact form auto-reply says the firm aims to respond within five business days. Ten business days pass. The limitation deadline closes in with no legal representation in place.
**What the Owner does:** Tell the client that documented research shows more than half of law firms fail to respond to initial contact — waiting further is not a strategy when a hard legal deadline is involved. A 34-day limitation period is a statutory cutoff in Ontario. They must find a new firm today using the Law Society of Ontario Lawyer Referral Service, which offers a free first 30-minute consultation, and brief that firm about the urgency of the deadline.

### Scenario 19-9: Solicitor Missed the Court Deadline — Client Lost Their Appeal Through Filing Failure (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Little v Bloomsbury Law Solicitors [2022] 11 WLUK 351; professional negligence commentary — Bindmans LLP, Limes Solicitors; England, UK, 2022
**The Problem:** A homeowner in London engaged solicitors at Bloomsbury Law to pursue a property dispute appeal. The court issued an "unless order" — a conditional order dismissing the appeal unless the appeal bundle was filed within 35 days. The solicitors became aware of this order nearly two years after it was issued, having failed to monitor court correspondence. They missed the filing deadline. The court refused relief from sanctions and dismissed the appeal. The client is now calling the firm to understand what happened and what their options are. The partner apologizes and says the matter is under internal review — but provides nothing in writing and does not mention professional negligence claims.
**What staff/org would do:** An internal review letter arrives three weeks later acknowledging an administrative oversight. The letter is written defensively, does not quantify the client's loss, does not refer the client to a professional negligence specialist, and makes no offer of compensation. The firm does not proactively explain the client's right to complain to the Legal Ombudsman.
**What the Owner does:** Inform the client clearly: a solicitor missing a court deadline that directly caused loss of their right to appeal is a textbook professional negligence case. The client has the right to claim compensation from the firm and its professional indemnity insurers. They must not accept any informal resolution from the firm without first taking independent legal advice. They have six years from the negligent act to bring a professional negligence claim.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "A solicitor missing a court-imposed deadline that caused you to lose your right of appeal is professional negligence. You have the right to claim compensation. Do not accept any informal offer from the firm without independent legal advice first."
- Who to direct to: Legal Ombudsman (0300 555 0333, legalombudsman.org.uk) for service complaint — must be brought within 6 years of the act and 3 years of the client knowing; SRA (0370 606 2555) for conduct failure; Professional Negligence Lawyers Association (pnla.org.uk) for a free referral to a specialist; Law Society Find a Solicitor (solicitors.lawsociety.org.uk) for independent advice
- Evidence to preserve: All court orders including the unless order and the date it was issued, all correspondence with Bloomsbury Law about the appeal, all instructions given to the firm, any records of when the firm told you about the unless order, the court decision refusing relief from sanctions, any settlement offers received from the firm in writing

### Scenario 19-10: Solicitor Abandoned Practice — Elderly Widow Cannot Reach Anyone or Retrieve Her File (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Solicitors Disciplinary Tribunal decision; Legal Futures ("Solicitor left abandoned law firm in chaotic state") — Universal Solicitors, sole practitioner, England, UK, struck off 2022
**The Problem:** An 80-year-old widow in England instructed a sole-practitioner solicitor two years ago to handle her late husband's probate and estate administration. For the past four months, every call to the firm rings out. Letters go unreturned. She visited the office and found it locked, with an overflowing letterbox and unopened mail visible through the glass. She has no copy of her file, does not know whether probate has been granted, and does not know whether the estate's assets are being protected. When she calls the SRA consumer helpline she is asked to submit a report online — a form requiring the solicitor's SRA registration number, which she does not have. The form takes 20 minutes to complete and generates a reference number with an estimated 8–12 week acknowledgement timeline. No emergency mechanism is offered.
**What staff/org would do:** The SRA acknowledges the online report with an automated email and a case reference number. No contact follows in the first four weeks. No one explains that the SRA can intervene and appoint a substitute solicitor to take over abandoned files. The widow is left managing bereavement and estate uncertainty entirely alone.
**What the Owner does:** Recognize this as a potential abandonment case — the SRA has the power to intervene in a firm and appoint a replacement solicitor to take over client files. Help the caller find the solicitor's SRA ID number using the SRA's public Register, searchable by firm name, so her report is filed accurately and triggers intervention review. Also advise her that the grant of probate is a public record held by the Probate Registry and can be checked to confirm the current status of the estate.
