### Scenario 11-1: Contaminated Deli Meat, Active Outbreak, Regulatory Complaint Intake Shows Zero Reports During Deaths (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** USDA-FSIS internal review report, October 22, 2024 ("No consumer complaints have been documented for this establishment for the past year"); CDC Listeria outbreak data — Boar's Head Provisions, 61 ill, 10 deaths, 19 states; FSIS Boar's Head January 2025 public review (18 pages); U.S. Senator Blumenthal federal investigation announcement; PissedConsumer reviews for Boar's Head customer service, 2024

**The Problem:** A 67-year-old immunocompromised consumer hears the Boar's Head deli meat recall on the news and realises she ate liverwurst from this brand three days ago. She now has a fever, muscle aches, and a stiff neck — early Listeria symptoms. She calls the company's published recall hotline (1-800-352-6277). After 22 minutes on hold a representative answers, says the company cannot provide medical advice, and reads back the same scripted recall notice language she already heard on the news. She asks whether her specific lot number is in the recall. The representative says he cannot confirm individual lot numbers. She asks whether the company had prior knowledge of contamination. He says he has no information on that. She asks about medical cost coverage. He says to "keep your receipt" and call back with a case number — no case number is issued. She hangs up. Unknown to her, the FSIS internal report produced that same month states verbatim: "No consumer complaints have been documented for this establishment for the past year" — despite 65+ critical violations logged since January 2022 and 10 consumer deaths.

**What staff/org would do:** Place the caller on hold, read from the recall script, disclaim medical advice, offer no referral to emergency services, collect no complaint data, issue no case number, and close the interaction. The company's complaint intake records the interaction as zero consumer escalations — consistent with the documented FSIS finding.

**What the Owner does:** Recognises this is a medical emergency, not a customer service call. Confirms the product is recalled and explains Listeria symptoms are time-critical in older and immunocompromised individuals. Immediately provides the nearest emergency department information and instructs the consumer to tell the ER she consumed Listeria-recalled deli meat. Notes that the FSIS regulatory complaint intake system showed zero complaints during an active deadly outbreak — meaning the formal system failed — and provides the correct FSIS consumer complaint line. Helps the consumer document symptoms and purchase for a potential legal claim.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a medical emergency. Fever, muscle pain, and stiff neck after consuming recalled Listeria deli meat are warning signs of serious infection. Please go to an emergency room now and tell them you ate Listeria-recalled Boar's Head liverwurst. Do not wait. Once you are safe, I will help you with the company and regulatory complaint."
- Direct the consumer to: Nearest emergency room (Listeria in elderly/immunocompromised persons has a 20-30% fatality rate without treatment); FSIS Consumer Complaint Monitoring System (1-800-674-6854); CDC FoodSafety.gov illness reporting; food safety attorneys handling Boar's Head class actions (filed U.S. District Courts, 2024)
- Evidence to collect: product packaging with lot number and use-by date (photograph all sides); purchase receipt; written log of symptoms with exact dates and times; full record of the customer service call (date, time, hold duration, what the representative said); all medical records from the emergency visit
- Feedback category tag: food_safety_emergency / listeria / product_recall / institutional_complaint_failure / regulatory_capture

### Scenario 11-2: Fake Paneer Sold by Vendor, FSSAI Complaint Portal Unresolved for Seven Months, Vendor Using Local Connections to Block Inspection (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** FSSAI surveillance data 2024 — 83% of paneer samples in Noida/Greater Noida failed quality norms; 40% declared unsafe for consumption; Jharkhand seizure of 4,000 kg fake paneer (2024); FSSAI Food Safety Connect portal statistics 2024-25: 7,705 adulteration complaints received, 1,753 (22%) unresolved; Newsgram, YouthInc Magazine, Medical Dialogues reporting (2024-2026)

**The Problem:** A family in Noida, Uttar Pradesh purchased paneer from a local shop for a dinner function. Three family members became ill within six hours — nausea, vomiting, and cramps. An informal laboratory test on the remaining paneer found it adulterated with starch, vegetable fat, and non-dairy whiteners, consistent with documented fake paneer operations. When the family returned to the vendor, he denied any problem and threatened to call his "contacts" at the municipal council. The family filed a complaint through FSSAI's Food Safety Connect portal in January 2024. Seven months later the portal still shows "Under Review." No inspector has visited the shop. The vendor continues to operate. When the family calls the NCH helpline about the FSSAI status, the representative confirms the complaint is "forwarded to the district food safety officer" but gives no timeline and says they cannot follow up on individual FSSAI cases.

**What staff/org would do:** The FSSAI portal auto-acknowledges and routes the complaint to a district food safety officer whose caseload far exceeds capacity. The DFSO may issue a notice; the vendor's local contacts facilitate delay. After months the case may be marked "resolved" without any enforcement visit. The NCH helpline correctly states it cannot compel FSSAI action.

**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the vendor's threatening behaviour as intimidation and flags it as a separate reportable offense. Explains the documented 22% non-resolution rate in FSSAI's own portal and provides a parallel escalation path through the State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission, which operates independently of the FSSAI administrative chain and has enforcement powers. Advises that media exposure of documented adulteration cases has historically accelerated FSSAI action where the administrative route is stalled. Recommends the family retain the adulterated sample in a sealed, refrigerated container for independent testing.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (acute illness has passed; ongoing community risk from the vendor continuing to operate)
- Opening reply: "I am sorry your family was made ill and that the vendor responded with threats rather than accountability. What you are describing — adulterated paneer and a complaint stuck for months — is a documented pattern in India right now. The FSSAI portal alone may not be enough. Let me show you the routes that actually have enforcement power."
- Direct the consumer to: State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (file at edaakhil.nic.in; parallel to FSSAI and independently adjudicated); FSSAI escalation to the State Commissioner of Food Safety (separate from district-level DFSO); local media with documented lab report (shown to accelerate enforcement in similar cases); Anti-Adulteration Cell where available in the state; NCH helpline 1915 (for the consumer complaint against the vendor, separate from the FSSAI regulatory complaint)
- Evidence to collect: informal laboratory adulteration test report; sealed sample of the remaining paneer (refrigerated, labelled with purchase date); purchase receipt; screenshots of the FSSAI Food Safety Connect complaint showing submission date and current "Under Review" status; written account of the vendor's threatening statements; medical bills from illness treatment; names and contact details of all family members who fell ill

### Scenario 11-3: Cantaloupe Salmonella — Serial Recall Expansions, Consumer Given Clearance on Day One, Ate the Product, Now Ill (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** CDC Salmonella outbreak investigation update, November 2023 (Malichita/Rudy brand cantaloupe, 407 ill in 44 US states, 6 deaths); Canada PHAC public health notice; CFIA recall warnings issued in four waves — November 1, 14, 17, and 24, 2023; CBC News; Health Canada food recall process documentation; 153 confirmed Canadian Salmonella cases, 6 Canadian deaths

**The Problem:** A retired teacher in Winnipeg, Manitoba purchased a Malichita brand cantaloupe from a major grocery chain on October 28, 2023. On November 3 she heard about the first recall on the radio and called the store's customer service line. A representative checked the recall list and told her the recalled lots were specific California batches and her melon's lot number was not included — she was safe to consume it. She ate the cantaloupe. On November 18 a second expanded recall now included her lot number and a broader date range. She called the store again; transferred three times, eventually confirmed her lot was now recalled — but she had already eaten the product. She began experiencing diarrhoea and stomach cramps two days later. She called the store a third time to report her illness. She was placed on hold for 31 minutes and disconnected.

**What staff/org would do:** Each recall expansion is treated as a separate event with no notification to consumers who received prior clearance. Store representatives have no unified tracking of which customers bought product across the multiple recall waves. The consumer is told to "contact Health Canada" or "call your doctor." No proactive notification, no compensation, and no follow-up on the disconnected call.

**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the documented serial recall failure — four expansions over 24 days meant consumers who acted on the first notice were still at risk, a pattern the CFIA recall record confirms. Validates that this was not the consumer's error. Confirms her symptoms are consistent with Salmonella (onset 12-72 hours post-consumption, diarrhoea is the primary symptom). Advises her to seek medical care and to report her illness to the local public health unit, which contributes to outbreak monitoring data. Helps her document the sequence of store calls and the conflicting information she received as evidence of retail notification failure.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (mild Salmonella illness is typically self-limiting; escalate to urgent if the consumer is elderly, immunocompromised, or experiences bloody diarrhoea, high fever, or severe dehydration)
- Opening reply: "This was not your fault. The recall was expanded four times over 24 days, which means consumers who did exactly the right thing on day one were still left at risk. Your illness needs to be reported — both for your own medical care and to make sure your case is counted in the outbreak data."
- Direct the consumer to: Health Canada food safety complaints (canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/food-nutrition/food-safety/information-consumers/report-complaint.html); CFIA consumer complaints 1-800-442-2342; local public health unit (to report Salmonella case — this adds to outbreak investigation); CFIA food recall database for all four November 2023 recall notices (archived at inspection.canada.ca); personal injury lawyer if medical costs are significant
- Evidence to collect: grocery receipt or loyalty card record showing purchase date; photograph of product label with lot number and best-before date; written log of all calls to the store — dates, times, hold durations, representatives' names, and exactly what each said; CFIA recall notices for all four November waves (print or screenshot each); medical records from the illness
- Feedback category tag: food_recall / serial_recall_failure / consumer_notification_failure / call_center_misinformation / salmonella / canada

### Scenario 11-4: Food Adulteration Complaint on National Helpline — Six Months of "Pending with Company", No Inspection, No Resolution (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** India Ministry of Consumer Affairs disclosure, March 2025 — National Consumer Helpline food and beverage complaints nearly doubled from 27,754 (2023) to 53,228 (2024); Karnataka highest volume at 7,811 complaints; FSSAI Food Safety Connect portal 2024-25: 7,705 adulteration complaints, 1,753 (22%) unresolved as of reporting; Storyboard18, Medical Dialogues, Newsgram reporting (2024-2026)

**The Problem:** A grocery shop owner in Bengaluru — Karnataka, the state with the highest food and beverage complaint volume in India in 2024 — discovered that a batch of branded spice powder he purchased for resale contained adulterants: brick dust and artificial colour. He filed a complaint on India's National Consumer Helpline (NCH) in February 2024. He was given a docket number and told the matter would be forwarded to the company within 30 days. In August 2024, six months later, the complaint portal shows "Pending with Company." The company replied once denying any issue. NCH treated that denial as a closure of the reply loop, effectively marking the complaint as "responded to" without verifying whether the consumer's grievance was addressed. He cannot get anyone at NCH to reopen the case. When he calls the helpline (1915), he is queued, eventually answered, told the case is "forwarded for arbitration," given no date, and the call ends. He has called eight times over three months.

**What staff/org would do:** NCH routes the complaint to the company; the company denies; NCH treats the company's response as sufficient closure. No inspector is dispatched. No penalty is assessed. The helpline does not distinguish between "responded to" and "resolved."

**What the Owner does:** Explains the documented NCH limitation — company denial alone does not constitute resolution under the Consumer Protection Act 2019. Escalates to the District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (CDRC), which has independent adjudication powers and can compel refunds, penalties, and corrective action. Advises filing a parallel complaint with the state food safety commissioner's office, which has enforcement authority separate from the consumer protection chain.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Six months of 'Pending with Company' with a denial response that closed your case is not a resolution — it is a documented failure of the NCH process. You have the right to escalate to a body that can actually compel an outcome, and that route is available to you now."
- Direct the consumer to: District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (file online at edaakhil.nic.in; claims under Rs 50 lakh; filing fee nominal; no lawyer required for straightforward adulteration cases); FSSAI state-level food safety commissioner (separate enforcement authority from NCH); Spices Board of India (separate regulatory jurisdiction for adulterated spice products); NCH grievance redressal officer (if helpline staff are not reopening a legitimate case, escalate within the NCH hierarchy)
- Evidence to collect: original purchase invoice for the adulterated spice batch; sealed sample of the adulterated product (labelled with purchase date); photographs of the adulterated product alongside a normal sample; NCH complaint docket number and all portal status screenshots with dates; record of all NCH calls — dates, times, and what was said; company's denial response from the NCH portal; any informal or formal laboratory test report on the adulterated product
- Feedback category tag: food_adulteration / ncH_process_failure / complaint_no_resolution / consumer_helpline_wait / india

### Scenario 11-5: Food Poisoning Reported to Authorities — Formally Told the System Does Not Investigate Individual Reports (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** UK Food Standards Agency official consumer guidance, "Report a food safety or hygiene issue" (gov.uk/food-safety); Citizens Advice official guidance, "Reporting to Trading Standards" (2024); multiple UK local council published food complaint policies citing FSA guidance — Chelmsford, Derby, Surrey, BCP Council; FSA Annual Report of Incidents, Resilience and Prevention 2024/25

**The Problem:** A single mother in Manchester spent four days bedridden after eating a takeaway meal she believes caused food poisoning. She missed four days of work, losing income she cannot recover, and incurred £180 in GP and prescription costs. She called Citizens Advice for guidance. Citizens Advice directed her to Trading Standards at her local council. She filed the Trading Standards report online with her medical records, receipt, and the restaurant's name. Three weeks later she received an email stating: "Individual reports of suspected food poisoning are not routinely investigated because it is very difficult to determine which meal, from all the food eaten in the last few days, may have caused illness." Case closed. She called Citizens Advice again. The adviser said she could consult a solicitor but could not advise further. She cannot afford a solicitor. There is no other free pathway she has been told about.

**What staff/org would do:** Apply the published FSA policy — close the individual complaint without investigation. Offer no pathway for refund, medical cost recovery, or confirmation the restaurant will be inspected. Citizens Advice correctly states its scope ends at the referral stage. The system by design produces a closure outcome before a case officer reviews the specifics — the threshold for investigation is formally set above any single illness report.

**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges this policy failure and names it plainly: the formal system discriminates against lower-income consumers who have no practical access to private legal redress, while higher-income consumers can obtain accountability through solicitors. Identifies two routes the consumer was not told about: the Environmental Health Officer (EHO) at her local council, who operates independently of Trading Standards and can act on intelligence reports without the single-poisoning investigation threshold; and Small Claims Court, which allows claims under £1,000 without a solicitor at minimal filing cost.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Being told your complaint 'cannot be investigated' is not the end of the road — but it is a real policy failure that hits people without legal resources the hardest. There are two routes you were not told about, and one of them does not cost you anything."
- Direct the consumer to: Environmental Health Officer at Manchester City Council (can act on intelligence reports without individual investigation threshold — file as an intelligence report, not a standard complaint); Small Claims Court (gov.uk/make-court-claim-for-money; claims under £1,000 require no solicitor; court fee approximately £35 for claims under £300); FSA complaint about local authority enforcement failure (fsasupport@food.gov.uk — if the EHO also refuses to act on a documented illness); Citizens Advice Extra services for low-income consumers facing discrimination in public service access
- Evidence to collect: GP medical records showing illness dates and diagnosis; takeaway receipt or delivery app order record (Deliveroo/Just Eat/Uber Eats records show exact order details); all Trading Standards correspondence including the formal closure letter — this document is the key evidence of rights denial; bank statements showing income loss during illness days; any other social media reviews or complaints from the same restaurant showing a pattern
- Feedback category tag: food_poisoning / complaint_refused / institutional_discrimination / trading_standards_failure / access_to_justice / rights_violation / uk

### Scenario 11-6: Lead-Contaminated Children's Applesauce Pouches — Company Files Bankruptcy Before Any Refund or Medical Compensation Can Be Reached (Pattern A+E)
**Real Basis:** CDC MMWR reports, April and July 2024 — 566 confirmed, probable, and suspected cases of elevated blood lead levels in children across 44 US states, the District of Columbia, and Puerto Rico; FDA advisory on WanaBana, Schnucks, and Weis applesauce pouches; WanaBana Chapter 7 (liquidation) bankruptcy filing; Motley Rice law firm and lawsuit-information-center.com class action documentation; NBC News; North Carolina Health News investigative report; Dollar Tree distribution documentation (2023) — product sold at $1.25 per pouch at discount retailer serving lower-income communities

**The Problem:** A mother from Charlotte, North Carolina purchased WanaBana apple cinnamon applesauce pouches from Dollar Tree throughout 2023 — affordable at $1.25, marketed as a healthy snack, sold in brightly coloured packaging. In October 2023, news reports revealed the pouches contained extremely high lead levels from cinnamon sourced in Ecuador that was deliberately adulterated with lead chromate — a chemical added to increase the cinnamon's weight and sale value. Her two-year-old daughter's blood test came back at 8.5 micrograms per deciliter (CDC action level: 3.5 mcg/dL). Lead exposure at this age causes permanent neurological damage. She tried to call WanaBana to demand medical cost coverage. WanaBana did not respond to media or consumer inquiries and subsequently filed for Chapter 7 bankruptcy — liquidating the company before affected families could obtain any refund, medical coverage, or accountability. There is no customer service line. Dollar Tree's representative refers her back to WanaBana. There is no entity left to call.

**What staff/org would do:** WanaBana has dissolved. Dollar Tree deflects to the manufacturer. No refund process, no medical fund, and no customer service pathway exists. The consumer receives nothing.

**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges this is among the most severe documented food safety outcomes — deliberate supply chain fraud that poisoned 566 children, sold through a discount retailer whose own investor disclosures document a customer demographic of lower-income families, compounded by corporate dissolution that removed all direct accountability. Explains that bankruptcy does not extinguish class action claims — creditors can file claims against the liquidation estate, and class action lawsuits allow families to pool claims when individual losses are insufficient to sustain solo litigation. Connects the consumer to the active class action filings and explains how to submit a creditor's claim in the WanaBana Chapter 7 proceeding.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (acute poisoning is past; ongoing concern is permanent neurological harm and long-term developmental monitoring)
- Opening reply: "I am so sorry about your daughter. What happened was caused by deliberate fraud — lead was added to cinnamon to increase its sale weight and price. The company closing does not mean you have no options. There are legal routes available through the bankruptcy court and class action process."
- Direct the consumer to: Motley Rice and other plaintiffs' firms handling WanaBana class action lawsuits (consumer can opt into existing class action or obtain independent representation); WanaBana Chapter 7 bankruptcy court creditor's claims filing (U.S. Bankruptcy Court — file before the claims bar date); local health department for ongoing free developmental lead monitoring (many US local health departments provide free lead monitoring for children under 6 with elevated blood lead); state attorney general's consumer protection office (several state AGs pursued separate enforcement action against WanaBana and Dollar Tree)
- Evidence to collect: Dollar Tree purchase receipts or credit/debit card statements showing purchase history throughout 2023; product packaging with lot numbers (FDA recall list specifies covered lots); child's blood lead test results and all medical records from 2023 onward; all developmental monitoring records; medical billing records for every lead-related appointment; documentation of WanaBana's Chapter 7 filing (publicly available via U.S. Bankruptcy Court PACER); any correspondence attempted with WanaBana or Dollar Tree
- Feedback category tag: food_safety_lead_contamination / supply_chain_fraud / company_dissolution / financial_harm / child_vulnerability / economic_discrimination / dollar_tree

### Scenario 11-7: Nine Years of Pursuing a National Consumer Forum Case — Dismissed in April 2024, No Affordable Appeal Route (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** NCDRC (National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission) ruling, April 2024, dismissing Government of India's Rs 640 crore (approximately USD 77 million) complaint against Nestle India; original FSSAI recall and nationwide ban, June 2015; FSSAI test results: lead at 17.2 ppm (permissible limit 2.5 ppm — 6.88 times above limit); undeclared MSG despite "no added MSG" labelling; 400 million packets recalled; Just-Food.com, Grokipedia, Vintage Legal, India court records

**The Problem:** A father of three from Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh served Maggi noodles to his family regularly between 2012 and 2015. After the FSSAI recall in 2015 confirmed lead levels nearly seven times the permissible limit and undeclared MSG in the "no MSG" labelled product, he gave a sworn statement supporting the government's NCDRC complaint, believing India's highest consumer protection body would obtain accountability for the millions of consumers exposed. He tracked the case for nine years. In April 2024 the NCDRC dismissed the government's complaint, ruling that post-reformulation tests showed lead within limits and that there was insufficient evidence of "actual harm" to individual consumers. When he called the NCDRC consumer helpline (1800-11-4000) to understand what happened to his case, he was told the matter was closed and he would need to consult a lawyer to challenge the ruling in the Supreme Court. He cannot afford Supreme Court litigation.

**What staff/org would do:** The NCDRC helpline staff confirm the case is dismissed and direct the consumer to seek legal counsel. They provide no information on alternative remedies, no guidance on individual filing options separate from the government case, and no referral to free legal aid. The call ends with no actionable pathway.

**What the Owner does:** Explains that the NCDRC dismissed the government's specific filing on evidentiary grounds particular to that complaint — which does not extinguish the consumer's individual claim based on documented exposure during the 2015 window, before reformulation. Individual claims in lower consumer forums carry a lower evidence threshold than the NCDRC's standards for a Rs 640 crore government action. Notes that district consumer forums allow pro se filing for claims under Rs 50 lakh and that the Supreme Court Legal Services Committee provides free legal aid for eligible consumers wishing to challenge the NCDRC ruling.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Nine years is a long time to wait, and the NCDRC dismissal is genuinely painful. But the dismissal of the government's Rs 640 crore case on specific evidentiary grounds is not the same as your individual case being over. Here is what is still available to you."
- Direct the consumer to: District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission for individual filing (pro se, under Rs 50 lakh, file at edaakhil.nic.in); Supreme Court Legal Services Committee (free legal aid for eligible consumers — nalsa.gov.in); Jago Grahak Jago consumer helpline (1800-11-4000 — press for escalation beyond the standard case status update); Consumer Coordination Council and other consumer advocacy organisations who tracked the Maggi case and may provide legal solidarity support
- Evidence to collect: any proof of Maggi purchase in 2015 — receipts, photographs, or family witness accounts; medical records from 2015 if any illness was documented; the NCDRC case reference number; personal copy of the sworn statement given to the consumer advocacy group; FSSAI 2015 recall notice (publicly archived) establishing that the product was recalled for lead at 17.2 ppm
- Feedback category tag: food_safety_complaint_dismissed / institutional_refusal / nine_year_wait / consumer_protection_failure / india / nestle_maggi

### Scenario 11-8: Dairy-Free Label, Allergic Reaction, Manufacturer Insists Label Is Accurate, No Product Withdrawn (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** FDA targeted survey results, September 17, 2024 — 210 dark chocolate products labelled "dairy-free" tested; 13 (6.2%) found to contain milk allergens; FDA warning letter to Knickerbocker 365 Inc., September 30, 2024 — inadequate allergen management on shared equipment for wheat, milk, and walnut; UK Food Standards Agency Annual Report of Incidents, Resilience and Prevention 2024/25 (documents systemic allergen incident patterns); FSA official enforcement guidance on allergen labelling under UK retained food law

**The Problem:** A woman in Birmingham with a clinically confirmed milk allergy purchases dark chocolate bars labelled prominently "Dairy-Free" from a health food chain. She has bought the same product before without incident. This time, within an hour of eating two squares, she develops hives, lip swelling, and difficulty swallowing — anaphylaxis warning signs. She uses her EpiPen and attends A&E. When she recovers she emails the manufacturer with her medical records attached. Two weeks later the company replies: "Our product is manufactured on dedicated dairy-free equipment and the dairy-free declaration on our packaging is accurate. We are confident in our quality assurance procedures." She also contacts the FSA consumer team. The FSA acknowledges her complaint, notes it has been logged as a food incident report, and advises the product will be reviewed "as part of ongoing surveillance" — with no specific timeline, no product test commitment, and no product withdrawal.

**What staff/org would do:** The manufacturer deflects with a quality assurance statement and does not offer product recall or re-testing. The FSA logs the complaint as a systemic incident rather than initiating a targeted product investigation. No proactive consumer warning is issued. The product remains on sale.

**What the Owner does:** Explains that an international FDA survey published in September 2024 found 6.2% of "dairy-free" dark chocolate products in the market contained undeclared milk — meaning the label cannot be trusted at face value in this product category, and the company's quality assurance confidence is directly contradicted by documented market evidence. Notes the FSA has powers under retained UK food law to compel product sampling and withdrawal when a documented medical event is linked to a specific product. Escalates the complaint to the FSA Incident Management System with a formal request for targeted product sampling — individual documented medical events strengthen enforcement cases. Advises on the personal civil claim route under UK consumer law for mislabelling.

### Scenario 11-9: Recalled Product Still on Shelf — Grocery Store Hotline Has No Information, Consumer Left Without Answer (Pattern B+D)
**Real Basis:** U.S. PIRG Education Fund "Food Recall Failure" report (2022) — 84% of the 26 largest US grocery chains received a failing grade on recall notification; 80% had no publicly documented consumer notification process; no chain provided in-store information on where recall notices are posted; Aldi, Publix, and Walmart named as failing chains; PIRG "Food for Thought 2024" follow-up report confirming: "No one has to contact grocery stores or restaurants. No one has to notify consumers."

**The Problem:** A consumer at a major US supermarket chain (rated failing by PIRG) receives a news alert on her phone: a ready-to-eat deli product she buys regularly has been recalled for Listeria contamination. She is standing in the store at that moment. She walks to the deli counter and asks the staff member if the recalled product is on the shelves. The staff member says she has heard nothing about a recall and sends her to customer service. Customer service contacts a supervisor, who also has no recall notification. The supervisor suggests she call the corporate customer service number. She calls from the parking lot — 14 minutes on hold. A representative answers and tells her "recalls are handled by the manufacturers" and that the store has no system to tell her whether her specific store stocked the specific lot numbers in the recall. She is told to check the FDA recall database herself. She does. The FDA database lists the manufacturer's recall notice. It does not tell her which stores received the recalled product.

**What staff/org would do:** No store in the chain has a documented internal process for notifying customers who bought recalled products — consistent with the PIRG finding that 80% of major chains lack any documented process. The corporate call centre representative deflects to the manufacturer. No supervisor with specific product knowledge is escalated to. The consumer leaves the store without knowing whether the product she was about to purchase is safe.

**What the Owner does:** Confirms the recall is real, provides the specific product name and lot numbers from the current FDA recall notice, and confirms this is an active Listeria risk. Notes that the structural failure the consumer experienced is documented — the PIRG 2024 report explicitly states "No one has to contact grocery stores or restaurants. No one has to notify consumers." Advises how to report both the product concern and the store's lack of recall response to the FDA, and how to request a full purchase history from the chain's loyalty card system to identify any previously purchased recalled items.

**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (no illness yet; consumer is acting proactively)
- Opening reply: "You did exactly the right thing, and the system failed you at every step.
