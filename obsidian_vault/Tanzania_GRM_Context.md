# Tanzania GRM Context — World Bank Infrastructure Projects

## Overview

Tanzania has a large portfolio of World Bank-financed infrastructure projects, primarily roads and transport corridors. The Riviwa GRM platform supports these projects by providing a structured, auditable grievance redress mechanism that meets World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) requirements.

## Project Affected Persons (PAPs)

### Who Are PAPs?
Project Affected Persons (PAPs) are individuals, households, businesses, or communities whose livelihoods, assets, or access to resources are affected — positively or negatively — by a World Bank-financed project.

PAPs in Tanzania road projects typically include:
- Households within the right-of-way (ROW) who must relocate
- Farmers whose land or crops are affected by construction
- Business owners whose premises are displaced or whose access is disrupted
- Community members who experience construction impacts (noise, dust, traffic)
- Women and vulnerable groups disproportionately affected by displacement
- Indigenous communities in areas traversed by roads

### PAP Registration
PAPs are registered in the Riviwa platform during the Resettlement Action Plan (RAP) baseline survey. Registered PAPs receive a unique PAP ID and can track their compensation claims through the app.

Anonymous PAPs (not in the registry) may still submit grievances. They will be guided to register if their grievance involves compensation or resettlement entitlements.

## Key Institutions

### TANROADS
**Tanzania National Roads Agency** — the executing agency for national highway projects (trunk roads, national corridors). TANROADS manages GRM at the national level and reports to the World Bank.

### TARURA
**Tanzania Rural and Urban Roads Agency** — the executing agency for regional and district road projects. TARURA manages GRM for rural road programs under World Bank financing.

### PCU (Project Coordination Unit)
The PCU sits within TANROADS or TARURA and provides overall coordination for project implementation, including GRM oversight. The PCU receives escalated grievances from LGA PIUs.

### LGA PIU (Local Government Authority Project Implementation Unit)
Each district or municipality has a PIU that manages day-to-day project implementation and GRM at the local level. LGA PIU officers are the first responders for most community grievances.

### WBCU (World Bank Country Unit)
The World Bank's Tanzania country office provides fiduciary oversight and reviews the GRM performance during supervision missions. Semi-annual GRM reports must be submitted to the WBCU.

## World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF)

### What Is the ESF?
The ESF is the World Bank's policy framework governing environmental and social risk management for all Bank-financed projects approved after October 2018. It replaced the earlier Safeguard Policies.

The ESF includes ten Environmental and Social Standards (ESS):
- **ESS1**: Assessment and Management of Environmental and Social Risks
- **ESS2**: Labor and Working Conditions
- **ESS3**: Resource Efficiency and Pollution Prevention
- **ESS4**: Community Health and Safety
- **ESS5**: Land Acquisition, Restrictions on Land Use and Involuntary Resettlement
- **ESS6**: Biodiversity Conservation and Sustainable Management of Living Natural Resources
- **ESS7**: Indigenous Peoples / Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities
- **ESS8**: Cultural Heritage
- **ESS9**: Financial Intermediaries
- **ESS10**: Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure

### GRM Requirements Under ESS10
ESS10 mandates that all World Bank projects have an accessible, culturally appropriate grievance mechanism. Key requirements:

1. **Accessibility**: The GRM must be accessible to all affected parties, including those without literacy or technology access (hence the SMS, phone, and in-person channels).
2. **Transparency**: PAPs must be informed about the GRM process at project outset and regularly during implementation.
3. **Timely response**: Grievances must be acknowledged and resolved within defined timeframes.
4. **Confidentiality**: Grievant identity must be protected unless the grievant consents to disclosure.
5. **No retaliation**: Project implementers must ensure no retaliation against grievants.
6. **Records**: All grievances must be documented and accessible for World Bank review.
7. **Reporting**: Semi-annual GRM reports must be published and shared with the World Bank.

### GRM Requirements Under ESS5 (Resettlement)
For projects involving land acquisition, the Resettlement Action Plan (RAP) must include:
- A specific GRM for compensation disputes
- Timeframes for resolving compensation grievances
- Independent arbitration mechanisms for unresolved disputes
- Regular consultation with displaced persons

## Common Grievance Categories in Tanzania Road Projects

Based on historical data from Tanzania road projects, the most common grievance categories are:

### Compensation Disputes
The largest category, typically 35–45% of all grievances. Issues include:
- Undervaluation of land or assets
- Disputed ownership or boundaries
- Delayed compensation payments
- Exclusion from compensation lists (missed during surveys)
- Currency of compensation (cash vs. replacement land)

### Construction Impact
Second largest category, 25–35% of grievances:
- Damage to crops during site clearing
- Damage to structures outside the ROW
- Dust and noise affecting nearby homes, schools, hospitals
- Blocked access to properties, markets, or water sources
- Contamination of water sources by construction runoff

### Traffic and Road Safety
Common near active construction zones:
- Accidents involving construction vehicles (particularly heavy trucks)
- Dangerous road conditions during construction (unmarked diversions, potholes)
- Speed of contractor vehicles in communities
- Children's safety near construction sites

### Worker-Community Relations
Recurring issues in long-duration projects:
- Harassment or disputes between migrant workers and local community members
- Non-hiring of local labor (complaints about contractors preferring outside workers)
- Non-payment or late payment of locally hired casual labor
- Sexual harassment or gender-based violence (GBV) — treated as Critical priority

### Environmental Grievances
- Extraction of gravel or sand from unauthorized locations
- Disposal of construction waste near water bodies or homes
- Destruction of trees or vegetation beyond the ROW
- Burning of waste on site

## GBV (Gender-Based Violence) Grievances — Special Handling

GBV grievances require special handling outside the standard GRM process:
- **Must not** be logged in the main GRM system with identifying information
- Referred immediately to the dedicated GBV specialist or focal person
- Confidential records kept separately from the main grievance log
- Survivor-centered approach: the survivor determines next steps
- GBV incidents involving project workers trigger immediate contractor action (investigation, suspension, or dismissal)

In the Riviwa platform, GBV grievances are flagged with the `safety_hazard` category and `critical` priority, and the system automatically notifies the GBV focal person.

## Resettlement Action Plan (RAP) Key Terms

- **RAP** (Resettlement Action Plan): the document that describes how displaced persons will be compensated and resettled. Required under ESS5 for projects involving involuntary displacement.
- **ARAP** (Abbreviated RAP): a simplified RAP for projects affecting fewer than 200 people or involving only minor land acquisition without physical displacement.
- **RAP Implementation**: the process of actually paying compensation and relocating households, usually 6–12 months before construction begins in the affected area.
- **Cut-off Date**: the date after which new settlers or new structures in the ROW are not eligible for compensation. Must be widely publicized.
- **Replacement Value**: compensation must cover the full replacement value of affected assets, not just market value. Replacement value includes transaction costs (legal fees, moving costs).
- **Livelihood Restoration**: for PAPs who lose their primary income source, the project must provide livelihood restoration programs (skills training, micro-enterprise support, etc.).

## Consultation Requirements

World Bank projects require meaningful consultation at multiple stages:
- **Pre-disclosure**: project information must be disclosed before construction begins
- **During RAP preparation**: affected communities must be consulted on the RAP design
- **During implementation**: regular community meetings (at least quarterly)
- **Grievance mechanism launch**: PAPs must be informed about how to submit grievances, in Swahili and local languages

## Reporting Requirements

### Semi-Annual GRM Reports (World Bank)
Must include:
- Total grievances received, by type, category, and priority
- Resolution status and average resolution times
- Analysis of trends and systemic issues
- Actions taken to address systemic issues
- Pending grievances and proposed actions

### Annual Social Audit
An independent social auditor reviews GRM performance annually and interviews a sample of grievants to verify that resolutions were satisfactory and that no retaliation occurred.

## Tanzania-Specific Context Notes

### Languages
- **Swahili (Kiswahili)** is the national language and must be supported in all GRM communications
- Many rural PAPs speak primarily local ethnic languages (Sukuma, Haya, Chagga, etc.)
- The Riviwa platform supports Swahili and English; in-person GRM officers must speak local languages

### Land Tenure
Tanzania has multiple land tenure types: granted right of occupancy, customary right of occupancy, and village land. Compensation disputes often involve unclear tenure documentation. The 1999 Land Act and Village Land Act govern land rights.

### Vulnerable Groups
Special attention must be given to:
- Female-headed households (often underrepresented in land ownership)
- Elderly persons with limited mobility (in-person channel is critical)
- Persons with disabilities
- Ethnic minorities and indigenous hunter-gatherer communities (especially in northern Tanzania)
