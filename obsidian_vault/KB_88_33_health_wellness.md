## 33. Health & Wellness

### Scenario 33-1: Sexual Assault Reported to Wellness Chain, Complaint Contained Rather Than Escalated (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** BuzzFeed News investigation (November 2017) and BusinessWire settlement announcement (October 2023) — Massage Envy franchise documented over 180 sexual assault reports; accused therapists remained employed and were transferred between franchise locations; victims who reported to the corporate complaint line received acknowledgment of "investigation" with no substantive action. Houston-area franchise paid $1 million policy-limit settlement to two assault victims (October 2023).
**The Problem:** A woman has just left a massage therapy session at a wellness chain franchise during which the therapist touched her inappropriately. She called the company's customer service line the same day. The agent told her a "regional manager would be in contact within 48 hours" and offered a complimentary session as goodwill. Five days have passed, no one has called, and she does not know whether the therapist is still working there.
**What staff/org would do:** The complaint is logged as a "customer experience" issue. The regional manager calls once, offers a refund or credit, and closes the ticket. The therapist continues working. No disclosure is made to licensing authorities or police. The complaint system absorbs the report without routing it to genuine resolution.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the severity immediately. Decline to offer vouchers or credits as a substitute for action. Escalate outside the business's own complaint system to an independent authority. Inform the consumer of her right to report to police and to the relevant therapy licensing board. Make clear she is not required to return to that location or engage further with the company's internal process.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "What you experienced is serious and it matters. You do not need to wait for the company to call you back, and you do not need to resolve this through their internal process alone."
- Who to direct: Local police (this is a criminal matter first); the national massage or beauty therapy licensing authority in her country to report an unlicensed or unsafe practitioner; a victim support line or legal aid organisation if she needs guidance; the consumer protection authority if the company is franchised under a regulated business licence
- Evidence to collect: Note the exact date, time, and room of the session and the name or description of the therapist; retain any messages or call records from the company; write a detailed account of what happened while it is fresh; document the name of the call centre agent and exactly what was offered in response
- Feedback category tag: Staff Misconduct / Safety Incident / Complaint Suppression / Urgent

### Scenario 33-2: University Athletes Hospitalised After Workout, Administration Treats Cases as Individual Rather Than Systemic (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Tufts University lacrosse team rhabdomyolysis, September 2024 — more than 20% of the roster hospitalised after a single conditioning session; DWBRLaw plaintiff firm documentation. University of Oregon football players Brenner and Poutasi filed separate suits seeking $11.5 million and $5 million respectively for trainer-induced rhabdomyolysis from a university strength coach (2017 incidents, 2019 lawsuits). Sports Litigation Alert legal analysis of exertional rhabdomyolysis case law.
**The Problem:** A student athlete at a university has been hospitalised with severe muscle pain, dark urine, and elevated kidney stress markers following an intensive conditioning session run by the university's strength coach. Two teammates are in the same ward from the same session. The athletic director visited once, suggested it was "probably dehydration," and said the university's insurance team would be in touch. No coach has been placed on administrative leave. Training continues for the rest of the squad.
**What staff/org would do:** University administration treats each hospitalised athlete's case individually. The coach is not suspended pending investigation. No independent safety review is ordered. The framing — "isolated dehydration" — prevents the pattern of multiple athletes harmed in a single session from being characterised as systemic institutional negligence.
**What the Owner does:** Treat this as a medical emergency with an institutional safety dimension. Support the athlete in securing independent medical documentation that names the probable cause. Make clear that three athletes hospitalised from the same session is not a coincidence — it is a pattern that carries legal and regulatory weight. Connect her to support outside the university's own system.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "You are dealing with both a medical emergency and an institutional response that is minimising what happened. Your health comes first — and the fact that multiple athletes were harmed in the same session is legally significant."
- Who to direct: The treating hospital medical team — ensure rhabdomyolysis is formally diagnosed and documented by name; a personal injury attorney independent of the university; the national student welfare body or higher education ombudsman; the relevant sports safety authority or national athletics regulator as a parallel escalation path
- Evidence to collect: Full hospital records including diagnosis, creatine kinase levels, treatment administered, and the physician's notes on likely cause; names of all teammates hospitalised from the same session; the exact training protocol administered that day (request this in writing from the coach or department immediately); any prior written communications about the session's intensity or objectives
- Feedback category tag: Physical Injury / Institutional Negligence / Complaint Minimised / Emergency Health

### Scenario 33-3: Gym Refuses Cancellation on Medical Grounds, Continues Direct Debit Under Undisclosed Rolling Contract (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** UK OFT/CMA Health and Fitness Clubs enforcement action (2011–2016) — Ashbourne Management Services Ltd found to use unfair contract terms; six major gym operators compelled to improve cancellation rights; £37 million in consumer harm quantified across affected gyms. Citizens Advice Consumer Helpline documented an average of 197 gym-related cases per month (12 months to November 2017), 14% concerning unfair contract terms and 35% substandard service. GOV.UK press release and CMA case record.
**The Problem:** A gym member in London attempted to cancel her membership three months ago after her doctor advised reduced physical activity during pregnancy. The front desk told her cancellation required 30 days' written notice posted to a processing centre. She sent the letter by recorded delivery. The gym continued charging her direct debit. When she called the billing line, the agent told her the letter arrived two days after the billing cycle closed and she owed another full month. She has since been charged for three additional months. The advice she receives changes with each call, and no written confirmation of her cancellation has ever been provided.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent cites the written contract and the missed billing cycle as determinative. The cancellation is logged as pending but unconfirmed. Direct debits continue. When the consumer escalates to the branch manager, the manager confirms the contract terms and states nothing further can be done at branch level.
**What the Owner does:** Confirm that verbal and phone guidance that varies with each call, combined with a failure to acknowledge a valid written cancellation, is itself a consumer protection concern. In the UK, pregnancy is a recognised ground for contract exit under consumer legislation. Advise the consumer to cancel the direct debit at her bank immediately as an interim protective step while pursuing the formal complaint.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You cancelled in writing on medical grounds, provided evidence of delivery, and the gym continued to charge you. You have clear rights here and practical steps you can take today."
- Who to direct: Citizens Advice Consumer Service (citizensadvice.org.uk) for contract review and complaint drafting; Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) — report under the health and fitness club unfair terms case record; her bank to cancel the direct debit under the Direct Debit Guarantee and raise a chargeback for charges after the cancellation letter was received; Trading Standards (via Citizens Advice) for the unfair terms complaint
- Evidence to collect: The recorded delivery proof of her original cancellation letter with the confirmation of receipt date; all bank statements showing charges after that date; a written log of every call with the gym — date, hold time, agent name, and what was said; a written request to the gym for its formal complaints procedure and a copy of the full contract she signed
- Feedback category tag: Billing Dispute / Contract Rights / Cancellation Refused / Call Center Inconsistency

### Scenario 33-4: Gym Sold "No Contract" Membership Verbally, Now Enforcing Exit Fee That Contradicts Sales Promises (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) — Fitness First Australia provided an enforceable undertaking after advertising membership flexibility while requiring signed agreements with cancellation terms and exit fees. Consumer Affairs Victoria maintains sector-specific guidance on health and fitness centre unfair contract terms in direct response to documented complaint volume. ACCC enforcement undertaking (accc.gov.au).
**The Problem:** A consumer in Melbourne joined a gym after a salesperson explicitly told him "there are no lock-in contracts here — you can leave any time." He signed digitally on a tablet in the gym and was not given a copy of the document. When he attempts to cancel after three months due to relocating interstate, the gym tells him he is in a 12-month contract with a $450 exit fee. When he calls the member services line, he is placed on hold for 28 minutes, transferred twice, and told that verbal promises are not binding on the written contract. He has now received the contract by email and it directly contradicts what the salesperson said.
**What staff/org would do:** Member services cites the written contract as the definitive agreement. The call is logged as a standard cancellation request, not as a misrepresentation complaint. The $450 exit fee stands. No reference is made to the ACCC undertaking or the protections available under Australian Consumer Law.
**What the Owner does:** Inform the consumer that under Australian Consumer Law, verbal representations made during a sale that influenced his decision to sign are legally binding even if they contradict the written contract. Misrepresentation during a sale is a separate cause of action from a contract dispute. Connect him to the ACCC and Consumer Affairs Victoria complaint channels.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What was said to you when you signed up matters legally. A written contract does not cancel out a salesperson's misrepresentation — especially if that misrepresentation was the reason you agreed to sign."
- Who to direct: Consumer Affairs Victoria (consumer.vic.gov.au) for a formal unfair contract terms or misleading conduct complaint; ACCC (accc.gov.au) to report misleading and deceptive conduct under the Australian Consumer Law; his bank or card provider to dispute any exit fee charge applied without his written agreement
- Evidence to collect: Request the full signed contract by email immediately and retain it; document the salesperson's exact words and the date and location of the sign-up conversation; a written log of each member services call with dates, hold times, agent names, and what was said; any promotional materials or website screenshots from the time of sign-up showing "no contract" or "cancel any time" language
- Feedback category tag: Sales Misrepresentation / Contract Dispute / Call Center Failure / Exit Fee

### Scenario 33-5: Memory Supplement Customer Calls About No Improvement, Agent Provides Marketing Script Despite Internal Study Failure (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** FTC and New York Attorney General v. Quincy Bioscience (Prevagen) — complaint filed January 2017; court ruled against the company December 2024 after seven years of litigation. Quincy's own Madison Memory Study failed to show statistically significant improvement over placebo on any of nine cognitive tasks. The active ingredient apoaequorin cannot cross the blood-brain barrier. The company continued advertising "clinically proven" memory benefits throughout the litigation period. Sources: FTC press release (December 2024); Center for Science in the Public Interest (CSPI); AARP reporting; National Consumers League.
**The Problem:** A 71-year-old woman has been purchasing a memory supplement for five months at $65 per month ($325 total) after seeing an advertisement claiming it was "clinically shown to improve memory." She has noticed no change. When she calls the customer service line to ask for evidence of the clinical study and to request a refund, the agent reads from a script citing the company's "own research," says the product "works differently for different people," and tells her she should continue for at least six more months before expecting results. No mention is made of any regulatory action against the company or of the fact that the company's own trial did not demonstrate statistically significant benefit over placebo.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent delivers the standard retention script. No refund is offered. The consumer is told the product requires more time. The call is logged as resolved. No disclosure is made that the FTC challenged the company's core clinical claims for seven years before a court ruled against the company.
**What the Owner does:** Inform the consumer that documented regulatory and legal action exists specifically against this type of "clinically proven" supplement claim. She has the right to request a full refund and to report to the FTC. Older adults spending fixed income on products with contested clinical bases deserve direct, factual support rather than retention scripting.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You asked for the clinical evidence and were given a marketing response. You deserve a factual answer — and you may have grounds for a refund based on misrepresentation."
- Who to direct: FTC complaint portal (reportfraud.ftc.gov); FTC refund database (ftc.gov/refunds) — check whether the specific product has an active refund programme; her state Attorney General's consumer protection division; National Consumers League Fraud Center (fraud.org)
- Evidence to collect: All purchase receipts and subscription records; a copy of the advertisement or webpage that led to the purchase — screenshot if found online; a written record of the call including the date, agent name, and what was stated; a written request to the company for a copy of the clinical study they referenced and their response to that request
- Feedback category tag: Supplement Fraud / Information Failure / Call Center Misrepresentation / Vulnerability — Older Adult

### Scenario 33-6: MLM Health Supplement Distributor Loses Savings, Company Redirects to More Recruiting Rather Than Acknowledging Loss (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** FTC v. AdvoCare International (October 2019) — $150 million settlement for operating an illegal pyramid scheme; FTC data showed 72.3% of distributors earned zero compensation; 18% earned between $0.01 and $250; only 6% earned between $250 and $1,000 in 2016. FTC returned $149 million to consumers (May 2022). FTC v. Herbalife (July 2016) — $200 million settlement; both companies' own agent channels directed struggling distributors toward renewed recruiting activity rather than exit, as documented in FTC enforcement records.
**The Problem:** A woman in Nairobi invested KES 85,000 in a health supplement multi-level marketing programme after a cousin recruited her with income projections showing potential monthly earnings of KES 40,000. Eight months in, her actual total earnings from product sales are KES 3,200. Her upline distributors on a WhatsApp group continue to tell her she must recruit more contacts to "unlock" her income level. When she contacted the company's regional office to ask for a refund of her initial product investment, the agent told her refunds are not available and that her losses reflect insufficient recruiting activity — not the business model.
**What staff/org would do:** The regional agent confirms the no-refund policy. The company's channel redirects the consumer toward increased recruiting rather than acknowledging that the income structure makes profitability statistically improbable for the overwhelming majority of distributors. No earnings disclosure is provided.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the financial harm without blame. Explain that the documented regulatory record of this model — globally, including major FTC enforcement actions — shows that the advice to recruit more contacts as the solution to low earnings is itself the mechanism of harm, not a genuine remedy. Advise her to stop recruiting further contacts to limit cascading harm to her network.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What you have described — investing money, earning very little, and being told the solution is to recruit more people — is a documented pattern in MLM supplement businesses that regulators have acted against worldwide. This is not your failure."
- Who to direct: Competition Authority of Kenya (CAK — competition.go.ke) to file a complaint about deceptive business practices and pyramid scheme structure; Consumer Federation of Kenya (COFEK); the FTC refund database (ftc.gov/refunds) if the specific company has been subject to US enforcement action, as some global refund programmes accept international claims
- Evidence to collect: The original income projection materials shown at recruitment; a full accounting of all money invested (product purchases, starter kits, materials, travel) versus all money received from sales; all written and WhatsApp communications from upline distributors including income claims; the original recruitment presentation or any printed or digital materials with earnings projections
- Feedback category tag: Financial Fraud / MLM Deception / Earnings Misrepresentation / Consumer Trap

### Scenario 33-7: Unlicensed Injectable Procedure at Aesthetic Clinic Causes Vision Damage, Business Denies Any Wrongdoing (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** NYC Council / NY State joint investigation (June–September 2024): 15 of 15 medical spas inspected had violations; 73% lacked medical oversight during procedures; investigators found expired and suspected counterfeit injectable products; documented consumer injuries included burns, double vision, and harmful poisoning from injections administered by unlicensed individuals. Report: "Moving the Needle" (December 2025). NY Department of State issued formal consumer warning. Statewide: 223 businesses inspected, 87 cited for possible violations. Four NYC businesses lost licences.
**The Problem:** A woman in Johannesburg received an injectable aesthetic treatment at a walk-in clinic for ZAR 950. The procedure was carried out by a receptionist who described the injecting person as a "qualified aesthetician." Within 36 hours she has developed drooping in her left eyelid and blurred vision in that eye. She returned to the clinic. The manager told her the reaction is "temporary and completely normal" and offered a complimentary follow-up session with the same staff. No doctor is present on site. The product used was not named or shown to her at any point.
**What staff/org would do:** The clinic manager characterises the reaction as a routine side effect, offers a return appointment, and produces no record of the injecting person's qualifications. No licensed medical practitioner is involved. The consumer is implicitly deterred from escalating externally by the reassurance that this is normal.
**What the Owner does:** Treat this as a medical emergency requiring independent evaluation, not reassurance from the same business that caused the harm. Advise the consumer to go to a licensed medical doctor or emergency room immediately. Make clear she should not return to the clinic for the follow-up session. Support her in preserving evidence and escalating to the health regulator.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Eyelid drooping and vision changes after an injectable procedure require immediate evaluation by a qualified medical doctor — not a follow-up at the clinic that administered the treatment. Please seek medical care today."
- Who to direct: The nearest emergency department or licensed ophthalmologist for same-day evaluation; Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA — hpcsa.co.za) to report unlicensed practice of medicine; South African Department of Health; National Consumer Commission (NCC — thencc.org.za) for the consumer protection complaint; South African Police Service if the practitioner performed a medical procedure while unlicensed
- Evidence to collect: Photograph the affected area immediately and at regular intervals; note the clinic's name, physical address, and the names of everyone she spoke to on both visits; request in writing the name and batch number of the product used and the formal qualifications of the person who administered it; retain the appointment booking, any WhatsApp communication with the clinic, and the payment receipt
- Feedback category tag: Unlicensed Medical Procedure / Physical Harm / Active Concealment / Emergency

### Scenario 33-8: Children's Health App Collects Weight and Diet Data Without Parental Consent, Support Agent Provides False Information About Storage (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** FTC v. WW International / Kurbo (March 2022) — Kurbo app marketed to children as young as 8 collected sensitive health data including weight, height, food intake, and exercise habits without verifiable parental consent, in violation of COPPA. The FTC found that no functioning parental access, correction, or deletion mechanism existed through any customer-facing channel. FTC official press release, March 2022.
**The Problem:** A parent in Germany discovered her 10-year-old daughter downloaded a health tracking app that collected height, weight, daily food diary entries, and mood check-ins. When the parent called the company's support number to ask what data had been collected and to request deletion, the agent told her "the app only stores data locally on the device." This contradicts the app's own privacy policy, which references cloud synchronisation and data processing by third-party analytics providers. The agent provided no data deletion mechanism and told her to "uninstall the app" as the resolution. No GDPR-compliant response was offered.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent repeats the inaccurate statement about local storage, provides no deletion pathway, logs the call as "resolved — user advised to uninstall," and closes the ticket. The child's health data remains stored. No Data Protection Officer contact is provided. No formal Subject Access Request process is initiated.
**What the Owner does:** Clarify that under GDPR, the consumer has a legal right to know what data was collected, where it is stored, and to have it deleted — and that "uninstall the app" is not a legally compliant response to a data erasure request. The agent's statement about local-only storage directly contradicts the company's own privacy policy, making this both a data protection failure and a misinformation incident.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You are legally entitled to a full account of what data was collected about your daughter and to have it deleted. The support agent's response was inaccurate and does not satisfy the company's obligations under GDPR."
- Who to direct: Germany's Federal Commissioner for Data Protection and Freedom of Information (BfDI — bfdi.bund.de) — file a formal GDPR Article 17 right-to-erasure complaint; the app company's Data Protection Officer (DPO), whose contact details are required to be published under GDPR Article 37 — request these in writing; if the company's lead EU establishment is in another member state, the relevant lead supervisory authority under the GDPR one-stop-shop mechanism
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot the app's privacy policy including the cloud synchronisation and third-party analytics sections; document every support interaction including date, agent name, and the exact claim that data is stored locally; send a formal written Subject Access Request under GDPR Article 15 to the company's Data Protection Officer requesting a full account of data collected, how it is stored, with whom it is shared, and the retention period; note the response date — GDPR requires a response within 30 calendar days
- Feedback category tag: Children's Data / Privacy Rights / GDPR Non-Compliance / Information Failure / Agent Misinformation

### Scenario 33-9: Parent Purchases Height Supplement for Child Based on Fabricated Social Media Reviews (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** FTC v. TruHeight (April 2026) — company marketed height-enhancing supplements to children and teenagers using deceptive advertising; fake customer reviews written by its own employees; and automated bots generating fabricated endorsements across social media platforms. $4 million FTC judgment; $750,000 immediate payment; company barred from false health claims and fake reviews. FTC official press release, April 2026.
**The Problem:** A parent in Mumbai purchased a supplement marketed to increase height in children aged 8–16, costing approximately INR 3,800 for a three-month supply, after finding dozens of five-star reviews from apparent verified customers on social media and the product's website. After two months of use with no visible result, she searched more carefully and found articles questioning the scientific basis of the product. When she contacted customer service for a refund, the agent cited a "no refund after 30 days" policy and told her the product "takes 6–12 months to show results." No mention was made of any regulatory action against the company or of the evidentiary basis for the product's claims.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent applies the standard 30-day refund cut-off and offers a discount on the next purchase. No information is provided about the authenticity of the reviews that drove the purchase or about any regulatory proceedings involving the company.
**What the Owner does:** Inform the parent that if her purchase decision was substantially influenced by reviews later found to have been fabricated by the company itself, she has grounds for a misrepresentation complaint that is separate from and overrides the standard return policy. The fabricated reviews are the mechanism of harm that induced the contract — not a peripheral marketing detail.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You made this purchase based on reviews that may have been manufactured by the company. That changes the nature of your complaint from a return request to a misrepresentation matter — and a no-refund policy does not override that."
- Who to direct: India's Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA — consumeraffairs.nic.in); National Consumer Helpline (toll-free 1800-11-4000); the Advertising Standards Council of India (ASCI — ascionline.in) if the fabricated reviews appeared in advertising formats; her credit or debit card provider to raise a chargeback on grounds of misrepresentation
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot and archive the specific reviews that influenced the purchase decision — including the product listing page, social media posts, and any testimonials — before they are removed; retain the purchase receipt and delivery confirmation; document the customer service interaction in writing including the date, agent name, and what was offered; search for any regulatory or news coverage of the specific product or company and save those records
- Feedback category tag: Fake Reviews / Supplement Fraud / Child Consumer / Sales Misrepresentation / Corruption

### Scenario 33-10: Personal Trainer Injures Client in Single Session, Gym Invokes Liability Waiver and Denies All Responsibility (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Makimba Mimms v. Manassas World Gym — jury awarded $300,000 (2008 verdict) for trainer-induced rhabdomyolysis from an uncertified CrossFit trainer; Washington Post coverage (October 2008) and VerdictSearch legal database. Block O'Toole & Murphy v. New York Sports Club — $2.25 million settlement for personal trainer negligence (firm's public case archive, blockotoole.com). In both documented cases, the gym's initial position at first consumer contact was a denial of liability.
**The Problem:** A man in Lagos attended a personal training session at a commercial gym. The trainer put him through 120 repetitions of weighted squats in a single session. The following day he was hospitalised with severe muscle pain, dark urine, and a creatine kinase count that the treating physician described as consistent with rhabdomyolysis. When he called the gym's general manager, the manager told him that the signed membership waiver covers "all training activities" and that the company bears no liability for exercise-related injury. The trainer is still working.
**What staff/org would do:** The general manager cites the liability waiver as a complete defence. No internal incident report is filed. The trainer is not stood down pending any review. The consumer is told his only option is to seek legal advice if he wishes to pursue the matter further.
**What the Owner does:** Inform the consumer that liability waivers in most jurisdictions do not protect a business from gross negligence or conduct that recklessly disregards a client's safety. An uncertified or inadequately supervised trainer administering 120 weighted repetitions in a single session is not a routine training programme, and rhabdomyolysis from a single training session is a documented legal pattern. Advise him to preserve all evidence before any records can be amended or lost.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "A liability waiver does not automatically protect a gym from responsibility when a trainer delivers a programme that is unsafe. Rhabdomyolysis following a single training session is a recognised pattern in personal injury case law — and you have grounds to pursue this."
- Who to direct: A personal injury lawyer experienced in negligence claims; the Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Commission (FCCPC — fccpc.gov.ng) for a consumer protection complaint; the relevant national council or body governing fitness trainer certifications to report the trainer's qualifications for review; the gym's insurance provider directly to file a personal injury notification
- Evidence to collect: Full hospital records including diagnosis by name, creatine kinase levels, kidney function results, duration of treatment, and the physician's notes on probable cause; a written account — drafted immediately — of the exact exercises, repetitions, weights, and duration of the session as administered; the trainer's name and any available information about their qualifications; a copy of the membership waiver; all communications from the gym following the injury, including the general manager's phone call summarised in writing the same day
- Feedback category tag: Physical Injury / Trainer Negligence / Liability Waiver Dispute / Complaint Denial at First Contact
