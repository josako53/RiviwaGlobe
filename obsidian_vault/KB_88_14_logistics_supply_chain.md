### Scenario 14-1: Missing Freight Denied — Delivery Receipt Technicality (Pattern A/D)
**Real Basis:** BBB complaint database (bbb.org/us/ct/greenwich/profile/logistics/xpo-logistics-inc-0111-87118220); WHNT News investigative report on XPO Logistics complaints, 2021–2024; XPO Logistics accumulated 285 BBB complaints, 133 unaddressed
**The Problem:** A business owner's shipment of 2 pallets (10 items, total value $40,500) was delivered short — only 1 pallet with 2 items arrived. The driver did not disclose the shortage, so the delivery receipt was signed without exception notation. The business owner has video footage clearly showing only one pallet being offloaded. XPO Logistics has denied the claim in full on the grounds that the consignee signed the delivery receipt without noting "short," and the customer service representative says the case is closed with no further escalation available.
**What staff/org would do:** Reiterate the liability exclusion clause — "the consignee signed the delivery receipt without notation, therefore no shortage claim is valid" — and close the case without requesting or reviewing the video evidence. Offer no escalation pathway beyond the original claims department.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the significant financial loss, validates the business owner's frustration, asks for the video evidence immediately, and confirms this is not a closed matter — a carrier's liability cannot be extinguished by a procedural signature when documented countervailing evidence of shortage exists.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (serious financial harm but no immediate safety risk)
- Opening reply: "This is a serious loss and XPO's position does not hold if you have video evidence of the shortage. That footage can override a signed receipt under the Carmack Amendment — federal law governing carrier liability. Let's make sure the evidence is preserved and in the right hands immediately."
- Direct the consumer to: File a formal freight claim under the Carmack Amendment (49 U.S.C. § 14706) — the federal statute governing carrier liability for loss and damage in US interstate commerce; re-escalate in writing directly to XPO's dedicated freight claims department (separate from general customer service); file a complaint with the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) at fmcsa.dot.gov; if the claim is denied again, consider a small claims or civil court action using the video as primary evidence
- Evidence to collect/preserve: The video footage of the delivery (download immediately and back up with original timestamp metadata intact), the delivery receipt (scan both sides), the original bill of lading, invoice for all 10 items, photographs of what was actually received
- Feedback category tag: Lost Freight / Claim Wrongfully Denied / Carrier Liability / Insufficient Evidence Standard

### Scenario 14-2: Business Told No Compensation Exists — 4-Year Systematic Deception (Pattern A/B)
**Real Basis:** ACCC media release, December 19, 2023 (accc.gov.au); court-enforceable undertaking accepted by ACCC; Australia Post and StarTrack Express $2.9 million AUD compensation programme for approximately 11,500 affected business contract customers
**The Problem:** A small business owner sent multiple packages via Australia Post's business contract over several years. Two packages were lost and one arrived damaged. Each time the business contacted Australia Post, the customer service representative stated clearly that no compensation was payable to business contract customers — framed as a firm and absolute policy. The business accepted this and did not pursue the matter further. The business has now received a letter from Australia Post notifying them of the ACCC compensation programme and is confused, angry, and unsure what they are owed or how the process works.
**What staff/org would do:** Direct the customer to a web form for the compensation programme without explaining what they are entitled to, how the compensation amount is calculated, or what the expected timeline is. No acknowledgment that the business was deliberately misinformed.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms directly that Australia Post's representatives gave the business false information over multiple years — conduct that the ACCC determined was likely deceptive under Australian Consumer Law — and that the customer is now entitled to compensation. Helps them understand their entitlement and how to claim it effectively.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You were given incorrect information by Australia Post — repeatedly, over years. The ACCC confirmed this was likely deceptive conduct under Australian Consumer Law, and the compensation programme exists precisely for businesses in your position. You have a right to claim and you should not accept a low offer without understanding the basis for the calculation."
- Direct the consumer to: Contact Australia Post directly referencing the December 19, 2023 ACCC court-enforceable undertaking and request a written breakdown of the compensation calculation; if dissatisfied with the amount offered or the process, contact the ACCC directly at accc.gov.au or 1300 302 502; for postal service disputes not resolved through Australia Post's process, contact the Commonwealth Ombudsman at ombudsman.gov.au or 1300 362 072
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Original business contract with Australia Post, tracking numbers for every lost or damaged parcel, any written, email, or chat records where Australia Post stated no compensation was payable, invoices or declared values for goods lost or damaged, records of any costs incurred as a result of the loss
- Feedback category tag: Lost Goods / Damaged Goods / Systematic Misinformation / Consumer Law Violation / Compensation Denied

### Scenario 14-3: Cargo Held at Airport — Customs Officer Demands Bribe Per Kilogram (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** CBI press release; Logistics Insider report ("CBI Arrests Mumbai Customs Official for ₹10.20 Lakh Bribery"); Deccan Herald news — CBI arrests Customs Superintendent at Air Cargo Complex, Sahar (CSMIA), Mumbai, demanding ₹10 per kg of cargo, total demand ₹10.20 lakh, August 2023
**The Problem:** An importer's time-sensitive goods — perishable components required for a manufacturing facility's production line — have been held without explanation at the Air Cargo Complex at Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International Airport, Mumbai for three days. The Customs House Agent acting on the importer's behalf has communicated informally that a Customs Superintendent is demanding payment before the consignment will be cleared. The importer is losing approximately ₹2 lakh per day in production stoppage costs and is under pressure from their agent to pay and resolve the situation quickly.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent advises the importer to pay to avoid further delay, framing it as the only practical solution and citing "how things work here." The Customs department's official inquiry desk provides only vague responses about "procedural checks still under way" with no timeline.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately identifies this as extortion by a government official — a criminal offence under the Prevention of Corruption Act 1988 — confirms the importer is not required to pay, and provides a clear, actionable escalation path to the same authority that arrested a Sahar Air Cargo Customs Superintendent for identical conduct in August 2023.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (financial harm escalating at ₹2 lakh per day; coercion by a government official is ongoing)
- Opening reply: "This is not a legitimate customs requirement — this is extortion, and it is a criminal offence under Indian law. You are not required to pay. The Central Bureau of Investigation arrested a Customs Superintendent at this same facility in August 2023 for demanding exactly this type of payment. There is a direct path to the authority that handled that case."
- Direct the consumer to: File a complaint immediately with the CBI Anti-Corruption Branch (CBI AC) — the unit responsible for the August 2023 Sahar arrest; file simultaneously with the Central Vigilance Commission (CVC) via the real-time complaint portal at cvconline.gov.in; send a written complaint (email creates a timestamped record) to the Commissioner of Customs at the Air Cargo Complex; if approached again, do not pay — any payment made after a formal complaint is logged strengthens the bribery case
- Evidence to collect/preserve: All written or WhatsApp communications from the agent quoting the demand and the rate per kilogram; the consignment documentation (airway bill, bill of lading, customs declaration, commercial invoice); a daily log of dates the cargo has been held; written calculation of daily production losses; names and designations of any officials mentioned by the agent
- Feedback category tag: Customs Extortion / Official Misconduct / Cargo Held / Bribery / Rate-Per-Kilogram Demand

### Scenario 14-4: First Class Mail Never Arrived — Legal Document, Deadline at Risk (Pattern A/D)
**Real Basis:** Ofcom press release ("Ofcom fines Royal Mail £10.5m for poor delivery performance," December 2024); Ofcom investigation into Royal Mail 2023/24 performance; Royal Mail First Class delivery rate 74.5% against 93% regulatory target; Ofcom Residential Postal Tracker 2024 (25% of residential users report delayed mail as most common issue)
**The Problem:** A consumer posted an important legal document by First Class mail on a specific date. The document never arrived. The recipient — a solicitor's office — has confirmed in writing that nothing was received. The consumer contacted Royal Mail customer service, which told them that First Class mail is untracked, no compensation is available unless the item was sent by Special Delivery, and there is nothing more Royal Mail can do. A filing deadline tied to the document's receipt is now at risk.
**What staff/org would do:** Confirm the standard terms and conditions — First Class without Special Delivery is uninsured and untracked — offer a standard lost mail report form with a 15-working-day response window, and close the interaction without mentioning any further escalation route.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the urgency of the legal deadline, confirms that Royal Mail's delivery performance during this period was formally found by Ofcom to be in systemic breach of its legal obligations, and provides the specific escalation route that Royal Mail's customer service did not disclose.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (legal deadline is at risk and the document cannot simply be re-posted in time)
- Opening reply: "Royal Mail's delivery performance was formally penalised by Ofcom for exactly this type of failure — First Class delivery reached only 74.5% against a 93% legal obligation, affecting millions of items. You have escalation rights beyond their standard customer service response, and the legal deadline needs to be addressed in parallel right now."
- Direct the consumer to: Contact the solicitor's office immediately to request a deadline extension, referencing the Ofcom enforcement action against Royal Mail for 2023/24 delivery failures as documented justification; file a formal written complaint with Royal Mail (this is a mandatory first step before POSTRS will accept the case); escalate to POSTRS — the Postal Redress Service — the independent postal redress scheme at cedr.com/postrs; if the document had financial value or the deadline causes material loss, check whether home or contents insurance covers postal loss
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Certificate of posting from the Post Office counter (this proves the item was tendered for delivery), the date and Post Office branch of posting, written confirmation from the solicitor's office that nothing was received, any Royal Mail customer service reference number, documentation of the legal deadline and any consequences of missing it
- Feedback category tag: Lost Mail / Delivery Failure / Legal Deadline / Escalation Not Disclosed / Regulatory Context

### Scenario 14-5: Camera Lost by Parcel Carrier — £20 Compensation Cap, 2.5-Month Wait, Item Excluded (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Consumer complaint documentation (GetParcelRefund.com claims analysis, September 2023); Citizens Advice Parcels League Table 2025 (Evri: 2.25 stars overall, delivery problem rate 37%, accessibility score 1.6 stars — lowest of all carriers); Evri standard £20 default compensation cap; Evri complaints procedure page directing formal complaints to Resolver
**The Problem:** A consumer sent a camera via Evri. The camera was lost in transit. After waiting two and a half months for a response to their claim — during which repeated webchat contacts produced only new reference numbers and no updates — Evri informed the consumer that cameras are on its "non-compensated items" list and no compensation will be paid. The consumer had not purchased enhanced insurance at booking. The camera's replacement value is over £300. Evri's webchat subsequently closed the session before the consumer could respond.
**What staff/org would do:** Confirm that cameras are listed as non-compensable under Evri's standard terms and conditions, close the claim with reference to the policy, offer the final webchat reference number, and make no acknowledgment of the 2.5-month delay or any partial goodwill offer.
**What the Owner does:** Validates the consumer's loss, confirms that a 2.5-month claims response delay is itself an unacceptable service failure, and identifies that a blanket policy exclusion does not necessarily override all consumer rights — particularly where Evri offered insurance at booking without clearly disclosing that cameras fell within an excluded category.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "A 2.5-month wait followed by a refusal citing a policy the booking flow did not clearly disclose is not necessarily the final answer. If Evri offered insurance at the point of booking without specifically warning that cameras were excluded from cover, that non-disclosure may be challengeable under the Consumer Rights Act 2015."
- Direct the consumer to: Contact the Citizens Advice consumer helpline (0808 223 1133) for a case assessment on whether the exclusion was adequately disclosed; submit a formal complaint to Evri through Resolver (as Evri explicitly states this carries more weight than webchat) to create a documented formal complaint record; if Evri's response is unsatisfactory within 8 weeks, escalate to POSTRS; consider a Small Claims Court claim via Money Claim Online (gov.uk/make-court-claim-for-money) — claims under £10,000 are low-cost to file and courts have found against carriers with blanket exclusion lists where the item description at booking did not trigger a clear warning
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Screenshots of every step of the Evri booking flow (particularly the insurance offer stage and any exclusion notices shown), all webchat transcripts from every contact attempt (download immediately — these expire), all three or more reference numbers in a single document, original purchase receipt or proof of value for the camera, the final Evri decision email or chat closing the claim
- Feedback category tag: Lost Parcel / Compensation Refused / Exclusion Policy / Complaint Delay / Webchat Loop

### Scenario 14-6: Fake Delivery Text — Consumer Pays "Customs Fee," Card Compromised (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** FTC Consumer Sentinel Network 2024 Data Book; FTC press release March 2025 ("New FTC Data Show a Big Jump in Reported Losses to Fraud to $12.5 Billion in 2024"); $470 million lost to text-based scams in 2024; FCC consumer alert on carrier impersonation smishing; FBI IC3 2024 Internet Crime Report
**The Problem:** A consumer received a text message appearing to come from USPS/FedEx/UPS stating their parcel was held at a sorting facility and a customs clearance fee of $3.49 was required via a link. They clicked the link, which displayed a convincing carrier-branded payment page, entered their debit card details, and paid. Within 24 hours, their card has been charged an additional $189 in unrecognised transactions from two separate merchants. A second text has now arrived from a "carrier support number" stating the parcel is still held and requesting further payment. The consumer is not sure whether they have a real package in transit or not.
**What staff/org would do:** The bank's fraud line places the consumer on hold and then instructs them to complete an online dispute form, which takes 5–7 business days to process. The real carrier's customer service confirms they do not handle scam reports and redirects the consumer to the FTC website with no further assistance.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately recognises this as an active fraud situation where the card details are compromised and further charges are possible in real time. Provides step-by-step emergency instructions prioritised by urgency.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (card details are actively compromised; unauthorised charges are ongoing; second contact from scammers increases risk of further financial loss)
- Opening reply: "Stop — do not click the second link or respond to the second text. Your card details have been stolen and need to be cancelled immediately, not just disputed. This is a documented scam that cost Americans $470 million in 2024 alone. There are clear steps to take right now, starting with your bank."
- Direct the consumer to: Call the card issuer's fraud line immediately using the number printed on the back of the physical card and specifically request card cancellation — not a dispute — to prevent further charges; report to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov (this creates an official record and helps track the scam network); file a report with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center at ic3.gov; forward the original scam text to 7726 (SPAM) which reports it directly to the mobile carrier network; if a door-tag physical version was also received, report it to the US Postal Inspection Service at postalinspectors.uspis.gov
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Screenshot the original scam text immediately before deleting it, note the exact URL of the fake site (do not revisit), note the phone number that sent the second contact, screenshot all unrecognised bank transactions with full dates and amounts, keep all legitimate order confirmation or shipping emails for comparison to distinguish real from scam communications
- Feedback category tag: Delivery Fraud / Smishing / Card Compromise / Financial Scam / Carrier Impersonation

### Scenario 14-7: Container Cargo Held at Port — "SOP" Bribe Demanded, Demurrage Accumulating (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Philippine Tribune investigative article ("Bribes, brokers and a broken customs system," August 2025); US State Department 2025 Investment Climate Report (Philippines Bureau of Customs named as one of the country's most corrupt agencies); Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 (Philippines rank 114 out of 180 countries); Philstar.com business reporting
**The Problem:** A Philippine importer of construction machinery has two container loads of equipment sitting at the port after arrival. Their licensed customs broker has communicated informally that P240,000 in "SOP" payments — unreceipted cash to Bureau of Customs officials — is required before the containers will be released for pick-up. The official BOC tracking system shows the containers as "under examination" with no scheduled completion date. Demurrage charges are accruing at P50,000 per day, creating compounding financial pressure to pay and end the situation.
**What staff/org would do:** The customs broker advises the importer to pay, citing that official channels create more complications and take far longer. The BOC's official inquiry line confirms only that the containers are "under examination" and provides no timeline or named officer responsible.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the real financial pressure from accruing demurrage but confirms this is a documented and systemic form of corruption that the importer is not legally or ethically obligated to participate in, and provides escalation routes that have produced enforcement outcomes.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (financial pressure is increasing but this is a systemic corruption pattern requiring careful documentation, not immediate payment)
- Opening reply: "What is being called 'SOP' is bribery — a criminal act — and you are not required to pay. The Bureau of Customs has been formally identified by the US State Department and Transparency International as operating with systemic corruption. Formal complaint channels exist, have produced results, and your documentation of this demand is itself valuable."
- Direct the consumer to: File a complaint with the Office of the Ombudsman (Philippines) — the constitutional body mandated to investigate and prosecute government misconduct (ombudsman.gov.ph); contact the BOC's Internal Affairs Service (IAS) directly and request the name and designation of the examining officer; contact the Presidential Anti-Corruption Commission (PACC) at pacc.gov.ph for cases involving government officials; if the broker is pressuring payment, this may itself constitute facilitation of bribery — they may be reported to the Professional Regulation Commission (PRC) which licenses customs brokers
- Evidence to collect/preserve: All message or written communications from the broker quoting the SOP amount, the broker's full name and PRC accreditation number, the container tracking numbers and current system status (screenshot with date/time), the date of arrival and each subsequent day held in a written log, demurrage invoices as they accrue, copies of all import documentation (bill of lading, commercial invoice, packing list, import permit if applicable)
- Feedback category tag: Customs Corruption / Unofficial Payment Demand / Port Delay / Official Misconduct / Broker Facilitation

### Scenario 14-8: Parcel Complaint Loop — Three Contacts, Three Reference Numbers, No Resolution (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Citizens Advice Parcels League Table 2025 (published November 2025); Opinium Research consumer survey n=8,000 UK adults (conducted August–September 2025); 47% of UK consumers who experienced a parcel problem faced further problems when trying to complain; overall complaint satisfaction at 49%, ranging from 32% to 56% by carrier
**The Problem:** A UK consumer's parcel containing a birthday gift was left visible on a public doorstep rather than in a safe place or with a neighbour, and was subsequently stolen. The consumer has contacted the carrier's webchat three times over two weeks. Each contact generated a new reference number, asked the consumer to resubmit the same photographs and tracking information, and stated the case was "under review." No callback has been offered, no compensation decision communicated, and no timeline given. The consumer does not know whether it is the carrier or the retailer who bears responsibility, or where they can take the complaint next.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue a fourth reference number, request the same evidence again via a new web form submission, state that investigations take up to 28 working days, and provide no explanation of who decides the outcome, what criteria are applied, or how to formally escalate.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the circular complaint loop — which Citizens Advice found in 2025 affects 47% of UK consumers who try to complain — and provides a direct, linear path that breaks the cycle by converting the interaction from an informal "contact" to a formal complaint carrying specific legal obligations and timelines.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You have landed in the complaint loop that nearly half of UK parcel customers experience — three contacts, three reference numbers, and no movement. The way out is to convert this from an informal webchat contact into a formal written complaint. That changes the carrier's legal obligations and starts a clock that enables independent escalation."
- Direct the consumer to: Send a formal written complaint by email to the carrier's registered complaints address (not webchat) — use the subject line "Formal Complaint — Stolen Parcel [tracking number]" to ensure it is logged as a complaint rather than an enquiry; this starts the 8-week clock after which POSTRS (Postal Redress Service at cedr.com/postrs) can accept the case for independent adjudication; contact the Citizens Advice consumer helpline (0808 223 1133) for advice on whether the retailer (as the contract holder with the carrier) should be pursuing the claim on the consumer's behalf under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 — in many cases the retailer is responsible for resolving delivery failures, not the consumer
- Evidence to collect/preserve: All three webchat transcripts (export or screenshot each one immediately — webchat logs expire and are not accessible after session closure), all three reference numbers compiled in a single document, the original tracking record showing the delivery event and location, photographs of the doorstep or delivery location if available, purchase confirmation from the retailer showing the declared delivery address
- Feedback category tag: Complaint Loop / Delivery to Unsafe Location / Stolen Parcel / Webchat Failure / No Escalation Pathway

### Scenario 14-9: Freight Forwarder Invoices Show "Special Handling" — Suspected Bribery Pass-Through (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** SEC press release 2010-214 (November 4, 2010) — SEC charges Panalpina and six oil-and-gas companies for FCPA violations; DOJ deferred prosecution agreement with Panalpina World Transport (Holding) Ltd., November 4, 2010; combined $81.89 million penalty (DOJ $70.56M criminal + SEC $11.33M disgorgement); FCPA Stanford Law enforcement dataset ID 308 — Panalpina invoiced clients using labels including "local processing," "special intervention," and "special handling" to conceal bribe payments
**The Problem:** A compliance officer at an oil and gas company is reviewing freight forwarding invoices from their logistics provider covering an 18-month period of West African operations. Multiple invoices contain line items labelled "special handling," "local processing," and "expedited customs clearance" for which no supporting documentation exists. The amounts — ranging from $2,000 to $15,000 per shipment — do not appear in any quoted service schedule in the freight contract. When the compliance officer raised this with the logistics account manager, they were told the charges were "standard industry practice in the region" and documentation was "not available." The company's own legal exposure under the FCPA is not yet determined.
**What staff/org would do:** The logistics provider's account management team continues to deflect, cites "commercial confidentiality" for the undocumented line items, and offers only to escalate to their own commercial director — who has a direct financial interest in retaining the contract.
**What the Owner does:** Validates the compliance officer's concern as serious and legally significant, notes that the invoice label language matches exactly the concealment method documented in the Panalpina FCPA enforcement action, and provides a clear internal and external escalation path that protects the compliance officer personally while managing the company's exposure.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (not an emergency but time-sensitive — each additional invoice processed increases the company's potential FCPA exposure)
- Opening reply: "The invoice labels you have described — 'special handling,' 'local processing,' 'expedited customs clearance' with no supporting documentation — are the exact concealment language documented in the Panalpina case, which resulted in an $81.89 million DOJ and SEC enforcement action. This requires internal escalation and external legal advice immediately, not further discussion with the account manager."
- Direct the consumer/officer to: Escalate immediately and in writing to the company's General Counsel and Chief Compliance Officer — this creates a documented escalation record; engage external FCPA counsel before making any further contact with the logistics provider; consider whether voluntary disclosure to the DOJ or SEC is appropriate — the DOJ's FCPA Corporate Enforcement Policy treats voluntary disclosure as a significant mitigating factor; if internal escalation is blocked or appears compromised, the SEC whistleblower programme (sec.gov/whistleblower) provides legal protections and potential financial awards for information leading to enforcement actions over $1 million
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Every invoice containing the undocumented line items, organised chronologically by shipment and date; the original freight forwarding contract and all agreed service schedules; all written communications with the account manager including the "standard practice" response; any prior internal audits or compliance reviews covering these invoices; the compliance officer's own contemporaneous notes from conversations, with dates
- Feedback category tag: FCPA Violation / Customs Bribery Pass-Through / Invoice Concealment / Compliance Whistleblower / Corporate Liability

### Scenario 14-10: USPS Lost Package — IVR Loop, No Callback, Elderly Recipient Needs Medication (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** ACSI Retail and Consumer Shipping Study 2024 (published January 30, 2024, theacsi.com); USPS scored 73/100 — lowest-rated major US carrier; study based on 40,264 consumer interviews (January–December 2023); primary satisfaction drivers cited as delivery timeliness and customer service contact quality; USPS IVR and callback limitations documented in consumer reporting
**The Problem:** A consumer mailed a package via USPS Priority Mail 2-Day containing a prescription medication for an elderly relative living alone. Seven days after the expected delivery date, the tracking shows "In Transit, Arriving Late" — unchanged for five consecutive days. The consumer has called the USPS customer service line (1-800-ASK-USPS) twice. Both times the multi-level IVR failed to recognise the tracking number, transferred to a recorded message, and disconnected before reaching a live representative. A Missing Mail search was submitted online six days ago with no response. The elderly relative has now run out of the medication.
**What staff/org would do:** The automated system continues to display the same unchanged tracking status. The USPS website directs the consumer back to the same Missing Mail form already submitted. The 1-800 line continues to disconnect after the IVR navigation fails.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises the health vulnerability dimension immediately — the medication shortage is a separate urgent need from the parcel recovery — and provides both a medical emergency action and a specific technical route to bypass the USPS IVR system that has been consistently failing.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (recipient is elderly, living alone, and has run out of prescription medication — health vulnerability is active)
- Opening reply: "The medication shortage is the most urgent issue right now — please contact the prescribing doctor or pharmacy immediately for an emergency supply or bridge prescription while we work the package. The USPS 1-800 line is the lowest-rated carrier contact experience in the country. Here is how to reach someone who can actually locate the parcel."
- Direct the consumer to: Address the medication need first — contact the prescribing doctor to request an emergency prescription or contact the pharmacy about an emergency supply under their dispensing rules; to reach USPS for the package: look up the specific destination ZIP code post office by name at tools.usps.com/find-location.htm and call that local facility directly — local postmasters can often locate and manually route parcels that the 1-800 system cannot; file a formal Consumer Affairs inquiry through the USPS postal system at usps.com/help/contact-us or request a supervisor callback; if the package has been in transit over 15 days or contains items of value, file a Mail Theft complaint with the US Postal Inspection Service at postalinspectors.uspis.gov or call 877-876-2455
- Evidence to collect/preserve: The full USPS tracking number and a screenshot of the complete tracking history with dates and times, the original USPS receipt from mailing showing declared contents, the Missing Mail search confirmation number and the date it was submitted, a dated log of both 1-800 call attempts with times and outcome (IVR failure, disconnect), documentation of the recipient's medical need in case an insurance or liability claim becomes relevant
- Feedback category tag: Lost Mail / IVR Failure / No Callback / Vulnerable Recipient / Medication / Priority Mail Failure
