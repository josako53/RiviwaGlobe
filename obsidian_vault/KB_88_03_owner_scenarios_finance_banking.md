### Scenario 3-1: Zelle Payment Fraud Denied — Transfer Loop Between Bank and Payment Network (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** CFPB federal lawsuit against Early Warning Services, JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, and Wells Fargo (filed December 20, 2024; consumerfinance.gov/enforcement); CFPB consumer complaint database narratives; CNBC and NPR reporting on $870M combined consumer losses across three banks over seven years; banks collectively reimbursed only 38% of fraud claims.
**The Problem:** A Chase bank customer transferred $1,400 via Zelle to someone they believed was their landlord after receiving a spoofed email with familiar details. The money reached a fraudster. The customer called Chase's fraud line immediately. The representative told them the transfer was "authorized by you" and Regulation E protections do not apply to authorized transfers. Over the next two hours, they were transferred four times — between Chase's fraud department and Zelle's customer service line — with neither party accepting responsibility. Chase has now issued a written denial. The customer's rent is still due.
**What staff/org would do:** Script the denial using "authorized transfer" language; cite Regulation E; transfer the consumer to Zelle, who transfers them back to Chase; close the case; offer no fraud investigation reference number; decline to initiate an inter-bank recall.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately acknowledge the loss; assign a senior fraud analyst rather than a front-line representative; open a formal fraud investigation; initiate a payment recall request to the receiving bank; provide a written case reference number; inform the consumer of their rights under the evolving regulatory landscape for socially-engineered authorized fraud.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (active financial loss; funds potentially recoverable if recall initiated quickly)
- Opening reply: "I'm so sorry this happened — losing money to fraud and then being passed between departments is completely unacceptable. Time matters here, so let me give you the fastest path forward right now."
- Direct to: File a complaint immediately with the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov/complaint (the CFPB filed federal suit against Chase specifically for this denial pattern in December 2024); contact your state Attorney General's consumer protection division; demand Chase initiate a payment recall in writing — they have the inter-bank mechanism to do this and are required to investigate under Regulation E; if Chase issues a second denial, contact your state banking regulator
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot or printout of the fraudulent email/message that triggered the transfer; timestamps and representative names/IDs from every call to Chase and Zelle; any reference numbers given; Chase's written denial letter; bank statement showing the transfer; your real landlord's correct payment details as proof of confusion
- Feedback category tag: Financial Fraud / Authorized Push Payment Denial / Call Center Transfer Loop

### Scenario 3-2: Unemployment Benefits Card Frozen — No Recovery Path (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** CFPB Consent Order against U.S. Bank, December 19, 2023 (ReliaCard prepaid debit card program; $21M CFPB fine + $15M OCC fine; CFPB found U.S. Bank "failed to provide an easy method to verify identities"; cardholders locked out for "a few weeks to a month or more"); CNBC and Banking Dive reporting.
**The Problem:** A recently unemployed consumer in Ohio receives state unemployment insurance benefits via a U.S. Bank ReliaCard prepaid debit card — their only current income. Their card was frozen after the bank's fraud detection system flagged it. They cannot buy groceries or pay rent. They have called U.S. Bank three times over two days. Each time a representative tells them their account is "under review for security purposes," provides no timeline, cannot explain what triggered the freeze, and cannot tell them what documentation would resolve it. No escalation path has been offered. There is no emergency access mechanism.
**What staff/org would do:** Read from the security review script; provide no timeline; list no specific verification pathway; offer no emergency fund release; transfer the consumer back to the same automated queue; take no exception action for financial hardship.
**What the Owner does:** Flag the case as a vulnerable-customer hardship situation immediately; open an expedited review track; provide the consumer with a specific list of identity documents that will satisfy verification within 24 hours; issue a partial emergency release of funds if same-day verification cannot be completed; escalate to a senior compliance officer given the active risk of harm.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (no income, no food or rent access — immediate risk of harm)
- Opening reply: "This is a genuine emergency — being locked out of your only income source when you have no other options is something we need to treat with full urgency. I am going to give you every avenue available to get this resolved today."
- Direct to: Contact your state's unemployment agency directly and ask them to intervene with U.S. Bank on your behalf — they have a direct relationship with the bank as the issuing authority; file an emergency complaint with the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov/complaint (the CFPB specifically fined U.S. Bank $21M in December 2023 for this exact practice with ReliaCard); contact your state banking regulator; call 211 (United Way helpline) for emergency food and rent assistance while the account is frozen
- Evidence to collect: All call logs — date, time, duration, representative name or ID for each call; any written or SMS notification of the freeze; screenshot or photo of the ReliaCard showing frozen status; your unemployment eligibility determination letter; any identity documents you offered during calls
- Feedback category tag: Account Access Denial / Government Benefits Disruption / System Failure

### Scenario 3-3: Unauthorized Overdraft Enrollment — Unexplained Fees on Account (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** CFPB enforcement action against Atlantic Union Bank, 2023 ($5M consumer refunds + $6.2M civil penalty for improperly enrolling customers in overdraft protection without consent, often during calls placed for unrelated reasons); CFPB Supervisory Highlights Issue 37 (Winter 2024) documenting "authorize positive" overdraft fee timing manipulation; Regions Bank ordered to refund $141M in similar overdraft violations.
**The Problem:** A consumer notices three $35 overdraft fees on their bank statement over six weeks — $105 total — for transactions they believed their account had sufficient funds to cover. They call their bank and are told they "enrolled in overdraft protection" during a call made six weeks ago for a different purpose. The consumer has no recollection of agreeing to this and suspects the representative enrolled them without clear explanation. The bank refuses to refund the fees and says their enrollment record is confirmed. When the consumer asks to hear the call recording to verify consent was given, they are told the recording is available for the bank's internal use but cannot be shared with the customer.
**What staff/org would do:** Pull up the enrollment record; state the customer agreed to overdraft protection; read from the fee disclosure script; decline to refund; decline to share the call recording; offer no unenrollment without the customer explicitly requesting it.
**What the Owner does:** Pull the actual call recording; review whether proper affirmative consent language was used and whether the enrollment was offered as an opt-in while the customer was calling for another reason; if consent is ambiguous or improper, reverse all three fees immediately; unenroll the customer from overdraft protection going forward; document the resolution.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Charges on your account that you do not remember agreeing to are something we take seriously. You have a right to understand exactly how and when that enrollment happened, and I want to help you get answers and your money back if this was done without your proper consent."
- Direct to: File a complaint with the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov/complaint — the CFPB's Supervisory Highlights 2024 and its 2023 enforcement action against Atlantic Union Bank specifically identified unauthorized call-center overdraft enrollment as an illegal practice; send a written request to the bank (email or certified letter) asking for the date and transcript or recording of the call on which they claim you consented, and the exact consent language used — banks are required to respond to written disputes; your state banking regulator can also investigate
- Evidence to collect: Bank statements showing the three $35 fees and the dates; a log of every call you made to the bank in the six weeks before the alleged enrollment and what you were calling about; any welcome email or letter about overdraft coverage (note the date); the bank's written refusal to refund; your phone call log showing the date and duration of the call they claim was your enrollment call
- Feedback category tag: Unauthorized Service Enrollment / Hidden Overdraft Fee / Call Center Misconduct

### Scenario 3-4: Bank IT Outage — Account Locked, Card Declined, Call Center Non-Functional (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** TSB Bank IT system migration failure, UK, April 2018; 1.9 million customers locked out; FCA and PRA fined TSB £48.6M combined (£29.75M FCA + £18.9M PRA); 114,399 formal complaints filed; TSB paid over £370M in compensation; Financial Ombudsman decisions (e.g., DRN1571723) documented customers receiving inaccurate information for approximately nine months after the incident; estimated call wait times exceeded two hours during the outage period.
**The Problem:** A TSB bank customer in Manchester cannot access their online banking account and their debit card is being declined at the supermarket checkout. They called TSB's customer service line — after 85 minutes on hold, the call disconnected. On a second call, a representative gave them a different account balance figure than their last paper statement showed, could not explain the discrepancy, and told them the account was "being looked into" with no timeline. Three direct debits are scheduled for today. The customer is getting contradictory information each time they call.
**What staff/org would do:** Provide inconsistent balance information across calls; give no timeline for resolution; not acknowledge the wider system outage publicly; not inform the customer of their rights to fee refunds caused by the outage; keep the customer cycling through the same script with no escalation.
**What the Owner does:** Proactively communicate the system outage scope; provide a dedicated priority queue for customers with time-sensitive financial obligations; guarantee in writing to cover any charges incurred due to the outage; offer a branch-based emergency cash option; assign a case reference number so the customer has continuity across contacts.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (direct debits due today; card declined; no access to funds; actively receiving wrong information from customer service)
- Opening reply: "I can hear how stressful this is — direct debits due today and a card that won't work is a real emergency. I am going to give you immediate steps and your rights, because the bank has specific obligations when an outage causes you financial harm."
- Direct to: Go directly to your nearest TSB branch with photo ID and ask for emergency access assistance; if branch cannot resolve same day, escalate immediately to the Financial Ombudsman Service (financial-ombudsman.org.uk or 0800 023 4567) — you do not need to wait the full 8 weeks if you face imminent financial harm from a service failure; ask TSB in writing to confirm they will cover any missed direct debit fees as a result of the outage; contact the FCA consumer helpline (0800 111 6768) to report you are receiving inaccurate information across multiple calls
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of error messages on online banking; a photo of the card-declined receipt at the supermarket; your last paper or app statement showing the balance before the outage; a written record of both call attempts (date, time, duration, what you were told); any direct debit bounce notification letters or emails you receive as a result
- Feedback category tag: Service Outage / Account Access Emergency / Call Center Misinformation

### Scenario 3-5: Car Finance Hidden Commission — Complaint Rejected Without Evidence (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** UK motor finance Discretionary Commission Arrangement mis-selling scandal; FCA CP25/27 consultation (March 2026) confirming mass redress scheme estimated at £7.5 billion; Court of Appeal ruling October 25, 2024 held undisclosed commissions were unlawful; Which? and MoneySavingExpert documented 99%+ initial rejection rate by lenders; over 4 million consumers have already filed complaints; average compensation estimated at £830 per agreement.
**The Problem:** A consumer in Birmingham purchased a car on finance in 2019. They recently learned that their lender paid a hidden commission to the dealership that arranged the loan, which gave the dealer financial incentive to charge a higher interest rate — without disclosing this conflict of interest to the consumer. They filed a formal complaint with their lender. The lender's response letter states that "commission arrangements are standard industry practice and were disclosed in the terms and conditions." The consumer does not recall seeing any commission disclosure and believes they were charged a higher interest rate than they would have been without the conflict. The letter says the lender considers the complaint closed.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue a boilerplate rejection citing standard industry practice; refuse to provide the specific commission amount paid to the dealer; offer no explanation of the Court of Appeal ruling; close the case; include a brief mention of the FOS without explaining that the FOS is actively upholding these complaints in consumers' favour.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the complaint; not issue a blanket rejection; inform the consumer of the Court of Appeal's October 2024 ruling that Discretionary Commission Arrangements were unlawful without full informed consent; suspend final resolution pending the FCA's announced mass redress scheme; proactively provide the consumer with the details of their finance agreement including the commission paid.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "This is a recognised legal issue that has now been confirmed by the Court of Appeal, and a mass compensation scheme is being set up covering millions of UK consumers. Do not accept this rejection — you have strong grounds to challenge it."
- Direct to: Do not accept the bank's closure letter — escalate immediately to the Financial Ombudsman Service (financial-ombudsman.org.uk or 0800 023 4567); use the free eligibility checker at MoneySavingExpert (moneysavingexpert.com/reclaim/car-finance-discount-commission) to file your claim and be registered for the FCA's mass redress scheme; the FCA mass redress scheme announced March 2026 may process compensation automatically, but you must have a complaint on record; contact Citizens Advice (citizensadvice.org.uk) for free support with the FOS process
- Evidence to collect: Your original car finance agreement paperwork including all pages of small print; the lender's rejection letter with the date (critical — FOS time limits apply from this date); any communications from the dealership at point of sale; records of all interest payments made under the agreement; the dates the finance was taken out and fully repaid or still running (claims cover agreements from 2007 to 2024)
- Feedback category tag: Financial Mis-Selling / Hidden Commission / Consumer Rights Violation / Transparency Failure

### Scenario 3-6: APP Scam Impersonating HMRC — Bank Refuses Recall, Four-Representative Transfer Loop (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** UK Financial Ombudsman Service Annual Report 2024/25 (305,726 new complaints; APP scam complaints up 83% to 4,956 in Q2 2024; FOS upheld 34% of all complaints in consumers' favour; Barclays received 5,207 FOS complaints in H2 2023 — highest of any single UK bank); PSR PS23/3 mandatory APP reimbursement rules effective October 7, 2024.
**The Problem:** A consumer in London received a call from someone claiming to be from HMRC stating their bank account had been flagged for suspicious activity and they needed to move their savings to a "government-protected account" immediately. The caller knew their name, partial account number, and address. Believing this was genuine, the consumer transferred £3,200. Within thirty minutes they suspected fraud and called Barclays. The first representative told them the transfer was "authorised by you" and there was "nothing we can do." They were transferred to a fraud specialist, who told them to "contact the receiving bank directly." The consumer does not know which bank the fraudster used. After 75 minutes across four representatives, no recall has been initiated and no fraud report reference number has been provided.
**What staff/org would do:** Apply the "authorized transfer" denial across every representative; provide no inter-bank payment recall initiation; give no internal fraud report reference; tell the consumer to contact the receiving bank themselves, knowing the consumer has no mechanism to do this; take no immediate protective action on the account.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately initiate a Faster Payments recall request to the receiving bank through the inter-bank scheme — this is the bank's responsibility, not the consumer's; log a formal internal fraud report with a reference number; under the UK's mandatory APP reimbursement rules (PSR PS23/3, effective October 2024), assess reimbursement eligibility without requiring the consumer to pursue the receiving bank themselves; place a note on the account.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (funds may still be recoverable if the receiving bank freezes the fraudster's account immediately; minutes matter)
- Opening reply: "Time is genuinely critical right now — the faster Barclays contacts the receiving bank, the higher the chance your money is still there to be frozen and returned. I am going to tell you exactly what to say to Barclays and what they are legally required to do under rules that came into effect in October 2024."
- Direct to: Call Barclays back immediately and use the specific phrase "I am requesting an inter-bank Faster Payments recall under the PSR mandatory APP reimbursement scheme — please initiate this now and give me a fraud reference number"; if Barclays refuses, escalate on the same call to a supervisor; file with the Financial Ombudsman Service immediately at financial-ombudsman.org.uk (you do not need to wait 8 weeks in active fraud cases); also report to Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk or 0300 123 2040) and the HMRC phishing line (phishing@hmrc.gov.uk)
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot of the transfer confirmation in your banking app the moment it was made; a record of the fraudster's call — note the time, caller ID shown, and every detail they knew about you; a written record of every Barclays call including representative names and what was said; any texts or emails from the fraudster; the timestamp when you first reported to Barclays (critical for establishing liability under the October 2024 reimbursement rules)
- Feedback category tag: APP Scam / HMRC Impersonation / Authorized Push Payment / Call Center Failure / Recall Refused

### Scenario 3-7: M-Pesa SIM Swap Fraud With Insider Facilitation — Deflected to Police (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Rest of World and Nation Africa investigative reporting (2022-2023); documented case of Nairobi police officer Peter Mwanzo — KSh 597,100 ($4,575) drained including mobile loans taken in his name (January 2022); Safaricom's FY2022 annual report confirmed 24 employees sacked for fraud, 10 specifically for SIM swap involvement; Kenya DCI arrested an 8-member SIM swap syndicate that defrauded users of KSh 500M/$3.8M (February 2023); CBK 2022 report found 25.9% of mobile money users had lost money through cybercrime; precedent-setting Kenyan court ruling in 2023 established Safaricom operator liability for SIM swap losses.
**The Problem:** A consumer in Nairobi wakes to find their phone SIM has stopped working — initially dismissed as a network issue. When they visit a Safaricom service center, they are told their SIM was replaced at a different branch the previous day. Their M-Pesa account shows KSh 85,000 in savings transferred out and a KSh 30,000 mobile loan disbursed and withdrawn in their name — neither transaction initiated by them. The fraudster used the new SIM to pass all M-Pesa verification. At the service center, staff tell the consumer to "go to the DCI" — no fraud reference number is opened, no transaction reversal is attempted, and no investigation into which branch employee processed the unauthorized SIM swap is initiated.
**What staff/org would do:** Refer consumer to the Directorate of Criminal Investigations; decline to open an internal fraud investigation; decline to attempt transaction reversal; decline to suspend the destination accounts; provide no fraud reference number; take no action against the branch that processed the unauthorized SIM replacement.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately flag the consumer's account for fraud investigation; escalate internally to identify which branch processed the SIM swap and who authorized it; suspend accounts to which funds were transferred pending investigation; provide a formal fraud reference number; advise the consumer that a 2023 Kenyan court ruling has established operator liability for SIM swap losses facilitated through internal processes; initiate an internal investigation into the branch employee involved.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (a mobile loan is accruing interest in the consumer's name right now; the longer the delay, the larger the debt grows)
- Opening reply: "This is extremely serious — your savings are gone and a loan has been taken in your name without your knowledge. Safaricom has a legal responsibility here that goes beyond telling you to call the police. Let me walk you through every step you need to take today."
- Direct to: Return to the Safaricom service center and demand — in writing — that they open a formal fraud investigation and provide a fraud reference number; tell them explicitly that a Kenyan court ruled in 2023 that Safaricom bears operator liability for SIM swap losses processed through their network, including where employees facilitated the swap; simultaneously report to the DCI Cybercrime Unit (+254 722 582 220, DCI Headquarters, Kiambu Road, Nairobi) AND the Communications Authority of Kenya (+254 709 040 000, complaints@ca.go.ke) as the telecoms regulator; contact the CBK Consumer Protection Department (consumerprotection@centralbank.go.ke) specifically about the mobile loan taken in your name — you can dispute this debt while the fraud investigation is open; if Safaricom refuses to investigate, consult a lawyer about filing a claim citing the 2023 operator liability precedent
- Evidence to collect: Written record from the service center visit showing they acknowledged the SIM was replaced at another branch; M-Pesa transaction history screenshots showing the unauthorized transfers and loan disbursement with exact timestamps; your own phone's record showing the SIM stopped working (time and date); any SMS received in the hours before the SIM stopped working; the name and branch location of the Safaricom center you visited; Safaricom's refusal to open a fraud report in writing
- Feedback category tag: Mobile Money Fraud / SIM Swap / Insider Staff Misconduct / Complaint Deflection

### Scenario 3-8: Unauthorized Digital Transaction — Bank Delays Response Past Regulatory Limit (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** RBI Integrated Ombudsman Scheme Annual Report 2023-24 (934,355 total complaints — a 32.81% increase year-on-year; mobile and electronic banking the single largest category at over 20% of all cases; Business Standard reported a "68% spike in bank complaints" with mobile banking as the top concern); RBI Circular RBI/2017-18/15 requiring banks to provide provisional credit within 10 working days for disputed unauthorized transactions; the mandatory 30-day bank-level resolution period that must expire before RBI Ombudsman access is triggered means every one of the 934,000 complaints first failed internal bank resolution.
**The Problem:** A consumer in Mumbai finds an unauthorized UPI debit of ₹47,000 from their savings account — a transaction they did not initiate, sent to an account number they do not recognize. They filed a written unauthorized transaction complaint with their bank the same day, receiving a complaint reference number. It has now been 28 days. Every time they call customer care they are told the investigation is "ongoing" with no update and no timeline. No provisional credit has been issued to their account. The funds were earmarked for an upcoming medical procedure. They are now two days away from the 30-day mark when they can escalate to the RBI Ombudsman.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue the "under investigation" script on each call; provide no provisional credit; give no specific timeline; at day 30 or later, tell the consumer to escalate to the RBI Ombudsman as if that were a satisfactory outcome rather than a failure of internal process.
**What the Owner does:** Under RBI Circular RBI/2017-18/15, issue provisional credit within 10 working days for a credible unauthorized transaction complaint; assign a named investigation officer who contacts the consumer; accelerate given the documented medical need; communicate a firm resolution date.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (approaching 30-day threshold — prepare escalation documents now so you can file immediately at day 30)
- Opening reply: "I understand how stressful this is, particularly when those funds were needed for medical reasons. The bank has already breached its RBI-mandated provisional credit obligation. Let me explain exactly what you are owed and how to escalate effectively on day 30."
- Direct to: On day 30 if unresolved, file immediately with the RBI Integrated Ombudsman via cms.rbi.org.in — it is free and fully online and there is no fee; also ask your bank in writing for the name and contact of their internal Nodal Officer for banking ombudsman complaints — all banks are required to have one and must escalate unresolved cases to them; RBI consumer education and protection helpline: 14448 (toll-free); report the UPI fraud to the National Cybercrime Reporting Portal at cybercrime.gov.in (file a cybercrime complaint); reference RBI Circular RBI/2017-18/15 when demanding the provisional credit in writing
- Evidence to collect: Your original complaint reference number and the date filed; bank statement screenshot showing the unauthorized UPI transaction with the destination account number; all call records — dates, times, what you were told, any reference numbers given; any SMS alerts from your bank around the time of the unauthorized transaction; medical documentation showing the urgency of the ₹47,000 (useful for making the case for expedited treatment at the Ombudsman)
- Feedback category tag: Digital Payment Fraud / Unauthorized UPI Transaction / Resolution Delay / Regulatory Escalation Required

### Scenario 3-9: Mobile Money Fraud Attributed to Victim — Insider Threat Dismissed (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Bank of Ghana fraud reports (13,451 fraud cases in 2023; mobile money fraud exceeded GH¢10M; fraud losses rose from GH¢33M in 2021 to GH¢56M in 2022; Bank of Ghana data shows over 50% of all financial fraud cases in Ghana involve insider threats from employees within financial or telecom institutions); documented April 2025 case where a customer lost GH¢11,000 from her MTN MoMo wallet, MTN MobileMoney Ltd conducted an investigation, concluded "no evidence of a technical breach," attributed the loss to "social engineering," and declined to refund (Modern Ghana, TechNext24, Graphic Online).
**The Problem:** A market trader in Accra discovers GH¢11,000 has been withdrawn from her MTN MoMo wallet without her authorization. She did not share her PIN with anyone and has biometric verification enabled. She reported the loss to MTN immediately. Three weeks later, MTN sent a formal letter concluding their investigation found "no evidence of a technical breach" and attributing the unauthorized withdrawal to "social engineering by a third party." The letter offers no refund, no breakdown of the investigation findings, no explanation of what social engineering evidence was identified, and no information about any internal appeals process. This was her entire savings.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue the social engineering conclusion letter; close the case with no refund; provide no investigation evidence; offer no appeals pathway; take no steps to examine whether an internal MTN employee may have facilitated the unauthorized access — despite Bank of Ghana data showing 50%+ of Ghana financial fraud involves insiders.
**What the Owner does:** Require the investigation report to be provided to the consumer in writing, including what specific evidence supports the social engineering conclusion; escalate internally given the statistical likelihood of insider involvement; open an appeals process; refer proactively to the Bank of Ghana Consumer Protection Department; not close the case until the insider-threat possibility is examined.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Losing your entire savings and then being told it was your own fault — without any evidence being shown to you — is not an acceptable resolution. MTN has obligations under Bank of Ghana regulations, and the data on Ghana mobile money fraud shows this conclusion deserves serious scrutiny. Let me help you push back effectively."
- Direct to: File a formal complaint with the Bank of Ghana Consumer Protection and Market Conduct Department (consumerprotection@bog.gov.gh or +233 (0)30 266 6174); send a written request to MTN MobileMoney Ltd demanding the full investigation report and the specific evidence used to conclude social engineering — they are required to provide this under Bank of Ghana consumer protection rules; file with the National Communications Authority of Ghana (complaints@nca.org.gh) as the telecoms regulator overseeing MTN; reference publicly that Bank of Ghana's own data shows over 50% of Ghana financial fraud involves insider threats and that the investigation should have examined this possibility; consider consulting a lawyer about a civil claim — MTN has been successfully challenged in documented cases
- Evidence to collect: MTN's written "social engineering" conclusion letter with the date; your own security practices documentation (PIN never shared, biometric enabled, no suspicious calls received); MTN MoMo transaction history showing the unauthorized withdrawal with timestamp; any suspicious calls, SMS, or contact you received in the days before the loss; full record of every interaction with MTN customer service from initial report through the conclusion letter; your registration documents for the MoMo wallet
- Feedback category tag: Mobile Money Fraud / Victim-Blaming / Insider Misconduct / Complaint Dismissal

### Scenario 3-10: Bank Refuses Scam Reimbursement After 45-Day Internal Review — AFCA Escalation Required (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** AFCA Annual Review 2023-24 (record 104,861 complaints; scam-related complaints surged 81% to approximately 11,000 cases; Consumer Action Law Centre reported banks systematically fail to reimburse scam victims at first contact; 40% of scam cases that progressed to full AFCA investigation resulted in no compensation; 60% resulted in full or partial compensation — meaning AFCA escalation meaningfully changes outcomes); Consumer Action Law Centre documentation of "authorised transfer" denial scripts mirroring the US Zelle pattern.
**The Problem:** A consumer in Sydney received a call from someone claiming to be from ANZ's fraud prevention team, who stated that their account had been compromised and instructed them to transfer their savings to a "secure account" set up in their name for protection. The caller had the consumer's full name, partial card number, and recent transaction details. The consumer transferred AU$8,500. They reported the fraud to ANZ within two hours of the transfer. After a 45-day internal investigation, ANZ sent a final decision letter stating the transfer was "authorised by you" and they are not entitled to reimbursement. The letter contains a single sentence mentioning AFCA as a complaints option, with no further explanation.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue the "authorised transfer" denial; refuse to discuss whether ANZ's own fraud prevention team warning systems flagged the unusual outgoing transfer in real time; issue the AFCA mention as a procedural requirement rather than a genuine referral; provide no explanation of the consumer's prospects at AFCA or the 2-year time limit for filing.
**What the Owner does:** Review whether ANZ's own systems generated any real-time warning about the transfer that was not acted on; acknowledge that impersonation scams where the fraudster poses as the bank's own fraud team represent a recognized failure of internal fraud prevention, not a consumer error; provide a substantive explanation of AFCA and the consumer's actual rights; not hide behind the "authorised" framing when the consumer was manipulated into authorising by someone impersonating the bank.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (internal process exhausted — now in the AFCA filing window, which has a strict 2-year time limit from ANZ's decision date)
- Opening reply: "I know receiving that denial after 45 days of waiting is devastating. But this is genuinely not the end of the road — AFCA exists exactly for situations like this, and in 2023-24, 60% of scam cases that reached AFCA resulted in full or partial compensation. Let me walk you through how to file effectively."
- Direct to: File with AFCA immediately at afca.org.au or call 1800 931 678 — the 2-year limitation period runs from the date of ANZ's final decision letter, so file now; in your AFCA application specifically note that (1) the fraudster impersonated ANZ's own fraud prevention team using your private account details, (2) ANZ did not warn you of an unusual outgoing transfer in real time despite this being a recognized impersonation scam pattern, and (3) ANZ's denial script uses "authorised transfer" language identical to the pattern AFCA overturned in 60% of comparable cases in 2023-24; report the fraud to Scamwatch (scamwatch.gov.au) and the Australian Cyber Security Centre (cyber.gov.au/report); contact Consumer Action Law Centre (consumeraction.org.au or 1800 466 477) for free support with the AFCA process
- Evidence to collect: ANZ's final decision letter with the date (start your 2-year AFCA clock from this date); a full transcript or summary of every interaction with ANZ from the initial fraud report through the final decision; bank statement showing the AU$8,500 transfer; any communications from the fraudster — call log showing caller ID, any texts or emails; evidence of the timing between the fraudster's call and ANZ's real fraud prevention calls to you previously (demonstrating why you believed the caller was genuine); your own phone's incoming call records showing when the fraudster called and the number displayed
- Feedback category tag: Scam Reimbursement Refusal / Bank Impersonation Fraud / AFCA Escalation Required / Authorised Push Payment
