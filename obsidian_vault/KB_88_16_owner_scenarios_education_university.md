### Scenario 16-1: Loan Servicer Gave Wrong PSLF Guidance Over Phone, Teacher Loses Five Years of Qualifying Payments (Pattern D+E)
**Real Basis:** CFPB Education Loan Ombudsman Annual Report 2023 (files.consumerfinance.gov); CFPB enforcement order against Navient, September 2024 — $120M fine, banned from federal student loan servicing; DOE PSLF limited waiver announcement 2021–2022; 9,284 PSLF-related complaints analyzed in a single 12-month window by the CFPB
**The Problem:** A public school teacher has been making federal student loan payments for seven years, believing she is on track for Public Service Loan Forgiveness after calling her loan servicer three times over five years to confirm her repayment plan qualified. Each time she was told she was enrolled correctly. A Department of Education notice has now informed her that most of her payments were made on a non-qualifying plan and do not count toward PSLF. She calls the servicer for an explanation, is placed on hold for 47 minutes, transferred twice, and the second agent gives her a different account of her repayment history than the first. She may be forced to restart a ten-year payment clock.
**What staff/org would do:** Place the caller on further hold to "review the account," issue a generic apology, tell her to contact the Department of Education directly, provide no written confirmation of the error, offer no escalation path, and give no acknowledgment that the servicer's own prior guidance caused the harm.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges immediately that this is a documented pattern of servicer misinformation affecting tens of thousands of borrowers, validated by a federal enforcement order. Validates the consumer's distress. Points her to the specific corrective mechanism — the PSLF reconsideration process — and the CFPB complaint channel, and advises her to preserve written records of all prior communications before the servicer can revise its logs.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "This is an extremely frustrating situation — you did everything right by calling to confirm, and the information you were given appears to have been incorrect. This is a documented, widespread problem that resulted in a $120 million federal enforcement action against the servicer involved. Let me help you understand your rights and who can hold them accountable."
- Direct consumer to: CFPB complaint portal (consumerfinance.gov/complaint) selecting "student loan" category; FSA PSLF reconsideration request form at studentaid.gov/pslf; FSA Ombudsman Group (877-557-2575); state Attorney General consumer protection office if servicer is Navient
- Evidence to collect: Phone call dates and times for all prior servicer contacts; repayment plan statements showing plan type for each period; any written confirmation emails from the servicer; full payment history from studentaid.gov; all employer certification forms previously submitted; screenshots of the servicer's online account portal showing plan enrollment history
- Feedback category tag: `financial_harm | service_failure | call_center_misinformation | loan_servicing | information_failure`

### Scenario 16-2: For-Profit College Trapped Student in Predatory Private Loan With Active Repayment Demands (Pattern C+E)
**Real Basis:** CFPB enforcement action against Corinthian Colleges (Everest, Heald, WyoTech), September 2014 (consumerfinance.gov); US Department of Education borrower defense data — 75,343 claims, 76.2% of all borrower defense claims ever filed; Sweet v. Cardona class action; $5.8 billion federal student loan cancellation for 560,000 individuals (2022)
**The Problem:** A 24-year-old enrolled at a for-profit campus after a recruiter called her and cited a 90% job placement rate for the medical billing program. The school enrolled her in its private loan program, which — unlike federal loans — requires repayment while she is still a student. She has just received a demand for a $340 monthly payment she cannot make. When she calls the financial aid office to ask about deferment, she is told there is no deferment option and that missing a payment will be reported to credit bureaus. On a second call she is told a collector will visit her home if she misses two payments. The job placement rate cited during enrollment cannot be verified; online searches reveal the school is under federal investigation.
**What staff/org would do:** Insist the loan terms she signed are legally binding, refuse to discuss the legitimacy of the job placement statistics used in the original recruitment call, and refer her to a third-party collections process if she does not pay.
**What the Owner does:** Flags this immediately as matching a documented federal enforcement pattern. Validates that the consumer has specific legal rights including a Borrower Defense to Repayment claim. Advises her not to make further payments until she has spoken with a student loan advocate, as payment history and acknowledgment of the debt may affect legal standing in a borrower defense claim.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — a private loan that demands repayment while you are still enrolled, combined with job placement figures that cannot be verified — matches a documented federal enforcement case that ultimately resulted in over five billion dollars in cancelled loans for more than half a million students. You have legal protections and you should not feel pressured to pay under these circumstances. Let me explain your rights immediately."
- Direct consumer to: US Department of Education Borrower Defense to Repayment application (studentaid.gov/borrower-defense); CFPB complaint portal (consumerfinance.gov/complaint); National Student Legal Defense Network (studentdefense.org) for free legal assistance; Federal Student Aid Information Center (1-800-433-3243); state Attorney General consumer protection division
- Evidence to collect: All enrollment documents and loan agreements; any written or recorded statements about job placement rates from the recruiter; documentation of current enrollment status; all payment demand notices received; records of all phone calls to the financial aid office including dates, times, and what was said on each call
- Feedback category tag: `financial_fraud | predatory_lending | for_profit_institution | urgent | borrower_defense`

### Scenario 16-3: PhD Student Reports Faculty Advisor for Sexual Misconduct, Nine Months Pass With No Action (Pattern B+E)
**Real Basis:** Dartmouth College $14M class-action settlement, August 2019 — three professors in the Department of Psychological and Brain Sciences, university found to have promoted an accused professor during the period of known misconduct (CBS News; VTDigger; Boston Globe; GBH News, August 2019); FSU three professors found guilty of sexual misconduct, inconsistent sanctions including one allowed to remain employed, WUFT/NPR November 2021
**The Problem:** A second-year PhD candidate submitted a formal written complaint to her university's Title IX office nine months ago, reporting that her faculty advisor — who controls her research funding, dissertation committee, and academic career — made repeated unwanted physical contact during lab meetings and privately warned her against further reporting. The Title IX office sent one acknowledgment email and has communicated nothing since. She has called the office four times: twice the call disconnected while on hold; once she was told the investigator "is no longer with the office"; once she was promised an update "soon." She is still required to attend weekly one-on-one meetings with the accused professor. Transferring to another advisor requires the department chair's signature — and the department chair is a close colleague of the accused.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue a boilerplate response that the investigation is "ongoing," take no interim protective measures such as reassigning supervision or excusing her from one-on-one meetings, and allow the structural conflict of interest involving the department chair to persist unchallenged.
**What the Owner does:** Recognizes this as an active safety situation involving a nine-month Title IX processing failure and an ongoing power imbalance. Provides the consumer with external federal oversight contacts she can use without waiting for the internal process to conclude, and advises on interim steps to create physical and documented distance from the accused.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This situation is serious. Nine months without a substantive update, disconnected calls, and continued required contact with the person you reported is not acceptable under federal law. You do not have to wait for the internal process to finish. There is a federal oversight body you can contact right now, and it can act independently of your university. Let me help you do that."
- Direct consumer to: US Department of Education Office for Civil Rights (OCR) complaint portal at ocrcas.ed.gov — can be filed while internal investigation is still open; Know Your IX (knowyourix.org) for free legal guidance written for students; RAINN campus sexual violence hotline (1-800-656-4673); student legal services if offered by the university; National Women's Law Center (nwlc.org)
- Evidence to collect: Dated copy of original written complaint submitted to the Title IX office; all email correspondence including the initial acknowledgment; a detailed log of all four phone calls with dates, times, and what each staff member said; any written or text communications from the accused professor that are relevant; documentation of attempts to transfer advisors and all responses received
- Feedback category tag: `rights_violation | title_ix | staff_misconduct | urgent | sexual_harassment | grievance_intake_failure`

### Scenario 16-4: Graduate Discovers His University Degree Is Among 36,000 Fraudulent Credentials, Work Permit Under Review (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Manav Bharti University investigation, India, 2019–2021 — 36,000 fraudulent degrees out of 41,000 total issued (88%), ₹387 crore in proceeds of crime, Enforcement Directorate seized ₹194.17 crore under PMLA; Singapore Ministry of Manpower opened investigation into 15 employees who used MBU qualifications in work permit applications (South China Morning Post; Integrity Asia; NewsGram)
**The Problem:** A 29-year-old in Singapore paid ₹1.8 lakh to obtain a Bachelor of Commerce degree from Manav Bharti University through a phone-based enrollment agent who assured him the degree was UGC-recognized. He used this credential on his Singapore work permit application three years ago. He has received a letter from Singapore's Ministry of Manpower stating that MBU has been found to have issued fraudulent degrees on a mass scale, that his credential is under review, and that his work permit may be curtailed. He calls the university's registered number and finds it disconnected. The money he paid cannot be recovered — the university's chairman has fled to Australia.
**What staff/org would do:** The institution is unreachable. Singapore government processing will proceed on its standard bureaucratic timeline without acknowledging the financial loss or offering alternative remedies. No authority proactively contacts him about potential victim status or compensation mechanisms.
**What the Owner does:** Validates that this is a known mass fraud with government-documented victims across multiple countries. Provides clear guidance on the Singapore MOM verification process, the Indian Enforcement Directorate victim channel for overseas complainants, and steps to document financial loss for potential compensation through Indian enforcement proceedings.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "What happened to you is a documented fraud affecting thousands of people in Singapore, Malaysia, the USA, Canada, and Australia — the university's own records showed nearly 88% of all degrees it ever issued were fake. You are not alone, and there are specific steps you can take in both Singapore and India to document your case and protect your immigration status."
- Direct consumer to: Singapore MOM Credentials Services (mom.gov.sg) for formal credential review status and timeline; India Enforcement Directorate complainant channel (enforcementdirectorate.gov.in) — the ED seized ₹194 crore in proceeds and is pursuing compensation recovery; Indian Consulate in Singapore for assistance coordinating with Indian authorities; National Consumer Helpline India (1800-11-4000) for cross-border consumer complaints
- Evidence to collect: Original degree certificate and transcripts; all payment receipts for tuition and fees paid to MBU or its enrollment agents; phone records or messages from the enrollment agent including any assurances about UGC recognition; MOM letter received; any prior written communications with the university confirming accreditation status; current work permit documents showing the credential was used
- Feedback category tag: `financial_fraud | credential_fraud | corruption | cross_border_harm | fake_institution`

### Scenario 16-5: Government Job Application Rejected Because Degree Is from UGC-Listed Fake University (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** University Grants Commission (UGC) India — official fake/unrecognized university database (ugc.gov.in/universitydetails/Fakeuniversity); 32 fake universities on the list as of 2026 update, concentrated in Delhi (12) and Uttar Pradesh (4); documented student harm including UPSC and SSC competitive exam disqualification for holders of degrees from listed institutions
**The Problem:** A 26-year-old in Delhi spent three years and paid ₹2.3 lakh completing a degree from an institution calling itself "United Global University." He enrolled after an unsolicited phone call from an admissions representative who cited UGC recognition and provided what he now believes was a fabricated affiliation certificate. He passed the written stage of an SSC competitive examination. During document verification, the SSC has rejected his application and barred him from the current recruitment cycle because his degree-conferring institution appears on the UGC's official fake university list. He calls the university's admissions number and reaches a recorded message directing him to visit the campus. The campus is permanently closed.
**What staff/org would do:** The SSC processing centre issues a rejection letter with no guidance on legal recourse or appeal. The UGC's public grievance channel responds slowly. The fake university is uncontactable and the money paid is unrecoverable through normal channels.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the compounded harm — financial loss of ₹2.3 lakh and a barred recruitment cycle. Provides the UGC complaint mechanism, the National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission path for financial restitution, and police complaint guidance for criminal fraud prosecution of the institution's operators.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "This is a serious injustice. You were deceived into paying for a degree that was never legitimate, and you are now being penalized for it. The institution that defrauded you is on an official government list of fake universities, which gives you a strong basis for both a consumer complaint and a criminal fraud report against its operators. Here is what you can do."
- Direct consumer to: UGC public grievance portal (ugc.gov.in) for formal complaint against the fake institution; National Consumer Helpline (1800-11-4000, toll-free); National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (ncdrc.nic.in) for financial compensation claim against the institution's operators; local police station to file FIR under IPC Section 420 (cheating) and Section 468 (forgery); SSC formal appeal citing victim of credential fraud with supporting documentation
- Evidence to collect: All enrollment documents and fee payment receipts; the fake affiliation certificate provided by the institution; phone records or saved messages from the admissions agent; SSC rejection letter; any promotional materials, website screenshots, or brochures from the fake university — capture these immediately as they may be removed; bank statements showing all payments made
- Feedback category tag: `financial_fraud | fake_institution | service_refused | consumer_turned_away | credential_fraud | corruption`

### Scenario 16-6: International Student Wrongly Removed from Course Due to Attendance System Error, Visa at Risk (Pattern A+B)
**Real Basis:** UK Office of the Independent Adjudicator Annual Report 2023 — specific upheld case: international student wrongfully terminated due to inaccurate attendance records; OIA received 3,137 complaints in 2023 (10% increase, record high); 34% of cases classified as service failures in academic delivery or administration (oiahe.org.uk; Advance HE summary)
**The Problem:** A Nigerian international student studying nursing at a UK university received a formal withdrawal letter stating she had been removed from her course for non-attendance. This triggered an automatic Tier 4 sponsor duty notification to the UK Home Office, placing her student visa at risk of curtailment. She attended all required sessions; the university's digital card-swipe system failed to register her presence on seven occasions across one term, and a paper register signed by her lecturers exists for those dates. She raised this through the Student Services portal three weeks ago and received only an auto-acknowledgment. She has called Student Services twice: the first time she was told it had been "escalated to the registry"; the second time a different agent had no record of her previous call or any escalation.
**What staff/org would do:** Process the case on the standard internal complaints timeline of six to eight weeks, during which her visa remains at risk and she is effectively suspended from her programme. The registry and Student Services continue to give inconsistent information because they operate on separate systems with no shared case record.
**What the Owner does:** Recognizes immediately that this involves an active immigration risk that cannot wait for a standard complaints timeline. Advises the student to pursue the internal complaint and external escalation simultaneously. Provides specific UKVI proactive contact guidance and advises her to obtain certified paper register evidence immediately before it becomes harder to locate.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is an urgent situation — being wrongly withdrawn from your course when your visa depends on your student status creates immediate immigration risk, and three weeks without a substantive response from the university is not acceptable. You have documented evidence that the error is on the institution's side. I am going to help you act on several fronts at the same time right now."
- Direct consumer to: UK Visas and Immigration customer contact centre (0300 790 6268) to proactively flag the situation before any Home Office action is taken; the university's Head of Registry directly — bypass Student Services and request a named contact; personal tutor or academic advisor for a written attendance statement; university International Student Support office, which typically has a dedicated visa compliance officer; OIA (oiahe.org.uk) — the internal process must be completed first, but begin building the paper trail now; Citizens Advice higher education guidance for international students
- Evidence to collect: Paper register sign-in sheets for all seven disputed dates — request certified copies from the academic department immediately; email or portal submission of original complaint with its timestamp; records of both phone calls including dates, times, and exactly what each agent said; the withdrawal letter and all subsequent correspondence; Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies (CAS) number and current visa documentation
- Feedback category tag: `service_failure | wrongful_withdrawal | attendance_error | international_student | urgent | visa_risk | administrative_error`

### Scenario 16-7: Disabled Student Denied Approved Exam Accommodations on the Day of the Exam (Pattern A+B)
**Real Basis:** UK OIA Annual Report 2023 — specific upheld case: student with disabilities received inadequate support, awarded £5,000 compensation; OIA 34% service failure category; Equality Act 2010 duty to make reasonable adjustments for disabled students in UK higher education (oiahe.org.uk; Advance HE summary 2023)
**The Problem:** A student with diagnosed dyslexia and anxiety — both documented with a medical certificate submitted to the disability support office before the academic year — was approved to receive 25% extra time and a separate quiet room for examinations. She arrived at her mid-term exam to find no separate room arrangement in place. An invigilator told her to sit in the main hall or leave and miss the exam. She sat under standard conditions, which she believes significantly harmed her performance. A formal complaint submitted immediately after the exam received a "holding letter" four weeks later stating the matter is "under review." The exam result has now been released and she has failed.
**What staff/org would do:** Offer a re-sit at the end of the academic year — months away — refuse to adjust the mark already recorded, and issue a generic apology without acknowledging institutional liability or offering any compensation for the concrete academic harm.
**What the Owner does:** Validates that this is a documented category of service failure that the OIA regularly upholds and awards compensation for. Advises the student on her rights under the Equality Act 2010, the internal complaints timeline she is entitled to enforce, and the OIA escalation path if the internal response is inadequate.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "What happened to you — arriving for an approved accommodation that was never set up, then being forced to choose between sitting under unfair conditions or missing the exam — is a failure entirely on the institution, not on you. Under the Equality Act 2010, universities have a legal duty to make reasonable adjustments for disability, and the OIA regularly upholds complaints exactly like this one. Let me help you document this properly."
- Direct consumer to: Equality and Human Rights Commission (equalityhumanrights.com) for guidance on Equality Act 2010 reasonable adjustment duties in education; OIA (oiahe.org.uk) after internal process is exhausted — universities have 90 days to provide a Completion of Procedures letter; Disabled Students UK (disabledstudents.co.uk) for peer advocacy; the university Student Union advice service for free complaint support and letter drafting
- Evidence to collect: Medical certificate confirming diagnosis and copy of the accommodation approval letter from the disability office; any email confirmation of the approved adjustments and exam arrangements; a written statement requested from the invigilator who directed her to the main hall (request this formally from the exam board); the failed exam result once official; the holding letter with its date; all complaint correspondence sent and received
- Feedback category tag: `rights_violation | disability_discrimination | service_failure | reasonable_adjustments | equality_act | consumer_turned_away`

### Scenario 16-8: University Sends Official Acceptance Letter to Rejected Applicant, Retracts It After Student Acts on It (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** University of South Florida 2019 — 430 rejected applicants received official acceptance letters; Johns Hopkins University 2014 — approximately 300 early decision rejects received acceptance email, corrected hours later; Middlesex University UK — 2,500 prospective students received incorrect offer letters; UNC Chapel Hill 2007 — 2,500 applicants emailed congratulatory admission notice incorrectly (Time magazine 2014; KYW Newsradio / Audacy legal analysis; The Student Room UK documentation)
**The Problem:** A 17-year-old received an official university acceptance letter with her name, proposed programme, and a provisional student ID number. She accepted, declined two other university offers including her backup, paid a non-refundable £500 enrollment deposit, and informed her school. Three days later, a second email arrived stating the original letter was sent in error and she is not admitted. She calls the admissions office and waits 34 minutes on hold. When she reaches an advisor, she is told the error "came from a system upgrade" and the offer cannot be honoured. She asks whether the deposit will be refunded and is told "that is a separate department" — then the call transfers and drops. Her two backup offers have now closed.
**What staff/org would do:** Refund the enrollment deposit after four to six weeks of administrative processing, send a generic apology letter, and point to a disclaimer in the application terms stating all offers are subject to verification — refusing to acknowledge liability for the consequential harm of having lost other university places.
**What the Owner does:** Validates the consumer's harm and explains the legal position clearly — courts have held no enforceable contract arose, but the institution has a moral and often procedural obligation to address consequential loss. Advises on formal complaints to document consequential loss, consumer protection rights regarding the deposit, and immediate practical steps to recover alternative places.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "Receiving an official acceptance letter with your name and student ID, then being told it was an error after you have already turned down other offers and paid a deposit — that is a real harm caused entirely by the university's system failure, not by anything you did wrong. There are steps you can take right now both to get your money back and to recover your position for this admissions cycle."
- Direct consumer to: UCAS (ucas.com) immediately to check Clearing availability and contact alternative institutions; the university's formal complaints procedure in writing, citing consequential loss including the declined offers — this creates a paper record for any potential dispute; OIA (oiahe.org.uk) for UK students if the internal complaints outcome is inadequate; Citizens Advice for consumer contract rights regarding the non-refundable deposit; the other universities whose offers were declined — contact them in writing immediately explaining the situation, as some will reinstate an offer when presented with evidence of an institutional error
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot or printout of the original acceptance letter with timestamp and all details including student ID number; copies of all offer decline communications sent to backup universities and the dates they were sent; deposit payment receipt and bank statement entry; the retraction email with its timestamp; dated record of the admissions call including the hold time and the dropped transfer
- Feedback category tag: `information_failure | admissions_error | financial_harm | transparency | consequential_loss | operational_error`

### Scenario 16-9: Welfare Recipient Enrolled in Diploma Course Without Meaningful Consent, $18,000 Government Debt Created in Her Name (Pattern C+E)
**Real Basis:** Phoenix Institute of Australia and Community Training Initiatives — ACCC Federal Court findings of unconscionable conduct and false or misleading representations; approximately 12,000 students enrolled in over 21,000 courses without informed consent; Commonwealth paid Phoenix $106 million in VET FEE-HELP before the scheme was exposed; ACCC also prosecuted Unique International College, Empower Institute, Australian Institute of Professional Education, and Captain Cook College (accc.gov.au/media-release/phoenix-institute-acted-unconscionably-and-misled-students; Wikipedia Ghost colleges in Australia)
**The Problem:** A 52-year-old woman on Centrelink welfare benefits was approached at a Centrelink office by a recruiter who told her she could sign up for a "free online diploma" that would help her find work at no personal cost. She signed what she understood to be a contact information form for a follow-up call. She never attended any classes and received no course materials. Six months later she received a government debt notice showing an $18,000 VET FEE-HELP loan registered in her name for a Diploma of Business Administration at Phoenix Institute. She calls the institute and is told she enrolled voluntarily. She calls the VET FEE-HELP line, waits 55 minutes in a queue, and the call disconnects. On a second call she reaches an agent who tells her to contact the institute directly to resolve any enrollment disputes.
**What staff/org would do:** Treat the enrollment as valid based on a signed document, refer all disputes back to the institution, and loop her between the institute and the government agency — neither of which acknowledges the ACCC's court findings or the government redress mechanism designed for exactly this situation.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies this immediately as matching the Phoenix Institute pattern found by the ACCC to constitute unconscionable conduct. Informs the consumer she may qualify for full loan re-credit under the government's VET Student Loans redress process and connects her to free specialist legal advocacy.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — being signed up for a course and a government loan you did not meaningfully consent to, by a recruiter who approached you at a Centrelink office — was found by Australia's competition authority to be illegal conduct by a group of providers in this sector. You are not responsible for this debt, and there is a specific government process to have it cancelled. I am going to walk you through that right now."
- Direct consumer to: Department of Education VET Student Loans re-credit application (education.gov.au/vet-student-loans) — specifically the "re-credit on grounds of provider non-compliance or misconduct" pathway; ASQA complaint form (asqa.gov.au/complaints); ACCC consumer complaint line (1300 302 502) citing the Phoenix Institute prosecution; Financial Rights Legal Centre (financialrights.org.au) for free specialist debt and consumer law advice; National Debt Helpline (1800 007 007)
- Evidence to collect: The government debt notice including loan amount, institution name, and reference number; any paperwork she signed at the Centrelink office and the date it was signed; detailed records of both phone calls including hold times, the disconnection, and what the second agent said; Centrelink records or MyGov statements confirming her benefit status at the time of enrollment; any text messages or calls from the recruiter before or after the Centrelink visit
- Feedback category tag: `financial_fraud | predatory_enrollment | vulnerable_consumer | unauthorized_debt | consumer_protection | call_center_failure`

### Scenario 16-10: Recruiter Phone Call Misrepresented VET FEE-HELP Loan as "Completely Free", Student Now Faces $14,000 Government Debt (Pattern C+E)
**Real Basis:** ACCC Federal Court findings against Phoenix Institute and Community Training Initiatives — recruiter phone scripts and verbal representations found to be false or misleading under Australian Consumer Law; ACCC also prosecuted Unique International College and Empower Institute for the same conduct; Commonwealth paid $106M before the scheme was exposed (accc.gov.au/media-release/phoenix-institute-acted-unconscionably-and-misled-students; ACS Information Age; ACCC enforcement timeline 2016–2020)
**The Problem:** A 38-year-old part-time cleaner received an unsolicited phone call from a recruiter for an online training institute offering a "completely free" Diploma of Business Administration, explaining that the Australian government covers all costs and there is "nothing for you to pay, ever." She asked three times about costs and was told each time there was no charge. She enrolled. Eighteen months later she receives a MyGov notification showing a $14,000 VET FEE-HELP government debt registered on her tax file. She calls the institute; an agent tells her the loan is "a normal part of government education funding" and she "would have signed a form." She calls the ATO and is placed on hold for 42 minutes before reaching an agent who confirms the debt is valid and enforceable through the tax system once her income crosses the threshold.
**What staff/org would do:** Confirm the debt is legally registered and enforceable, treat the signed enrollment form as the definitive record of consent, and provide no acknowledgment that the verbal representations inducing the enrollment were materially false and the subject of federal court proceedings.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies this as a documented ACCC-prosecuted pattern of unconscionable conduct in which the verbal misrepresentations during phone enrollment calls — identical to what this consumer describes — were found to be illegal. Informs her that the specific re-credit pathway exists precisely because the verbal pitch, not the signed form, is what the law focuses on.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "The phone call you received — where a recruiter told you three times it was completely free — is exactly the kind of misrepresentation that Australia's competition regulator prosecuted multiple training providers for in federal court. You were misled, and there is a government process specifically designed to cancel VET FEE-HELP debts created this way. Let me explain what you can do."
- Direct consumer to: Department of Education VET Student Loans re-credit application (education.gov.au/vet-student-loans) — "re-credit on grounds of provider non-compliance or misconduct" pathway; ACCC complaint portal (accc.gov.au/consumers/consumer-complaints) citing the ACCC's prior Phoenix Institute and Empower Institute prosecutions; ASQA complaint form (asqa.gov.au/complaints); Financial Rights Legal Centre (financialrights.org.au, 1800 007 007) for free specialist advice on disputing the debt; National Debt Helpline (1800 007 007)
- Evidence to collect: A written account of the phone call drafted immediately — date, time, the recruiter's name if given, and the exact words used about cost including her three specific questions and the answers; any SMS or email follow-up from the recruiter after the call; enrollment paperwork signed and the date it was completed; the MyGov notification showing the debt amount; records of the call to the institute including what the agent said about the loan; the ATO call record with hold time and the agent's statement about enforceability
