### Scenario 29-1: Participant Collapses Inside Sweat Lodge After Staff Dismiss Safety Concerns (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** James Arthur Ray "Spiritual Warrior" retreat, Sedona, Arizona, October 2009 — criminal conviction records; ABC News jury verdict coverage June 2011; CNN sentencing coverage November 2011. Participants paid $9,695 each. Three died (Kirby Brown, James Shore, Liz Neuman), 19 hospitalized. Pre-retreat telephone briefings dismissed participant health and safety questions as "part of the transformation process."
**The Problem:** A participant at a "Spiritual Warrior" multi-day retreat in Sedona, Arizona has paid $9,695 to attend. During the sweat lodge ceremony on day four, she watches the person next to her lose consciousness and begin convulsing. The retreat facilitator standing at the lodge entrance tells the group to stay in and that they are "just experiencing transformation." She has made it outside the lodge. She cannot find any staff member with a phone. There are 55 other people still inside and the entrance is being blocked. She is submitting feedback right now through a QR code at the retreat venue.
**What staff/org would do:** Retreat staff circulate telling participants that intense physical experiences are expected and direct people to return to the lodge. A staff member takes her contact details for a "follow-up call" but provides no emergency number. Nobody has called emergency services.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately flags this as a life-threatening emergency. Instructs the person to call emergency services now before doing anything else. Provides the local emergency number. Directs her to move others away from the lodge and document staff actions.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a medical emergency. Please stop reading and call 911 now. Move yourself and as many people as you can safely away from the lodge immediately. Do not wait for staff permission or instructions. Your safety and the safety of others in that lodge is the only priority right now."
- Who to direct the consumer to: 911 (US emergency services); Yavapai County Sheriff's Office (928-771-3260); Arizona Department of Health Services; National Emergency Management for documentation after the incident
- What evidence the consumer should collect/preserve: Photographs of the lodge structure and surroundings, count of participants present, names of staff members identified, the retreat itinerary and brochure, any waivers or medical disclosure forms signed before arrival, notes on exact words used by facilitator when dismissing concerns
- Feedback category tag: `emergency / participant_safety / physical_harm / urgent_escalation`

### Scenario 29-2: Member Threatened With Release of Blackmail Material When She Tries to Leave the Group (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** NXIVM / Keith Raniere — DOJ press releases, US Attorney's Office Eastern District of New York; jury conviction on all counts June 19, 2019; 120-year sentence October 27, 2020. The DOS subgroup within NXIVM used "collateral" (nude photographs, signed confessions, property deeds) submitted by members to coerce compliance and prevent exit. Members who attempted to leave were contacted through the recruiter chain with threats of collateral release. Documented in court evidence and verified by ABC News and the Washington Examiner.
**The Problem:** A woman in Albany, New York joined what she believed was an "Executive Success Programs" personal development organization. She has now learned that nude photographs and personal confessions she submitted as required "collateral" to her coach are being held by the organization. Tonight she received a phone voicemail from her recruiter stating: "If you leave or speak to anyone outside the group, what you have shared will be released." She wants to leave but is terrified of what will be released. She is submitting this through the feedback platform late at night.
**What staff/org would do:** The organization has no independent external complaint channel. All internal coaches are members of the same hierarchy and would report any complaint back to leadership. Calling the organization's listed number would reach administration that is part of the same structure.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an active criminal coercion and potential sex trafficking situation. Does not advise the person to contact the organization. Directs immediately and clearly to law enforcement and specialist victim support.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**

- Opening reply: "What you are describing is criminal blackmail and coercion. This is not your fault, and you are not alone. Please do not respond to the recruiter's message. ### Scenario 29-3: Consumer Calls to Cancel $10,000 Coaching Package — Transferred Six Times, Line Dropped Twice (Pattern A+B)
**Real Basis:** Lurn / Anik Singal — FTC enforcement action September 2023; FTC press release "FTC Acts to Stop Online Business Coaching Scheme Lurn"; FTC blog post "Lurn didn't learn from Penalty Offense Notice." After a low-cost online entry purchase, telemarketers called consumers to pressure-sell $10,000 consulting packages. The FTC specifically cited the two-stage telephone upsell as deceptive. Consumers attempting to cancel reported repeated transfers and being told to continue "30 more days" before reassessment.
**The Problem:** A consumer in Maryland paid $97 for an online business course from Lurn after seeing advertising claiming she could "Make More Than $13,700 Per Day." Within 48 hours, a Lurn telephone agent called and pressure-sold her into a $10,000 consulting package, assuring her it carried a 30-day satisfaction guarantee. She saw no results. On day 28 she called to cancel. She was transferred to a "success coach," then to billing, then to "member services," then to billing again. On the fifth transfer the call was dropped. She called back twice over the next two days — both times she was placed on hold for over 25 minutes before the line disconnected. She is now submitting a formal complaint.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center agent repeats that the 30-day satisfaction window "started when coaching began, not when the package was purchased" and offers an "extended support package" at additional cost instead of a refund. The refund request is recorded as "pending review" with no timeline given and no written confirmation sent.
**What the Owner does:** Documents the call center obstruction pattern in detail, validates her legal entitlement to a refund, and directs her to regulatory and financial remedies.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You are describing a documented pattern of deceptive selling followed by deliberate refund obstruction through the call center. Your experience is valid, your refund entitlement is real, and you have practical options to recover this money. Let's build a clear record together."
- Who to direct the consumer to: FTC complaint portal at ReportFraud.ftc.gov (the FTC has an active enforcement record against Lurn specifically); Maryland Attorney General Consumer Protection Division (410-528-8662 or oag.state.md.us); dispute the $10,000 charge with your bank or credit card issuer citing deceptive sales tactics and failure to honor a stated satisfaction guarantee
- What evidence the consumer should collect/preserve: A log of every call attempt with date, time, duration, and name of every representative spoken to; the text or screenshot of any written guarantee shown at point of sale; screenshots of the earnings claim advertisements; the original $97 purchase confirmation email; any written communications from Lurn about the $10,000 package
- Feedback category tag: `financial_fraud / call_center_failure / refund_obstruction / upsell_trap`

### Scenario 29-4: MLM Member Cannot Get a Straight Answer on His Compensation Structure — Routed Back to His Own Recruiter (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Lyoness International AG and associated entities — ACCC Federal Court proceedings August 2014; ACCC media release October 2015. Consumer complaints about the opacity of the Lyoness compensation structure preceded and directly triggered the ACCC investigation. Australian consumers who contacted Lyoness support to understand their earning structure reported being transferred to "member advisors" who were themselves Lyoness recruits with a financial interest in keeping them active — documented as a conflict-of-interest support model.
**The Problem:** A consumer in Melbourne, Australia joined Lyoness (trading as Cashback World) after a friend's invitation. He paid AUD $2,400 in required "down payments" toward future purchase credits, believing these would generate cashback earnings as explained at the recruitment meeting. Eight months later he has received AUD $32 in cashback against his AUD $2,400 outlay. He called Lyoness customer support three times across three weeks to request a written explanation of how the compensation model actually works. Each time he was transferred to a "member advisor" who turned out to be his own upline recruiter — the friend who signed him up — who told him he needed to recruit more people to unlock higher earning levels. He is requesting a clear written compensation breakdown and a refund of his down payments.
**What staff/org would do:** The "member advisor" (his recruiter) explains that earnings "accelerate with additional unit purchases and team growth" and invites him to a team meeting to learn more. No independent written compensation breakdown is provided. When he presses for a refund, he is told a written application takes "6 to 8 weeks to process" and is given a form with no submission confirmation mechanism.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies the structural conflict of interest in the support model, validates the consumer's request for independent information, and directs to the ACCC and state consumer rights channels.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You are right to be concerned. Being routed back to the person who recruited you every time you ask about your earnings is a direct conflict of interest — that person benefits financially from keeping you in the program. You are entitled to a clear, independent written explanation of how this compensation model works."
- Who to direct the consumer to: ACCC (1300 302 502 or accc.gov.au/consumers — the ACCC has directly investigated Lyoness); Consumer Affairs Victoria (1300 558 181); request in writing via email directly to Lyoness corporate (not through your upline) so there is a documented record of the request and their response
- What evidence the consumer should collect/preserve: All receipts and bank statements for every down payment made, the original written or printed materials shown at the recruitment meeting including any income projections, a record of all three support calls (dates, names of agents, what was said), any written communications from Lyoness or your recruiter
- Feedback category tag: `transparency_failure / mlm_support_conflict / refund_dispute / information_failure`

### Scenario 29-5: Retired Nurse Loses AUD $40,000 to Multi-Step Telephone Coaching Upsell — Refund Refused Citing Terms (Pattern A+B)
**Real Basis:** MOBE (My Online Business Education) / Matt Lloyd (Australian founder) — FTC enforcement action June 2018 (FTC case 172-3072); FTC refund announcement April 2022 ($23M returned to more than 37,000 consumers). MOBE's telephone upsell telemarketers specifically targeted older adults and veterans. Refund requests were refused on grounds that consumers had "agreed to non-refundable terms." The FTC found the earnings claims deceptive and the refund refusals unlawful.
**The Problem:** A retired nurse in Brisbane, Australia paid AUD $79 for a MOBE "21-step" online business starter kit after seeing a Facebook advertisement promising passive income from home. Over the following six months, she was telephoned by MOBE sales representatives at each step and systematically pressure-sold into successively higher coaching tiers. She has now paid a total of approximately AUD $40,000 — drawn from two credit cards and a partial superannuation withdrawal — across the escalating tier purchases. She has generated no income. She submitted a written refund request. MOBE responded that she "agreed to non-refundable terms" and the "coaching investment was her personal responsibility to leverage." A third written request has received no response.
**What staff/org would do:** MOBE's refund team sends a scripted response citing terms and conditions and offers a "coaching pause" instead of a refund. No telephone number is provided to reach a supervisor. A third escalation attempt receives no response within 30 days.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as potential elder financial exploitation in addition to consumer fraud, provides both Australian and international enforcement remedies, and directs her to dispute the charges through her financial institutions.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What has happened to you is financial fraud. The systematic phone calls escalating your payments step by step is the exact pattern Australian and US regulators have found illegal. You did not fail — you were deliberately targeted. There are real options to recover this money, and I want to walk through all of them with you."
- Who to direct the consumer to: ACCC (1300 302 502 or accc.gov.au — the ACCC coordinates with the FTC on MOBE-type international schemes); Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA) at 1800 931 678 to dispute the credit card charges; ACORN (Australian Cybercrime Online Reporting Network) at acorn.gov.au for the online fraud component; your superannuation fund to document the withdrawal under financial hardship provisions; your bank and credit card providers to lodge fraud disputes on each transaction
- What evidence the consumer should collect/preserve: All receipts and bank/credit card statements for every payment at every tier, the original $79 purchase confirmation, all emails and text messages from MOBE sales representatives, notes or recordings of each telephone call including the name of the representative and the date, the original Facebook advertisement if saved or screenshotted
- Feedback category tag: `financial_fraud / elder_exploitation / upsell_trap / refund_refused / vulnerability`

### Scenario 29-6: Woman Pressured to Phone-Recruit Her Sister Mid-Seminar and Denied Permission to Leave (Pattern C+B)
**Real Basis:** Landmark Forum (successor to Werner Erhard's "est" program) — France 3 hidden camera investigation broadcast 2004; French authorities effectively shut down Landmark's French operations following the broadcast. The investigation documented participants being handed phones during seminar breaks and instructed to recruit friends and family while in an emotionally elevated state. Landmark has pursued litigation against critics including clinical psychology professor Margaret Singer and the Cult Education Institute to suppress coverage of these practices.
**The Problem:** A 34-year-old woman in Lyon, France paid EUR 600 to attend a Landmark Forum weekend seminar on personal development. It is now Saturday afternoon of the first weekend. The facilitator has instructed every participant to telephone someone they love and invite them to join the Forum during the current break. When the woman says she does not want to make the call, the facilitator challenges her in front of the group, asking why she is "resisting growth." When she quietly tells a staff member she wants to leave the seminar, the staff member says: "Leaving now means you lose your entire investment and you will damage the progress you have made — you should ask yourself what you are running away from." She feels she cannot leave. She is using her phone now to submit a complaint.
**What staff/org would do:** Staff continue applying social pressure and frame her desire to leave as a psychological issue to be examined inside the seminar room. The door is not physically locked, but social pressure and sunk-cost framing are used to prevent her departure. No refund procedure is offered.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms her absolute right to leave at any time regardless of payment, makes clear the seminar's social pressure tactics do not override her legal rights, and directs her to French consumer and cult-oversight authorities.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You have the absolute right to leave this seminar right now. No payment, no contract, and no seminar rule removes that right. You do not owe anyone an explanation for leaving. You are also not obligated to call anyone. Please feel confident walking out whenever you choose."
- Who to direct the consumer to: DGCCRF (Direction generale de la concurrence, de la consommation et de la repression des fraudes) at dgccrf.bercy.gouv.fr for the consumer protection complaint; MIVILUDES (Mission interministerielle de vigilance et de lutte contre les derives sectaires) at miviludes.gouv.fr — this is France's official government body for cult-like influence and is the correct authority for this specific type of complaint; file a consumer signal at signal.conso.gouv.fr

### Scenario 29-7: Herbalife Distributor Calls Support About Earnings Gap — Transferred to Her Own Upline Recruiter (Pattern D+A)
**Real Basis:** Herbalife FTC settlement July 15, 2016 ($200 million consumer redress; required fundamental restructuring to reward retail sales rather than recruitment). FTC investigation documented that Herbalife distributor support lines routed earnings-related calls to "business coaches" within Herbalife's own distributor network — individuals financially incentivized to retain callers as active distributors rather than resolve their complaints. Verified by NPR, CNBC, and the Christian Science Monitor.
**The Problem:** A distributor in Guadalajara, Mexico joined Herbalife after her cousin showed her printed income projections suggesting she could earn MXN 25,000 per month working part-time. After eight months of consistent effort, she has earned a total of MXN 1,800 while spending approximately MXN 18,000 on inventory she has been unable to sell to end customers. She called the Herbalife Mexico distributor support line to ask why her earnings do not match the projections she was shown. The support agent told her she needed "more training" and transferred her to a "business mentor" — who turned out to be her own upline cousin, the same person who recruited her. The cousin told her she needed to purchase the next product tier to qualify for higher earning levels. She wants a transparent, independent explanation of the actual compensation structure and to return the unsold inventory for a refund.
**What staff/org would do:** The upline cousin explains that the projected income figures apply to distributors at Senior Consultant level and above and encourages her to invest in the next qualifying purchase. When she presses specifically for a refund on inventory, the call ends. Herbalife's official support line refers her back to "your local business mentor" for further guidance.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies the circular support model as a documented conflict of interest, validates the earnings misrepresentation concern, and directs her to independent regulatory channels in Mexico and internationally.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Being transferred to the person who recruited you every time you ask about your earnings is a conflict of interest — your recruiter earns money when you stay active and buy more product, which means they cannot give you independent advice. You are asking exactly the right questions, and you deserve answers from a source that has no financial stake in your decision."
- Who to direct the consumer to: PROFECO (Procuraduria Federal del Consumidor) at profeco.gob.mx or 800 468 8722 for the consumer fraud complaint; CONDUSEF (Comision Nacional para la Proteccion y Defensa de los Usuarios de Servicios Financieros) at 800 999 8080 for the financial aspects; FTC international complaint portal (ftc.gov/es) which coordinates with Mexican authorities on MLM complaints
- What evidence the consumer should collect/preserve: All income projection documents and printed materials shown at the original recruitment meeting; all purchase receipts and bank records for every inventory purchase; a written record of all support calls including dates, agent names, and what was said; the current count and value of unsold inventory; any written communications from Herbalife or the cousin-recruiter about "qualifying levels"
- Feedback category tag: `staff_misconduct / circular_support / mlm_earnings_misrepresentation / corruption`

### Scenario 29-9: Seminar Relocated Internationally After Cancellation — Attendee Denied Refund Under Non-Refundable Terms (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Tony Robbins / Robbins Research International — BuzzFeed News year-long investigative report published May 2019 (verified by NBC News; 10+ women interviewed, leaked internal documents); BBB complaints against Robbins Research International on record for refund refusals and billing disputes; participants who sought refunds following the BuzzFeed investigation reported extended customer service hold times and inability to cancel future event bookings.
**The Problem:** A participant in Toronto, Canada paid CAD $5,200 for a four-day "Unleash the Power Within" event. The event was suspended partway through day two due to a health and safety issue at the venue. Attendees were told by a stage announcement that rescheduling information would be emailed within 72 hours. Seventy-two hours passed with no email. She called Robbins Research International customer service: 38 minutes on hold before reaching an agent, who told her the event was being rescheduled to a date in Phoenix, Arizona. She cannot travel internationally and requested a refund for the portion of the event not delivered. The agent said: "Tickets are non-refundable under the terms you accepted and rescheduling to the next available event is the only option we can offer." She asked to speak to a supervisor, who read the same policy.
**What staff/org would do:** Customer service repeats the non-refund policy and offers only a ticket transfer to another attendee — but she must find the buyer herself. No written record of the cancellation notice or rescheduling offer is provided by the company.
**What the Owner does:** Documents that a cancelled event with a fundamental change of location triggers specific Canadian consumer rights that override standard non-refundable terms, and directs her to both the credit card dispute process and provincial consumer protection.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**

- Who to direct the consumer to: Consumer Protection Ontario (1-800-889-9768 or ontario.ca/page/consumer-protection-ontario) to file a complaint and understand your rights under the Consumer Protection Act 2002; Competition Bureau Canada (1-800-348-5358) if the advertising of the event did not disclose cancellation-for-relocation policies; dispute the CAD $5,200 charge with your credit card issuer citing non-delivery of contracted service and a fundamental change of location
- What evidence the consumer should collect/preserve: The ticket purchase confirmation and receipt; the original event advertised location and dates; the text of the non-refund terms shown at time of purchase; notes from the stage announcement on the day of cancellation (date, time, exact words); call logs from the customer service call (date, hold time, agent name, supervisor name); any email eventually received about the Phoenix rescheduling

### Scenario 29-10: Consumer Harmed by Unqualified "Trauma Coach" — Every Regulatory Body Turns Her Away (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** UK regulatory gap — UK Parliament Research Briefing CBP-10849 (May 2026) and HCPC "Misuse of Protected Title" guidance; Training Journal UK 2024 regulatory analysis. The UK has no statutory regulation of the titles "life coach," "wellness coach," or "trauma coach." The HCPC protects specific titles such as "psychologist" and "psychotherapist" but not "coach." This means any person in the UK can use these titles and offer services to the public without any qualifications, and consumers have no licensing-based protection and no single regulatory body with authority to act.
**The Problem:** A woman in Leeds, UK paid GBP 3,600 over four months for weekly sessions with a practitioner who advertised herself online as a "certified trauma coach and healing specialist" and listed several self-issued certificates. The practitioner used techniques she called "trauma release exercises" and "somatic reprogramming." Following the sessions the consumer experienced worsening anxiety and recurring intrusive episodes. Her GP assessed that no evidence-based trauma processing methodology had been used and that some of the techniques may have been counterproductive. The consumer then contacted: (1) the HCPC — told "coach" is not a protected title and they have no jurisdiction; (2) the BACP — told the practitioner is not a member; (3) the ICF (International Coaching Federation) — told the practitioner is not ICF-accredited; (4) Trading Standards — told this is a civil dispute outside their remit. She has been turned away by every body she could identify. The practitioner is still advertising on the same platforms.
**What staff/org would do:** Each regulatory body legitimately explains it has no jurisdiction under current law. None can de-certify or sanction the practitioner. No body refers her to a body that can help. The practitioner remains active.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the genuine regulatory gap documented in Parliament, maps every available remedy however imperfect, supports the consumer in pursuing civil and advertising-based routes, and documents the systemic failure for policy advocacy.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You have done everything right. You contacted every body a reasonable person would contact, and you have been failed by a genuine gap in UK law that Parliament has documented and is reviewing. This is not your fault and it is not a reflection on the validity of what happened to you. There are still some options even within this gap, and I want to help you pursue them."
- Who to direct the consumer to: Citizens Advice (citizensadvice.org.uk or freephone 0800 144 8848) for guidance on the Small Claims Track to recover GBP 3,600 if the practitioner made false credential or qualification claims in advertising; Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk or 0300 123 2040) if the "certified" and "specialist" credential claims were fabricated; Advertising Standards Authority (asa.org.uk) to report the online advertising as misleading regarding qualifications; your GP to formally document the harm in your medical record for any future proceedings; NHS Talking Therapies (previously IAPT) for referral to evidence-based support
- What evidence the consumer should collect/preserve: Screenshots of the practitioner's full online profile and advertising exactly as it appeared when you paid (including all stated qualifications and certificates); all invoices and payment records for every session; your GP's written assessment; a written timeline of sessions and the effects experienced after each; all email and messaging communications with the practitioner; records of all four regulatory contacts (who you spoke to, what they said, dates)
- Feedback category tag: `information_failure / regulatory_gap / unqualified_practitioner / consumer_turned_away / harm_from_service`
