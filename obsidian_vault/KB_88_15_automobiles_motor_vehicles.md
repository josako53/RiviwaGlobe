### Scenario 15-1: Recalled Airbag Still Installed — Driver Receives Letter Addressed to Previous Owner (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** NHTSA Takata Recall Spotlight; CarComplaints.com confirmed death of Huma Hanif, Fort Bend County, Texas, March 31, 2016 — 10th US Takata fatality; NHTSA confirmed 28th US death September 2024; 67 million airbags recalled across 19 automakers; six recall notices mailed to prior registered owner, never reaching actual occupant
**The Problem:** A 19-year-old driver in Texas has just received a forwarded letter addressed to a previous registered owner of her 2003 Honda Civic stating that the driver-side airbag may be subject to a Takata recall. She drives the car daily for work. She does not know whether her specific VIN is affected, whether replacement parts are available, or whether it is safe to drive the vehicle at all.
**What staff/org would do:** Transfer her to the dealer, who places her on hold and tells her replacement parts are on national backorder with no estimated timeline — and that she can "continue using the vehicle at her own discretion."
**What the Owner does:** Treat this as a safety emergency. Look up her VIN on NHTSA's recall tool in real time. If the vehicle is recalled and unrepaired, advise her not to drive it until the airbag has been replaced. Confirm whether a loaner vehicle is available under NHTSA's remedy requirements and escalate to Honda's urgent recall line.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a serious safety matter and I want to help you find out right now whether your specific car is affected. Please do not drive this vehicle until we confirm it is safe — the airbag fault involved in this recall has caused fatalities."
- Direct to: NHTSA VIN recall lookup at nhtsa.gov/recalls (instant, free, VIN-specific); Honda Recall Hotline 1-800-999-1009; NHTSA Vehicle Safety Hotline 1-888-327-4236 to report an unresolved recall; if parts are unavailable, Honda is obligated under NHTSA guidance to provide interim transportation
- Evidence to collect: VIN from dashboard or door jamb sticker, the forwarded recall letter including original envelope, any prior service records indicating whether an airbag repair was ever performed, date and name of dealer contact if already spoken to
- Feedback category tag: Product Safety / Recall Notification Failure

### Scenario 15-2: Dealer Blames "Heavy Key Chain" While Car Cuts Out at Speed (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** GM Ignition Switch Recall; DOJ Deferred Prosecution Agreement September 17, 2015 — $900 million forfeiture; GM Compensation Fund final report (Kenneth Feinberg, August 2015) — 124 confirmed deaths; documented scripted dealer responses "driver error" and "heavy key chain" used across service network during 2003–2014 cover-up period; NHTSA enforcement record
**The Problem:** A consumer in Michigan has brought her 2006 Chevrolet Cobalt to a GM franchise dealer three times over six months. Each time she reports that the engine cuts out without warning at highway speed, power steering fails momentarily, and the airbag warning light activates. The first two visits were logged as "no fault found." On her second visit, the service advisor suggested the problem might be caused by the weight of her key ring on the ignition key. She now drives to work in fear and refuses to carry her children in the car.
**What staff/org would do:** The service advisor repeats the key chain explanation, closes the third ticket as "customer advised," and does not mention any open NHTSA investigation or recall. No escalation to GM corporate.
**What the Owner does:** Recognise this as a documented decade-long pattern in which GM dealer staff were scripted to deflect consumer safety complaints using the key chain explanation. Escalate to NHTSA as a safety-critical report. Advise her the vehicle should not be driven with passengers until the ignition switch has been inspected.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — engine cut-out at speed, power steering loss, and airbag light activation — is a pattern that the US Department of Justice confirmed was linked to 124 deaths and a decade-long cover-up. The explanation you have been given is not accurate. Please do not drive this car with your children until this has been properly investigated."
- Direct to: NHTSA Vehicle Safety Hotline 1-888-327-4236 to file a Vehicle Safety Complaint (these reports trigger formal investigation); GM Owner Center recall lookup at gm.com/recall; Center for Auto Safety at autosafety.org for documentation and legal guidance; if the vehicle is covered by the extended GM ignition switch warranty, a free inspection is available at any GM dealer
- Evidence to collect: All three dealer repair orders (request printed copies of every visit ticket); written list of dates, speeds, and road conditions for each stall and airbag light event; dashcam or phone recording of any future event if safe to capture; names and employee numbers of all service advisors involved
- Feedback category tag: Product Safety / Staff Misconduct / Recall Cover-Up

### Scenario 15-3: $5,295 Fake Inspection Fee Added to Her Contract — Dealer Refuses to Reverse It (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** FTC and Connecticut Attorney General v. Chase Nissan LLC d/b/a Manchester City Nissan, January 2024; FTC complaint documents a $5,295.65 "multi-point inspection fee" added to a vehicle already certified as pre-owned; $3,300 service contract added without consumer knowledge; unauthorized add-ons present in more than 90% of dealer transactions; FTC CARS Rule rulemaking record: $3.4 billion per year in documented dealer junk fee harm to US consumers; 72 million consumer-hours annually spent resolving the aftermath
**The Problem:** A woman in Connecticut purchased a certified pre-owned vehicle advertised at $15,700. After collecting the vehicle and reading her financing contract at home, she found a $5,295.65 "multi-point inspection fee" and a $3,300 service contract she has no recollection of signing for. She returned to the dealer the following morning. The finance manager told her the inspection fee is standard procedure, the service contract was "disclosed and initialled," and the contract has been sent to the lender and cannot be altered.
**What staff/org would do:** Finance manager declines to produce an itemised breakdown of the inspection work performed, insists all items were disclosed, and tells her to call the lender if she disagrees.
**What the Owner does:** Validate her concern — these exact charges match the FTC enforcement action against this dealer type. Explain that the FTC has documented these as deceptive practices and that she has the right to dispute unauthorized charges with both the dealer and the lender.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "A $5,295 inspection fee on a car that is already certified as pre-owned, added without clear prior disclosure, is exactly the type of practice the FTC has taken enforcement action against. Your concern is completely valid and you have the right to challenge these charges."
- Direct to: FTC complaint portal at ReportFraud.ftc.gov; Connecticut Department of Consumer Protection at ct.gov/dcp (860-713-6300) for a dealer licence complaint; Consumer Financial Protection Bureau at consumerfinance.gov/complaint to dispute the charges with the finance company; a consumer protection attorney for potential CUTPA (Connecticut Unfair Trade Practices Act) claim
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot or print of the original advertised price; full signed purchase contract showing every line item; any written materials provided at the time of signing; a written account of the signing session including how each document was presented; names of all staff involved and the date and time of the transaction

### Scenario 15-4: PCP Finance Broker Never Disclosed the Commission — Lender Now Telling Her to Wait for a Letter (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** FCA PS26/3 Motor Finance Consumer Redress Scheme confirmed March 30, 2026 — 12.1 million agreements eligible, average payout £830, total scheme cost £9.1 billion; Court of Appeal October 25, 2024 ruling: commission arrangements unlawful without full informed consumer consent; Financial Ombudsman Service v. Barclays Partner Finance December 17, 2024 — commission "not adequately disclosed"; FCA banned Discretionary Commission Arrangements January 2021
**The Problem:** A consumer in Manchester took out a Personal Contract Purchase agreement for a Vauxhall Astra in 2019. After seeing FCA media coverage of the motor finance redress scheme, she called her lender to ask whether her agreement involved a Discretionary Commission Arrangement. The call centre agent told her: "There's nothing to tell you at this stage — the scheme has not started yet. We will write to you when we have information." She cannot locate her original credit agreement and does not know how to confirm whether she is eligible.
**What staff/org would do:** Call centre agent provides the minimum permissible response, does not explain what a DCA is, does not tell her how to obtain a copy of her original agreement, and does not mention the Financial Ombudsman as an escalation route if the lender fails to engage properly.
**What the Owner does:** Explain what the FCA's confirmed redress scheme means for her, how to establish whether her agreement involved a DCA, and what her rights are now that the scheme is formally confirmed and binding on lenders.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You are right to follow this up. The FCA has formally confirmed a redress scheme covering 12.1 million agreements — this is legally binding on your lender, and telling you to simply wait for a letter is not a complete answer. Here is what you can do now."
- Direct to: FCA motor finance information page at fca.org.uk/consumers/car-finance-complaints; Financial Ombudsman Service at financial-ombudsman.org.uk (0800 023 4567) — a formal complaint can be raised if the lender does not respond substantively within 8 weeks; MoneySavingExpert free eligibility checker at moneysavingexpert.com for a preliminary DCA assessment; send a Subject Access Request to the lender under UK GDPR to obtain the original agreement and any internal commission records
- Evidence to collect: Name of lender and dealership, date and term of the PCP agreement, vehicle make, model, and registration, any documents retained from the point of sale, written record of today's call with the lender including agent name, date, and what was said
- Feedback category tag: Financial Mis-selling / Transparency Failure / Commission Non-Disclosure

### Scenario 15-5: Transferred Three Times, Then Disconnected — Same Fault, Nine-Day Wait for a Return Repair (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** UK Motor Ombudsman Q3 2024 official report — 4,847 service and repair cases in first three quarters of 2024, up 29% year-on-year; all-time record 1,657 new cases in Q2 2024; 32% of all disputes cited customer service failure as the primary cause; J.D. Power 2024 US Customer Service Index Study — average 5.2-day wait for mass-market dealer service appointment, with VP Chris Sutton noting "capacity and wait time issues have gotten progressively worse since the pandemic"
**The Problem:** A consumer in Birmingham had his Ford Focus repaired at a franchise dealer three weeks ago for a recurring automatic gearbox fault. The fault has returned. He called the dealer's customer service line on a Monday morning and was placed on hold for 22 minutes, transferred to the service department, then transferred again to the workshop manager. After a further 14 minutes on hold he was disconnected. When he called back, he was told he would need to book in "like any other customer" — a nine-day wait — despite this being a return repair for the identical fault.
**What staff/org would do:** Receptionist books him nine days out in the standard queue, treats the return repair as a new job, declines to escalate to a service manager, and makes no commitment regarding a courtesy vehicle or any priority given to a recurring unresolved fault.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the unacceptable handling and clarify that a return repair for the same fault carries distinct rights under consumer law and is not equivalent to a first booking. Help him formally escalate within the dealer group and to the Motor Ombudsman.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Being disconnected twice and told to wait nine days for a fault that was supposed to have been fixed is not acceptable, and a return repair for the same issue is legally distinct from a new booking. You have the right to a faster resolution."
- Direct to: Motor Ombudsman dispute resolution service at motorombudsman.org (0345 241 3008) — the Motor Ombudsman's Vehicle Warranty Products Code and Service and Repair Code both apply; the dealer's Group Customer Relations department (request this contact in writing); Citizens Advice consumer helpline 0808 223 1133; under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, a repair that fails to fix the fault within a reasonable time entitles the consumer to a price reduction or a further free repair
- Evidence to collect: Original repair invoice and job card; date the fault recurred; written description (via email or text to the dealer) of the fault returning, sent immediately to create a timestamped record; call log showing hold times and disconnections with approximate times; any promise of a courtesy vehicle or collection and delivery made at the time of original repair
- Feedback category tag: Call Centre Failure / Faulty Repair / Service Delay

### Scenario 15-6: Returned Her Leased Car in Good Standing — Now Facing a Credit Default for a Product She Tried to Cancel (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** CFPB enforcement action against Toyota Motor Credit Corporation, 2023 — $60 million order; 27,507 consumers falsely reported as delinquent to credit bureaus after returning leased vehicles; $32 million redress to consumers whose add-on product cancellations were blocked or processed incorrectly; $6 million specifically to consumers whose credit records were damaged; Financial Ombudsman Service equivalent jurisdiction in UK for illegal credit reporting by lenders
**The Problem:** A consumer in Glasgow returned her leased vehicle at lease end in December 2023 and received written confirmation from her lender that all amounts were settled. Three months later, she received a notice from a credit reference agency showing her as seriously delinquent on the account. When she called the lender, she was told the entry related to a GAP insurance product she had attempted to cancel six months earlier, which was "never processed." The disputed balance is £312. Her credit score has dropped by over 80 points and she has since been rejected for a mortgage.
**What staff/org would do:** Call centre agent acknowledges the GAP cancellation was not processed internally but states the account "shows as outstanding" and that she must pay the £312 and request a review — framing the lender's own processing failure as her debt.
**What the Owner does:** Make clear this is the lender's failure, not hers. A delinquency reported to a credit reference agency for a product the consumer attempted to cancel is an illegal credit reporting act. Help her dispute the entry and raise a formal complaint to the Financial Ombudsman immediately.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You should not have a delinquency on your credit file for a product you attempted to cancel and an account you settled in full. This is the lender's own processing failure — and it is currently affecting your ability to get a mortgage. You have a strong right to challenge this and have the entry corrected."
- Direct to: Financial Ombudsman Service at financial-ombudsman.org.uk (0800 023 4567) — submit a formal complaint against the lender for incorrect credit reporting and failure to process cancellation; Information Commissioner's Office at ico.org.uk for a complaint about inaccurate personal data held by the credit reference agency; add a Notice of Correction immediately via Experian, Equifax, or TransUnion explaining the dispute; request the lender's Data Protection Officer contact under UK GDPR Article 16 (right to rectification)
- Evidence to collect: Lease-end settlement confirmation letter showing zero balance signed off by the lender; proof of the GAP cancellation request — email, letter, or a written note of date and content of any phone call; credit reference report showing the default entry and its date; the mortgage rejection letter citing the default; all correspondence with the lender since December 2023
- Feedback category tag: Rights Violation / Illegal Credit Reporting / Add-On Product Blocking

### Scenario 15-7: Three Engine Replacements, Fault Still Present — Mazda Refuses Refund (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** ACCC v. Mazda Australia, Federal Court of Australia — A$11.5 million penalty 2021; 49 documented false or misleading representations made to nine consumers across seven vehicles including Mazda CX-5; three engine replacements on a single vehicle without resolution documented in the judgment; ACCC Deputy Chair Delia Rickard described conduct as "run-around," "evasions and subterfuges," and "appalling customer service"; Federal Court appeal dismissed March 23, 2023
**The Problem:** A consumer in Melbourne purchased a new Mazda CX-5 in 2016 for A$37,000. Over two years, the vehicle developed a serious engine fault causing jerking, power loss, and excessive oil consumption. Mazda Australia arranged three complete engine replacements under warranty, none of which resolved the fault. When the consumer formally wrote to Mazda Australia's national customer service requesting a refund or replacement under the Australian Consumer Law, he received a written response stating the only remedy available under "the terms of his manufacturer's warranty" was a further repair attempt. He has been without a reliable vehicle for eleven cumulative months.
**What staff/org would do:** National customer service issues a written response citing warranty terms only, makes no reference to his independent Australian Consumer Law rights, and offers no refund, replacement, or compensation for the period the vehicle was unusable.
**What the Owner does:** Clearly explain that the Australian Consumer Law provides consumer guarantee rights that exist independently of and override the manufacturer's warranty, and that after multiple failed repair attempts for a major failure the consumer is entitled by law to a full refund or replacement vehicle. Connect him to the ACCC and the relevant state fair trading office.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Three engine replacements that have all failed to fix the same problem is exactly the situation Australian Consumer Law was designed for. Your right to a refund or replacement is a separate legal right from what the manufacturer's warranty says — and Mazda cannot remove it."
- Direct to: ACCC complaint portal at accc.gov.au/consumers/consumer-rights-guarantees; Consumer Affairs Victoria at consumer.vic.gov.au (1300 558 181); Victorian Civil and Administrative Tribunal (VCAT) for a binding order; a consumer law solicitor for advice on out-of-pocket transport costs and consequential losses
- Evidence to collect: Original purchase contract and invoice; complete workshop records from every dealer visit across all three engine replacements (request formally in writing); all written correspondence with Mazda Australia national customer service including today's refusal letter; a timeline of all fault recurrences with dates and vehicle odometer readings; all receipts for alternative transport, hire vehicles, or other out-of-pocket costs during the repair periods
- Feedback category tag: Consumer Guarantee Refusal / Faulty Repair / Escalation Loop

### Scenario 15-8: Dealer Confirmed in Writing the Diesel Met Australian Standards — It Did Not (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** ACCC v. Volkswagen AG, Federal Court of Australia — A$125 million penalty December 2019, highest total penalty ever imposed under Australian Consumer Law at the time; Full Federal Court appeal dismissed April 2021; High Court special leave refused; ACCC documented dealers made misleading representations to consumers about compliance with Australian diesel emissions standards; separate class action representing approximately 100,000 VW, Skoda, and Audi owners settled for approximately A$127 million (approximately A$1,400 per vehicle) in 2019
**The Problem:** A consumer in Sydney purchased a new Volkswagen Tiguan TDI diesel in 2014. At the point of sale, the dealer provided written documentation confirming the vehicle met all applicable Australian emissions standards. After the September 2015 global VW emissions disclosure, the consumer learned the vehicle contained defeat device software producing substantially higher NOx in real-world driving than in regulated test conditions. VW has offered a software update, which the dealer describes as "approved by Australian regulators." She has not received any financial compensation and does not know whether the software fix changes the vehicle's performance or fuel economy.
**What staff/org would do:** VW dealer confirms the fix was submitted to and approved by regulators, declines to provide written documentation of what the update modifies, and tells her no further compensation is available beyond the fix itself.
**What the Owner does:** Explain her rights clearly — the dealer's written representation about emissions compliance was false, the Federal Court confirmed Volkswagen's conduct was unlawful, and a class action settlement was available to Australian owners. Help her establish whether she can still access any remaining remedy.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You were given a written confirmation that was false. The Federal Court confirmed this and fined Volkswagen A$125 million for it. You were also entitled to participate in the Australian class action settlement. Let me help you understand what options remain."
- Direct to: ACCC at accc.gov.au for a consumer guarantee complaint against Volkswagen AG and the dealership; Maurice Blackburn Lawyers (administered the Australian VW class action) for advice on whether any residual remedy remains available; relevant state fair trading office — NSW Fair Trading at fairtrading.nsw.gov.au (13 32 20); request written documentation from VW Australia of exactly what the software update modifies and any measurable impact on performance or fuel economy
- Evidence to collect: Original purchase contract and the written dealer confirmation of emissions compliance; all correspondence from VW about the software update; any independently measured fuel economy figures before and after the fix; sales brochure or dealer materials specifying the vehicle's emissions rating at point of sale; documentation of any VW communication about the class action and whether she was notified
- Feedback category tag: Emission Fraud / Information Failure / Misleading Dealer Representation

### Scenario 15-9: A$2,800 in Products She Did Not Agree To — First-Time Buyer, Finance Already Lodged (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** ACCC consumer guarantee and dealer add-on enforcement framework; Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) documented pressure selling of add-on insurance in the motor dealer market in Report 470 (2016) and subsequent enforcement actions; consistent with CFPB/NY AG v. Credit Acceptance Corporation (2023) and FTC v. Manchester City Nissan (2024) establishing this as a globally documented dealer practice; Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA) jurisdiction over dealer-sold financial products
**The Problem:** A 22-year-old nursing student in Brisbane, purchasing her first car, financed a used Toyota Corolla through a dealer for A$18,500. After delivery she reviewed her loan contract and found A$2,800 in products she does not recall being explained: a paint protection package (A$1,200), an extended warranty (A$900), and tyre and wheel protection (A$700). When she raised this with the finance manager, he told her she "agreed to them during the finance signing," that they are "already activated," and that they are "non-refundable."
**What staff/org would do:** Finance manager points to her signature on the contract as proof of consent, declines to produce individual product terms and conditions, and tells her that once the contract is lodged with the lender the products cannot be cancelled.
**What the Owner does:** This is a documented predatory practice targeting a financially vulnerable first-time buyer. Explain her rights under Australian Consumer Law regarding products added without genuine informed consent, and the cooling-off provisions that may apply to the financial products under ASIC regulation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "If these products were added to your contract without being clearly explained before you signed, you have the right to challenge them. A signature obtained in a fast-moving signing session does not necessarily mean genuine informed consent, and Australian law provides specific protections for dealer-sold financial products."
- Direct to: Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA) at afca.org.au (1800 931 678) for a complaint about the finance company and the add-on products — AFCA has jurisdiction over insurance and credit products sold through dealers; Queensland Office of Fair Trading at qld.gov.au/fair-trading (13 74 68) for a dealer licence complaint; ASIC MoneySmart at moneysmart.gov.au/complaints; the lender directly with a written request to cancel the add-on products and cite any applicable cooling-off period under the Insurance Contracts Act 1984
- Evidence to collect: Full signed loan contract with all line items clearly visible; any product disclosure statements given at signing, or a written record that none were provided; a written account of the finance signing session — how long it lasted, how each document was presented, whether she was given time to read; all communications with the dealer and finance manager since delivery; payslips or evidence of income as context for an AFCA complaint about the suitability of the products
- Feedback category tag: Financial Overcharge / Unauthorized Add-ons / Consumer Vulnerability

### Scenario 15-10: EU Consumer Told "Only a Software Fix" — US Buyers Received Thousands in Compensation (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** European Parliament briefing EPRS_BRI(2016)583793 on the Volkswagen emissions scandal; approximately 8.5 million EU vehicles affected; VW global cost estimated at US$33.3 billion by 2020 including fines, penalties, and buyback costs; US consumers received buybacks valued at $12,475–$44,176 per vehicle plus additional restitution; EU consumers received only a free software update with no financial compensation — a two-tier outcome documented by the European Parliament and challenged across EU member state courts
**The Problem:** A consumer in the Netherlands purchased a Volkswagen Golf TDI 2.0 in 2013 for €24,000. After VW's September 2015 admission, she learned her vehicle contained defeat device software. She was offered only a free software update — no buyback option, no financial compensation. She has since read that the software fix in some vehicles negatively affected fuel economy and engine responsiveness. She contacted VW Netherlands customer service, who confirmed the fix is complete and told her "no further compensation is available." She wants to know why US buyers received thousands of euros in compensation while she received nothing, and whether any legal route remains open to her.
**What staff/org would do:** VW Netherlands customer service confirms the software update was applied and the vehicle is now compliant, states the matter is closed, and does not explain the legal distinction between EU and US outcomes or mention any ongoing court proceedings in the Netherlands.
**What the Owner does:** Validate her frustration — the two-tier outcome between US and EU consumers is documented by the European Parliament and has been challenged in courts across Europe. Explain what legal routes remain, including Dutch consumer organisation support and any active collective proceedings.
