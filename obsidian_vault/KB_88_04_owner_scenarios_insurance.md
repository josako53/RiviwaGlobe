### Scenario 4-1: Elderly Medicare Patient Denied Rehab Coverage by AI Algorithm While Still in Recovery (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Class action lawsuit against UnitedHealth Group / NaviHealth, U.S. District Court Minnesota, November 2023; CBS News investigation 2023; Becker's Payer Issues 2023–2025; court filings documenting denial rate jump from 10.9% (2020) to 22.7% (2022) after nH Predict algorithm deployment; approximately 90% of denials reversed by federal administrative law judges on appeal.
**The Problem:** An 83-year-old Medicare Advantage patient has been in a skilled nursing rehabilitation facility for 16 days following hip fracture surgery. Her treating physician has certified she requires a minimum of 30 days of rehabilitation to regain the ability to walk independently. Today, her family received a notice that UnitedHealthcare is terminating coverage for her stay effective in 3 days — the insurer's nH Predict algorithm has calculated her "expected" recovery window has elapsed. The physician's appeal has been ignored. Without continued coverage, the facility is preparing to discharge her to a family home where no one can provide clinical care.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue a standard termination of benefits letter citing plan terms. Route family calls to general member services with no direct access to the case managers who applied the algorithm. Tell the family they may submit a formal appeal — a process that takes weeks — without disclosing the expedited appeal right or the 24-hour external review option available for ongoing urgent care under Medicare rules.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms this is a medically urgent situation. Tells the family there is a legal right to an expedited appeal under Medicare Advantage rules — insurers must respond within 24 hours for urgent ongoing care denials. Advises them to demand in writing that the insurer convert this to an expedited review immediately. Tells them to have the physician document in writing why discharge in 3 days poses imminent clinical risk. Notes that federal administrative law judges reverse approximately 90% of these AI-driven rehab denials when appealed, but that under 1% of patients ever actually appeal.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a serious situation and you have legal rights the insurer is obligated to honour. Your family member's physician has certified she needs continued care — that carries real legal weight under Medicare. Here is what you can do right now to stop the discharge clock."
- Direct consumer to: Demand an expedited Medicare appeal immediately in writing — insurers must respond within 24 hours for ongoing urgent care; call 1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227) to request an expedited appeal; contact a local State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP) counsellor for free guidance (shiphelp.org); file a complaint with the U.S. Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) at medicare.gov; ask the facility's social worker to assist — they handle these situations routinely
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Written copy of the termination notice with the stated effective date; the treating physician's written certification of continued need; all prior correspondence with the insurer; the facility's discharge plan; a written log of every call made to the insurer including date, time, hold duration, representative name, and what was said
- Feedback category tag: Claim denial — AI-driven coverage termination overriding physician judgment; Medicare Advantage; expedited appeal rights; elderly patient; rights violation

### Scenario 4-2: Insurer's Physicians Clicked Through 60,000 Claim Denials in One Month Without Reading Individual Files (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** ProPublica and The Capitol Forum investigative report, March 2023 — Cigna PXDX automated batch denial system; U.S. Congressional inquiry launched 2023; class action lawsuit filed California federal court, 2023; 80% of Cigna Medicare Advantage appeals subsequently overturned; one physician documented denying approximately 60,000 claims in a single month, averaging 1.2 seconds per denial.
**The Problem:** A 47-year-old man received a denial letter from Cigna for a cardiac stress test his cardiologist ordered following an abnormal EKG. The letter states a Cigna physician reviewed the claim and determined the test is "not medically necessary." The claim was submitted yesterday and the denial letter arrived today. He has since read the ProPublica investigation revealing that Cigna physicians used an automated batch system to deny tens of thousands of claims per month without opening individual files — spending an average of 1.2 seconds per denial. He wants to know whether his claim was legally reviewed at all, and what rights he has to demand transparency and challenge the decision.
**What staff/org would do:** Defend the denial as medically reviewed per internal protocols. Route the consumer to a standard written appeal process taking 30–60 days. Provide no information about the PXDX system, how the denial was generated, or how much time was spent on his specific case. State only that the decision was made by a licensed physician and is therefore compliant.
**What the Owner does:** Validates the consumer's concern as legitimate and factual — the PXDX investigation is real, on the public record, and has been the subject of Congressional scrutiny and a class action lawsuit. Advises that the consumer has the right to request the specific physician reviewer's name, credentials, and the stated basis for their determination — information the insurer must provide under the ACA and ERISA. Advises filing both a formal internal appeal and a simultaneous complaint with the state insurance commissioner.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: Ask the consumer whether the cardiologist has flagged the withheld test as clinically time-sensitive — if so, flag is_urgent=true; otherwise proceed as priority complaint
- Opening reply: "Your concern is well-founded and based on documented fact. The investigation you are referring to is real, it led to a Congressional inquiry, and it directly affects your right to know how your specific claim was handled. Here is what you can do to challenge this."
- Direct consumer to: File a formal internal appeal in writing citing the cardiologist's referral and requesting disclosure of the reviewer's name, credentials, and the time and basis of their review; submit a complaint to the state Department of Insurance (searchable at naic.org/state_web_map.htm); if employer-sponsored plan, file with the U.S. Department of Labor Employee Benefits Security Administration (EBSA) at 1-866-444-3272; for Medicare Advantage plans, file with CMS at medicare.gov
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Denial letter including date, any reviewer name referenced, and stated reason; cardiologist's referral or order for the test; date the claim was submitted versus date of denial (document the timeline); the plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage; the ProPublica article URL for reference in formal complaints (propublica.org)
- Feedback category tag: Claim denial — algorithmic batch denial without individual physician review; staff misconduct; rights violation; transparency failure; cardiac diagnostic test

### Scenario 4-3: Consumer Spends Four Hours on Hold Over Four Days Just to Confirm Health Insurance Enrollment Before Surgery (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** United Policyholders consumer documentation report "Calling Your Health Insurer? Prepare to Hold" (2023–2024); documented case of Silver Spring, Maryland consumer spending four hours over four days to confirm enrollment; separate documented account of San Diego insurance broker spending up to two hours on hold with Covered California when helping clients resolve coverage questions; industry benchmark of 13-minute average hold time.
**The Problem:** A recently self-employed woman signed up for health insurance through her state marketplace after leaving her employer. Her surgery is scheduled in nine days. She needs to confirm her enrollment is active and her surgeon is in-network before the hospital will proceed. She has called her insurer four times over four days and spent just over four hours on hold. On the first call she was told to call back. On the second she was transferred twice and then disconnected. On the third she was told there was a "processing delay" with no timeline given. On the fourth call today she was finally told there may be a problem with her enrollment. She does not know what the problem is, whether her surgery will be covered, or who can actually resolve this.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue routing to the general member services queue. Offer to "submit a ticket" with no resolution timeline. Decline to transfer to an enrollment specialist directly. Advise checking the online portal, which shows enrollment as pending.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises this as both a practical time-sensitive problem — surgery in 9 days — and a documented structural failure of insurer call center systems. Advises on specific escalation tactics that bypass the standard queue: contacting the state marketplace (not the insurer) directly to verify and resolve enrollment status; requesting a Navigator or Certified Application Counsellor who can intervene on her behalf at no cost; and obtaining a written coverage determination letter from the insurer before the surgery date.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: Flag as time-sensitive with surgery 9 days away — escalate urgency framing accordingly
- Opening reply: "Four calls and four hours on hold just to confirm you are enrolled — that should not be happening, and you should not be navigating this alone with surgery approaching. Here is how to reach people who can actually fix this faster than the standard member services line."
- Direct consumer to: Call the state insurance marketplace help line directly (separate number from the insurer — for ACA plans, 1-800-318-2596 for Healthcare.gov); find a free Navigator or Certified Application Counsellor at findacenter.healthcare.gov who can intervene with the marketplace on your behalf; request in writing (email) that the insurer provide a written coverage determination letter before the surgery date; contact the state Department of Insurance consumer assistance if the insurer continues to be unresponsive
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Screenshots of the online portal showing enrollment status on each date checked; a written log of all four calls with dates, durations, hold times, names of any representatives, and what each person said; the hospital's written pre-surgery coverage requirement; the surgeon's in-network status documentation if available
- Feedback category tag: Call center runaround — four calls, four hours on hold, repeated transfers, one disconnection, no resolution; health insurance enrollment failure; pre-surgery coverage uncertainty; information failure

### Scenario 4-4: Loss Adjuster Causes Further Structural Damage, Homeowner Left Without Running Water and Electrical Hazard for Eleven Months (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** UK Financial Ombudsman Service case records 2024/25; documented complaint against Aviva and appointed loss adjuster Sedgwick (FOS described loss adjuster scope as "deplorably insufficient" and cash settlement as "woefully inadequate"); FOS buildings insurance complaints at 10-year high in Q2 2024/25; FOS upheld 41% of buildings insurance complaints in consumers' favour; Aviva later acknowledged further dialogue before settlement "would have been preferable."
**The Problem:** A homeowner submitted a buildings insurance claim to Aviva nine months ago after discovering rising damp and water ingress in two ground-floor rooms. Aviva appointed Sedgwick as their loss adjuster. During Sedgwick's initial inspection, their contractor broke the external water feed pipe and damaged the damp proof course, creating a new and larger leak. The subsequent cash settlement offer does not include the additional damage caused by Aviva's own loss adjuster and fails to address a serious health and safety hazard identified by the homeowner — water is now in direct contact with electrical wiring in the floor cavity. The homeowner has been without running water since the incident. It has now been eleven months. Aviva's only communication is a letter saying the case is "under review." Calls to Aviva are transferred to Sedgwick; calls to Sedgwick are told to wait for instructions from Aviva.
**What staff/org would do:** Repeat that the case is under review. Route calls between Aviva claims handling and Sedgwick with no single contact owning the problem. Keep the original inadequate settlement offer on the table. Take no action on the electrical hazard.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately identifies the water-near-wiring hazard as a potential risk to life that must be treated as an emergency today, entirely separately from the insurance dispute. Advises the homeowner to get an independent registered electrician to assess and document the hazard urgently and to place Aviva on written notice of a life-safety risk requiring emergency contractor attendance. Advises escalating to the Financial Ombudsman Service without further delay — the internal complaint period has clearly elapsed.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Eleven months without running water is not a delay — it is a failure. And water near electrical wiring is a risk to life that needs to be treated as an emergency today, separate from the insurance dispute. Here is what to do right now for your safety, and in parallel for the insurance complaint."
- Direct consumer to: Emergency (today): contact a registered NICEIC or NAPIT electrician to assess and document the electrical hazard — do not delay this; send Aviva a written notice by email today stating a health and safety risk exists and requesting emergency contractor attendance within 48 hours, keeping a copy. Insurance complaint: file with the Financial Ombudsman Service immediately (financial-ombudsman.org.uk or 0800 023 4567) — the eight-week internal complaint period is clearly expired; also put Sedgwick's conduct causing additional damage on the record in your FOS submission as the insurer is responsible for their appointed loss adjuster
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Photographs and video of all visible damage including the area where water is near wiring; a complete written log of all communications with Aviva and Sedgwick including dates; original claim reference number and date of submission; the original settlement offer in writing; any contractor reports or scope of work documents from Sedgwick; the registered electrician's written hazard assessment; receipts for any costs incurred including alternative accommodation, bottled water, and laundry
- Feedback category tag: Delayed payout — multi-party claim causing 11-month resolution failure; additional damage caused by insurer's own loss adjuster; unresolved electrical and water safety hazard; staff misconduct; vulnerability

### Scenario 4-5: Insurer Refuses Travel Claim Under Blanket COVID Exclusion for Random Government-Mandated Testing — FOS Overturns Decision (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** UK Financial Ombudsman Service published decision DRN-4409621 (provisional decision August 23, 2023) — FOS investigator upheld complaint against Vitality Insurance, finding the COVID-19 "known event" exclusion was "not fair and reasonable" as applied to random government-mandated testing; 4,466 travel insurance complaints received by FOS in 2023/24 — highest level in ten years outside the COVID pandemic; approximately 20% of travel insurance claims rejected 2022–2024 per FCA data.
**The Problem:** A couple booked a family holiday to Southeast Asia in late 2022 and purchased travel insurance. At their destination airport on arrival, their teenage son was randomly selected by local authorities for a mandatory COVID test under government entry protocols still in place at the time. He tested positive. The family incurred significant additional costs — extended hotel stay, rebooking flights, daily meals, and medical consultation — totalling over £3,200. The insurer rejected the entire claim citing a blanket COVID-19 exclusion stating that COVID is a "known event" since March 2020 and therefore excluded from all coverage. When they called the insurer to query the decision, the call center agent told them "no further internal review is available" and directed them to use the formal eight-week written complaint process if they disagreed.
**What staff/org would do:** Maintain that the blanket exclusion applies. Provide no explanation of what specific scenarios the exclusion was designed to cover. Give no information about the Financial Ombudsman Service or the consumer's right to external review. Require the consumer to navigate the full eight-week complaint process before any escalation.
**What the Owner does:** Informs the consumer that the UK FOS has already adjudicated on a virtually identical case — decision DRN-4409621 — and found that a broad COVID "known event" exclusion applied to costs arising from random government-mandated testing was not fair and reasonable, because the exclusion wording did not specifically cover that fact pattern and the consumer had no means to avoid the testing. This gives the consumer a strong and documented basis for an FOS complaint.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: No
- Opening reply: "The insurer's refusal is not the end of this. The UK Financial Ombudsman has already ruled on a case that is almost identical to yours and found in the consumer's favour. You have a strong basis to escalate, and here is exactly what to do and what to say."
- Direct consumer to: File a formal written complaint with the insurer first — this is required before the FOS will accept a case; if not resolved within eight weeks or if the insurer issues a final response you reject, escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service (financial-ombudsman.org.uk or 0800 023 4567, free of charge); reference FOS decision DRN-4409621 explicitly in your FOS submission; contact Citizens Advice (citizensadvice.org.uk) or Which? for free guidance on drafting a strong complaint letter
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Copy of the policy wording including the exact COVID exclusion clause; official government travel advisory confirming the mandatory random testing protocol was in place at the destination at the date of travel; all receipts for extended hotel, flights, meals, and medical costs; the insurer's written rejection letter; a written record of the call center conversation where further review was refused; booking confirmation showing the trip was booked before the testing requirement existed
- Feedback category tag: Small print exclusion — blanket pandemic clause applied to random government-mandated testing; FOS precedent available (DRN-4409621); consumer refused internal escalation by call center; transparency failure; information failure

### Scenario 4-6: Ghost Broker Sells Void Motor Insurance — Consumer Discovered Uninsured When Stopped by Police (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** City of London Police / Action Fraud UK annual data 2024 — 817 ghost broker reports, 24% year-on-year increase over 2023; social media car insurance scam reports nearly doubled (90 to 179 cases), losses £185,369; Aviva reported 22% surge in detected ghost broking cases since 2023; 84% of young drivers affected experienced declined claims, falsified policy details, or police stop while unknowingly uninsured; Sheikh Tanvir Uddin, 22, sentenced to 32 weeks' imprisonment (suspended 12 months) as serial ghost broker, 2024.
**The Problem:** A 20-year-old driver purchased what appeared to be a legitimate car insurance policy through a social media contact presenting as an insurance broker, at roughly half the price quoted on comparison sites. She received a policy document bearing a recognisable insurer's name and a real-looking policy number. Three months later she was stopped by police during a routine check. The Police National Computer showed her vehicle as uninsured. When she called the number on her certificate, the insurer confirmed the policy had been cancelled two weeks after inception — the ghost broker had taken out the policy using falsified personal data to lower the premium, then cancelled it and kept her payment. She received no cancellation notice and had been driving without insurance for over two months. She now faces potential police proceedings for driving without insurance, despite being the victim of a criminal fraud.
**What staff/org would do:** Confirm the policy no longer exists. Direct her to the police and Action Fraud. Decline any liability since the policy was obtained by the broker using falsified data. Provide no practical guidance on her legal exposure or how to regularise her insurance position.
**What the Owner does:** Validates that she is the victim of a known and documented criminal fraud type — Action Fraud recorded 817 cases of this exact pattern in 2024. Tells her that reporting to Action Fraud promptly and preserving all evidence of the social media transaction is important for any police proceedings against her and for any civil claim against the broker if identifiable. Advises her to obtain genuine insurance immediately before driving again.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: Flag as time-sensitive — she must not drive again until a genuine policy is in place; immediate financial and legal risk
- Opening reply: "You are the victim of a criminal fraud. Action Fraud UK recorded 817 cases of exactly this pattern in 2024. What happened to you has a name — ghost broking — there is a specific reporting process for it, and your status as a fraud victim matters for any police proceedings. Here is what to do now."
- Direct consumer to: Report to Action Fraud immediately (actionfraud.police.uk or 0300 123 2040) specifying ghost broking — get a crime reference number; obtain genuine motor insurance today before driving again under any circumstances; contact the Motor Insurers' Bureau (mib.org.uk) if you were involved in any accident while uninsured — they provide limited compensation for uninsured accidents; preserve all social media evidence of the broker; consult a solicitor about any police proceedings — victim-of-fraud status is a recognised mitigating factor and prosecutors have issued guidance on this
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Screenshots of all social media messages, advertisements, and communications with the ghost broker (do not delete anything); the original policy certificate, certificate of motor insurance, and any schedule documents; any receipts or bank transfer records showing your payment; the broker's phone number, social media accounts, and any account names used; the police report reference from the roadside stop; the insurer's confirmation that the policy was cancelled and when
- Feedback category tag: Policy voided without consumer notice — ghost broker criminal fraud; consumer turned away at point of claim; financial fraud; corruption; young driver vulnerability; Action Fraud referral

### Scenario 4-7: Cancer Patient's Chemotherapy Claim Rejected — Drug Not on Insurer Formulary Despite No Alternative Existing (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India) Annual Report 2024-25; BusinessToday India, December 2024 — disallowed and repudiated health insurance claim value rose 19.1% year-on-year to Rs 26,000 crore (approximately USD 3.1 billion) in FY2023-24; 11% of health insurance claims denied outright, 6% pending as of March 2024; 2,15,569 grievances received on IRDAI Bima Bharosa portal in 2023-24.
**The Problem:** A 55-year-old school teacher in Pune is undergoing chemotherapy for stage II breast cancer. Her employer-provided health insurance policy has a stated oncology cover limit of Rs 10 lakh. After her third chemotherapy cycle, the insurer rejected an Rs 85,000 hospitalisation and treatment claim. The stated reason is that the specific chemotherapy drug her oncologist prescribed is not on the insurer's approved formulary. Her oncologist has confirmed in writing that no formulary-approved alternative drug exists for her specific cancer subtype. The insurer's rejection letter names no medical reviewer, provides no clinical justification, and states only that the claim is "not admissible under policy terms." She cannot self-fund further treatment and her oncologist has said delaying the next cycle will affect her prognosis.
**What staff/org would do:** Maintain the formulary exclusion applies. Offer no information about the IRDAI grievance process or Insurance Ombudsman. Decline to connect her with any medical reviewer. Provide no timeline for any reconsideration.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises this as both a clinical necessity case and a regulatory rights case. Informs the consumer that she can file a grievance directly with IRDAI through the Bima Bharosa portal, which carries formal regulatory weight and triggers a mandated response from the insurer. Advises approaching the Insurance Ombudsman for Maharashtra within 30 days of the insurer's final rejection. Notes that IRDAI data shows the regulator is actively tracking the 19.1% rise in rejected claims and has issued guidance on formulary disputes where no alternative drug exists.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Rejecting a chemotherapy claim when your oncologist has confirmed there is no alternative drug is a decision that can and should be challenged through formal regulatory channels — and India's insurance regulator takes cases like this seriously. Here is what to do immediately to protect your treatment."
- Direct consumer to: File a grievance on the IRDAI Bima Bharosa portal immediately (bimabharosa.irdai.gov.in) — this creates a formal regulatory record the insurer must respond to; contact the Insurance Ombudsman for Maharashtra (Mumbai office, 022-22044960) within 30 days of receiving the rejection letter; contact a Consumer Forum under the Consumer Protection Act 2019 if the Ombudsman process is too slow given your treatment timeline; ask your oncologist to prepare a formal letter of medical necessity specifically addressing why no formulary alternative exists for your cancer subtype
- Evidence to collect/preserve: The written rejection letter with date and reference number; the oncologist's letter confirming no formulary-approved alternative drug exists for this cancer subtype; all hospitalisation bills, prescription records, and treatment summaries; the original policy document with the oncology cover terms and any formulary reference; records of any prior approved claims for earlier cycles of the same treatment; a log of any call center interactions with dates and what was told
- Feedback category tag: Claim denial — formulary exclusion applied despite no clinical alternative; oncology; active treatment disruption; financial harm; IRDAI escalation available; consumer refused service

### Scenario 4-8: Health Insurance Pre-Authorization Stuck for Eight Weeks — Six Calls, No Supervisor, No Callback, Surgery Cannot Proceed (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** IRDAI Annual Report 2024-25 — 2,15,569 grievances received on Bima Bharosa portal in 2023-24 (94,843 general insurance); IRDAI's explicit acknowledgment that the Bima Bharosa portal was created because consumers have no effective escalation channel through standard insurer call centers; 6% of health claims pending unresolved as of March 2024 across the sector.
**The Problem:** A 38-year-old IT professional in Bengaluru filed a health insurance pre-authorization request eight weeks ago for a planned orthopaedic surgery to correct a degenerative knee condition affecting his ability to work. The claim shows as "under review" on the insurer's app and has not moved. He has called the insurer's toll-free number six times over the eight weeks. Each call provides a different answer: documents are incomplete despite successful uploads, the case is with the internal medical team, or the case has been transferred to another department. On the sixth call he asked to be transferred to a supervisor and was told no supervisor was available and that he would receive a callback within 48 hours. No callback came. The hospital will not schedule surgery without the pre-authorization in writing.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue routing to the general call center queue. Issue no escalation callback. Accept no ownership of the case across the six interactions. Continue marking the claim "under review" with no resolution date.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms this is a documented systemic failure — IRDAI created the Bima Bharosa portal specifically because insurer call centers routinely fail to resolve legitimate pre-authorization requests and complaints. Guides the consumer to the formal regulatory escalation pathway, which creates a legal obligation for the insurer to respond within IRDAI's mandated timeframe, and to the insurer's Grievance Redressal Officer, which the insurer is required to designate under IRDAI rules.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: Flag as time-sensitive — ongoing delay to medically indicated surgery; escalate accordingly
- Opening reply: "Six calls and eight weeks with no resolution is exactly why India's insurance regulator built a formal complaints portal. Filing there creates a legal obligation for the insurer to respond within a regulated timeframe. Here is how to use it and what else to do in parallel."
- Direct consumer to: File on the IRDAI Bima Bharosa portal immediately (bimabharosa.irdai.gov.in) — this is the primary escalation route for exactly this situation; contact the insurer's Grievance Redressal Officer (GRO) in writing by email — IRDAI requires every insurer to have one and to respond within 15 days; contact the Insurance Ombudsman for Karnataka (Bengaluru office, 080-22210771) within 30 days if the insurer has issued no response; put your complaint to the insurer in writing (email to their complaints address) so you have a timestamped record
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Screenshots of the pre-authorization status on the insurer app for each date checked, showing eight weeks of inaction; a written log of all six calls with dates, duration, representative names if given, and what each said; copies of all documents uploaded to the portal with upload confirmation timestamps; the hospital's written requirement for pre-authorization before scheduling; the original policy document; the orthopaedic surgeon's referral and recommendation for surgery
- Feedback category tag: Call center runaround — six contacts over eight weeks, no supervisor access, no callback, no resolution; pre-authorization delay blocking surgery; health insurance; information failure; IRDAI formal escalation available

### Scenario 4-9: Motor Insurance Claim Unresolved Four Months — No Repairer Assigned, Hire Car Withheld, Delivery Driver Losing Income (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA) Annual Review 2024-25 — 34,231 general insurance complaints received, 17% increase over 2023-24; motor vehicle claim delays are the single largest complaint category at 25% of all general insurance complaints; AFCA described motor delays as "a longstanding pain point for customers"; 45% of general insurance complaints resolved at first AFCA referral stage — indicating systemic internal failure before external escalation; AFCA stated industry needs "to do more to prevent avoidable complaints."
**The Problem:** A self-employed delivery driver in Melbourne lodged a motor vehicle insurance claim four months ago after a not-at-fault collision. The insurer accepted liability in principle within the first two weeks. Since then, no repairer has been assigned. Every two to three weeks he calls the claims line and is told a repairer is "being sourced due to parts and workshop availability." He has not been offered a hire vehicle despite his policy explicitly including a hire vehicle provision. His monthly income has fallen by approximately AUD 2,400 because the vehicle is off the road. Every call is handled by a different claims consultant who starts the conversation from scratch with no apparent record of prior contact.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue advising a repairer is being sourced. Decline to activate the hire vehicle provision until a repairer has formally accepted the job. Route each incoming call to whichever consultant is available, requiring the consumer to re-explain the situation from the beginning each time.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies that four months without a repairer assigned is well outside any reasonable claims handling standard and now constitutes a complaint-worthy failure. Advises that the consumer can file directly with AFCA, which resolved 45% of general insurance complaints at the very first referral stage — often faster than internal processes. Also advises putting the hire vehicle demand in writing immediately to create a documented record and trigger the insurer's obligation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: No life risk, but flag as ongoing financial harm — AUD 2,400 per month income loss
- Opening reply: "Four months without a repairer, and no hire car despite your policy covering it — AFCA deals with this exact situation regularly. Motor claim delays are their single largest complaint category. You have a clear basis to escalate now, and it typically moves faster than waiting for the insurer to act."
- Direct consumer to: Send one final written complaint to the insurer by email giving 10 business days to respond with a repairer assigned and hire vehicle activated — AFCA requires evidence of an insurer complaint attempt before accepting a case; then file immediately with AFCA (afca.org.au or 1800 931 678) — free, decisions are binding on insurers up to AUD 1,085,000; specifically demand in your insurer complaint that the hire vehicle provision be activated from the original claim date; contact the Insurance Council of Australia (insurancecouncil.com.au) to note a systemic complaint about claim handling standards
- Evidence to collect/preserve: Original claim reference number and the written liability acceptance from week two; a complete log of every call made with dates, hold times, names, and what each consultant said; the policy document evidencing the hire vehicle provision; income records documenting the monthly loss — invoices, bank statements, platform earnings records; photographs of the damaged vehicle; any repair estimates obtained independently; evidence of any written or verbal promises made about the repairer timeline
- Feedback category tag: Delayed payout — four-month motor claim with no repairer assigned and hire car withheld; call center failure (no continuity between contacts); financial harm to self-employed consumer; AFCA escalation available

### Scenario 4-10: Life Insurance Claim Denied After Partner's Death — Insurer Voids Policy Citing Eight-Year-Old GP Cholesterol Consultation as Non-Disclosure (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** AFCA Annual Review 2024-25 — life insurance denial of claim complaints rose 40% in 2024-25 over the prior year; general insurance complaints up 17%; AFCA explicitly stated insurers need "to do more to prevent avoidable complaints"; non-disclosure disputes are the primary driver of life insurance denial complaints received by AFCA; AFCA noted high rates of insurer-side errors in post-death underwriting reviews.
**The Problem:** A 44-year-old nurse in Sydney lost her partner to a sudden cardiac event six months ago. He held a life insurance policy of AUD 750,000. They have one dependent child. The insurer conducted a post-death underwriting review and issued a denial letter three months after the death, stating the policy is void because the deceased did not disclose a GP consultation about elevated cholesterol that occurred eight years before the policy was taken out. That consultation resulted only in lifestyle advice — no medication was prescribed and no heart disease diagnosis was made. The insurer's position is that this constitutes material non-disclosure voiding the entire policy. The denial letter offers no settlement and no partial payment. The consumer was not told about her right to escalate to AFCA and was given only the insurer's internal 45-day dispute process.
**What staff/org would do:** Maintain the void determination on the basis of non-disclosure of a material pre-existing condition. Route the consumer to the internal dispute process. Provide no information about AFCA rights, applicable time limits, or whether a proportionate partial settlement is available. Keep the complete void as the standing position.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the extreme difficulty of this situation — grief, a dependent child, and a complete loss of expected financial protection. Advises that non-disclosure disputes are the most commonly challenged category of life insurance denial at AFCA, and that AFCA applies a proportionality test: a GP consultation for cholesterol eight years prior with no prescription and no diagnosis is unlikely to meet the legal threshold of "material non-disclosure" under Australian contract law and the Insurance Contracts Act 1984. Strongly advises filing with AFCA without delay.
