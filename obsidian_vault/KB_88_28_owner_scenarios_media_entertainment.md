## 28. Media / Entertainment

### Scenario 28-1: Tabloid Journalists Hacked Personal Voicemails — Evidence May Still Exist (Pattern C+B)
**Real Basis:** Leveson Inquiry (UK judicial inquiry, 2012); Metropolitan Police Operation Weeting investigation (2011); News International phone hacking — Glenn Mulcaire files documented approximately 4,000 potential victims; UK Parliament Research Briefing SN06368; court records in Mulcaire litigation (Incidents 1 and 2)
**The Problem:** A person discovers that a tabloid newspaper may have accessed their personal voicemails without consent during coverage of a sensitive story about a family member several years ago. The journalist "knew things" that were never made public and could only have come from private communications. They want to know whether their phone was hacked, whether any record exists in the investigator's files, and whether they can pursue compensation or formal remedy.
**What staff/org would do:** The newspaper's internal complaints team asks for written details, then passes the matter to legal, which responds with a standard denial citing "no evidence" of hacking. They do not disclose whether the seized investigator files — which documented approximately 4,000 potential victims — contain the complainant's name. The complaint is closed.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the seriousness immediately. Confirms that the complainant has a recognised right to request disclosure of whether their data appears in the Operation Weeting files — a right established through the Metropolitan Police investigation process. Guides the complainant toward specialist media law representation and the formal escalation pathway through IPSO or civil court. Documents all details and stresses the importance of preserving evidence before it is lost.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (potential ongoing breach; evidence in files may be time-sensitive; criminal acts may still be subject to civil action)
- Opening reply: "What you are describing is a serious potential violation of your right to privacy — and you are not alone. Nearly 4,000 people were identified as possible victims of this practice. There is a documented process for finding out whether you are one of them, and I will walk you through every step."
- Direct to: Hacked Off campaign (hackinginquiry.org) for support and legal referrals; Metropolitan Police via Operation Weeting records; IPSO (ipso.co.uk) formal complaint if a specific article is involved; specialist media law firms with hacking case experience (Bindmans LLP, Mishcon de Reya)
- Evidence to collect: Any original news articles that cited information the complainant never disclosed publicly; call logs and voicemail records from the relevant period; any prior contact from the newspaper or its journalists; records of what was "known" by journalists that could only have come from private communications
- Feedback category tag: Staff Misconduct / Privacy Violation / Media Rights / Regulatory Escalation

### Scenario 28-2: Meta/Facebook Ignores GDPR Data Access Request — Six Weeks Pass, Only Automated Replies (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Irish Data Protection Commission decision against Meta Platforms, May 2023 — €1.2 billion fine for unlawful EU-US personal data transfers (largest GDPR fine in history); Forrester CX Index data showing tech/media platforms rank lowest for complaint resolution responsiveness; DPC enforcement analysis documenting pattern of delayed responses to individual data rights requests (Incident 12)
**The Problem:** A user in the EU submitted a formal Subject Access Request to Meta/Facebook under Article 15 of the GDPR six weeks ago, asking what personal data Meta holds and how it is used for advertising targeting. They have received three automated acknowledgement emails and then silence. They cannot reach a human agent. Their legal right to a response within 30 days has now expired.
**What staff/org would do:** Meta's automated system sends template responses. When the user contacts support chat, the agent confirms the request "is being processed" but cannot give a timeline or escalate to a data protection officer. Phone support does not exist for this category of request. The user is directed back to the online portal they already used. The wait continues.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises immediately that the 30-day legal deadline has been missed, which is itself a GDPR breach independent of the original request. Explains clearly that the user now has the right to escalate directly to their national Data Protection Authority — which can compel Meta to respond and impose additional penalties. Provides a direct escalation pathway and helps the user draft a letter referencing the missed deadline and Meta's existing enforcement history.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (rights violation, but no immediate physical safety risk)
- Opening reply: "Your 30-day legal deadline under GDPR Article 12(3) has passed. Meta is now in breach of EU law — not just of your request, but of a time-bound statutory obligation. You have a clear right to escalate this directly to your national regulator, and they have enforcement powers to compel a response."
- Direct to: National Data Protection Authority for the user's country (full list at edpb.europa.eu); NOYB (noyb.eu) — specialised NGO that accepts individual GDPR complaints at no cost; European Data Protection Board (edpb.europa.eu) for cross-border escalation
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot of original SAR submission with date and time; all automated response emails with timestamps; any chat transcripts from support interactions; proof of identity submitted with the original request; confirmation that 30+ days have elapsed since submission
- Feedback category tag: Call Center Failure / Rights Violation / Data Privacy / Wait and No Resolution / Regulatory Escalation

### Scenario 28-3: Family Subjected to Death Threats After Being Named as "Crisis Actors" in Media Broadcasts (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Alex Jones / Infowars Sandy Hook Elementary School defamation litigation — Texas jury awarded $49.3 million (August 2022); Connecticut jury awarded $965 million (October 2022); court records documenting families forced to relocate multiple times due to harassment; CBS News and CNN coverage of trial proceedings (Incident 6)
**The Problem:** The family of a victim of a mass tragedy have been falsely named in online broadcasts by a media personality claiming the tragedy was "staged" and that they are "crisis actors." Since the broadcasts, they have received death threats by email and social media, strangers have appeared at their home, and they have had to move twice. Law enforcement in their area says the broadcaster operates in another jurisdiction and the case requires federal involvement. They want the content taken down immediately and the broadcaster held accountable.
**What staff/org would do:** The platform hosting the content applies its standard content review process, which takes days to weeks per item. Legal correspondence to the broadcaster's registered company is answered by counsel denying liability and asserting free speech protections. Law enforcement says the threats require federal jurisdiction. The family is left navigating multiple uncoordinated systems simultaneously with no single point of escalation.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an immediate safety emergency. Helps the family escalate simultaneously across platforms for rapid content removal, connects them with press freedom and defamation legal specialists, and begins coordinating incident documentation. Notes that courts have found such broadcasts to be actionable defamation — confirmed in a $965 million verdict — meaning civil litigation is a proven and successful pathway.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (physical safety threat — family has received death threats and strangers have attended their home address)
- Opening reply: "Your safety is the first priority. What is happening to your family has been validated by courts — you are not alone, and there are real remedies available. Let me help you take immediate steps to protect yourselves while building the record you need for accountability."
- Direct to: FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov) for threat reporting; National Center for Victims of Crime (victimsofcrime.org); defamation attorneys via RCFP Legal Defence Hotline (rcfp.org); First Draft (firstdraftnews.org) for media misinformation documentation support; FTC (ftc.gov) for platform accountability where content is monetised
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of all threatening messages with timestamps and sender details; records of all home visits (dates, descriptions, any surveillance footage); copies of every broadcast or post in which the family is named; all police report numbers; records of all platform content removal requests and responses received
- Feedback category tag: Urgent Safety / Defamation / Harassment / Media Misinformation / Emergency

### Scenario 28-4: Television News Channel Broadcast False Fraud Claims — Employee's Job and Contracts Lost (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Fox News v Dominion Voting Systems — $787.5 million settlement (April 2023); Delaware Superior Court summary judgment ruling (31 March 2023) confirming "none of the statements relating to Dominion about the 2020 election are true"; internal Fox communications introduced in evidence showing executives privately knew the claims were false but continued broadcasting them to retain viewers; NPR and CNBC reporting (Incident 5)
**The Problem:** A mid-level employee of an election technology company has been personally named in reports broadcast by a national cable news channel as allegedly complicit in election fraud. The reports are false — the channel's own internal communications, made public through a separate corporate lawsuit, showed that executives knew the claims were baseless while continuing to air them. The employee has lost their job, been dropped by a professional association, and has had two consulting contracts cancelled. They want a formal on-air correction and financial remedy.
**What staff/org would do:** The news channel's legal department responds to a correction request with a standard denial, asserting editorial independence. Internal communications are not disclosed voluntarily — they became public only because separate litigation compelled disclosure. The broadcaster's public-facing complaints mechanism does not cover business loss or individual naming. The employee is told there is nothing more the channel will address.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that this situation falls directly within established defamation law, and that the broadcaster's own public acknowledgement — made in the context of the $787.5 million settlement — constitutes an admission of falsity. Guides the employee toward formal legal action for defamation and financial loss, with the court-confirmed finding of untruth as foundational evidence.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (financial harm is ongoing, but no immediate physical safety risk)
- Opening reply: "What was done to you — being named falsely in broadcasts that a court has since confirmed were untrue — is documented defamation with serious financial consequences. The broadcaster's own settlement included a public acknowledgement of falsity. Courts have already established the factual foundation you need. Here is how to pursue formal remedy."
- Direct to: Media defamation attorneys (RCFP legal referral directory at rcfp.org); FTC (ftc.gov) for complaints about false commercial broadcast claims; RTDNA (Radio Television Digital News Association, rtdna.org) ethics complaint mechanism; local bar association media law committee for practitioner referral
- Evidence to collect: All broadcast clips or transcripts naming the employee with dates; documentation of job termination and contract cancellations with written correspondence citing the broadcasts; financial records showing income before and after the broadcasts; the broadcaster's public statement acknowledging falsity in the settlement; the Delaware Superior Court summary judgment (publicly available)
- Feedback category tag: Financial Harm / Defamation / Transparency Failure / Information Integrity

### Scenario 28-5: Health Misinformation Broadcast Without Challenge — Viewer Acted on False Claims, Complaint Dismissed (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Ofcom enforcement against GB News — 12 breach rulings since March 2023; £100,000 financial penalty imposed (2024); Ofcom upheld breach finding specifically for The Mark Steyn Programme, which included uncontested vaccine claims that generated 422 public complaints; Ofcom.org.uk enforcement decisions (Incident 9)
**The Problem:** A viewer of a UK news channel watched a broadcast in which a presenter made uncontested claims about widely used vaccines being linked to serious undocumented harm — with no challenge, no counter-expert, and no balance. Based on this broadcast, the viewer advised a vulnerable family member not to proceed with a medically recommended vaccination. The family member's condition subsequently deteriorated. When the viewer raised a formal complaint with the channel's viewer relations team, they received a form response stating the programme was "within editorial guidelines" and no further action would be taken. The viewer was given no information about the regulator.
**What staff/org would do:** The broadcaster's viewer relations team sends a template response closing the complaint. The viewer is not informed that Ofcom received 422 complaints about the same category of broadcast and subsequently issued a formal breach finding. No information about Ofcom's role or escalation pathway is provided. The complaint is closed with no remedy offered.
**What the Owner does:** Informs the viewer that Ofcom formally found this type of uncontested health claim on this specific channel to be a breach of the Broadcasting Code — a matter of public record — and that the viewer has the right to escalate directly to Ofcom regardless of the broadcaster's internal response. Also signposts NHS and public health channels for accurate medical information.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (the health impact has occurred; complaint escalation is the current action needed)
- Opening reply: "You were right to be concerned. Ofcom — the UK's independent broadcasting regulator — formally found that this type of content on this channel breached the Broadcasting Code. What happened when you complained is a known failure in how some broadcasters handle viewer concerns. You have a direct route to the regulator that the broadcaster did not tell you about."
- Direct to: Ofcom (ofcom.org.uk/complaints) — file formal complaint directly; NHS (nhs.uk) for accurate vaccination information; Full Fact (fullfact.org) for UK broadcast fact-checking; MHRA (gov.uk/mhra) yellow card scheme for reporting concerns about medical misinformation in regulated media
- Evidence to collect: Date and time of the broadcast; name of the programme and presenter; the written form response received from the broadcaster with date; any medical documentation of the impact on the family member (with appropriate privacy maintained for the family member)
- Feedback category tag: Information Failure / Consumer Turned Away / Broadcast Misinformation / Health Harm / Regulatory Escalation

### Scenario 28-6: Reader Files Discrimination Complaint About Sexist Column — Complaint Closed Without Investigation (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** IPSO ruling, June 2023 — first-ever sexism complaint upheld by IPSO in its nine-year history, against The Sun's Jeremy Clarkson column; 25,100 complaints received — highest volume about a single article in IPSO history; IPSO Annual Report 2023 showing 0.65% of all complaints in 2023 resulted in an uphold; five-year aggregate rate 2018-2022 of 0.56% (Incident 8)
**The Problem:** A woman read a national newspaper column in which a high-profile contributor described wanting a prominent female public figure to be "paraded naked" through streets while people "threw excrement" at her. She found the language threatening and degrading to women generally, and submitted a formal complaint to the press regulator on grounds of sexism and harassment. After several months, she received a letter stating her complaint had not been selected for investigation. No specific reasons were given. She does not know that over 25,000 other people filed similar complaints and that the regulator ultimately upheld a discrimination finding — the first of its kind in its history.
**What staff/org would do:** IPSO's triage process routes the large majority of complaints to a standard closure — only 0.65% of all 2023 complaints were upheld. The woman receives a template closure letter. No individual explanation of why her specific complaint was not selected is provided. She is told she cannot re-submit the same complaint. The process ends there.
**What the Owner does:** Explains the systemic structure of UK press self-regulation and confirms that the IPSO discrimination finding did ultimately uphold the sexism complaint — meaning her concern was valid and vindicated. Signposts alternative routes including civil legal action, organisations that formally brought the upheld complaint, and press reform campaigns for those who want to contribute to systemic change.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (rights violation, but the immediate complaint process has run its course; seeking further remedy)
- Opening reply: "Your complaint had merit — IPSO did find that this column breached the Editors' Code for sexism, a first in its nine-year history. The problem is structural: IPSO investigates fewer than 1% of complaints, which means most valid concerns are closed without individual review. Here is what remains available to you."
- Direct to: Women's Aid (womensaid.org.uk) and Imkaan (imkaan.org.uk) — organisations that brought the formal successful complaint; Hacked Off (hackinginquiry.org) for press reform support; civil legal route for harassment under the Protection from Harassment Act 1997; NUJ (National Union of Journalists, nuj.org.uk) press ethics resources
- Evidence to collect: Original complaint submission with date and reference number; IPSO closure letter; the original column text and publication date; any evidence of personal impact since reading the column (altered behaviour, distress, impact on sense of safety)
- Feedback category tag: Discrimination / Rights Violation / Complaint System Failure / Gender-Based Harm

### Scenario 28-7: Television Programme Broadcast Name, Address, and Face Without Consent — Subject Told No Breach Occurred (Pattern B+A)
**Real Basis:** ACMA (Australian Communications and Media Authority) investigation finding, May 2023 — Nine Network's A Current Affair found in breach of the Commercial Television Industry Code of Practice; broadcast included subject's full name, partial residential address, and unobscured video footage without consent; ACMA ruled disclosure was not in the public interest and identification was not necessary for the story; ACMA.gov.au official investigation report (Incident 11)
**The Problem:** A person discovered that a national television programme broadcast footage that included their full name, part of their home address, and clear unobscured video of their face — taken without their knowledge or consent — during a story they were not aware was being produced about them. They found out from a neighbour. When they contacted the network's viewer complaints line, they were told the programme had been reviewed internally and met editorial standards. They were not told about any external regulator. The footage remains on the network's catch-up streaming platform.
**What staff/org would do:** The network's complaints team conducts an internal review and informs the subject the broadcast met editorial standards. No route of appeal is offered. The subject is not told that an independent regulator exists with authority to investigate and order compliance measures. The footage remains publicly available online.
**What the Owner does:** Informs the subject that ACMA — Australia's communications regulator — found a broadcaster in breach in a factually identical situation and that the subject has the right to file a formal external complaint with ACMA. Helps the subject request urgent interim removal of the online footage while the formal complaint is assessed, and explains the full process clearly.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (the privacy breach is complete; the ongoing risk is the footage remaining accessible online)
- Opening reply: "What this broadcaster did — showing your face, name, and address without your consent — has been found by Australia's communications regulator to be a breach of the broadcasting code in an almost identical situation. You were told there was no breach, but you were not told that an independent regulator can review that finding. Here is how to escalate."
- Direct to: ACMA (acma.gov.au/make-a-complaint) — formal external complaint pathway; eSafety Commissioner (esafety.gov.au) for removal of identifying content from online platforms; Australian Privacy Foundation (privacy.org.au); Free TV Australia (freetv.com.au) — industry body for additional escalation
- Evidence to collect: Record of when and how the broadcast was discovered; screenshot or recording of any online version of the footage with URL and access date; the network's written internal review response with date; any unwanted contact from strangers or other impacts on personal safety since the broadcast
- Feedback category tag: Privacy Breach / Rights Violation / Consumer Turned Away / Regulatory Escalation

### Scenario 28-8: Small Business Named as Fraudulent by Cable News — Broadcaster Refuses Correction for Four Months (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Smartmatic v Newsmax — $40 million settlement, November 2024; Newsmax public acknowledgement that claims made about Smartmatic were false; Smartmatic v Fox News — $2.7 billion suit ongoing (New York appellate court rejected Fox's dismissal motion, January 2025); NBC News and NPR reporting (Incident 7)
**The Problem:** A regional technology supplier that provided polling equipment in a US county has been named by cable news hosts as allegedly linked to election fraud. The claims are false — a broadcaster in the same position later settled for $40 million and publicly acknowledged the claims were untrue. The supplier has lost three government contracts citing "reputational concerns," received harassing emails from members of the public, and has had prospective clients refuse meetings. When they contacted the cable channel directly to request a correction and retraction, they received no response for four months, then a letter from the channel's legal counsel denying all claims and asserting editorial freedom.
**What staff/org would do:** The broadcaster's legal team denies wrongdoing and cites editorial freedom. No correction is broadcast. Contract officers at affected government agencies say they cannot reinstate contracts while adverse media coverage continues. The supplier's own legal counsel advises that defamation litigation is expensive and may take years. No formal complaint mechanism is available that would compel the broadcaster to respond.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that this situation is directly analogous to the Smartmatic case, where a broadcaster falsely named a voting technology company, denied wrongdoing, and ultimately settled for $40 million with a public acknowledgement of falsehood. Guides the supplier toward specialist media defamation counsel and helps them document all financial harm systematically as evidence for potential litigation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (financial harm is ongoing; no immediate physical safety risk)
- Opening reply: "What happened to your business — being falsely named in broadcasts that a broadcaster in an identical position has since been required to publicly acknowledge were untrue — has clear legal precedent and a proven litigation pathway. Here is how to build and move your case."
- Direct to: Media defamation attorneys specialising in business defamation; SPJ (Society of Professional Journalists, spj.org) ethics complaint mechanism; FTC (ftc.gov) for complaints about false commercial broadcast claims causing verifiable financial harm; local bar association media law committee for specialist referral
- Evidence to collect: All broadcast clips or transcripts naming the company, with dates; dated written correspondence from each cancelled or refused contract citing the media coverage; records of all contact attempts to the broadcaster and all responses received; financial documentation showing revenue before and after the broadcasts; the comparable broadcaster's public settlement statement acknowledging falsity
- Feedback category tag: Refusal / Financial Harm / Defamation / Information Failure / Refused Escalation

### Scenario 28-9: Journalist Assaulted by Security Forces During Protest Coverage — No Official Redress Provided (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** CPJ "Attacks on the Press in 2024" (published January 2025) — 56 journalists documented as assaulted or harassed by security forces in Nigeria during #EndBadGovernance protests; CPJ recorded 124 journalists and media workers killed globally in 2024 — the highest annual total since CPJ began systematic tracking in 1992; CPJ "Journalist Jailings Near Record High in 2024" (January 2025) (Incident 13)
**The Problem:** A freelance journalist was covering peaceful urban protests when security forces forcibly detained them, confiscated their recording equipment, and physically assaulted them. They were held for several hours without charge and released without any documentation of the incident. Their organisation has filed a police report, but the local station says the matter is "under review" with no reference number issued. Their press credentials were destroyed. They want the incident formally documented, their equipment returned or compensated, and the officers responsible identified.
**What staff/org would do:** The police station issues no formal incident number for the assault. The journalist's accreditation body says it cannot intervene in law enforcement matters. The government's press office does not respond to the organisation's enquiries. Local media organisations are reluctant to report on security force conduct out of fear of similar retaliation. The journalist has no formal record of what happened.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an urgent case requiring immediate international documentation and escalation. Notes that CPJ operates a 24-hour rapid-response mechanism for journalist assaults, and that formal documentation with international press freedom bodies creates a record that deters further targeting and supports future accountability. Helps the journalist initiate parallel escalations immediately.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (journalist may still be at risk; equipment confiscated; no formal record of assault; perpetrators unidentified and in positions of ongoing authority)
- Opening reply: "What happened to you is a serious, documented violation of your rights as a journalist — one that international press freedom bodies are mandated to respond to urgently. Getting this formally recorded with international organisations right now creates accountability pressure and protects you from further targeting. I will help you take every step."
- Direct to: CPJ Emergency Response (cpj.org/emergency-assistance) — 24-hour rapid response for journalists in danger; RSF Reporters Without Borders journalist safety helpline (rsf.org); Article 19 West Africa office (article19.org/regional/africa) for regional press freedom advocacy; Nigerian Press Council (presscouncil.gov.ng) formal complaint; Amnesty International Nigeria (amnesty.org/en/countries/africa/nigeria); UN Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Expression (ohchr.org)
- Evidence to collect: Medical documentation of any injuries sustained; any photos or video captured before equipment was confiscated; names and contact details of any witnesses; the original police report submission with reference number if issued; all communications with the police station and government press office; records of equipment serial numbers for compensation claim
- Feedback category tag: Urgent Safety / Journalist Safety / State Corruption / Staff Misconduct / Press Freedom / Emergency

### Scenario 28-10: Family of Imprisoned Journalist Cannot Obtain Any Information — Six Months With No Response (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** CPJ "Journalist Jailings Near Record High in 2024" (January 2025) — China identified as the world's leading jailer of journalists globally; 361 journalists imprisoned as of December 1, 2024; CPJ documentation that imprisoned journalists are routinely denied family contact, consular access, and legal representation in countries heading the jailing list; CPJ "Attacks on the Press in 2024" (Incident 13)
**The Problem:** The elderly parents of a journalist who was detained and imprisoned for their reporting have received no official information about their family member's condition, location within the prison system, or the specific charges against them. All domestic channels — the local journalists' association, the official press council, and the court registry — have refused to respond or redirected the family without substantive answers. It has been six months. The family cannot afford international legal representation, do not speak English, and do not know how to reach the organisations that handle exactly these cases. They are isolated, frightened, and exhausted.
**What staff/org would do:** Domestic institutional channels either refuse to engage or provide non-committal procedural responses. The state broadcaster issues a brief official statement saying the matter is "under judicial proceedings" and cannot be discussed further. The journalists' association does not respond to the family. Local lawyers decline to take the case. The family is left with no information and no pathway forward.
**What the Owner does:** Provides the family with clear, step-by-step guidance to reach international press freedom organisations that have multilingual capacity and dedicated expertise in exactly this situation. Treats this as an urgent human rights matter involving a vulnerable family. Documents all known details to support advocacy and contact with the organisations best placed to act.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (person is imprisoned, fundamental rights are violated, family has received no information for six months — ongoing harm with no domestic remedy available)
- Opening reply: "Your family member's situation is serious, and you should know that international organisations have helped families in exactly your position before — and they have staff who can communicate in your language. You are not without options. Here is exactly who to contact and what to tell them."
- Direct to: CPJ Emergency Response (cpj.org/emergency-assistance) — accepts contacts in multiple languages and operates a rapid case-opening process; RSF (rsf.org) — operates regionally with local language capacity; Amnesty International Urgent Action Network (amnesty.org/en/get-involved/urgent-action) — publishes urgent actions for imprisoned journalists; PEN International writers-at-risk programme (pen-international.org); UN Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Expression (ohchr.org/en/special-procedures/sr-freedom-of-expression) — accepts individual communications
- Evidence to collect: Full name, press credentials, and employer of the detained journalist; exact date of detention; last known location and any court, facility, or case reference provided; any official communications received from authorities; copies of published reporting that may have preceded the detention; any statements made by authorities to the family
- Feedback category tag: Vulnerability / Urgent Rights Violation / Press Freedom / Wait and No Resolution / Family Advocacy / Journalist Safety
