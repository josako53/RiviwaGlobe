### Scenario 12-1: Phone Caught Fire During Documented Recall — Carrier Refused to Process Claim (Pattern B + E)
**Real Basis:** CPSC official recall notices for Samsung Galaxy Note 7 (September and October 2016); NPR reporting "The Troubled Galaxy Note 7 Leaves Some Samsung Customers Frustrated" (September 2016); 92 US reports of overheating batteries, 26 burn injuries, 55 property damage incidents including fires in cars and a garage; consumers documented being transferred between AT&T, Samsung, and Best Buy across five or more visits with no resolution
**The Problem:** A consumer's Samsung Galaxy Note 7 has caught fire inside their car. The CPSC recall was already in effect. They called AT&T's support line and were transferred three times — to Samsung, back to AT&T, then to Best Buy — before the call dropped. The car interior is damaged. They do not know how to file for property damage compensation and cannot get a straight answer from any party.
**What staff/org would do:** Transfer the caller between the carrier, Samsung, and the retailer indefinitely, each directing them to "the other party." Eventually offer a basic phone exchange form while ignoring property damage to the vehicle entirely, or suggest the consumer file a personal insurance claim.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately confirm the consumer's physical safety, acknowledge this as a documented recall incident with legal remedies specifically covering property damage, and give them a direct path to the CPSC claim process — not the carrier's standard exchange queue.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "First — are you and anyone with you physically safe right now? What happened to your phone is connected to a federal product recall, which means you have a legal right to full compensation — including damage to your car — not just a replacement handset. Let me get you to the right place directly."
- Direct to: CPSC official recall page (cpsc.gov) and CPSC property damage claim process under the recall; Samsung's dedicated recall line (separate from standard consumer support); FTC complaint portal (reportfraud.ftc.gov) if the carrier is actively obstructing a valid recall claim; document the car fire separately if the vehicle was damaged while parked
- Evidence: Photograph the burned phone and car interior damage before any cleanup; preserve the original purchase receipt, box, and IMEI; save all call logs including dates, hold times, and names of every representative spoken to; file a formal CPSC incident report at cpsc.gov/report documenting both injury and property damage separately
- Feedback category tag: Product Safety / Battery Fire / Recall Obstruction

### Scenario 12-2: Authorized Repair Technician Stole Explicit Personal Photos and Posted Them Online (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Apple/Pegatron Technology Services Inc. case — a 21-year-old consumer sent her iPhone to Apple's official subcontractor PTSI at its Elk Grove, California facility for a screen repair (January 2016); technicians employed through a third-party staffing vendor stole explicit personal photos and video and posted them to the victim's Facebook page; Apple reportedly settled for millions of dollars; case only became public in 2019 through unrelated insurance litigation; reported by iFixit and Tom's Guide
**The Problem:** A consumer sent their iPhone to Apple's official repair service for a cracked screen. Three days after getting the phone back, friends are calling to say private photos have appeared on their social media account. The photos were stored only on the device. The consumer has just understood what has happened and is in shock.
**What staff/org would do:** Apple standard support would suggest the social media account was hacked by an external actor, deny any possibility of data access during a screen repair, and offer no escalation beyond a standard tech support ticket — deflecting responsibility entirely to a third-party staffing vendor with no direct consumer contact.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the full severity without minimization, treat this immediately as a potential criminal offence requiring law enforcement involvement, and help the consumer secure evidence before any further harm or deletion occurs.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "I am very sorry this has happened to you. What you are describing is a serious violation — potentially a criminal offence — and you deserve immediate, concrete support. Before anything else: can you take a screenshot of the unauthorized post with the timestamp visible, before removing it? That screenshot is your evidence."
- Direct to: Local law enforcement for a criminal complaint regarding theft and unauthorized distribution of private material; FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov) for digital privacy crimes; Apple's formal legal escalation team in writing — not standard support — citing the PTSI precedent specifically; a consumer rights attorney if seeking civil remedy (the original victim reportedly recovered millions)
- Evidence: Screenshot every unauthorized post before deletion, capturing timestamps; preserve all repair receipts, shipping tracking records, and any Apple device tracking documentation; request in writing from Apple the complete chain of custody for the device and the names of every technician who handled it
- Feedback category tag: Data Privacy / Staff Misconduct / Authorized Repair Breach

### Scenario 12-3: RAM Sold at Advertised Speed Does Not Work at That Speed — Support Deflects to BIOS (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Corsair Gaming $5.5 million class action settlement (preliminary approval July 2, 2025; claim deadline October 28, 2025) — DDR4 and DDR5 memory kits advertised as running at stated speeds without requiring manual XMP or EXPO BIOS profile activation, a configuration step not disclosed at point of sale; reported by Tom's Hardware and ClassAction.org; consumer forum records document Corsair support directing consumers to BIOS tutorials rather than acknowledging the misrepresentation
**The Problem:** A consumer purchased a Corsair DDR5 memory kit labelled "6000MHz" for a new gaming build. After installation, the system runs at 4800MHz — the JEDEC default — because XMP/EXPO profiles must be manually enabled in the BIOS, a fact not mentioned in advertising or packaging. Corsair customer support told them to "enable XMP in your BIOS" and closed the ticket. When the consumer enabled XMP, the system became unstable and crashed. The support case is now closed and the consumer is being asked to pay for return shipping.
**What staff/org would do:** Direct the consumer to BIOS configuration guides, suggest their motherboard may be incompatible, decline any refund on the grounds that "the product physically functions," and close the ticket.
**What the Owner does:** Tell the consumer this is a documented and settled marketing misrepresentation affecting their exact product, explain their right to participate in the class action, and identify their parallel path to a retailer refund under "not as described."
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening: "This is not a user error — this is a settled legal dispute. Corsair has agreed to a $5.5 million class action settlement covering exactly this issue: DDR4 and DDR5 kits advertised at speeds that only activate after BIOS changes that were never disclosed in the marketing. You may be entitled to compensation, and you have a right to a refund from the retailer regardless."
- Direct to: Corsair class action settlement claim page (via ClassAction.org — claim deadline October 28, 2025); the retailer for a refund under "not as described" consumer rights; FTC complaint portal (reportfraud.ftc.gov) if Corsair continues to deflect
- Evidence: Keep the original packaging showing the advertised speed prominently; take a screenshot of the BIOS screen showing the actual clock speed at boot before any XMP changes are applied; save all Corsair support chat logs and the closed ticket reference number; retain the purchase receipt
- Feedback category tag: Product Misrepresentation / Advertised Performance / Class Action Eligibility

### Scenario 12-4: Repair Shop Accessed Personal Files During a Battery Replacement — History Was Cleared to Cover It (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** University of Guelph study "No Privacy in the Electronics Repair Industry" (ArXiv preprint, November 2022) — 6 of 16 repair technicians (37.5%) accessed customer data without permission during repairs requiring no system access; 2 technicians copied data to external devices; 3 deleted Windows Quick Access and Recently Accessed Files logs in apparent attempts to cover their activity; none of the 18 shops posted any privacy notice; reported by The Register and The Globe and Mail
**The Problem:** A Canadian consumer dropped off their laptop at a local repair shop for a battery replacement — a job that requires no access to the operating system. After getting the laptop back, they noticed Windows Quick Access history had been cleared and found an unfamiliar USB device listed in Windows' device history. Personal documents and financial files were on the machine. No privacy terms or conditions were ever provided by the shop.
**What staff/org would do:** The shop would deny any data access, claim the history was "cleared as a standard part of the service process," provide no explanation for the unknown USB device entry, and offer no further avenue — no privacy policy exists to hold them to.
**What the Owner does:** Validate the consumer's concern as well-founded based on documented research patterns, explain exactly what the signs indicate, and guide them to preserve evidence and report to the appropriate authority before logs are overwritten.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening: "Your concern is completely founded. A peer-reviewed Canadian study found that 37.5% of repair technicians accessed customer data without permission during jobs that required no system access — and deliberately deleting Windows access history was one of the documented methods used to cover those tracks. What you have described matches this pattern exactly. Here is what to do before any remaining logs are lost."
- Direct to: Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre (antifraudcentre.ca) to report the incident; Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (priv.gc.ca) for a formal privacy complaint against the repair business; local police if financial data was on the machine
- Evidence: Before touching the laptop further, open Windows Event Viewer and check the System log for USB device connection events with timestamps; take a screenshot of the Quick Access history (or its absence) and the device manager's USB device history; keep the repair receipt showing the work order was a battery replacement only — it establishes no data access should have occurred
- Feedback category tag: Data Privacy / Repair Shop Unauthorized Access / Evidence Preservation

### Scenario 12-5: New Phone Developed a Display Fault After Four Days — Authorized Service Centre Refused Replacement (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Delhi District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission-I, Case CC/282/2024 — Samsung Galaxy A35 purchased March 22, 2024; horizontal line appeared across display March 25 (three days later); Samsung authorized service centre refused DOA replacement citing a missing IMEI sticker despite the IMEI being clearly visible on the phone's own screen (dial *#06#) and printed on the original box; Commission ruled this constituted unfair trade practice and deficiency in service; ordered full refund of ₹30,999 plus ₹25,000 in compensation and legal expenses (ruling October 2025, cited by IndiaLaw.in)
**The Problem:** A consumer in India bought a new Samsung smartphone four days ago. A horizontal line now runs across the display — a clear manufacturing defect. They visited the Samsung authorized service centre and asked for a replacement under the Dead on Arrival (DOA) policy. The representative refused, citing a missing IMEI sticker on the back of the handset — even though the IMEI is visible on the phone's own screen and printed on the original box. The consumer is being sent away with a defective phone and no remedy.
**What staff/org would do:** Insist that the missing sticker voids the DOA claim, offer a paid repair as the only alternative, and give no written documentation of the refusal — only a verbal denial with no further escalation path offered.
**What the Owner does:** Tell the consumer that this exact refusal has already been ruled an unfair trade practice by the Delhi Consumer Commission, give them the precise documentation to collect before leaving the service centre, and direct them to file.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening: "Do not accept this refusal. The Delhi Consumer Commission issued a ruling on a case identical to yours — Samsung A35, display defect within days of purchase, refusal citing a missing IMEI sticker — and found Samsung's position to be an unfair trade practice. The IMEI is verifiable on your own screen and your box. The service centre's refusal is not legally defensible. Here is what to collect right now before you leave."
- Direct to: District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (file at your nearest district forum — you can claim refund, compensation, and legal costs); National Consumer Helpline at 1800-11-4000 (free, open 8am to 8pm) for immediate guidance; consumerhelpline.gov.in for online complaint filing in regional languages
- Evidence: Dial *#06# on the phone to display the IMEI on screen and photograph it; photograph the IMEI on the original box; photograph the display defect clearly under good light; ask the service centre representative to give their refusal in writing — if they decline, note the exact date, time, representative's name, and the words used verbatim before leaving
- Feedback category tag: DOA Refusal / Warranty Rights / Consumer Commission

### Scenario 12-6: Six Weeks of Unfulfilled Callbacks for a Defective Laptop; Different Agent Every Call (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** India National Consumer Helpline (NCH) — monthly complaint registrations rose from 37,062 (2017) to 1,70,585 (2025); inbound calls reached 1,55,138 per month (December 2024); electronics purchased online identified as a major complaint cluster driving this volume; NCH introduced AI-enabled sector-wise grievance analysis in 2024; Government of India Press Information Bureau (PIB) official releases and NCH portal (consumerhelpline.gov.in); the NCH functions as the last-resort escalation channel after corporate call centers fail to resolve
**The Problem:** An Indian consumer purchased a laptop online and received a unit with a completely non-functional keyboard. They filed a complaint with the e-commerce platform six weeks ago. Every time they call the platform's helpline — four calls in six weeks, each with 30 to 45 minutes on hold — they reach a different agent who has no record of previous interactions. Each agent promises a callback within 48 hours that never arrives. The laptop remains unusable and the consumer feels completely stuck.
**What staff/org would do:** Acknowledge the complaint number, promise a callback, log nothing with continuity across agents, and repeat the cycle — while the warranty period quietly counts down to expiry.
**What the Owner does:** Break the loop by providing a formal escalation pathway with legal enforcement consequences, so the company faces consequences for non-response rather than suffering none.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening: "Six weeks of unfulfilled callbacks from different agents with no case continuity is not a coincidence — it is a pattern. You have a right to resolution under India's Consumer Protection Act 2019, and escalating to the National Consumer Helpline gives your complaint legal weight the company cannot simply ignore. Here is the route with actual enforcement power."
- Direct to: National Consumer Helpline 1800-11-4000 (free, 8am to 8pm, available in regional languages); consumerhelpline.gov.in for online complaint filing; e-Daakhil portal (edaakhil.nic.in) to file directly with the Consumer Commission if the company does not respond meaningfully within 30 days of the NCH complaint
- Evidence: Write down the date, call duration, agent name or ID if given, and the exact promise made during every call to date; save all order confirmation emails, delivery records, complaint reference numbers, and any written communication from the platform; photograph the keyboard defect clearly
- Feedback category tag: Call Center Failure / No Resolution / E-Commerce Electronics

### Scenario 12-7: Fitbit Call Center Said Warranty Rights Expired on a Replacement Device — That Statement Is Illegal (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** ACCC v. Fitbit LLC — AU$11 million penalty ordered by the Federal Court of Australia (December 2023); Fitbit admitted that call center staff told 40 consumers their replacement devices carried no warranty rights because the "original purchase date" had passed; 18 further consumers were falsely told refunds required return within 45 days and only from Fitbit's own online store; both sets of statements were false under Australian Consumer Law; consumers incurred expense purchasing new devices because they believed their rights had expired; ACCC official media release and Gadget Guy AU reporting
**The Problem:** An Australian consumer's Fitbit replacement device — originally issued because their first device was defective — has now also failed. They called Fitbit customer service and were told: "Your device does not meet the requirements for a replacement based on the original purchase date." The representative stated this confidently and with finality. This statement is false under Australian Consumer Law. The consumer now believes they have no rights remaining and is about to purchase a new device at full price.
**What staff/org would do:** Repeat the original-purchase-date policy, offer a discounted upgrade as "a goodwill gesture," and close the case — the script is designed to end with a denial, and the majority of consumers accept it as authoritative.
**What the Owner does:** Tell the consumer directly and clearly that the statement they were given is both false and has been penalised by law, explain their actual statutory rights, and provide the direct ACCC enforcement path.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening: "What the Fitbit representative told you is false — and it is not a misunderstanding. Fitbit was ordered to pay AU$11 million by the Australian Federal Court for telling consumers exactly what you were just told. Under Australian Consumer Law, your consumer guarantee rights apply to each replacement device from the date it was supplied to you — not from the date you bought the original. You do not need to buy a new device."
- Direct to: ACCC consumer complaints portal (accc.gov.au/consumers/consumer-rights-and-guarantees); your state Fair Trading office (e.g. NSW Fair Trading, Consumer Affairs Victoria) for a formal complaint with state-level enforcement power; if Fitbit continues to refuse, a consumer rights attorney can pursue the matter citing the Federal Court precedent
- Evidence: Write down the exact words the representative used and the time of the call; if a follow-up email was sent, save it; keep all documentation for both the original device and the replacement — purchase receipts, any warranty correspondence, and both devices' serial numbers
- Feedback category tag: Consumer Rights Violation / Call Center False Information / Warranty Denial

### Scenario 12-8: Counterfeit Laptop Charger Overheated and Started to Melt — Marketplace Seller Has Disappeared (Pattern C + B)
**Real Basis:** UK Trading Standards Operation Furlong (2021–2022) — 30,000 counterfeit laptop chargers seized by Redbridge and London Trading Standards, 48 high-risk failure reports, counterfeit fuses found to contain no ballast whatsoever; Electrical Safety First testing — 397 of 400 (99.25%) counterfeit chargers failed UK electrical safety tests; genuine laptop charger contains 60+ safety components vs. approximately 25 in a counterfeit; UK Government publication "Trading Standards Successes 2021–2022" (GOV.UK) and Electrical Safety First charity testing data
**The Problem:** A UK consumer bought a replacement laptop charger from a third-party seller on an online marketplace at a significant discount. Last night the charger became extremely hot, the plastic casing began to melt, and a burning smell filled the room. They unplugged it. This morning they tried to contact the seller for a refund but the marketplace listing has vanished and the seller account is inactive. They are asking whether they can just buy another cheap charger.
**What staff/org would do:** The marketplace platform instructs the consumer to "contact the seller directly" — but the seller account no longer exists. Platform policy offers no expedited resolution for disappeared third-party sellers, and the standard resolution window may not have opened yet.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately treat this as a continuing fire safety risk, advise the consumer not to use any unverified replacement, and direct them to report the product and pursue their refund through the two channels still available to them: the platform's buyer protection scheme and their bank.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "Do not plug in any replacement until you have a genuine charger from an authorised source. A melting charger is a fire and electric shock hazard — UK Trading Standards investigations found counterfeit laptop chargers sold online in London that contained fuses with no safety ballast at all. You need to act on three things right now: your immediate safety, getting your money back, and reporting the product."
- Direct to: Report the product immediately to the Office for Product Safety and Standards (OPSS) at gov.uk/product-safety-concerns; contact the marketplace platform's buyer protection team for a refund (most major platforms have policies covering non-delivery or unsafe goods even when sellers disappear); raise a chargeback with your bank or card provider — Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act applies if paid by credit card over £100; report to Citizens Advice (0808 223 1133) which forwards to Trading Standards; purchase a genuine replacement only from the laptop manufacturer's website or a UK-authorised retailer
- Evidence: Do not dispose of the charger — it is physical evidence; photograph the melted casing and any markings or logos; check for a CE or UKCA mark (counterfeits typically lack these or display poorly reproduced versions); save the order confirmation email and any marketplace seller details before the listing disappears entirely
- Feedback category tag: Counterfeit Electronics / Fire Hazard / Disappeared Marketplace Seller

### Scenario 12-9: Paid for a Genuine Apple Charger at a Shop — Received a Counterfeit; Shop Refuses Any Refund (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Southwark Council Trading Standards prosecution of Safi Electronics and Communications Ltd., Rye Lane, Peckham, London — 631 counterfeit Apple chargers discovered (131 on display, 500 in concealed storage); criminal conviction under the Electrical Equipment (Safety) Regulations 1994 and the Trade Marks Act 1994; Trading Standards UK newsroom (CTSI), 2017; Electrical Safety First — 18 million UK adults (34% of the adult population) reported purchasing counterfeit or substandard electrical goods online or in shops
**The Problem:** A UK consumer purchased what appeared to be a genuine Apple charger from a mobile phone accessories shop in South London. At home, a friend with an authentic charger noticed the Apple logo looked slightly wrong, the cable felt different, and the plug pins were not the right weight. They returned to the shop and asked for a refund. The shopkeeper told them "all sales are final" and insisted the charger is "genuine, just from a different batch." The consumer paid £25 and is being turned away.
**What staff/org would do:** Deny the product is counterfeit, cite a "no returns" policy with confidence, and attempt to end the conversation quickly — relying on the consumer's uncertainty about their legal rights.
**What the Owner does:** Inform the consumer that no "no returns" policy can override the Consumer Rights Act 2015 when goods are not as described, explain that selling a counterfeit as genuine is a criminal matter, and give them the two parallel routes: refund and Trading Standards report.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening: "A shop cannot enforce a 'no returns' policy when the product is not what it was described to be. Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 you have an absolute right to a full refund within 30 days for goods not as described — and selling a counterfeit product as a genuine branded item is a criminal offence under UK trade marks law. You have two routes running simultaneously: your refund and a Trading Standards report."
- Direct to: Citizens Advice consumer helpline (0808 223 1133) — they pass the complaint directly to Trading Standards for enforcement, which can prosecute the retailer; raise a chargeback with your bank or card provider if paid by debit card, or invoke Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act if paid by credit card; report the product to OPSS (gov.uk/product-safety-concerns) as an unsafe counterfeit electrical item
- Evidence: Keep the charger and any packaging — do not return it to the shop; photograph the product alongside an official Apple charger image from apple.com showing the differences; save the receipt; note the exact shop name, address, and the date of purchase and the date and words of the refusal; photograph the shop exterior if you can
- Feedback category tag: Counterfeit Product / Refusal of Refund / Trading Standards

### Scenario 12-10: Phone Battery Purchased at a Market Stall Is Now Swelling — Consumer Does Not Know It Is Counterfeit (Pattern C + B)
**Real Basis:** UK Trading Standards Operation Altham (2021–2022) — Greenwich Trading Standards and the Redbridge Regional Investigation Team seized large quantities of counterfeit phone batteries and unsafe laptop chargers from multiple premises in Woolwich and Ilford (London); products confirmed counterfeit and non-compliant with UK electrical safety standards; prosecution pursued; UK Government publication "Trading Standards Successes 2021–2022" (GOV.UK); Electrical Safety First — 397 of 400 counterfeit batteries and chargers failed UK electrical safety tests; 18 million UK adults purchased counterfeit electrical goods
**The Problem:** A UK consumer bought a replacement phone battery from a market stall in East London for considerably less than the manufacturer's own part. It seemed to work for two weeks. The back of the phone is now being physically pushed apart by a visibly swollen battery. The consumer does not know the battery is counterfeit, does not recognise swelling as a fire hazard, and is asking whether they should buy another cheap battery to swap it out.
**What staff/org would do:** Not applicable — there is no support channel for a market stall vendor. The seller is untraceable, there is no receipt, and no route back to whoever supplied the product exists.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately treat this as a fire and explosion safety emergency, explain clearly what lithium-ion swelling means and why the phone must not be used or charged, and give step-by-step instructions for safe handling and disposal before any harm occurs.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "Stop using the phone immediately and do not charge it. A swelling lithium-ion battery is a fire and explosion risk — this is called thermal runaway, and it can escalate without warning. Do not put it in a pocket, place it near anything flammable, leave it on a soft surface, or put it in general household waste. Here is exactly what to do right now."
- Direct to: If the battery is hot, emitting a smell, or any smoke is visible — call 999 immediately and move away from the device; for safe disposal when it is not actively dangerous, contact your local council's household hazardous waste service or take the device to a Currys, Argos, or council recycling point that accepts lithium batteries; report the market stall seller to Citizens Advice (0808 223 1133) which forwards to Trading Standards; report the unsafe product to OPSS at gov.uk/product-safety-concerns; purchase a genuine replacement battery only from the phone manufacturer's official service centre or an authorised retailer
- Evidence: Photograph the swelling visible on the device without applying any pressure to the battery; save any transaction record from the market stall — a bank payment, text message to the seller, or physical receipt; note the market location, stall description, and approximate date of purchase as specifically as possible for a Trading Standards report
- Feedback category tag: Counterfeit Battery / Thermal Runaway / Fire Safety Emergency
