### Scenario 6-1: Vulnerable Mother with Newborn Has Prepayment Meter Forcibly Installed (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** The Times undercover investigation, January 2023; Ofgem enforcement and British Gas settlement (up to £70 million debt write-off); Bloomberg regulatory report, 2023 — Arvato Financial Solutions agents documented breaking into homes of customers with active vulnerability flags including a mother with a four-week-old baby
**The Problem:** A mother who gave birth four weeks ago is at home with her newborn when a debt collection agent arrives under warrant and forces entry to install a prepayment meter. Her account shows an outstanding debt of £480 but also carries a flag indicating a newborn in the household. She was never offered a payment plan or referred to a hardship fund. She calls her energy supplier in distress — the baby's room is now cold and she cannot afford to top up the meter immediately.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center agent reads the outstanding debt on screen, confirms the meter installation was carried out under a valid warrant, and offers to send a top-up instructions leaflet. The account vulnerability flag is visible but not acted upon. No hardship referral is made.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately flags this as a Priority Services Register (PSR) case requiring emergency intervention. Suspends any further debt action. Arranges an emergency credit top-up under the supplier's hardship provision. Escalates to a vulnerability team specialist within minutes. Opens a formal complaint and documents the PSR flag breach in writing.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a serious situation involving your newborn's safety and welfare. What has happened to you should not have happened — suppliers have a legal duty to protect households with babies under 12 months from forced meter installations. You have rights here and we are going to help you use them right now."
- Direct to: Ofgem consumer helpline (0808 223 1133); Citizens Advice Extra Help Unit for vulnerable consumer escalation; the supplier's dedicated vulnerability team (request by name on every call); Priority Services Register enrolment if not already enrolled
- Evidence to preserve: Photograph the installed meter showing the installation date; request written confirmation of the warrant from the supplier; keep all debt correspondence and call logs noting times and agent names; preserve any maternity or medical records confirming the newborn's age
- Feedback category tag: Vulnerability rights violation / Forced meter installation / Welfare emergency — is_urgent

### Scenario 6-2: Energy Ombudsman Binding Decision Ignored — Customer Still Waiting 14 Months Later (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Ofgem formal compliance engagement with OVO Energy, July 2023; Ofgem press release September 2024; £2.37 million settlement (£378,512 direct compensation plus £2 million to the Energy Industry Voluntary Redress Scheme); 1,395 customers affected with delays up to 18 months; 706 classified as vulnerable
**The Problem:** A customer lodged a complaint with their energy supplier about a billing dispute. After eight weeks without resolution they escalated to the Energy Ombudsman, which ruled in their favour and issued a binding decision requiring a credit of £340 and a formal written apology. That decision was issued 14 months ago. The credit has never appeared. No apology has been received. Every call to the supplier results in being told the case is "being processed" and being transferred to a different team, where the process starts again from the beginning.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center agent acknowledges the Ombudsman reference number, places the customer on hold, and returns to say the case is with the "resolutions team" and will be actioned "within 28 days." This is the third time the customer has been given this answer in six months.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately retrieves the Energy Ombudsman decision document and the compliance deadline. Confirms the decision has not been actioned. Opens a compliance breach case internally. Contacts the Energy Ombudsman's compliance monitoring team directly to report non-implementation. Confirms in writing to the customer what specific actions will occur and by what date.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "You are owed a remedy by a binding legal decision and your supplier is not delivering it. This is a compliance failure, not a process delay, and you have the right to escalate it immediately — both to the Energy Ombudsman's compliance team and to Ofgem directly."
- Direct to: Energy Ombudsman compliance team (0330 440 1624) to report that a binding decision is not being implemented; Ofgem consumer complaints line referencing the OVO enforcement action; Citizens Advice consumer helpline (0808 223 1133) for support preparing the escalation
- Evidence to preserve: Copy of the Energy Ombudsman decision with reference number and compliance deadline; full call log with dates, agent names, and what was said at each call; any written communications from the supplier; bank statements confirming the credit has not arrived
- Feedback category tag: Non-compliance with Ombudsman decision / Call center transfer loop / Complaint delay — vulnerable customer

### Scenario 6-3: Supplier's Call Center Fails to Signpost Ombudsman After Nine Weeks — Back-Bill of £1,200 Unresolved (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** UK Energy Ombudsman Annual Report July 2023–June 2024; 92,938 cases accepted; 58% were billing disputes; only 43% of consumers were correctly signposted to the Energy Ombudsman by their supplier; 77% of cases upheld in the consumer's favour; over £16 million paid to wronged customers in 2023 alone; back-billing disputes totalled 3,218 separate cases
**The Problem:** A customer has been disputing a back-bill for £1,200 — their supplier claims it covers 18 months of estimated rather than actual meter readings and demands immediate payment. The complaint was opened nine weeks ago. The customer has called five times. Each call center agent says the complaint is "still under review." On the fifth call an agent suggests contacting Citizens Advice or Trading Standards. No one has mentioned the Energy Ombudsman or the legal eight-week threshold after which the customer is entitled to independent arbitration.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent reads from a script stating complaints are handled "in the order received" and suggests the customer "be patient." When pressed, the agent offers to submit a callback request. The Energy Ombudsman is not mentioned. No deadlock letter is offered.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms the complaint has been open for nine weeks, exceeding the eight-week legal threshold. Informs the customer of their right to a deadlock letter and their right to escalate to the Energy Ombudsman at no cost. Provides the correct contact details. Does not close the internal complaint.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "After eight weeks your supplier is legally required either to resolve your complaint or to issue you a deadlock letter — and you have the automatic right to take your case to the Energy Ombudsman, a free and independent body. You are already past that threshold. The fact that no agent told you this is itself a failure."
- Direct to: Energy Ombudsman (0330 440 1624; ombudsman-services.org/energy); Ofgem for reporting failure to signpost; Citizens Advice if support is needed preparing the Ombudsman submission
- Evidence to preserve: All call records with dates and agent names; the original back-billing notice with amounts and the period claimed; any meter reading history provided or withheld by the supplier; written confirmation of when the complaint was formally opened
- Feedback category tag: Information failure / Wrong escalation signposting / Back-billing dispute — call center failure

### Scenario 6-4: Thames Water Refuses Meter Re-Read Dispute Escalation While Sending Debt Collection Letters (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Consumer Council for Water (CCW) Household Complaint Handling Report 2023–2024; meter-related complaints rose 30% in a single year; Thames Water rated "poor" on both CCW performance measures; CCW escalated complaints up 29%, highest in nearly a decade; Ofwat issued largest-ever fine of £122.7 million against Thames Water, 2024
**The Problem:** A Thames Water customer has received a bill for £2,400 based on a meter reading that is triple their previous year's usage with no evidence of a leak. They have called three times over six weeks; each time they are told a meter re-read "has been requested." No re-read has occurred. A debt collection letter has now arrived. The agent tells the customer to pay the bill and "apply for a refund if the re-read shows a discrepancy."
**What staff/org would do:** The agent confirms the bill reflects an accurate reading as of the reading date and that the re-read request is "in the queue" with no date available. The agent cannot suspend debt action. The call ends without any committed timeline or written confirmation.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately suspends debt action pending resolution of the dispute. Commits in writing to a meter re-read within five working days. Informs the customer of their right to escalate to CCW if not resolved within ten working days. Documents all prior contacts on the account.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "You are not obligated to pay a disputed bill of this size while a meter accuracy investigation is outstanding — and your supplier should have suspended debt action the moment you raised the dispute. Let us help you move this to a resolution track with a deadline."
- Direct to: Consumer Council for Water (CCW) — free independent escalation body for water complaints; Ofwat if Thames Water does not respond within the regulatory timeframe; Thames Water's formal written complaints address (different from the billing line)
- Evidence to preserve: Photograph your meter today showing the current reading; request in writing the full meter reading history for the past two years; save all bills, letters, and call logs; note whether any construction or plumbing work occurred near your property
- Feedback category tag: Billing dispute — meter accuracy / Debt threat while dispute active / Escalation refused

### Scenario 6-5: Resident Reports Gas Smell to Utility Emergency Line — Fire Services Are Never Notified (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** NTSB Accident Report PAR1902 — Merrimack Valley gas explosions, September 13, 2018; CBS Boston and WBZ-TV reporting on Columbia Gas call center protocol; Lawrence Fire Chief Brian Moriarty public testimony that fire services were not notified of gas odor calls received by Columbia Gas; NiSource criminal guilty plea and $143 million restitution; Town of Andover and MEMA After Action Report
**The Problem:** A resident in a dense residential neighbourhood smells gas strongly inside her home. She calls the gas company's emergency line. The operator takes her address, advises her to open windows and wait outside, and tells her a technician will be dispatched. She does not call 911 because she believes the utility is handling it. Forty minutes later no technician has arrived. The smell is intensifying and a neighbour's house has begun to smoke.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center operator logs the call as a standard gas odor service request and dispatches a field technician based on normal queue priority. No simultaneous notification to fire or emergency services is initiated. There is no mandatory step in the operator's script to trigger emergency dispatch.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately instructs the caller to call 911 now. Simultaneously triggers an emergency services notification from the call center regardless of whether 911 has been called. Escalates to a supervisor for emergency coordination. Treats this as a life-safety event, not a standard service queue call.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Please call 911 immediately if you have not already done so — this is a live gas emergency and fire services must be notified now, independently of anything the gas company does. Do not re-enter your home under any circumstances."
- Direct to: 911 immediately (parallel to all other steps); National Gas Emergency number; relevant state Public Utility Commission to report the call center protocol failure once the emergency is resolved
- Evidence to preserve: Note the exact time you called the utility and what you were told; after the emergency is resolved, request the utility's dispatch log for your address from that time under applicable state public records law; retain all phone records showing the call time
- Feedback category tag: Gas safety emergency / Call center failed to notify emergency services / is_urgent

### Scenario 6-6: $16,752 Auto-Debited in Four Days During Declared Grid Emergency — Account Overdrawn (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Texas Tribune, NPR, CNN, ABC News, and KTVU FOX 2 coverage of Griddy Energy and Winter Storm Uri, February 2021; Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton lawsuit against Griddy; Griddy bankruptcy filing; ERCOT wholesale price cap at $9,000 per megawatt-hour for approximately two days; individual customer debits documented in class-action filings; 400+ formal complaints to the State of Texas within two weeks
**The Problem:** A Griddy Energy customer in Texas had $16,752 automatically debited from his bank account across four days during Winter Storm Uri, when ERCOT held the wholesale electricity price at the regulatory ceiling. His account is now overdrawn. He called Griddy during the storm and was told there was no mechanism to pause the automatic debit. He called his bank to stop the charges and was told this would trigger overdraft fees on top of amounts already taken. He cannot switch providers because ERCOT suspended all switching during the grid emergency.
**What staff/org would do:** The Griddy call center — overwhelmed with thousands of simultaneous calls — provides recorded messaging or a long hold queue. The agent who eventually answers confirms the charges reflect actual wholesale market rates as disclosed in the customer's contract and cannot authorise a billing suspension or reversal.
**What the Owner does:** Documents every auto-debit amount and date. Confirms the ERCOT emergency declaration dates and the duration of the price cap period. Advises the customer to dispute the charges with their bank under Regulation E and to file an emergency complaint with the Texas Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division, which has an active investigation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "What has happened to your account is the subject of an active Texas Attorney General investigation. You are not alone — hundreds of customers experienced the same automatic debits during this storm. There are specific steps you can take right now to begin recovering these funds."
- Direct to: Texas Attorney General Consumer Protection Division (800-621-0508); Texas Public Utility Commission consumer complaint line; your bank's dispute resolution team citing Regulation E for electronic fund transfer disputes; any active class-action participation — document your case now for future inclusion
- Evidence to preserve: Download and save all bank statements showing each debit with exact dates and amounts; save all Griddy account records or screenshots; document the ERCOT emergency declaration dates and the period prices were held at the cap; keep records of all calls to Griddy including hold times and what agents said
- Feedback category tag: Financial overcharge / Auto-debit during declared emergency / No customer protection mechanism — is_urgent

### Scenario 6-7: Resident Reports Discolored Water for Three Weeks — Told It Is Safe — Children Are Now Ill (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Wikipedia documented Flint water crisis timeline; NRDC Flint report; CDC CASPER case study; NIH PMC peer-reviewed publications; Consumer Notice timeline; 18-month period of official public dismissal April 2014–October 2015; approximately 140,000 individuals affected; at least 12 Legionnaires' disease deaths; more than 1,700 residents filed EPA complaints totalling approximately $722 million
**The Problem:** A mother in a low-income neighbourhood has called the city water department three times over three weeks about water that is visibly brown and smells metallic. A call center operator told her on each occasion that the water "meets all regulatory standards" and is "safe to drink." Her two young children have developed skin rashes and hair loss since the water quality changed. She cannot afford bottled water. She is calling again and being told the same thing.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center operator reads the official regulatory compliance status on screen — no active violations — and closes the call with a reference number. No home inspection is scheduled. No independent testing is offered. The complaint is logged as resolved.
**What the Owner does:** Treats discolored water combined with child health symptoms as a potential water contamination emergency regardless of official compliance status. Advises the family to stop drinking or cooking with tap water immediately. Connects the complainant to a state environmental or public health authority for independent testing. Does not rely on the utility's self-reported compliance data.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Your children's health symptoms combined with a visible and ongoing change in water quality are serious warning signs that must be treated as a health emergency, regardless of what official sources say. Please stop using the tap water for drinking, cooking, or bathing your children immediately while this is investigated by an independent authority."
- Direct to: US EPA Safe Drinking Water Hotline (800-426-4791); State Department of Environmental Quality or Department of Health to file a formal complaint separate from the municipal utility; local pediatrician for immediate blood-lead level testing; legal aid organisations if contamination is confirmed
- Evidence to preserve: Collect a sample of the discolored water in a clean sealed bottle labelled with date and time; photograph the water colour; keep all medical records for children's symptoms with dates of onset; document every call to the city with dates, agent names, and what was communicated; retain any written correspondence received
- Feedback category tag: Water safety — health emergency / Official complaint dismissed / Children at risk — is_urgent

### Scenario 6-8: Gas Utility Customer Transferred Four Times — Billing Dispute Unresolved After Seven Months (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** J.D. Power 2024 U.S. Gas Utility Residential Customer Satisfaction Study; 63,363 online interviews across 86 large gas utility brands; customer care satisfaction fell 26 points in a single year — the largest single-dimension decline in the study's history; billing and payment satisfaction fell 22 points; October 2024
**The Problem:** A residential gas customer has been trying to resolve a billing dispute for seven months. His bill doubled following a rate adjustment that was applied retroactively across six months of prior consumption, generating an unexpected $1,140 back-bill with no prior notification. He has called four times. Each call results in a transfer — first to billing, then to rate adjustments, then to customer accounts, then back to billing. No one has ever sent him a written summary of the dispute and no case has been formally opened in his name.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center agent apologises, confirms the back-bill is "under review," and transfers the customer to the rate adjustments department. The rate adjustments line goes to voicemail. No callback is received.
**What the Owner does:** Opens a formal written complaint record, assigns a case reference number, and communicates it to the customer in writing before the call ends. Commits to a single point of contact in the resolutions team. Does not transfer the customer again without first fully briefing the receiving agent on the complete case history.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "Seven months without a written summary of your dispute or a single point of contact is a systemic failure, not an oversight. You have the right to a formal written response and to escalate to your state Public Utility Commission if the company does not resolve this in writing within a defined deadline."
- Direct to: State Public Utility Commission — each US state has one and accepts formal consumer complaints online; state Attorney General Consumer Protection Division if retroactive rate application was not properly disclosed; local consumer legal aid if the amount warrants formal dispute
- Evidence to preserve: Request in writing — email or certified letter — a full billing history for the past 12 to 24 months; save all call records with dates, agent names, and department names; request written confirmation of the rate change effective date and how and when it was communicated to customers; keep all paper bills and statements
- Feedback category tag: Back-billing dispute / Transfer loop — call center failure / No written case management

### Scenario 6-9: Indore Resident Reports Foul Tap Water Twice — Complaint Closed Without Inspection — Cholera Outbreak Follows (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Wikipedia documented Indore water contamination crisis December 2024–January 2025; Down to Earth investigative report; Outlook India coverage; 15 deaths, approximately 270 hospitalised; sewage from a police outpost toilet draining directly into a municipal drinking water pipeline; Comptroller and Auditor General audit had previously identified systemic infrastructure flaws in Indore's water system; authorities described by Down to Earth as having remained "unconcerned" about audit findings
**The Problem:** A resident of Bhagirathpura, Indore, has called the municipal water authority twice in the past two weeks about tap water that is visibly discolored and foul-smelling and is causing stomach cramps in her household. Both calls resulted in the complaint being logged and closed with no inspection. This morning two family members have severe diarrhoea and vomiting. Neighbours are also reporting illness. She is calling the complaint line again but it is engaged.
**What staff/org would do:** The municipal call center operator logs the complaint against the previous reference number, notes "complaint repeated," and schedules a routine inspection for the following week. No escalation to public health authorities is initiated. No boil-water advisory is issued to the neighbourhood.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately escalates to the district health officer. Advises the resident to stop all consumption of tap water. Connects the resident to the district hospital emergency line. Documents this as a potential cluster event requiring immediate multi-household investigation rather than a routine single-address complaint.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "The symptoms you are describing — severe diarrhoea, vomiting, stomach cramps appearing in multiple people — combined with visibly polluted water are signs of a possible waterborne disease outbreak. This is a public health emergency. Please take your family members to hospital immediately and do not use the tap water for any purpose."
- Direct to: District hospital emergency line; District Collector's office for immediate escalation beyond the municipal body; Madhya Pradesh Public Health Engineering Department; National Consumer Helpline India (1800-11-4000); Madhya Pradesh Chief Minister's helpline (181)
- Evidence to preserve: Collect a water sample in a sealed bottle labelled with date and time; photograph the water colour; preserve all medical records and hospital receipts; document every prior complaint call with reference numbers and dates; contact neighbours to document their own symptoms and complaints separately
- Feedback category tag: Water contamination emergency / Prior complaints dismissed / Health crisis — is_urgent

### Scenario 6-10: Ranchi Resident Complains About Sewage-Contaminated Water for Six Weeks — Only Temporary Repair, Problem Returns (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Down to Earth investigative report on contaminated municipal water across Indian cities 2023–2024; 5,500+ people sick and at least 34 dead nationwide over a 12-month period; Ranchi identified with over 300 locations of damaged pipelines exposing drinking water to sewage; Patna's Kankarbagh Housing Colony documented for repeated complaints met only with temporary repairs allowing recurrence; governance described as "reactive, with interventions triggered only after people fall ill"
**The Problem:** A family in Ranchi has reported sewage-contaminated tap water to their ward office six weeks in a row. After the first complaint, a crew arrived and repaired a section of pipe. Within ten days the problem returned. The second and third complaints have produced no response. The family has been buying bottled water at significant personal expense for six weeks and cannot sustain this. Written complaints submitted to the municipal corporation have never been acknowledged.
**What staff/org would do:** The ward office receives the call, logs it, and says a team will be dispatched. The team does not arrive. On a follow-up visit to the ward office, a staff member confirms the complaint is registered and tells the resident to wait for the scheduled inspection cycle with no timeline given.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges that a temporary repair followed by rapid recurrence is a systemic infrastructure problem requiring escalation beyond ward level. Documents the full complaint history with dates. Escalates in writing to the Municipal Commissioner and the Public Health Engineering Department, not only the ward office, and requests a written response within a defined period.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- Opening reply: "A problem that keeps returning after temporary repairs is not a routine service issue — it is a governance failure. Your complaint history across six weeks is now your strongest tool, and you have escalation routes that go well above the ward office."
- Direct to: Municipal Commissioner's office — formal written complaint by registered post with all prior complaint reference numbers; Jharkhand Public Health Engineering Department; National Consumer Helpline India (1800-11-4000); State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission if the municipal body fails to respond within 30 days; local civil society organisations or media if systemic neglect is confirmed
- Evidence to preserve: Keep all written complaint copies with submission dates and ward office stamps or receipts; photograph the tap water each time the problem recurs; retain all bottled water receipts as evidence of costs incurred; document dates the repair crew visited and what work they said was done; obtain medical records if any household member fell ill during this period
- Feedback category tag: Water quality — chronic complaint / Temporary repair and recurring failure / Municipal governance failure
