### Scenario 22-1: Mercury Spilled on Road, Children Already Collected It — No Warning Given (Pattern A+D)
**Real Basis:** IFC CAO published case Peru: Yanacocha-01/Cajamarca (December 2000); Peru Support Group 20-year review (April 2020); Business & Human Rights Resource Centre
**The Problem:** A resident of Choropampa, Peru, is calling from the roadside: a truck contracted by Yanacocha gold mine has spilled liquid mercury along the dirt road through the village. Her children, drawn by the shiny silvery liquid, picked it up by hand. She does not know what the substance is. She has been trying to reach the mine's community relations number for 40 minutes — no one answered. She has now walked to the local community hall to file a complaint. No emergency information about mercury toxicity has been posted by the company. The municipal health post has two nurses and no mercury protocols.
**What staff/org would do:** The mine's community liaison, when eventually reached, asks the caller to "wait for an official statement" and refuses to confirm the substance is mercury. The health post advises her to wash her hands and go home.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately acknowledges the severity. Treats this as a chemical poisoning emergency. Provides mercury safety instructions right now — do not touch, do not sweep, leave the area, ventilate. Confirms that the company has a legal duty to notify health authorities immediately under Peruvian environmental law. Gives the caller the number for the regional health authority (DIRESA Cajamarca) and Peru's emergency environmental response line. Documents the incident for the IFC CAO complaint pathway.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a medical emergency. Please stop touching the substance immediately and move everyone away from the area — especially children. I am treating this as the highest priority."
- Direct to: DIRESA Cajamarca (regional health authority); Peru's National Emergency Operations Centre (COEN); IFC CAO complaints portal (cao-ombudsman.org) for formal company accountability
- Evidence to collect: Photograph the spill location and substance without approaching it; note the vehicle plate number and time; record names of all people who made contact; preserve clothing worn by children
- Feedback category tag: Environmental Emergency / Health Hazard / Company Non-Disclosure

### Scenario 22-2: Children's Blood-Lead Results Sent to the Mining Company Before the Mother Received Them (Pattern B+E)
**Real Basis:** Scientific Reports / PubMed (November 2021); EnviroLink independent review (February 2026); Mongabay (November 2023); provincial health emergency declaration, Cerro de Pasco, Peru 2017
**The Problem:** A mother in Cerro de Pasco has taken her 8-year-old son to the regional health post after noticing he is slower to learn and more fatigued than peers. A blood test was carried out three weeks ago. The health post tells her the results were "sent to the mining company's environmental team for review" before being returned to her — and she still has not received them. When she asks directly what the lead levels showed, she is told: "The company will be in touch." A regional study published in 2021 found 86% of children in this area had blood-lead above WHO safe limits; a 2026 study found lead concentrations 43 times above safe limits in children's hair samples.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue redirecting the mother to the company. The company's community hotline places her on hold for 22 minutes, then advises the results are "under technical analysis." No timeline is given.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that a parent has the legal right to receive their child's medical results directly from the health post — not through the mining company. Explains the documented pattern of lead contamination in Cerro de Pasco. Advises her to demand the results in writing from the health post today under Peru's patient rights law (Ley 29414). Provides the contact for Peru's Ministry of Health patient rights ombudsman (Defensoría del Asegurado) and the Ministry of Environment's complaints portal (OEFA).
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You have every right to receive your son's test results directly — not through the mining company. This is your legal right under Peru's health law, and I want to help you get that information today."
- Direct to: Peru OEFA (environmental enforcement — oefa.gob.pe); Defensoría del Pueblo (human rights ombudsman); local NGO CooperAccion for community legal support
- Evidence to collect: Obtain written confirmation of when blood test was taken and where results were sent; photograph any water sources used at home; document the child's symptoms with dates
- Feedback category tag: Information Suppression / Children's Health / Patient Rights

### Scenario 22-3: Nine Years After the Dam Collapsed, Still No Compensation — Two Transfers, Two Disconnections (Pattern A+D)
**Real Basis:** Al Jazeera (October 2024); Finance Uncovered investigative report (2015–2024); Business & Human Rights Resource Centre; Mariana dam disaster legal proceedings; R$170 billion Supreme Court settlement approved 6 November 2024
**The Problem:** A former resident of Bento Rodrigues village in Mariana, Brazil, whose home was destroyed on 5 November 2015 when the Fundao tailings dam collapsed, has been living in temporary housing for nine years. She registered with the Renova Foundation (the company-established reparations body) in 2016 but has received only small interim payments. When she calls the Renova helpline to ask about the Supreme Court's November 2024 settlement, she is told her case file is "under processing" — the same response she has received for three years. She asks to speak to a case manager; the call is transferred twice and disconnected. She calls back and is placed on hold for 34 minutes before the line drops again.
**What staff/org would do:** The Renova Foundation helpline logs the call as resolved and sends a generic email confirming her file number.
**What the Owner does:** Validates her frustration and immediately documents the specific call failures — two transfers, disconnection, 34-minute hold, second disconnection — as a formal service failure. Confirms the R$170 billion agreement was approved by Brazil's Supreme Federal Court on 6 November 2024 and provides the specific reference. Advises her to contact the Federal Public Ministry (Ministerio Publico Federal), which oversees settlement implementation, and to contact Conectas Direitos Humanos, who have represented Mariana survivors in international forums.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Nine years is an unacceptable wait for justice. What happened on that call — two disconnections after being transferred — is a documented service failure, and I am recording it now."
- Direct to: Ministerio Publico Federal (mpf.mp.br); Conectas Direitos Humanos (conectas.org); Brazil's reparacao online portal; Inter-American Commission on Human Rights petition pathway
- Evidence to collect: Save all Renova Foundation case reference numbers; document every call with date, time, duration, and outcome; keep all written communications received; preserve proof of property loss from 2015
- Feedback category tag: Call Center Failure / Compensation Delay / Corporate Accountability

### Scenario 22-4: Silicosis Claim Rejected — Mine Doctor Said Lungs Were Fine After 25 Years Underground (Pattern A+C+E)
**Real Basis:** ACTSA (Action for Southern Africa) mineworker case documentation; Johannesburg High Court approval of R5 billion Tshiamiso Trust settlement (July 2019); GroundUp investigative reporting; Tandfonline academic paper (2021); South Africa ODIMWA statutory scheme — only 1.5% of eligible claimants paid
**The Problem:** A 58-year-old former gold mineworker from the Eastern Cape, South Africa, who spent 25 years underground at an AngloGold Ashanti shaft, has a confirmed silicosis diagnosis from a private pulmonologist. When he presented this diagnosis to the company's occupational health clinic, the company doctor told him his lung scarring was "pre-existing" and "not caused by mine dust." He was directed to the ODIMWA statutory compensation process, which rejected his claim on grounds of insufficient service records. He is now coughing blood and cannot work. He has heard about the Tshiamiso Trust but does not know how to register or whether he qualifies.
**What staff/org would do:** The Tshiamiso Trust helpline advises him to obtain a new medical certificate from a designated doctor at his own cost and to submit original employment records, which the mining company says are "unavailable."
**What the Owner does:** Recognises this as both a rights violation and a documented case of medical misrepresentation — the company doctor's "pre-existing" finding is consistent with the pattern of systematic denial found across 30+ mining companies in the class action. Confirms he qualifies for Tshiamiso Trust registration even without the company's employment records, as alternative proof of employment is accepted. Provides the direct Tshiamiso Trust registration contact and advises him to engage the Legal Resources Centre, which provided pro bono representation to class action claimants.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What the company doctor told you — that your lungs were fine — is exactly the kind of misrepresentation that led to a R5 billion court-approved settlement. You have rights, and I can help you use them."
- Direct to: Tshiamiso Trust (tshiamisotrust.com, free call 0800 023 772); Legal Resources Centre (lrc.org.za); South African Human Rights Commission (sahrc.org.za); National Institute for Occupational Health (NIOH) for independent re-assessment
- Evidence to collect: Preserve private pulmonologist's report; write down the company doctor's verbal finding with date, clinic name, and doctor name; gather any pay slips, union records, or co-worker statements confirming employment
- Feedback category tag: Medical Misrepresentation / Consumer Turned Away / Occupational Rights

### Scenario 22-5: Cyanide in the River, Three Hours of Unanswered Calls, Company Never Warned the Village (Pattern A+B)
**Real Basis:** Earthworks regulatory release (Newmont Ahafo mine cyanide spill, October 2009); Ghana Ministerial Panel finding on delayed community notification; Business & Human Rights Resource Centre / WACAM statement; Ghanavoicereport.com River Tano follow-up
**The Problem:** A farmer in a village downstream of Newmont's Ahafo mine in Brong-Ahafo Region, Ghana, found dead fish along a 3-kilometre stretch of river this morning. His family drinks from this river daily; his wife and two children bathed in it yesterday. He has been trying to call Newmont's community line for three hours — the number rings out with no answer. He then called the district environmental health office; they told him to "wait for the mining company to make a statement." He is asking whether the water is safe.
**What staff/org would do:** The mining company's community relations line continues to go unanswered. The district office advises no action until a company statement is issued. No emergency advisory is posted.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an immediate public health emergency. Instructs the family not to use the river water for any purpose — drinking, bathing, or irrigation — until testing confirms safety. Documents the three-hour unanswered call as a critical notification failure, consistent with what the Ghana Ministerial Panel cited as a specific regulatory violation in 2009. Directs him to the Ghana Environmental Protection Authority emergency line and the Minerals Commission, both of which have authority to order Newmont to disclose the substance and extent of any spill immediately.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Do not use the river water for anything right now — not for drinking, bathing, or crops. Dead fish are a serious warning sign and I am treating this as an emergency. The company's failure to answer your call for three hours is itself a documented violation."
- Direct to: Ghana EPA emergency line (+233 302 664 697); Ghana Minerals Commission; WACAM (Wassa Association of Communities Affected by Mining — wacam.org); Ghana Health Service district officer for human exposure assessment
- Evidence to collect: Photograph the dead fish and river condition with GPS timestamp; collect a water sample in a sealed container; document all unanswered call attempts with time logs; record the names of everyone who had contact with the water and when
- Feedback category tag: Environmental Emergency / Delayed Notification / Call Center Failure

### Scenario 22-6: Regulator Cited the Same Violation Four Times — Company Still Has No Permanent Fix, 6 Million at Risk (Pattern C+E)
**Real Basis:** DeSmog (July 2020); Chile SMA (Superintendencia del Medio Ambiente) formal charges against Anglo American Los Bronces; War on Want (2025); Chile Directorate General of Waters findings (copper, manganese, arsenic in Mapocho River, 2025); Fundacion Glaciares Chilenos
**The Problem:** A municipal water engineer in a commune east of Santiago has submitted four formal environmental complaints to the SMA over 18 months regarding the Los Bronces copper mine's acid drainage contaminating the upper Mapocho River. The SMA's own published statement says the company "has not implemented a definitive solution — it constitutes a repetition of acts previously sanctioned." She has now received the SMA's latest response letter, which acknowledges the breach but grants the company a further 180-day compliance timeline. She is asking what remedies exist when a regulator identifies repeated violations but enforcement does not stop the conduct. The Maipo River Basin — under threat from the same mine — supplies drinking water to approximately 6 million people in Greater Santiago.
**What staff/org would do:** The SMA refers her back to the formal objection process and advises that the 180-day extension is "standard procedure" for complex technical remediation.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that the SMA's own documented language — "repetition of acts previously sanctioned" — constitutes an on-record finding of regulatory failure that is admissible in escalated proceedings. Explains that Chile's Environmental Courts (Tribunales Ambientales) provide an independent pathway to challenge inadequate SMA enforcement. Notes the 6 million people whose drinking water supply is at risk creates grounds for urgency classification before the court.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The SMA's own words — 'repetition of acts previously sanctioned' — are a formal finding that works in your favour. You are not starting from zero. You have a regulatory record that supports escalation to an independent court."
- Direct to: Segundo Tribunal Ambiental (environmental court, Santiago — tribunalambiental.cl); Contraloria General de la Republica (government auditor — can investigate regulatory non-enforcement); Fundacion Glaciares Chilenos; Chile's Defensoria del Pueblo
- Evidence to collect: Obtain certified copies of all four SMA complaint responses; document the 180-day extension decision in writing; compile SMA's "repetition" language from official correspondence; request independent water quality test from a SEREMI-certified laboratory
- Feedback category tag: Regulatory Non-Enforcement / Environmental Transparency / Repeat Violations

### Scenario 22-7: Company Told Villagers the Eviction Was Government-Ordered — No Written Order Exists (Pattern B+C+D)
**Real Basis:** Amnesty International report "Powering Change or Business as Usual?" (September 2023); Al Jazeera (September 2023); Business & Human Rights Resource Centre; Amnesty Canada; field research Kolwezi, Lualaba Province, DRC — 130 interviews across 6 mining projects
**The Problem:** A community elder from a village near Kolwezi, DRC, reports that mining company representatives arrived last week and told residents they must vacate their homes and farmland within 15 days because "the government has ordered the land for the mine." The elder asked for written proof of the government order; none was provided. Residents who asked questions were told they would "lose any compensation" if they refused to leave immediately. Several families have already relocated. The elder believes this may not be a real government order and wants to verify and understand her community's rights before more families move.
**What staff/org would do:** Local government officials give vague responses and decline to confirm or deny whether any order exists. The company's local representative refuses to provide documentation. There is no accessible company grievance mechanism.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies this as a documented company tactic: Amnesty International's 2023 field research across six Kolwezi projects found companies explicitly told communities evictions were government-mandated to prevent communities seeking redress directly. Advises that any forced eviction without a verified government order, a Free Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) process, and a written compensation agreement violates DRC law and the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights. Urges the elder to pause further relocations until the order is verified in writing, and provides contact for the Initiative pour la Bonne Gouvernance et les Droits Humains (IBGDH).
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "This is a recognised tactic. Amnesty International documented exactly this — companies claiming evictions are government orders — across six projects in your region last year. You have rights before any eviction can lawfully proceed, and I can help you use them."
- Direct to: IBGDH (Initiative pour la Bonne Gouvernance et les Droits Humains, Kinshasa); UN OHCHR DRC country office (ohchr.org/en/countries/drc); Global Legal Action Network; IFC CAO if any IFC-linked financing is involved
- Evidence to collect: Request the government order in writing and do not sign any document without it; record names of company representatives who visited the village; document which families have already left and when; photograph all homes and land before any demolition begins

### Scenario 22-8: Herder Lost Winter Pasture, Company Denies the Mine Affected Water Wells (Pattern B+D)
**Real Basis:** IFC CAO published cases Mongolia: Oyu Tolgoi-01/Khanbogd and Oyu Tolgoi-02/Khanbogd (complaint filed 2012); Oxford Academic Community Development Journal (2020); TMU article "How Mongolian herders took on a mining giant"
**The Problem:** A nomadic herder in Omnogovi Province, Mongolia, whose family relocated their winter camp in 2011 following pressure from Oyu Tolgoi mine (Rio Tinto / Turquoise Hill) representatives. The replacement grazing land is 30 km further from water. Three of his animals died during last winter's harsh conditions because the water source they relied on had dried up after the mine's Undai River diversion. When he raised this with the mine's community relations officer, he was told: "There is no scientific evidence the mine affected water levels in your area." He received no written compensation agreement at the time of relocation and has no documentation of what was promised.
**What staff/org would do:** The mine's community liaison says the herder should contact the local government soum office to access the Tripartite Council dispute process. The soum office says it has received no formal notification from the mine about his specific case.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that this denial — "no scientific evidence of impact" — is precisely what the CAO investigation found: the CAO mandated a joint fact-finding exercise because the company had withheld hydrological impact information from affected herders. Advises the herder to file a complaint with the IFC CAO, which oversaw the original 2012 dialogue process and can reopen monitoring, and to contact the Centre for Citizens' Alliance Mongolia (CCA). Explains that the absence of a written compensation agreement is itself a documented violation of the mine's IFC performance standards obligations.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The company telling you there is 'no scientific evidence' of impact is the same denial that triggered a full CAO investigation in 2012. You do not need to accept that answer — there is an international process that can require independent water testing."
- Direct to: IFC CAO (cao-ombudsman.org — complaint reopening or new submission); Centre for Citizens' Alliance Mongolia (ca-mongolia.org); Mongolia's National Human Rights Commission (mn-nhrc.mn)
- Evidence to collect: Document livestock deaths with dates and counts; photograph the dry water source and current pasture condition; gather witness statements from neighbouring herders; locate any maps or verbal agreements made at the time of relocation
- Feedback category tag: Displacement Without Documentation / Information Withheld / Pastoralist Rights

### Scenario 22-9: Acid Spill Destroyed Crops, Company Offered $84 — Take It or Leave It, Then Disconnected (Pattern A+D+E)

**The Problem:** A smallholder farmer from a village near the Mwambashi River, Zambia, whose two-hectare maize and groundnut plot was burned by acidic effluent from the Sino-Metals Leach Zambia tailings dam failure on 18 February 2025. His entire crop — his family's food supply and only cash income — was destroyed. Company representatives visited two weeks later and offered K2,000 (approximately $84 USD) as "full and final settlement," handing him a form to sign. He asked how the figure was calculated; the representative refused to explain. He asked to speak to someone senior; the representative said this was "the company's final decision." When he called the number on the representative's card the next day, the line was disconnected.
**What staff/org would do:** No response. The disconnected line is never restored. Local government officials advise him to "accept what is offered."
**What the Owner does:** Documents this as a take-it-or-leave-it compensation offer without explanation — consistent with PBS reporting that the company "covered up the extent of the spill" and with independent environmental firm Drizit Zambia finding contamination 30 times higher than official estimates. Advises him not to sign any settlement form. Informs him that 176 farmers filed a collective lawsuit seeking $80 billion in damages. Provides contact for the Zambia Environmental Management Agency (ZEMA) formal complaint pathway and for the Law Association of Zambia (LAZ) pro bono legal services.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Do not sign that form. An offer of $84 with no explanation and a disconnected follow-up line is not a fair settlement — it is a tactic to close your claim before you know your rights."
- Direct to: Zambia Environmental Management Agency (ZEMA — zema.org.zm); Law Association of Zambia (LAZ) pro bono desk; Human Rights Watch Zambia contact (hrw.org); the 176-farmer collective lawsuit — contact Zambia Daily Mail or Lusaka Times for referral to legal representatives
- Evidence to collect: Photograph the destroyed crops and affected land; do not sign the K2,000 settlement form; photograph the form itself on both sides; record the representative's name and company details; document the disconnected call with a screenshot of the dialled number and time

### Scenario 22-10: Coal Company Cleared Adivasi Forest Without Gram Sabha Consultation — 45-Day Objection Ignored (Pattern B+C)
**Real Basis:** Amnesty International "When Land is Lost, Do We Eat Coal?" (2016) — Central Coalfields Limited, Tetariakhar mine, Jharkhand; Mongabay India (December 2025); HRW "Out of Control: Mining, Regulatory Failure, and Human Rights in India" (June 2012); Vinoba Bhave University survey (2024); Social Science Journal study (2025)
**The Problem:** An elected gram sabha president of an Adivasi village in Jharkhand, India, near the Tetariakhar coalfield reports that Central Coalfields Limited (CCL) began preparatory operations — clearing trees and installing equipment — on land that includes the village's traditional forest without holding a gram sabha consultation, which is legally required under India's Forest Rights Act (FRA, 2006) and the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act (PESA, 1996) before any such clearance. He submitted a written objection to the district collector 45 days ago. No response has been received. CCL's public relations office told him the land was "notified forest, not village forest" and that consultation was not required.
**What staff/org would do:** The district collector's office logs the objection and takes no further action. CCL's PR office repeats the "notified forest" position without providing any legal basis.
**What the Owner does:** Identifies that CCL's "notified forest" claim does not negate FRA or PESA obligations — both Acts apply where Adivasi communities hold customary rights, regardless of forest classification. Notes that Amnesty International specifically found Central Coalfields Limited excluded Adivasi communities from consultation at Tetariakhar in 2016, making this a documented pattern rather than an isolated event. Advises the gram sabha president to escalate to India's National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) and to file a formal petition with the Ministry of Tribal Affairs. Notes that the National Green Tribunal (NGT) has jurisdiction over FRA and forest clearance violations.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The company's claim that consultation was not required because it is 'notified forest' is legally incorrect. The Forest Rights Act and PESA protect your community's customary rights regardless of forest classification. Your gram sabha had the right to be consulted before any clearance."
- Direct to: India National Human Rights Commission (nhrc.nic.in — online complaint); National Green Tribunal (greentribunal.gov.in); Ministry of Tribal Affairs (tribal.gov.in); Jharkhand Janadhikar Mahasabha (local Adivasi rights network); Mines Minerals and PEOPLE (mmpindia.in) — legal advocacy network
- Evidence to collect: Preserve gram sabha records showing customary land use; retain written proof of the 45-day-old objection submission and any delivery confirmation; photograph the clearance work with date stamps; compile a list of community members who can testify to customary use of the land
- Feedback category tag: Discrimination / Indigenous Rights Violation / Regulatory Non-Compliance
