## 5. Telecommunications

### Scenario 5-1: Unable to Call Emergency Services During National Network Outage (Pattern A+D)
**Real Basis:** ACMA investigation into Optus national network outage, 8 November 2023; Optus pays $8M in fines for Triple Zero failure — LightReading; Wikipedia — 2023 Optus outage
**The Problem:** It is 6am. A customer's elderly parent has collapsed at home. The customer dials Triple Zero (000) from their Optus mobile — the call fails. They try again. It fails again. They have no landline. Their neighbour is on the same carrier. They are unable to reach emergency services because the entire Optus network is down — a nationwide outage that will last 14 hours, affecting over 10 million people. There is no push notification from Optus, no outage notice on the website, and no public guidance on how to reach emergency services from an affected mobile.
**What staff/org would do:** No call center is reachable during the outage. The Optus website shows no outage notice at 6am. Customers who later discover they can use Wi-Fi calling through a different network receive no proactive guidance from Optus during the outage itself.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately treat this as a life-safety emergency. Provide practical alternatives to reach emergency services right now, advise the customer to seek help from anyone nearby on a different carrier, and document this as a critical service failure requiring regulatory notification.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: Acknowledge this is a life-safety emergency first. Provide immediate alternatives — use a landline, go to a neighbour with a different carrier, use any available Wi-Fi calling app on a different network, or ask someone nearby with a working phone to call for help. Every second matters before addressing the complaint.
- Who to direct the consumer to: ACMA (Australian Communications and Media Authority) at acma.gov.au for a formal outage complaint once the emergency is resolved; Telecommunications Industry Ombudsman (TIO) at 1800 062 058 for compensation inquiry; the ACMA-Optus $8M enforcement finding is on the record and supports formal redress
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of failed emergency call attempts with exact timestamps, any Optus app or SMS notifications received during the outage period (or documented absence thereof), neighbour or witness confirmation that the same carrier had no service
- Feedback category: Emergency Access Failure / Network Outage

### Scenario 5-2: Small Business Refused Cash Compensation After Outage Destroyed a Trading Day (Pattern A+D)
**Real Basis:** Optus compensated users $1.2m for lost income from 2023 outage — ITNews; Wikipedia — 2023 Optus outage; ACMA investigation findings
**The Problem:** A small café owner lost an entire trading day because their EFTPOS payment terminals depend on the Optus network to process card payments. On 8 November 2023, the 14-hour outage meant the café could only accept cash — costing an estimated $3,200 in lost card sales. The owner contacts Optus to claim compensation. The response: 200 GB of bonus mobile data. The owner's business does not run on mobile data. They ask for cash compensation to cover documented income loss and are told this is not available — only service credits.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent confirms the outage is acknowledged. They offer the 200 GB data package. When the owner requests cash, the agent reads a script stating Optus's liability is limited to service credits. No escalation to a compensation panel is available from the front line. The customer is told to monitor the website.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the real financial loss and confirm clearly that a data credit is not adequate compensation for documented business income loss. Validate the customer's right to pursue cash redress through formal channels and record the refusal.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Acknowledge the genuine business harm and make clear that bonus data is not a substitute for documented revenue loss. Confirm the customer's right to pursue cash compensation through formal channels and that their refusal experience should itself be on record.
- Who to direct the consumer to: Telecommunications Industry Ombudsman (TIO) at 1800 062 058 — can award up to $1,500 per complaint; for larger documented losses, advise the small business claims tribunal in the relevant state; ACCC (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission) if Optus's compensation terms are found to be misleading under Australian Consumer Law
- Evidence to collect: EFTPOS terminal records showing transaction volume drop on 8 November 2023 compared to comparable trading days, business financial records for that period, written refusal from Optus (email confirmation or transcript of the call), documentation of the data credit offer
- Feedback category: Financial Harm / Compensation Refusal

### Scenario 5-3: Overcharged for Years, Call Center Told Customer the Bill Was Correct (Pattern B+D)
**Real Basis:** Ofcom fines O2 £10.5m for overcharging customers; Ofcom enforcement decision 2021; billing failure ran 2011–2019 affecting 250,000 customers
**The Problem:** A customer reviews their monthly O2 mobile bill and notices a recurring charge they do not recognise — a duplicate line item that appears to have been present for some time. When they call O2's customer service line to dispute it, the agent reviews the account and states: "The bill is correct, all charges are valid." The customer pushes back. The agent repeats the same line and offers to send a bill summary. No mention is made of any systemic billing fault. The customer ends the call unsatisfied but uncertain — perhaps they are wrong.
**What staff/org would do:** The first-line agent has no visibility of the systemic billing error affecting over 250,000 customers. They verify that the charge exists in the billing system and confirm it is legitimate. The complaint is logged as resolved at first contact.
**What the Owner does:** Take the customer's dispute seriously regardless of what the agent says. Flag that a systemic billing error is the documented cause of this type of charge at O2, and that verbal confirmation from an agent is not the end of the matter.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Validate the customer's concern directly. An agent telling a customer their bill is correct does not make it correct — O2's own billing system contained an error that front-line agents had no access to for years. The customer has the right to a formal written explanation and refund review.
- Who to direct the consumer to: Ofcom complaint portal at ofcom.org.uk/complaints if O2 does not resolve within 8 weeks; Ombudsman Services: Communications (free ADR, binding on O2); cite the 2021 Ofcom enforcement decision against O2 for systemic overcharging; request a written itemised explanation of all charges under Consumer Rights Act 2015
- Evidence to collect: Minimum three months of bills showing the disputed charge, date and reference number of the call where the agent confirmed the charge as correct, formal written complaint submitted to O2 with response received
- Feedback category: Financial Overcharge / Billing Error / Agent Misinformation

### Scenario 5-4: Exit Fee Collected on a Contract That Was Legally Unenforceable (Pattern B+D)
**Real Basis:** Ofcom fines BT £2.8m for failing its EE and Plusnet customers; July 2023; 1.1 million customers found to be in void contracts
**The Problem:** A customer who joined EE 18 months ago decides to switch to a cheaper provider and calls to cancel. The agent states that an Early Termination Fee of £187 applies because the customer is still inside their minimum contract term. The customer pays the £187, trusting the agent is correct. What the agent does not know — and what EE never disclosed to the customer — is that EE failed to supply legally required pre-contract information and contract summaries to over one million customers, rendering those contracts unenforceable under Ofcom's General Conditions. The customer has paid an exit fee in a contract that did not legally bind them.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent processes the exit fee payment as routine. No flag is raised about the underlying contract compliance failure. The customer is not told they had the legal right to leave without charge. The charge is collected and the interaction is closed.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately flag this as a rights violation. Inform the customer about the Ofcom enforcement finding and their clear entitlement to a full refund of the exit fee under a contract that was never legally binding.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Acknowledge that paying an exit fee is distressing, particularly when there is a specific regulatory finding that may mean it should never have been charged. Explain that the Ofcom July 2023 enforcement action against EE found over one million contracts were non-compliant and customers had the right to leave without penalty.
- Who to direct the consumer to: Ofcom complaint portal at ofcom.org.uk/complaints — cite the July 2023 BT/EE/Plusnet enforcement decision and request refund of exit fees under Ofcom General Conditions; if EE does not resolve within 8 weeks, escalate to Ombudsman Services: Communications for a binding determination
- Evidence to collect: Contract documents — particularly the absence of a pre-contract information summary and contract summary, which is itself the evidence; payment receipt or bank statement showing the exit fee deduction; any written communication from EE confirming the fee was mandatory
- Feedback category: Rights Violation / Unlawful Exit Fee / Contract Compliance

### Scenario 5-5: Three Agents, Three Different Answers About the Same Bill (Pattern B+C)
**Real Basis:** Ofcom Q4 2024 complaints data — Virgin Media and Three cited with billing and pricing as top complaint category; Communications Ombudsman Q3 2024 rise; Citizens Advice "Hanging on the Telephone" research 2024
**The Problem:** A Virgin Media customer is being charged £12 per month more than the price quoted when they signed up 14 months ago. They call customer service. Agent A says the increase was in the terms they agreed to and is non-negotiable. They call back and reach Agent B, who says this was a mid-contract price rise and offers a £6 monthly goodwill discount. They call a third time to confirm the discount is applied and Agent C says no discount is on the account and the current price is the correct contractual price. Not one agent can see the notes from the previous calls. Not one agent has authority to authorise a billing correction unilaterally.
**What staff/org would do:** Each agent interprets the account independently. None can access what the previous agent discussed or promised. The customer ends each call with a different understanding of their contract and charges. The issue remains unresolved after three separate contacts.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge that receiving three contradictory answers from the same company is a service failure, not a customer misunderstanding. Help the customer build a documented record of each contact and provide a clear route to formal escalation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Acknowledge the exhaustion of calling three times and getting three different answers. Confirm that this is a recognised call center failure — not something the customer misunderstood — and that there is a formal path to get a single definitive written answer.
- Who to direct the consumer to: Ofcom complaint portal after 8 weeks unresolved; Ombudsman Services: Communications (free, binding on Virgin Media) — request a deadlock letter from Virgin Media to trigger ADR eligibility immediately; cite the right to a written explanation of any mid-contract price change under the Electronic Communications (Consumer Contracts) Regulations
- Evidence to collect: Dates, times, and agent reference numbers for all three calls, any SMS or email following any call that referenced a discount or confirmed a price, a copy of the original signed contract or welcome email showing the agreed monthly price
- Feedback category: Call Center Failure / Misinformation / Billing Dispute

### Scenario 5-6: Cancellation Request Refused, Hidden Number, Extended Hold, Billed Again the Following Month (Pattern C+B)
**Real Basis:** FTC action against Vonage — $100 million in refunds to 389,106 consumers; FTC complaint filed November 2022; refunds disbursed October 2023
**The Problem:** A small business owner wants to cancel their Vonage internet phone service. They email Vonage — no response. They use live chat — an agent tells them cancellation must be done by phone and provides no number. They search the website for over 20 minutes before finding a cancellation number buried several pages deep. They call and are told the wait time is 45 minutes. They hang up. The following month, Vonage charges an early termination fee. The month after that, Vonage charges the full monthly subscription again — the account was never cancelled. The customer has now been billed for two months after their initial cancellation attempt.
**What staff/org would do:** Email and chat agents deflect to phone. The phone line is deliberately understaffed with restricted hours. When a customer does reach a retention agent, they face further pressure to stay. No cancellation confirmation is issued. Charges continue.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge that this is a documented pattern of deliberate consumer entrapment — not an isolated mistake. Confirm the customer's rights under FTC enforcement findings and guide them to dispute all charges made after the first cancellation request.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Acknowledge that this matches a pattern the FTC found to be illegal and that the customer has clear grounds to dispute all charges made after their first documented cancellation attempt. This is not a service failure — it is a documented practice the FTC has already acted against.
- Who to direct the consumer to: FTC complaint portal at ftc.gov/complaint; dispute all post-cancellation charges directly with the credit card or bank as unauthorised billing; contact Vonage in writing by certified mail stating the original cancellation date; note that the FTC refund programme distributed funds to 389,106 consumers in October 2023 and any eligible customer who did not receive a refund can contact the FTC administrator
- Evidence to collect: Date-stamped emails and chat transcripts of all cancellation requests, screenshots of the Vonage website showing how the cancellation phone number was buried, bank or card statements showing all charges after the first cancellation request
- Feedback category: Consumer Entrapment / Cancellation Refusal / Call Center Barrier

### Scenario 5-7: SIM Transferred by Agent Despite Active PIN, Cryptocurrency Accounts Drained Overnight (Pattern E+A)
**Real Basis:** T-Mobile hit with $33M arbitration award over SIM swap attack; fraud February 2020; arbitration finalised 2023–2024; SecurityWeek; MobileIDWorld
**The Problem:** A customer woke at 3am to find their phone had lost mobile service entirely. Within 90 minutes, attackers who had persuaded a T-Mobile agent to transfer the customer's phone number to a SIM card they controlled — bypassing an eight-digit PIN the customer had specifically set on the account — used the hijacked number to intercept two-factor authentication codes and drain cryptocurrency accounts worth over $2 million. The customer calls T-Mobile the following morning. The agent confirms a SIM change was processed and that it was "verified successfully." When the customer asks how an eight-digit PIN was bypassed, the agent says they cannot see that information and transfers the call to the fraud team — which has a three-hour hold time.
**What staff/org would do:** The agent confirms the SIM transfer but cannot explain the PIN bypass. No immediate account freeze or number restoration is offered at first contact. The fraud team escalation wait exceeds three hours. T-Mobile's broader response is to deny responsibility and attempt to keep the arbitration award sealed from public view.
**What the Owner does:** Treat this as an active financial crime with additional accounts still potentially at risk. Confirm every immediate step to stop further access and preserve all evidence, before addressing any complaint process.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: Acknowledge this is a serious financial crime. Before anything else, the customer must contact every financial institution linked to the compromised phone number to freeze accounts and disable SMS-based two-factor authentication — additional accounts may still be at risk right now.
- Who to direct the consumer to: All financial institutions immediately to freeze accounts and disable SMS MFA; FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center at ic3.gov; FCC Consumer Complaint Center at consumercomplaints.fcc.gov; FTC at ftc.gov/complaint; T-Mobile arbitration is available under their subscriber agreement — at the scale of financial loss involved, legal counsel is strongly recommended
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot of the phone losing service with exact timestamp, all cryptocurrency exchange transaction records from the night of the attack, T-Mobile account records showing the SIM change timestamp, any prior written confirmation from T-Mobile that the eight-digit PIN was active and required for account changes
- Feedback category: Security Failure / SIM Swap Fraud / Agent Misconduct

### Scenario 5-8: Discovered Carrier Sold Location Data for Years — Call Center Agents Have No Script (Pattern A+E)
**Real Basis:** FCC fines AT&T $57M, T-Mobile $80M, Verizon $47M, Sprint $12M for selling customer location data without consent; FCC enforcement April 2024; Krebs on Security reporting
**The Problem:** A customer reads a news report confirming the FCC has fined all four major US wireless carriers — including their own — for selling precise real-time GPS location data to third-party aggregators for years without obtaining valid customer consent. The customer calls their carrier's customer service line to ask: was their location data sold, to whom, for what purpose, and how can they opt out going forward? The agent who answers has no information about this matter. They do not know what data was shared, who received it, or what the customer can do. They offer to transfer the call. The customer is transferred twice and then disconnected.
**What staff/org would do:** No briefing or script was provided to call center staff about the FCC enforcement action. Customers are transferred between departments and disconnected. No written disclosure of what data was shared is made available to customers. No opt-out mechanism is proactively communicated.
**What the Owner does:** Validate the customer's concern as legitimate and grounded in a real regulatory enforcement action. Provide the formal channels through which the customer can demand disclosure and file a complaint that creates a paper trail.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Acknowledge that the customer's alarm is entirely justified and based on a documented FCC enforcement action. Confirm that they have the right to a formal account of what data was collected and shared, and that the agent's inability to answer is itself part of the failure pattern the FCC identified.
- Who to direct the consumer to: FCC Consumer Complaint Center at consumercomplaints.fcc.gov; submit a formal written data disclosure request to the carrier citing the April 2024 FCC enforcement findings; if based in California, submit a CCPA data access request demanding a list of all third parties the carrier shared location data with; Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) at eff.org for further privacy guidance
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot of the news article with publication date, dates of calls where agents were unable to provide information (document the transfers and disconnection), any prior privacy opt-out settings the customer had configured with the carrier, carrier billing records establishing their account was active during the period of the FCC investigation
- Feedback category: Privacy Violation / Transparency Failure / Data Sold Without Consent

### Scenario 5-9: Unauthorised Charge Persists Three Months, Complaint Never Formally Registered (Pattern C+D)
**Real Basis:** TRAI received 44,734 telecom complaints in 2023–24; Rs 2.69 crore in penalties for Quality of Service violations; TRAI complaint framework documentation; Storyboard18 reporting
**The Problem:** A customer in India notices a recurring charge of Rs 49 per month on their postpaid mobile bill for a "value added service" they never subscribed to. They call the carrier's customer care line. After 22 minutes on hold, the call is answered and then disconnected before they can speak. On the second call, after 18 minutes on hold, an agent gives a verbal assurance the charge will be removed — but does not issue a docket number. The following month, the charge appears again. When the customer calls to follow up, the agent has no record of any previous complaint. This cycle has now repeated three times across three months.
**What staff/org would do:** Each call produces a verbal assurance and no docket number. Without a docket number in the TRAI Telecom Consumer Complaint Monitoring System, the customer cannot formally escalate. The 7-day mandatory resolution window restarts every time. The operator faces no accountability because the complaint is never formally entered into the system.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge the frustration of a complaint loop that resets every month. Explain that being denied a docket number is itself a regulatory failure — not a normal outcome — and provide the bypass path to force formal registration.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Acknowledge this is a documented failure pattern in India's telecom complaint process. A carrier is legally required to issue a docket number and resolve within 7 days — denying one is a regulatory breach, not a normal outcome. The customer has the right to escalate directly.
- Who to direct the consumer to: TRAI TCCMS portal at tccms.trai.gov.in — customers can register complaints directly when carriers fail to issue a docket number within 3 days; TRAI consumer helpline at 1800-110-420 (toll-free); after registering on TCCMS, if unresolved within 10 days the customer can approach the carrier's Appellate Authority, details of which must appear on the carrier's website and billing statements
- Evidence to collect: Bank or UPI statements showing the Rs 49 charge across all affected months, dates and approximate times of all calls made to customer care, screenshot of the bill showing the "value added service" label, any SMS received from the carrier acknowledging a complaint (or documented absence of such SMS)
- Feedback category: Unauthorised Charge / Complaint Access Barrier / Vulnerability

### Scenario 5-10: Paid for Broadband Activation, Technician Never Came, Refund Denied (Pattern C+D)
**Real Basis:** TRAI 2023–24 annual data — non-activation of services documented as a major complaint category; TRAI Quality of Service regulation violations; financial penalties imposed on operators in 2023–24
**The Problem:** A customer in India paid Rs 1,499 for a new broadband connection. The technician visit was scheduled, then cancelled by the operator without notice. A second appointment was made and also did not occur. When the customer calls to request either service activation or a refund, the agent tells them "the order is under processing" and to wait 5 to 7 more business days. Three weeks have now passed since payment. The customer has no broadband, no confirmed activation date, and no refund — and cannot dispute the charge with their bank because the operator has issued no written cancellation or delay notice.
**What staff/org would do:** Each call receives a "processing" response. No supervisor is available. No written confirmation of the delay is issued. A refund request, when submitted, is given a 10 to 15 business day timeline with no written acknowledgment. The agent offers to re-raise a technician visit but cannot commit to a date.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledge that payment without service delivery after three weeks is a consumer protection violation. Confirm the customer's right to either a firm activation date or a full refund within a defined and enforceable period, and provide the escalation path.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: Acknowledge that paying for a service and receiving neither activation nor a refund after three weeks is not acceptable and is actionable. Confirm clearly that the customer has the right to a firm commitment — a date for service or a full refund — and that verbal "processing" assurances are not sufficient.
- Who to direct the consumer to: TRAI TCCMS portal at tccms.trai.gov.in to formally register the non-activation complaint; carrier Appellate Authority (details must appear on the carrier's website under regulatory compliance — mandated by TRAI); National Consumer Helpline at 1800-11-4000 (toll-free) or consumerhelpline.gov.in for disputes involving paid amounts; if no resolution within 30 days, the customer can file at the District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission
- Evidence to collect: Payment receipt or bank/UPI statement confirming Rs 1,499 debit with date, any SMS or email confirming the broadband order and scheduled installation dates, a written record of each call to customer care including dates and any agent names provided, documentation of both failed technician visits
- Feedback category: Service Non-Activation / Refund Denial / Consumer Protection
