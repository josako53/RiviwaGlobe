## 18. Business Consultancy

### Scenario 18-1: Project Deliverable Never Delivered After Full Contract Value Paid (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Court filing, Superior Court of Delaware, Case N19C-04-232 EMD — Hertz Corporation v. Accenture LLP, April 2019; CIO Magazine; Adweek investigative reporting
**The Problem:** A company hired a major consultancy to rebuild its digital platform for an agreed fixed price. The go-live date was missed twice. Mid-project, the consultancy replaced its experienced team with junior staff without disclosing this to the client. When the client's project managers escalated concerns, responses were evasive and misleading about the true state of the code. The contract term has now ended, the platform is still unusable, and the consultancy is demanding additional fees for work the original contract explicitly covered. The client has paid nearly the full contract value and has nothing deployable.
**What staff/org would do:** Direct the client to a generic account manager who insists the disputed features are "out of scope" and offers a change order for additional fees. The partner relationship team closes ranks. Internal escalation produces no result. The client is told disputes must go through the formal legal process only.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the severity immediately — the client has paid substantial funds and received no working product. Asks for the contract scope document and the specific deliverables list. Offers to escalate to senior leadership the same day. Does not push the client toward legal channels as a first response.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: false
- Opening reply: "This is a serious situation — you have paid for a finished product and do not have one, and we understand that is actively harming your business. Let me make sure this reaches the right people today and that your position is fully documented."
- Direct to: Senior partner / client relationship director of the consultancy in writing; if no resolution within 5 business days, refer to the arbitration clause in the contract; USA — consult a commercial litigation attorney specialising in IT delivery disputes; UK — refer to CEDR (Centre for Effective Dispute Resolution, cedr.com) or the Chartered Institute of Arbitrators; Australia — refer to the Australian Centre for International Commercial Arbitration (acica.org.au)
- Evidence to collect: Signed contract and scope of work document with full deliverables list; all milestone communications and change request logs; written confirmation of go-live dates (emails, meeting notes); any internal communications about staff changes made without client disclosure; all invoices paid and payment records; an independent technical audit report documenting code quality and what is and is not functional
- Feedback category tag: Service Quality — Deliverable Not Met / Project Abandonment

### Scenario 18-2: Cold-Call Investment Fraud by Fake Consultancy — Funds Gone (Pattern B+A)
**Real Basis:** FCA press release — conviction of Raymondip Bedi and Patrick Mavanga for operating CCX Capital / Astaria Group LLP fake investment consultancy; sentences of 6 years 6 months and 5 years 4 months handed down 2024; FCA confiscation orders of £305,284; Decrypt investigative report; 65 victims defrauded of £1,541,799
**The Problem:** A consumer received an unsolicited professional-sounding call from a firm calling itself a "regulated investment consultancy." The agent was knowledgeable, confident, and sent polished follow-up documentation. The consumer invested £15,000 based on their advice, received regular "performance update" emails, and had access to what appeared to be an investment portal showing gains. Three months later, all calls go to voicemail, the website is offline, the portal login no longer works, and no funds have been returned. The consumer cannot confirm whether the firm was ever FCA-authorised.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center number is disconnected or rings out — the operation was dismantled after funds were collected. If the consumer contacts their bank to recover funds, the bank's fraud team may initially say the transfer was "authorised" by the consumer and decline to refund automatically.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately identifies the profile as a known investment fraud pattern. Does not wait for more information before advising urgent action. Makes clear that speed is critical — the faster reports are filed, the higher the chance of fund tracing and potential recovery.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: true
- Opening reply: "What you are describing matches a documented investment fraud pattern — a professional-looking cold-calling firm presenting false credentials to steal investor money. This is urgent. Stop all further transfers and take the steps below today."
- Direct to: (UK) FCA ScamSmart register at register.fca.org.uk to verify the firm's authorisation status immediately; Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk or 0300 123 2040 to file a fraud report today; the consumer's own bank fraud team to request a Faster Payments recall and raise a fraud claim under the Contingent Reimbursement Model; Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) at fscs.org.uk if any authorised firm was involved; (USA) SEC Investor Complaint Center at sec.gov/tcr and FINRA at finra.org/investors; (Australia) ASIC ScamWatch at scamwatch.gov.au; (India) SEBI SCORES portal at scores.sebi.gov.in
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of all communications including performance update emails; the firm's name, phone number, website URL, and any registration numbers quoted; the investment portal URL and login page; screenshots of the portal showing supposed account balance; bank transfer records including payee account name, account number, sort code, date, and amount; names of all agents who called; any correspondence sent by post
- Feedback category tag: Financial Fraud — Fake Credentials / Disappeared with Payment / Boiler Room Cold-Call

### Scenario 18-3: Consultancy Advising Government Secretly Helped Clients Evade That Same Government's Laws (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** PwC Australia Tax Scandal — Wikipedia (PwC tax scandal); Australian Treasury Ministers press release (Minister Jim Chalmers, 2023); Senate Economics Legislation Committee findings; International Tax Review; Accountancy Age; Australian Federal Police criminal investigation opened 2023; CEO Tom Seymour removed; 8 partners fired; government consulting division sold for $1
**The Problem:** A business owner discovers through media reporting that their accounting and tax consultancy — the same firm they paid for independent tax compliance advice — was simultaneously advising multinational clients on how to circumvent the very tax laws the firm helped the government design. The business owner is unsure whether: (a) their own advice was compromised by this dual relationship; (b) their confidential tax information was shared internally; (c) the fees they paid were tainted by a conflict the firm was legally obligated to disclose but never did.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue a generic public statement about "taking the matter seriously" and "cooperating with authorities." Existing client communications teams tell affected clients that the matter "does not relate to your engagement" and that their files were managed by separate teams. No proactive notification is sent to clients whose information may have been accessible to the conflicted partners.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges that discovering your adviser had a hidden conflict is distressing and legitimately raises questions about the integrity of past advice. Does not minimise the concern. Offers to help the client document their specific situation and identify the correct reporting and remediation channels.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: false
- Opening reply: "Finding out your adviser had an undisclosed conflict of interest — advising you while simultaneously working against your interests elsewhere — is a serious matter. Your concerns about the integrity of your advice and the confidentiality of your information are completely valid. Let me help you document this and identify your next steps."
- Direct to: (Australia) Tax Practitioners Board (TPB) at taxpractitionersboard.gov.au — regulates tax advisers and can investigate professional conduct breaches; Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) at asic.gov.au if investment advice was involved; the Australian Federal Police if criminal conduct is suspected; the firm's Ethics Partner or Complaints team in writing (not by phone); (UK) Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales (ICAEW) Professional Conduct Department at icaew.com/complaints; (USA) AICPA Ethics Hotline at aicpa.org
- Evidence to collect: All engagement letters and terms of service signed with the firm; copies of all advice received including tax opinions and compliance recommendations; any communications where the firm disclosed (or failed to disclose) its other advisory relationships; invoices paid; any consent or confidentiality agreements signed; media reports and Senate inquiry findings naming the firm
- Feedback category tag: Transparency Failure — Conflict of Interest / Undisclosed Dual Advisory Role

### Scenario 18-4: Whistleblower Alleges Consultancy Billed Government for Hours Never Worked (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** ABC News investigative report cited in Canberra Times, August 2023 — KPMG Australia overbilling allegations on Department of Defence contracts totalling $1.8B over a decade; parliamentary testimony; calls for formal inquiry; Consultancy.com.au reporting; KPMG denied the allegations citing internal checks
**The Problem:** A government contract manager has discovered billing anomalies — the consultancy's invoices claim hours for activities that, cross-referenced with meeting records and deliverable logs, were never performed. When the contract manager attempts to formally flag this internally, they are told the matter is "being handled" and directed away from the department's own procurement complaints process. A senior colleague has told them informally that raising this further could damage the department's relationship with the firm and affect future contract renewals.
**What staff/org would do:** Deflect the concern at middle management level. Describe the billing discrepancy as a "categorisation or rounding matter." Suggest the contract manager raise it directly with the firm's account team — routing the complaint to the very party under accusation. Offer an informal meeting but no written acknowledgement of the concern.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as a potential whistleblower situation requiring confidential and protective handling immediately. Does not route the complaint back to the accused party. Notes that the internal pressure to stay silent is itself a concern that must be documented separately.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: false
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — billing for hours not worked on a government contract — is a serious matter that warrants formal investigation, not a quiet internal conversation. The pressure you are being placed under to stay silent is itself a concern we need to document. Your report will be treated confidentially."
- Direct to: (Australia) Australian National Audit Office (ANAO) at anao.gov.au for government contract fraud concerns; National Anti-Corruption Commission (NACC) at nacc.gov.au for public sector corruption referrals; the department's own Audit and Risk Committee directly, bypassing line management; the Australian Federal Police (AFP) Economic and Cyber Crime Group for suspected fraud; Public Interest Disclosure Act 2013 protections — refer to the Commonwealth Ombudsman at ombudsman.gov.au for advice on how to make a protected disclosure; (UK) Protect (formerly Public Concern at Work) at protect-advice.org.uk for independent confidential whistleblowing advice
- Evidence to collect: Copies of invoices with full line-item billing detail; meeting records, calendar entries, and deliverable logs covering the same billing periods; email records showing what work was actually requested and confirmed as received; any internal messages where the complaint was deflected or discouraged; contemporaneous notes of all conversations about this matter with dates and names of those present
- Feedback category tag: Financial Misconduct — Overbilling Government Contract / Whistleblower Suppression

### Scenario 18-5: Small Business Received No Services from Government-Funded Consultancy That Billed Anyway (Pattern E+A)
**Real Basis:** DOJ District of Minnesota press release — United States v. Tezzaree El-Amin Champion and Marcus Alexander Hamilton; Futuristic Management Group LLC; guilty pleas entered 2024; $3,479,575 restitution order; KARE11 and MPR News investigative reporting; 100+ fraudulent invoices submitted under Hennepin County Elevate Hennepin COVID-19 relief programme
**The Problem:** A small business owner enrolled in a government-funded recovery programme and was assigned a contracted consultancy to provide free bookkeeping support, digital presence assistance, and grant writing — at no cost to the client. Six months later the owner has received two phone calls and no actual work, documents, or guidance. Calls to the consultancy now reach an unanswered line or a full voicemail. The programme administrator, when contacted, says they "cannot interfere in service delivery" and directs the owner back to the consultancy. The owner has since seen local media reporting that the firm is under federal investigation for billing the programme for services never rendered.
**What staff/org would do:** The consultancy's phone goes to a full voicemail or rings indefinitely. The programme administrator says the client must contact the consultancy directly and will not open a formal complaint. The client is trapped in a referral loop between two organisations that each point to the other.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges that the business owner was owed services they never received and that the circular referral they are caught in is unacceptable. Treats their documented experience as evidence that is relevant to the federal investigation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: false
- Opening reply: "Being passed back and forth between a consultancy that is not answering and a programme administrator who will not intervene — while you receive nothing you were promised — is not acceptable. Let me help you put your experience on record with the people who can actually act on it."
- Direct to: (USA) SBA Office of Inspector General at sba.gov/oig — online complaint form for misuse of SBA or government-funded small business assistance; FBI Tips portal at tips.fbi.gov to report the firm if it is already named in a federal investigation; the relevant County or State programme administrator's formal written complaints process (not the phone line — write directly to the programme director); local elected representative's office for casework assistance cutting through the administrative referral loop; (general) the Federal Trade Commission at reportfraud.ftc.gov for fraudulent business practices
- Evidence to collect: Any signed enrolment or service agreement with the consultancy; full log of all calls made including dates, times, and whether they were answered; any written or email communications from the consultancy; a list of all deliverables promised versus what was actually received; any media or court documents naming the firm; records of all contacts with the programme administrator and their responses
- Feedback category tag: Service Non-Delivery — Publicly Funded Programme / Consumer Turned Away Without Resolution

### Scenario 18-6: Financial Adviser Complaint Unresolved After Weeks — Firm's Own Complaint Line Fails Repeatedly (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) Annual Complaints Data 2023/24 — 1,459 new financial adviser complaints received; 61% (892 complaints) related to advice suitability, up from 48% the prior year; FOS upheld 57% of suitability complaints; largest single category was defined benefit pension transfers (368 complaints); Oxford Risk analysis published in IFA Magazine; FCA requires firms to resolve complaints within 8 weeks
**The Problem:** A client received advice from an independent financial adviser to transfer their defined benefit pension into a self-invested personal pension. The transfer has resulted in significant and measurable financial loss. Six weeks ago the client raised a formal complaint directly with the adviser's firm and received an automated email acknowledgement. Since then: three calls to the complaints line resulted in hold times exceeding 40 minutes; one call was dropped after 28 minutes on hold with no callback; one agent promised to call back by end of day but did not; and one agent confirmed the complaint was "still under review" but could not name who was handling it or give any timeline. The FCA 8-week resolution deadline is now two weeks away.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue processing the complaint through a backlogged internal system. Customer-facing agents cannot access the complaint file and can only confirm "it is open." On week 7 a Final Response Letter arrives offering a partial settlement significantly below the actual documented loss, with language designed to appear generous while leaving the majority of the harm uncompensated.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges that the client has done everything correctly, been patient through a broken process, and is now at the regulatory escalation point. Notes the 8-week FCA deadline and makes the next step immediately clear.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: false
- Opening reply: "You have done everything right — you raised this formally and have been patient while the firm's complaint process has repeatedly let you down. There is a free, independent route available at exactly this stage, and it has a strong track record: the Financial Ombudsman Service upholds more than half of advice suitability complaints."
- Direct to: Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) at financial-ombudsman.org.uk — free to consumers, available once 8 weeks have passed from the original complaint or upon receipt of a Final Response Letter; FOS helpline: 0800 023 4567; file a separate complaint with the FCA about the firm's complaint-handling failures at fca.org.uk/consumers/how-complain; Citizens Advice financial section at citizensadvice.org.uk for independent navigation support; if the adviser is no longer trading, check FSCS eligibility at fscs.org.uk
- Evidence to collect: The original financial advice letter and suitability report; all pension transfer paperwork; the firm's automated complaint acknowledgement email with the date it was received; a written log of every call attempt (date, time, hold duration, agent name if given, what was said); any Final Response Letter or settlement offer received; current pension value statements compared to the original defined benefit scheme entitlement; evidence of the financial loss in monetary terms
- Feedback category tag: Call Center Failure — Complaint Not Resolved / Financial Advice Suitability / Regulatory Escalation Required

### Scenario 18-7: Fake Job Consultancy Conducts Multiple Interview Rounds by Phone Then Vanishes with Fees (Pattern A+B)
**Real Basis:** Delhi Police Cyber Cell official advisory — cyber.delhipolice.gov.in/Jobfraudcallcentre.html — formally documenting modus operandi of fake job consultancy firms using call center infrastructure as the primary fraud delivery mechanism; pattern described as ongoing; CVs sourced in bulk from Naukri.com and Shine.com; sequential fee extraction documented
**The Problem:** A job seeker's CV was sourced from a legitimate employment portal. They received a call from what presented as a reputable recruitment consultancy, referencing the CV accurately. After three professional-sounding phone "interview rounds" conducted over two weeks, the seeker was told they had been selected for a position at a named company. They were then asked to pay sequentially: a registration fee of Rs 3,000, a document verification fee of Rs 5,000, an interview scheduling fee of Rs 4,000, and a uniform advance of Rs 6,000 — totalling Rs 18,000. All calls now go unanswered. The named employer confirms they have no record of this hiring process and no relationship with the consultancy.
**What staff/org would do:** The operation has been dismantled — the number is switched off or answered by someone claiming it is a wrong number. There is no firm to contact, no complaint channel to use, and no refund mechanism. The call center infrastructure that projected credibility was specifically designed to be abandoned once payment collection was complete.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately validates the experience as a formally documented fraud type. Makes clear this is not the victim's fault and that the fraud was deliberately professional in its presentation. Stresses urgency — there is a narrow window for payment recovery through the banking system.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: true
- Opening reply: "What you have described matches a fraud pattern that Delhi Police Cyber Cell has formally documented and warned about. You are not alone — this scheme targets thousands of job seekers every year using professional call centers to appear legitimate. This is not your fault, and you need to act today to maximise the chance of recovering your money."
- Direct to: National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal at cybercrime.gov.in — file a complaint immediately; National Cyber Crime Helpline: 1930 (available 24 hours); local police Cyber Cell in person — bring all evidence; the bank used to make the payments — request an immediate payment recall and report as fraud, citing the specific UTR transaction numbers; the job portal where the CV was listed (Naukri.com, Shine.com, LinkedIn) — report the fraudulent scraping of your profile so they can warn other users; Consumer Helpline at 1915 (National Consumer Helpline) to register the fraud; Sanchar Saathi portal at sancharsaathi.gov.in to report the mobile number used by the fraudsters so it can be blocked
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of all call logs — incoming and outgoing — with dates and times; any WhatsApp or SMS messages received; all emails including full headers; any website or social media profiles of the fake firm (screenshot immediately before they disappear); payment receipts and bank transfer records with UTR numbers; the named employer's written or spoken denial; the names and titles of all agents who called; any documents or offer letters sent by the fake firm
- Feedback category tag: Financial Fraud — Fake Credentials / Disappeared with Payment / Call Center as Fraud Infrastructure

### Scenario 18-8: Consultancy Partner Helped a Bidder While Simultaneously Advising the Buyer — Conflict Never Disclosed (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Australian National Audit Office finding and Canberra Times reporting, May 2021 — Deloitte Australia Department of Home Affairs contract terminated for undisclosed conflict of interest; contract grew from $151,800 to $1.5M before termination; probity breach confirmed by independent law firm Maddocks; no individual disciplined
**The Problem:** A business owner discovers through a media report that the consultancy they contracted as a procurement adviser — helping them evaluate vendors and structure the selection process — was simultaneously providing support to one of the companies actively bidding in that same process. This dual role was never disclosed. The vendor that was ultimately selected may have benefited from the consultancy's insider knowledge of the buyer's evaluation criteria and weighting. Contracts based on that evaluation have already been signed.
**What staff/org would do:** Issue a written statement confirming the engagement has been "terminated" and that the firm "takes conflicts of interest seriously." Decline to state specifically what information was shared between its buyer-advisory team and its bidder-support work, citing "client confidentiality obligations to both parties." Point to the termination of the engagement as resolution.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges this is a serious governance breach with potential legal and financial implications for the business. Does not accept the firm's statement as closure or wait for the consultancy's internal review to conclude before advising the client on their rights.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: false
- Opening reply: "Discovering that your adviser was helping one of your vendors at the same time — without telling you — is a serious conflict of interest that may directly affect the validity of the contract you signed. You have the right to know exactly what information was shared, and there are formal channels to pursue that."
- Direct to: A commercial solicitor to assess whether the undisclosed conflict of interest provides grounds to void, renegotiate, or claim damages on the vendor contract; (Australia) Accounting Professional and Ethical Standards Board at apesb.org.au to report the professional conduct breach; Australian National Audit Office at anao.gov.au if a government body was involved; (UK) ICAEW Professional Conduct team at icaew.com/complaints; (USA) AICPA Ethics Division; the consultancy's Conflicts of Interest partner or Chief Risk Officer in writing, formally requesting full disclosure of what information was shared and with whom
- Evidence to collect: Signed engagement letter and scope of work with the consultancy; all evaluation reports, vendor scoring matrices, and selection recommendation documents it produced; the contract signed with the selected vendor; any communications from the consultancy about conflicts of interest checks (or the absence of any such communication); all invoices paid; media reports or audit office findings referencing the firm
- Feedback category tag: Corruption — Undisclosed Conflict of Interest / Procurement Misconduct / Staff Dual Role

### Scenario 18-9: Consulting Firm's Strategy Advice Directed Targeting of Vulnerable Patients — Community Harm Documented (Pattern C+E)
**Real Basis:** DOJ press release, December 2024 — United States v. McKinsey and Company Inc.; $650M federal civil and criminal settlement (False Claims Act violations and obstruction); approximately $115M in prior state and local government settlements; total approximately $765M; first instance of a management consulting firm facing criminal liability for advice that caused a client to commit a crime; former senior partner Martin Elling indicted for deleting electronic materials to obstruct the investigation; NPR and CBS News reporting
**The Problem:** A healthcare professional or community advocacy organisation is raising a formal concern: a major consulting firm's strategic advice directed a pharmaceutical manufacturer to target the highest-volume opioid prescribers with the most aggressive sales tactics, specifically designed to drive higher dosages and broader prescribing regardless of clinical need. Patients in their community have suffered documented harm as a result. The professional wants to know whether this constitutes a recognised legal wrong, where to direct their concern formally, and how to ensure it is part of the accountability record.
**What staff/org would do:** Refer the concern to the firm's Communications or Legal department. Issue a statement noting that "the firm has resolved this matter with the Department of Justice and accepts responsibility as described in the resolution." Decline to provide specifics of how the strategy was developed or who authorised it. Present the settlement as closure and point to the firm's "transformation" since.
**What the Owner does:** Validates that the concern is grounded in a formally adjudicated legal record and that community-level documentation strengthens the ongoing public accountability process. Notes that settlement distribution processes exist for affected communities. Does not accept "the settlement resolves it" as a complete answer.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: false
- Opening reply: "Your concern is grounded in a formally documented legal record — this conduct led to the largest settlement ever imposed on a management consulting firm, including criminal charges and obstruction of justice. Your report matters both for accountability and for ensuring affected communities can access any remediation available to them."
- Direct to: (USA) DOJ Office of Public Affairs at justice.gov to confirm participation in any victim notification or settlement distribution process related to the resolution; state Attorneys General offices in states that reached separate settlements — many have victim assistance processes; the court-appointed monitor or compliance officer under the Deferred Prosecution Agreement if one has been established; opioid community advocacy organisations such as End Overdose (endoverdose.net) and Community Anti-Drug Coalitions of America (cadca.org) for coordination with affected community efforts; academic and public health research institutions documenting opioid harm for addition to the evidence base
- Evidence to collect: Any documented patient outcome records the professional is authorised to hold; clinical communications or directives referencing the consulting firm's strategy in the prescribing context; published academic research or court documents linking the strategy to outcomes in the relevant community; the professional's own contemporaneous case notes and observations; local epidemiological data showing prescribing trends correlated with the strategy period
- Feedback category tag: Rights Violation — Consulting Advice Causing Documented Community Harm / Conflict of Interest / Deliverable Causing Public Harm

### Scenario 18-10: Elderly Client's Life Savings Transferred by Trusted Financial Adviser into Personal Account (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** FCA enforcement record — Paul Michael Grant, convicted 11 January 2019 on 11 counts of fraud by abuse of position and 3 counts of theft; £2.1 million stolen from clients including specifically vulnerable individuals; subsequent lifetime FCA ban; Professional Adviser trade publication; Ashcotts Solicitors case commentary; FCA documented pattern of adviser non-response after funds transferred
**The Problem:** An elderly client trusted their financial adviser — a relationship of many years — with management of their retirement savings. The adviser persuaded the client to consolidate funds into a new account "for more effective management." The client has now tried to access their savings and found the account balance is zero. When the client calls the adviser's number, there is no answer. A family member has contacted the advisory firm's main office; the firm says the adviser is "no longer with us" and directs the family to a general customer service line that places them on hold for extended periods without any resolution or protective action. The family believes the adviser has stolen the money.
**What staff/org would do:** The firm's customer service team logs the call and says the matter has been "referred to our compliance team for review." The family is told to expect contact within 10 working days. No immediate protective action is taken. The firm declines to confirm whether the client's funds are held anywhere recoverable and does not proactively contact any regulator.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an emergency — a vulnerable elderly person has had their life savings stolen by someone in a position of professional trust. Immediate action is required and the 10-working-day compliance timeline is not acceptable.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent: true
- Opening reply: "This is an emergency. A vulnerable person may have had their entire life savings stolen by someone they trusted with their finances. Please do not wait for the firm's compliance team — there are steps you need to take right now that may make the difference between recovering funds and not."
- Direct to: (UK) FCA Consumer Helpline immediately at 0800 111 6768 — report theft by a financial adviser and verify the adviser's current regulated status; Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk or 0300 123 2040 — report financial fraud citing "fraud by abuse of position" specifically; Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) at fscs.org.uk — may cover up to £85,000 per institution if the firm is FCA-authorised and has failed; the client's own bank — request an immediate freeze on any outgoing transfers from accounts the adviser had signatory or third-party access to; Police via 101 (or 999 if the client is in immediate distress) — financial abuse of a vulnerable adult is a criminal matter independently of the FCA process; local authority Adult Safeguarding team if the client is an adult at risk under the Care Act 2014; the firm's CEO and Compliance Director simultaneously in writing, requesting immediate account-level protective action — not a standard complaints process
- Evidence to collect: All account statements and balance records before and after the consolidation transfer; signed letters of authority granted to the adviser; any communications from the adviser requesting fund movements; the adviser's full name, FCA registration number (if known), and any contact details held; a log of all calls to the firm with dates, hold times, and names of agents spoken to; the firm's name, address, and FCA firm reference number (searchable at register.fca.org.uk); any Power of Attorney documents that gave the adviser access; emails or letters from the adviser about the account consolidation
- Feedback category tag: Financial Fraud — Theft by Adviser / Vulnerable Client / Emergency Safeguarding
