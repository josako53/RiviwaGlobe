## 8. Embassy / Immigration

### Scenario 8-1: Passport Confiscated, Wages Withheld — Debt Bondage on Stadium Construction Site (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Amnesty International primary investigation, Qatar Khalifa International Stadium migrant workers, March 2016; ILO formal complaint (Convention No. 29 Forced Labour), 2014–2017; Business and Human Rights Centre documentation
**The Problem:** A Nepalese metalworker on a World Cup stadium construction site in Qatar paid USD 2,400 in recruitment fees before arrival. His employer confiscated his passport on his first day and has now withheld two months of wages. He cannot change jobs, cannot leave the country, and was verbally threatened by a supervisor when he asked about the overdue payment. He is filing a grievance through Riviwa's voice channel because he has no functioning internet access.
**What staff/org would do:** The site supervisor dismisses the complaint as a misunderstanding about Qatari custom, tells the worker the passport is being held for safekeeping, and promises wages will be paid soon. No written record of the complaint is created. The worker is told to return to work.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-equipped HR and compliance officer at the contractor receives the alert immediately, logs it as a formal grievance under forced labour indicators, freezes further deductions on the worker's account, and initiates a passport return protocol. The officer contacts the worker in Nepali via Riviwa's translation layer and begins documenting the full recruitment fee chain.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — your passport being held by your employer and your wages being withheld — is a serious violation of your rights under international law and Qatar's own Labour Law. You are not alone, and this is not acceptable. We are recording this as a formal complaint right now."
- Who to direct to: Qatar Ministry of Labour complaint portal (adlsa.gov.qa); ILO Project Office in Doha; Migrant Rights legal helpline (migrant-rights.org); Embassy of Nepal in Doha (+974 4467 1548) for emergency consular support
- Evidence to collect and preserve: photograph of any pre-departure contract showing promised wages; screenshots of any supervisor messages containing threats; note supervisor's name, date, and time of each incident; formally request written documentation of the employer's passport retention policy
- Feedback category tag: Labour Rights Violation / Forced Labour Indicator / Financial Exploitation / Passport Confiscation

### Scenario 8-2: British Tourist Held 19 Days — Signed Border Statement Under Exhaustion and Duress (Pattern C/D)
**Real Basis:** Newsweek, 2025 ("British Tourist Detained by ICE for 19 Days Warns Against All US Travel"); confirmed ICE detention record, Tacoma Northwest ICE Detention Facility, Washington State, USA
**The Problem:** A 27-year-old British graphic designer arrived at a US land border after being denied entry to Canada. Exhausted and disoriented, she was asked by a CBP officer to sign a summary statement that characterised her house-sitting arrangement as work in exchange for accommodation. She signed without reading it fully. She is now held at an ICE detention facility in Washington State. Her family in Wales does not know where she is. She has reached Riviwa via a facility phone.
**What staff/org would do:** The ICE intake officer processes her as a tourist visa violator, places her in general detention, and advises that a hearing will be scheduled. No contact is made with the British Consulate. She is handed a printed rights sheet in English only.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled detention facility complaint officer immediately creates a case record, flags it for mandatory consular notification under Vienna Convention Article 36, and contacts the British Consulate-General in Seattle within 30 minutes. The officer provides the detainee's location, booking reference, and the stated basis for the charge, and arranges a phone call to her family in the UK.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Being detained in a foreign country is frightening, and you have rights right now — including the right to consular assistance from the UK government. We are documenting this case and helping you activate those rights immediately."
- Who to direct to: UK Consular Emergency Line (+44 20 7008 5000, available 24/7); ACLU National Immigration Project; Immigration Equality legal aid (US immigration detention); submit a written Vienna Convention Article 36 consular access request to the facility duty officer now
- Evidence to collect and preserve: retain a copy of the signed border statement; write down the exact words the CBP officer used before asking her to sign; note the time, location, and officer badge number; preserve any electronic messages showing the house-sitting arrangement was unpaid
- Feedback category tag: Wrongful Detention / Consular Access Denied / Coerced Statement / Due Process Failure

### Scenario 8-3: UK eVisa Displays Trafficking Survivor's Abuser Name — Student Loses Job Offers, Helpline Charges Per Minute (Pattern E/D)
**Real Basis:** Computer Weekly, 2024–2025 ("UK's error-prone eVisa system is anxiety-inducing"); ILPA Parliamentary submission ("The Digital Status Crisis"); Right to Remain; Seraphus immigration lawyers; WhatDoTheyKnow FOI request on UKVI contact centre performance metrics, declined by Home Office on cost grounds
**The Problem:** A woman who survived human trafficking and was granted refugee status in the UK is trying to generate a right-to-work share code via the UKVI eVisa portal. The system is returning her trafficking name — the name her abuser registered her under — and her trafficker's date of birth, not her own. She has called the UKVI helpline three times over three days. Each call costs 12 pence per minute from a landline. She has been on hold for more than 40 minutes per attempt and disconnected twice without reaching a live agent. She is now at risk of losing her housing benefit because the name on the eVisa does not match any of her identification documents.
**What staff/org would do:** When an agent is eventually reached, the UKVI call centre staff opens a routine Change of Conditions case, gives a standard 20 working day processing estimate, and does not flag the trafficking disclosure or escalate to the vulnerable persons unit.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled UKVI case handler who receives the complaint immediately applies the Safeguarding Protocol for vulnerable persons, flags the case to the Modern Slavery and Vulnerable Persons Hub, suspends the incorrect eVisa generation, issues a temporary manual right-to-work letter bearing the correct identity, and contacts the employer directly with a confirmation reference.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The error you have described — your eVisa displaying a name linked to your trafficking situation — is a serious safeguarding failure that UKVI is required to treat as a priority. You should not be paying per-minute call charges to resolve a government system error. We are escalating this under safeguarding protocols now."
- Who to direct to: Migrant Help UK (0808 8010 503, free 24/7 for trafficking survivors); Unseen UK Modern Slavery Helpline (08000 121 700, free); Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman (PHSO) if UKVI does not resolve within 20 days; local MP's constituency office for parliamentary intervention on the UKVI case file
- Evidence to collect and preserve: screenshot the eVisa share code output showing the incorrect name and date of birth; save a log of all UKVI call dates, times, and durations (for a cost recovery claim); obtain written confirmation from the benefit provider of any action taken on the mismatched identity; document all job offers received and lost due to the error
- Feedback category tag: IT System Failure / Safeguarding — Trafficking Survivor / Identity Error / Call Centre Failure / Cost Barrier to Complaint

### Scenario 8-4: Embassy Contractor of Six Years Told to Leave Kabul — "Nobody Called, Nobody Emailed" During Taliban Takeover (Pattern D/E)
**Real Basis:** Washington Post, September 5, 2021; NPR; US State Department OIG Report AUD-MERO-23-33 (classified, existence confirmed by OIG public record); Wikipedia, 2021 Kabul airlift
**The Problem:** A man who worked as a contractor at the US Embassy in Kabul for six years was told to leave the compound on August 15, 2021, as Taliban forces entered the city. Embassy staff had collected his and his family's personal details weeks earlier as part of evacuation contingency planning. He was told someone would contact him. No contact came. He is now sheltering with his family in a Kabul building as the security situation deteriorates rapidly. He has intermittent internet access and is submitting this through Riviwa's emergency channel.
**What staff/org would do:** The State Department consular duty officer has no record of the contractor in the active evacuation roster. The officer directs him to call the Bureau of Consular Affairs public emergency line (202-501-4444), which routes to an automated message advising callers not to rely on the US government for evacuation — a message that was simultaneously live and incorrect.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled State Department emergency officer immediately cross-references the contractor's name against the pre-collected contingency data set, confirms the record exists, classifies the case as Priority 1 (documented US government connection, confirmed imminent threat), creates a live case ID with a direct callback number, and routes the family's location to the nearest active evacuation coordination point.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Your safety and your family's safety is the priority right now. You provided your information to the Embassy weeks ago, and that record exists. We are treating this as a Priority 1 emergency case immediately — stay in your current location until you receive a direct call from us."
- Who to direct to: State Department 24-hour Operations Center (+1 202-647-4000, not the automated public line); contact your home country embassy if you hold non-US citizenship; International Rescue Committee Afghanistan emergency contacts; if you hold any US documentation, contact a US Congressional representative's constituent services office immediately
- Evidence to collect and preserve: retain any written or verbal communications from Embassy staff who collected the family's information; note any staff names, dates, and what was said; preserve employment records or contractor identity documents; note current safe location and share it with a trusted contact outside Afghanistan
- Feedback category tag: Emergency Evacuation Failure / Consular Duty of Care Breach / Communication Breakdown — Is Urgent

### Scenario 8-5: US Government Emergency Consular Helpline Played Automated Message Telling Citizens in Crisis "Don't Expect Help" (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** Newsweek, March 2026 ("State Dept Scraps Message Telling Americans in Middle East It Won't Help"); US Bureau of Consular Affairs emergency line 202-501-4444; State Department acknowledgement that the message was operated by a third-party vendor and was outdated
**The Problem:** An American citizen in a Middle East country is experiencing an active security emergency. They call the US State Department's official emergency consular line — the number listed on their travel advisory and their Smart Traveler Enrollment Program registration confirmation. An automated recording tells them: "Do not rely on the US government for assisted departure or evacuation." They are now unsure whether any help is coming. They are calling back to report the message and ask what they should do.
**What staff/org would do:** The call routes to the same automated system. A subsequent daytime call reaches a consular phone bank where the agent is unaware the automated message exists and cannot confirm current evacuation policy. The agent advises the caller to monitor travel.state.gov.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled State Department duty officer who receives this complaint identifies the automated message immediately as an active misinformation incident, escalates to the Bureau of Consular Affairs Operations Center for emergency correction, issues a direct outbound callback to the citizen with accurate current guidance, and opens a priority welfare case for the citizen's physical situation.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "The automated message you heard was incorrect and did not reflect current US government policy. It has since been corrected. You are entitled to consular assistance. We are connecting you to the right channel right now — please stay on this line."
- Who to direct to: State Department 24-hour Operations Center (+1 202-647-4000, staffed by live agents); nearest US Embassy or Consulate direct line; update your STEP registration at step.state.gov with your current location; if the US Embassy is unreachable, contact the embassy of a Five Eyes partner country — the UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand routinely provide consular assistance to allied nationals
- Evidence to collect and preserve: note the exact wording of the automated message, including the date and time you heard it; save any travel advisory emails or STEP registration confirmations showing 202-501-4444 as the officially listed emergency number; record timestamps of each call attempt; document any conflicting guidance received from different agents
- Feedback category tag: Call Centre Failure / Emergency Information Error / Third-Party Vendor Failure / Transparency Failure — Is Urgent

### Scenario 8-6: Man Held Four Years in Australian Immigration Detention Despite Holding a Valid Bridging Visa — Internal Complaint Went Unanswered (Pattern C/E)
**Real Basis:** Commonwealth Ombudsman, "Righting Wrongful Detention," August 2025 (PDF); Commonwealth Ombudsman December 2018 investigation report; UNHCR Australia overview; UN Working Group OHCHR press release December 2025; case of "Mr G" as documented in Ombudsman records
**The Problem:** A man has been held in Australian immigration detention for 14 months. He holds a valid Bridging visa and has provided the reference number to facility staff on multiple occasions. The Department of Home Affairs has not corrected the system record. The internal complaint form he submitted six weeks ago has received no written response. He is now submitting a formal grievance through a Riviwa terminal installed at the facility.
**What staff/org would do:** The duty officer checks the internal system, which displays a refused Partner visa application and no active visa status in the current view — the Bridging visa was never updated in the system's display record. The officer tells the detainee his status is under review and that the legal aid queue is currently 10 weeks long.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled facility compliance officer immediately creates a Priority Review case, contacts the Department of Home Affairs Visa Verification line for an urgent status cross-check, and requests the detainee's complete visa history as a matter of emergency. The officer confirms the Bridging visa status in writing within 24 hours and escalates to the Commonwealth Ombudsman immigration team if the Department has not confirmed within 48 hours.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "If your Bridging visa is valid, your continued detention may not be lawful, and you have the right to an immediate independent review. We are raising this as an urgent case and connecting you with independent legal support today — not in 10 weeks."
- Who to direct to: Commonwealth Ombudsman immigration complaint line (1300 362 072); Refugee Advice and Casework Service (RACS) — 02 9114 1600; Asylum Seeker Resource Centre (ASRC) legal clinic; Federal Court of Australia habeas corpus application process via Legal Aid NSW
- Evidence to collect and preserve: secure and preserve your Bridging visa grant letter and visa reference number; obtain written confirmation showing the date your internal complaint was filed; note every staff member who reviewed your case, the date, and their response; formally request a written copy of your current official visa status from the Department of Home Affairs
- Feedback category tag: Wrongful Detention / Data Error / Processing Failure / Internal Complaint Ignored / System Record Discrepancy

### Scenario 8-7: Legal US Visa Holder Denied Insulin for Six Days Inside ICE Detention — Collapsed and Hospitalised (Pattern C/D)
**Real Basis:** Human Rights Watch, July 21, 2025 ("You Feel Like Your Life Is Over: Abusive Practices at Three Florida Immigration Detention Centers"); The Hill; Globe and Mail; Harpinder Singh Chauhan case, Krome North Service Processing Center, Broward Transitional Center, and Federal Detention Center Miami
**The Problem:** A 55-year-old British entrepreneur with a valid E-2 investor visa, a documented diabetes diagnosis, and a known heart condition has been in ICE detention at a Florida facility for nine days. He has been denied insulin for five consecutive days despite disclosing his conditions at intake and at two subsequent medical request submissions. He is experiencing symptoms consistent with diabetic emergency — extreme thirst, confusion, and inability to stand without dizziness. A fellow detainee is submitting this complaint on his behalf via Riviwa because he is too weak to type.
**What staff/org would do:** The facility medical officer notes that insulin is on order and informs the fellow detainee that the sick man needs to submit a paper medical request form, which will be reviewed at the next scheduled medical rotation — four days away. The duty guard logs medical concern noted with no escalation flag.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled facility compliance officer receives the alert as a medical emergency, immediately contacts the on-site medical contractor for an urgent glucose check and insulin administration, notifies the ICE Field Office Detention Compliance Officer, and documents the welfare check with a time-stamped record completed within 30 minutes.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a medical emergency. Withholding insulin from a person with documented diabetes is a serious and potentially life-threatening harm. We are escalating this right now — the four-day medical rotation schedule is not acceptable in this situation."
- Who to direct to: ICE Enforcement and Removal Operations medical complaint escalation line; DHS Office of Inspector General Hotline (1-800-323-8603, available 24/7); ACLU Florida immigration detention unit (+1 305 576 2337); UK Consulate-General Miami (+1 305 400 6400) for an urgent consular welfare visit
- Evidence to collect and preserve: record the exact date insulin was last administered before the denial began; document the exact words used by the medical officer and duty guard on each occasion; collect names and badge numbers of all staff involved; ask fellow detainees who witnessed the request denials to record what they observed in writing; preserve all paper medical request forms submitted with dates
- Feedback category tag: Medical Emergency / Staff Misconduct / Wrongful Detention / Consular Welfare — Is Urgent

### Scenario 8-8: 1,300 Migrants Expelled to Libyan Desert Without Any Process — 28 Deaths, 16-Year-Old Among Those Expelled (Pattern C/D)
**Real Basis:** Human Rights Watch, July 6, 2023 ("Tunisia: Crisis as Black Africans Expelled to Libya Border"); HRW October 10, 2023 follow-up; NPR, August 23, 2023 ("Rights groups say Tunisia expels migrants into the desert, where some have died"); Al Jazeera
**The Problem:** A 16-year-old Cameroonian girl and an adult travelling companion were seized by Tunisian National Guard officers and transported by truck to the Libya border zone on July 2, 2023. They were given no documents, no water, and no information about their rights. The companion has reached a mobile signal and is filing a Riviwa emergency report. They are in the desert border zone with no shelter, no food, and no identity documents. The temperature is above 40 degrees Celsius and conditions are deteriorating.
**What staff/org would do:** There is no functioning state complaint channel in the border zone. The Tunisian National Guard has no publicly accessible grievance process. The Libyan border zone has no functioning authority capable of processing asylum claims. No one responds.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled humanitarian organisation receiving the alert immediately initiates an emergency geolocation trace, dispatches the nearest mobile protection team with water and medical supplies, contacts UNHCR's Libya emergency desk and the IOM Libya response team, and activates child protection protocols because a minor is involved.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "Your safety and the safety of the child with you is the only priority right now. Help is being directed to your location. Stay in place if it is safe to do so. Your report has been received and is being acted on immediately."
- Who to direct to: UNHCR Libya Emergency (+218-21-333-8000); IOM Libya Emergency Operations (iom.int/countries/libya); Alarm Phone desert distress support line (+33 4 86 51 71 61); Cameroonian Embassy in Tunis (+216 71 894 027) for the minor's consular protection and repatriation coordination
- Evidence to collect and preserve: share GPS coordinates or the nearest visible landmark immediately; note the time and location where National Guard officers seized them; record any officer identification seen on uniforms or vehicles; describe the truck, the route taken, and the number of other people expelled at the same time; preserve photographs if it is safe to take them
- Feedback category tag: Emergency Expulsion / Child Protection — Unaccompanied Minor / Racial Discrimination / Refoulement Risk / No Due Process — Is Urgent

### Scenario 8-9: Labor Rights Blogger Held in Solitary Confinement and Disappeared — Family Cannot Locate Him for 16 Days (Pattern C/D)
**Real Basis:** Human Rights Watch, May 28, 2021 ("Qatar: Kenyan Labor Rights Activist in Solitary Confinement"); Amnesty International joint statement; Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ); Malcolm Bidali / pen name "Noah," case documentation; charged with spreading disinformation, released after fine, subsequently expelled from Qatar
**The Problem:** A 28-year-old Kenyan security guard and labor rights blogger who documented migrant worker abuses was removed from his dormitory by state security officers four days ago. His colleagues do not know where he is being held. His family in Kenya learned of his detention only through a third-party contact. No arrest warrant or formal charges have been provided to his family or employer. He has had no access to a lawyer. His employer's HR department has filed a missing person report with the Kenyan Embassy in Doha, which is now submitting a Riviwa grievance on behalf of his family.
**What staff/org would do:** The Qatari Ministry of Interior inquiry line states it cannot confirm or deny the whereabouts of any individual in state custody. The security services do not respond to the embassy's written request for 72 hours. The employer's legal team is told the matter is under investigation, with no further detail provided.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled Kenyan Embassy duty officer in Doha activates the Consular Emergency Protocol immediately, sends a formal diplomatic note to the Qatar Ministry of Foreign Affairs demanding the person's location and the charges against him within 24 hours, and simultaneously contacts the Committee to Protect Journalists and Amnesty International's Urgent Action Network to alert them to the case.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The removal of a person into state custody without access to a lawyer, without charges, and without family notification is a serious human rights concern under international law. The embassy is right to escalate this formally. We are recording every detail now and activating every available channel."
- Who to direct to: Amnesty International Urgent Action Network (amnesty.org/en/get-involved/urgent-action/); Committee to Protect Journalists Emergency Line (+1 212 465 1004); UN Special Rapporteur on Torture complaint submission (ohchr.org/en/special-procedures); Kenyan Ministry of Foreign Affairs Consular Division (+254 20 3318888) for diplomatic escalation and parliamentary pressure
- Evidence to collect and preserve: document the last known contact with the blogger — date, time, and what was said; preserve all published articles and social media posts that may have prompted the arrest; collect names of colleagues who witnessed his removal; record every attempt to contact Qatari authorities with date, method, and response received; preserve any employer communications referencing his work or complaints filed
- Feedback category tag: Enforced Disappearance / Retaliation Against Whistleblower / Press Freedom Violation / Government Misconduct / Consular Access Refused

### Scenario 8-10: Asylum Seekers Held in Transit Zone for Eleven Weeks With No Detention Order and No Complaint Channel Inside the Zone (Pattern C/A)
**Real Basis:** European Court of Human Rights, R.R. and Others v. Hungary (2021), Article 5 violation; ECHR Guide on Immigration Case-Law, February 2026 update; Statewatch ECHR immigration detention judgment; J.R. and Others v. Greece (2018), Article 5 violation (Vial hotspot, Chios island, Afghan nationals held one month without individual detention order)
**The Problem:** A family that fled persecution has been held in a Hungarian border transit zone for 11 weeks. They applied for asylum on arrival. They have received no decision on their application and no hearing date. They cannot leave the transit zone. They have never been served with a formal detention order. There is no complaint form available inside the zone. A legal aid volunteer visiting the zone is submitting this Riviwa complaint on their behalf.
**What staff/org would do:** The border authority officer states the family is not detained but is in an administrative processing zone pending case review. The officer explains that no formal detention order is required under the current administrative framework and that processing timelines depend on circumstances. No legal remedy information is provided. The family is directed to a printed information sheet written in Hungarian.
**What the Owner does:** The Riviwa-enabled border authority compliance officer who receives the complaint immediately opens a formal case record, provides the family with their rights under the EU Asylum Procedures Directive in their language, notifies the legal aid coordinator and UNHCR Hungary office, and schedules a formal asylum hearing within five working days with written confirmation of the date provided to the family.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What you are describing — being held in a transit zone for nearly three months without a formal detention order and without a hearing — is a violation of your rights under the European Convention on Human Rights, as confirmed by the European Court. You have the right to a legal remedy, and help is available to you now."
- Who to direct to: UNHCR Hungary (+36 1 374 3200); Hungarian Helsinki Committee legal aid (+36 1 321 4323, specialist in transit zone cases); European Court of Human Rights application portal (echr.coe.int) — the family may qualify for interim measures under Rule 39 if removal or further harm is threatened; ECRE (European Council on Refugees and Exiles) for referral to a member legal organisation
- Evidence to collect and preserve: document every day of holding from the date of arrival, including each interaction with border authority staff; request copies of all written communications received — if none have been issued, the absence of a detention order is itself evidence for the ECHR application; note the language in which information was or was not provided; record names and badge numbers of all officers encountered
- Feedback category tag: Unlawful Detention / Asylum Process Denial / No Legal Remedy / Refusal of Service / Rights Violation — ECHR Article 5
