### Scenario 10-1: Exploding Juicer Causes Abdominal Injury Before Product Recall (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** CPSC recall announcement, Empower Brands PowerXL Pro Performance Juicer, June 2023; US PIRG Education Fund "Safe at Home in 2024" consumer product safety report — 261 consumer complaints, 47 injuries, 469,000 units recalled across the USA and Canada
**The Problem:** A 53-year-old woman is using her PowerXL juicer at home when the unit explodes, shooting a metal component into her abdomen. She is in pain and bleeding. She cannot find a phone number that connects to a live person at the retailer — only an automated returns portal. Her husband calls the store where it was purchased; staff say they have "no information about any recall" and direct him to Empower Brands' 1-800 number, which plays hold music for 38 minutes before disconnecting.
**What staff/org would do:** Tell the family the appliance "may have been misused" and that any claim must go through the manufacturer. Empower Brands, when eventually reached, opens a warranty claim and requires the product to be returned at the consumer's expense for inspection before any remedy is considered.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately acknowledges this as a physical injury emergency. Does not ask the consumer to return the product before seeking medical care. Documents the full incident and flags it for urgent escalation. Instructs the consumer not to use the product and to preserve all physical evidence.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is serious — your safety comes first. Please seek medical attention now if you have not already. We are documenting everything and will support you at every step."
- Direct the consumer to: CPSC SaferProducts.gov to file an incident report online; CPSC consumer hotline 1-800-638-2772; nearest emergency medical care if not already obtained; Empower Brands product liability claim once the consumer is safe
- Evidence to preserve: photographs of the injury and the damaged appliance, the broken metal component (sealed in a bag, do not discard), purchase receipt with date and retailer name, any medical records from treatment
- Feedback category tag: Product Safety / Physical Injury / Defective Good / Urgent

### Scenario 10-2: Counterfeit Cosmetics from Temu — Caused Skin Rash, Refund Refused With Only Template Replies (Pattern B + C + E)
**Real Basis:** FTC enforcement action against Whaleco Inc. (Temu), $2M civil penalty under INFORM Consumers Act, 2024; European Commission investigation into Temu for counterfeit and unsafe goods, October 2024; documented Trustpilot complaint patterns cited in FTC proceedings — template-only responses, refusal to process refunds when consumers explicitly invoked their rights
**The Problem:** A consumer paid $67 for what appeared to be CeraVe skincare products listed on Temu. The items arrived with packaging that differs from genuine CeraVe — the font is slightly wrong, the batch code is absent, and the product has an unusual chemical smell. After applying the cream she developed a skin rash. She has contacted Temu customer support four times over 12 days. Each response is a different template — first a "quality guarantee" assurance, then a request to upload photos, then notice that the seller has been "notified," then a message that the "investigation is ongoing" with no timeline. No refund has been issued.
**What staff/org would do:** Continue sending template responses asking for further photographs. Eventually close the case with a partial store-credit offer redeemable only on future Temu purchases. No acknowledgment that the product may be counterfeit or the cause of the rash.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the health risk posed by the rash. Does not ask the consumer to keep waiting through an endless chain of template messages. Advises on the chargeback right and documents the transaction details for regulatory referral.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (rash is uncomfortable but not life-threatening; escalate to urgent if the reaction worsens or spreads)
- Opening reply: "I'm sorry — receiving a potentially fake product that has caused a skin reaction is both frustrating and a genuine health concern. Let me help you get a refund and report this properly."
- Direct the consumer to: FTC ReportFraud.ftc.gov to report the counterfeit goods; contact their bank or credit card issuer immediately to initiate a chargeback — Temu's repeated non-response and failure to resolve constitutes grounds for reversal; report the counterfeit listing to CeraVe/L'Oreal USA brand protection; if located in the EU, file through the BEUC complaint portal and the EU ODR platform
- Evidence to preserve: screenshots of all four Temu chat responses with dates, photographs of the packaging discrepancies against genuine CeraVe, photos of the skin rash, order confirmation and payment record, the product itself (do not use further, retain for potential testing)
- Feedback category tag: Counterfeit Goods / Refund Refused / Online Platform Fraud / Financial Harm

### Scenario 10-3: Bought Based on Thousands of Fake Reviews — Company Was Suppressing All Negative Feedback for Four Years (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** FTC enforcement action against Fashion Nova, $4.2M settlement finalized January 2022; FTC refund distribution to 148,351 consumers, January 2025 — company used a third-party tool to automatically post 4- and 5-star reviews while holding all reviews below 4 stars, never approving or posting the held reviews between late 2015 and November 2019
**The Problem:** A consumer purchased three dresses from an online fashion retailer totaling $184, choosing the brand specifically because it showed thousands of reviews with a 4.8-star average and she could not find a single negative review on the site. The items arrived poorly made — stitching came apart on first wear. When she tried to post a 2-star review, the submission appeared to go through but the review never appeared publicly. A friend sent her a news article confirming the company was fined $4.2 million by the FTC for systematically hiding all customer reviews below 4 stars for years. She contacts support to request a refund and is offered a 20% discount code on her next order.
**What staff/org would do:** Apologize for the "quality issue," offer the discount code, and state that refunds are only available within 30 days of purchase. The review suppression is not acknowledged. No information about the FTC settlement or consumer refund eligibility is volunteered.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges that the consumer was deceived before purchase — the reviews she relied on were a manufactured impression, not an accurate record of customer opinion. Does not deflect with discount codes. Explains the FTC settlement and the consumer's potential eligibility for a refund from the settlement fund.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You were deceived at the point of decision — the reviews you relied on were deliberately hidden from the public. You deserve both a refund and the full picture of what happened."
- Direct the consumer to: FTC refund page for Fashion Nova claimants at ftc.gov — check eligibility and filing window; if the refund window has closed, file a complaint at FTC ReportFraud.ftc.gov; dispute the charge with their credit card issuer for goods that did not match their advertised quality if within the dispute period
- Evidence to preserve: screenshots of the product page showing the star rating and review count at time of purchase, order confirmation, photographs of the defective stitching, records of the discount-code-only response from customer service
- Feedback category tag: Deceptive Advertising / Review Manipulation / Information Failure / Staff Misconduct

### Scenario 10-4: Elderly Woman Told Her Refund Rights "Expired" — Call Centre Staff Misrepresented Australian Consumer Law (Pattern C + A)
**Real Basis:** ACCC v Mosaic Brands (Noni B, Rivers, Katies), Federal Court of Australia, $25.05M penalty ordered August 2025 — 739,114 items not delivered within advertised timeframes; company falsely represented to consumers that refund rights did not apply after six months of purchase, directly misrepresenting Australian Consumer Law
**The Problem:** A 74-year-old woman ordered winter clothing worth AUD $312 from Katies eight months ago. The parcel was marked "in transit" for weeks and tracking then stopped permanently. She has called Katies customer service three times. On the first call she was placed on hold for 22 minutes and disconnected. On the second call she was told to wait because of "supply chain delays." On the third call today a customer service agent told her that her refund rights have expired because "our policy is that refund requests must be submitted within six months of purchase." She accepted this and hung up. A neighbour told her this did not sound right, and she is now contacting Riviwa.
**What staff/org would do:** Reiterate the 6-month policy as though it is law. Offer a store credit valid only for future Katies purchases. Decline to explain the legal basis for the policy. Not mention the ACCC investigation.
**What the Owner does:** Immediately corrects the misinformation. Australian Consumer Law does not impose a 6-month refund limit — the right to a refund for non-delivery applies for a "reasonable time" with no fixed cap. The 6-month rule told to this consumer is the exact misrepresentation for which Mosaic Brands was penalised $25.05 million.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (elderly and financially vulnerable consumer was given false legal information by a call centre agent and believed she had no remaining rights; her statutory entitlements are fully active)
- Opening reply: "Your neighbour was right — what you were told about the 6-month limit is legally incorrect, and it is the exact misrepresentation an Australian court ordered $25 million in penalties for. Your rights have not expired. Let's get this resolved."
- Direct the consumer to: ACCC online complaint form at accc.gov.au/consumers/complaints; the relevant state fair trading office (Consumer Affairs Victoria, NSW Fair Trading, or equivalent); call Katies again and state explicitly "I am exercising my rights under Australian Consumer Law — non-delivery of goods entitles me to a full refund, with no six-month limit"; if not resolved within 7 days, proceed to the state consumer tribunal (VCAT in Victoria, NCAT in New South Wales)
- Evidence to preserve: order confirmation email, all tracking screenshots including the stopped tracking record, written notes on each of the three calls with date, time, and the agent's exact words about the 6-month policy, any written confirmation from Katies of their refusal
- Feedback category tag: Refund Refused / Consumer Rights Violation / Call Centre Misinformation / Vulnerable Consumer

### Scenario 10-5: Smartphone Used in Pool Exactly as Advertised — Samsung Denied Warranty Citing Water Damage (Pattern D + A)
**Real Basis:** ACCC v Samsung Electronics Australia, Federal Court, $14M penalty 2022 — Samsung advertised smartphones being used in swimming pools and at the beach; Samsung admitted in court that water resistance did not perform in pool or salt water conditions; consumers who made warranty claims for exactly that type of damage were simultaneously refused under the "water damage exclusion"
**The Problem:** A consumer bought a Samsung Galaxy S21 specifically because Samsung's advertising campaign showed the phone being used in a swimming pool. She used the phone briefly at the edge of a lap pool — not submerged, only exposed to water splashing during a swimming lesson she was supervising — and it stopped working the same evening. She took it to a Samsung Service Centre, where a technician confirmed water ingress as the cause and refused the warranty claim, citing a "water damage exclusion clause." When she pointed out that Samsung's own advertisement depicts the phone in a pool, the staff member said "the advertising shows ideal conditions" and offered her a $200 discount on a new Samsung device.
**What staff/org would do:** Read from the warranty script: water damage exclusion applies regardless of advertising claims. Offer the replacement discount as final resolution. Not acknowledge the contradiction between the advertising and the warranty exclusion. Not disclose the ACCC court action.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the documented legal contradiction: Samsung admitted in Federal Court that its water resistance advertising was misleading, and warranty claims for exactly this type of damage were unlawfully denied. Under Australian Consumer Law, goods must conform to their advertised description — the warranty exclusion cannot override this statutory guarantee.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "The warranty refusal you received contradicts both Australian Consumer Law and a Federal Court finding against Samsung Australia on exactly this issue. You have a valid legal claim here."
- Direct the consumer to: ACCC complaint portal at accc.gov.au; cite the 2022 Federal Court order against Samsung Electronics Australia specifically on water resistance misrepresentation; write a formal letter to Samsung citing Australian Consumer Law s 56 (goods must match their description and advertising) and demanding repair, replacement, or refund; if Samsung refuses in writing, proceed to the relevant state consumer tribunal
- Evidence to preserve: the Samsung advertisement showing pool use (screenshot or video link, or note the campaign name), proof of purchase with model number, the Samsung Service Centre's written refusal citing the water damage exclusion, a record of the $200 counter-offer, photographs of the phone
- Feedback category tag: False Advertising / Warranty Denied / Staff Misconduct / Consumer Rights Violation

### Scenario 10-6: Funeral Flowers That Were Never Discounted — Bloomex Fake Strikethrough Prices and Hidden Checkout Fees (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** ACCC enforcement action against Bloomex Australia, $1M penalty — company systematically displayed false "was/now" strikethrough prices on products that had never been sold at the higher "was" price; also engaged in drip pricing by adding mandatory fees at checkout not disclosed in the advertised price; false star ratings also found; cited in Holding Redlich 2024 Australian Consumer Law enforcement review
**The Problem:** A consumer ordered a sympathy flower arrangement from Bloomex for a colleague's funeral. The website advertised the arrangement at AUD $54, displayed as reduced from $89 — a claimed saving of 39%. At checkout, three additional fees appeared without prior disclosure: a handling fee of $12.95, a fuel surcharge of $7.50, and a same-week premium of $7.00, bringing the total to $81.45. She paid because the flowers were time-sensitive. After the funeral she discovered through a news article that the $89 "was" price has never existed — Bloomex had never sold this product at that price. She contacts Bloomex to request a refund of the undisclosed fees; the agent tells her "all fees are disclosed at checkout" and offers a $10 voucher.
**What staff/org would do:** Insist the fees were technically visible during checkout and that the customer accepted them by completing the purchase. Offer the $10 voucher as final resolution. Not acknowledge that the strikethrough "was" price was fictitious.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges that both the fake discount and the mandatory fees added without prior disclosure are practices the ACCC found to be unlawful under Australian Consumer Law. The consumer was deceived at the point of choice, not after it.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What you have described — a discount from a price that never existed, and fees that appeared only at checkout — are the exact practices the ACCC took enforcement action against Bloomex for. You are right to challenge this."
- Direct the consumer to: ACCC complaint portal at accc.gov.au; the relevant state fair trading office; cite Australian Consumer Law ss 18 and 29 (misleading representations about price) and the ACCC's own enforcement action against Bloomex as direct precedent; contact the issuing bank or credit card provider to dispute the undisclosed fee component as a misrepresented charge
- Evidence to preserve: screenshot of the advertised price showing the strikethrough "was/now" display, screenshot of the checkout page showing when and how fees appeared, the order confirmation email with the total charged, all customer service correspondence including the $10 voucher offer
- Feedback category tag: Drip Pricing / False Discount / Deceptive Advertising / Transparency Failure / Corporate Misconduct

### Scenario 10-7: Flipkart Marked Order "Delivered" — Item Never Arrived, Six Calls Over Three Weeks With No Resolution (Pattern C + A + E)
**Real Basis:** India National Consumer Helpline (NCH) data 2023-24 — 1.236 million consumer calls received, 36.1% from e-commerce complaints; Flipkart the most-complained-about platform with over 1.6 lakh (160,000) documented grievances; non-delivery and refund not received the top documented complaint categories; Ministry of Consumer Affairs data via FACTLY analysis
**The Problem:** A consumer in Mumbai paid INR 8,500 for an air purifier ordered from Flipkart. Tracking changed to "Delivered" on a Tuesday morning — but no delivery was made. He was home all day. No delivery agent visited, no OTP was sent to his registered number (Flipkart requires OTP confirmation for high-value deliveries), and no parcel was left with a neighbour. He has called Flipkart customer support six times over three weeks. Every call produces the same response: "Our system shows the item was delivered. We are unable to issue a refund." He has spoken to four different agents; none have escalated the case. He has already filed a complaint with the National Consumer Helpline at 1800-11-4000 and asks what else he can do.
**What staff/org would do:** Repeat "delivered per system records" and close the ticket. Suggest he check with neighbours. Issue a new reference number with no resolution commitment or timeline.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the consumer has already taken every correct step. Confirms that six contacts without resolution and a GPS "delivered" record with no OTP confirmation is a strong, escalation-ready case under the Consumer Protection Act 2019, which explicitly covers e-commerce platforms.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (no safety risk; significant financial harm of INR 8,500; time-sensitive for formal escalation)
- Opening reply: "You have done everything right, and Flipkart's refusal to act after six contacts on a provably undelivered order is exactly the kind of platform failure the Consumer Protection Act 2019 was designed to address. Let's escalate this formally."
- Direct the consumer to: Follow up on the existing NCH complaint using the reference number; escalate to the District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission under the Consumer Protection Act 2019 — e-commerce platforms are explicitly covered and a written complaint can be filed without a lawyer; use the Umang app or NCH app to submit a written record if not already done; if paid by credit or debit card, file a chargeback with the issuing bank citing non-delivery — the absence of an OTP delivery confirmation is strong evidence
- Evidence to preserve: order confirmation and tax invoice, all six customer service chat or call logs with dates and agent names if given, screenshots of the tracking page showing "Delivered" status, documentary evidence that no OTP was sent to the registered mobile on the delivery date (mobile carrier records or simply a screenshot of SMS history for that day), a written statement confirming the consumer was home
- Feedback category tag: Non-Delivery / Refund Refused / Platform Failure / Wait Without Resolution

### Scenario 10-8: Empty iPhone Box Delivered — Apple Refuses Refund or Replacement, Consumer on Fourth Transfer (Pattern C + E)
**Real Basis:** UK Government Consumer Detriment Survey 2024 (NatCen) — 38.5 million UK consumers affected by unresolved product or service problems; Which? "Most Frustrating Consumer Problems of 2024" — documented case of a consumer receiving an empty Apple iPhone box via DPD and being refused both a replacement and a refund by Apple until Which? intervened
**The Problem:** A consumer in Bristol ordered an iPhone 15 Pro at £1,199 directly from Apple online. DPD delivered a sealed Apple box. When she opened it at home the box was completely empty — no device, no accessories, no documentation. She called Apple immediately. After 90 minutes on the phone and three transfers, an Apple representative told her that the package was weighed at Apple's fulfilment centre before dispatch and the weight confirmed the phone was inside, so Apple will not issue a replacement or refund. She is told to report the theft to the police and contact her home insurer. She has no home insurance. She has now been put on hold a fourth time and is in tears.
**What staff/org would do:** Maintain the weight-verification position as the final word. Provide a crime reference template for the police. Suggest contacting an insurer. Decline any further internal escalation. Close the case as post-delivery theft and outside Apple's liability.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises this as an urgent, high-value loss situation. Apple's weight-verification argument places the entire burden of proof on the consumer for a delivery chain Apple contracted and controlled. Under the UK Consumer Rights Act 2015, Apple's obligation is to deliver the goods — if the item was not received, the contract has not been fulfilled and a refund is legally due regardless of what DPD's records show.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (£1,199 financial loss on an essential device; consumer is distressed; all Apple contact has been exhausted; no insurer available as backstop)
- Opening reply: "I understand how devastating this is — £1,199 and no phone, refused at every turn. Apple's position has a direct legal flaw in your favour under UK law. Let's address this right now."
- Direct the consumer to: UK Consumer Rights Act 2015 — Apple bears the obligation to deliver; if the item was not received, Apple must issue a full refund; send Apple a formal written complaint immediately (written complaints trigger a different escalation obligation than phone calls); if paid by credit card, invoke Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 — purchases over £100 make the card issuer jointly liable with Apple, meaning the bank must refund even if Apple refuses; if paid by debit card, raise a Chargeback claim with the bank for non-delivery; Citizens Advice Consumer Helpline 0808 223 1133; Which? free legal rights guidance on undelivered online orders; if Apple does not resolve within 14 days of the written complaint, proceed to the Retail Ombudsman or Money Claim Online (Small Claims Court) for claims up to £10,000
- Evidence to preserve: DPD delivery confirmation with timestamp and any GPS or photo proof-of-delivery record, video of opening the empty box if recorded (film immediately if not already done), all Apple call reference numbers and the written record of each call, a written request to Apple to confirm their final position by email, bank or card payment records
- Feedback category tag: Non-Delivery / Refund Refused / High-Value Financial Loss / Call Centre Escalation Failure

### Scenario 10-9: EU Return Rights Rejected — Polish Retailer Refuses Refund to German Consumer for Unwanted Clothing (Pattern C + E)
**Real Basis:** European Consumer Centres Network (ECC-Net) Annual Report 2023 — 125,000+ consumer inquiries handled across 29 centres; 22,500 cases required direct trader intermediation; EUR 9M recovered; cross-border e-commerce returns and refusal to honour the EU 14-day cooling-off right were the dominant complaint category; 33% surge in cross-border cases in 2023
**The Problem:** A consumer in Berlin ordered three items of clothing totalling €340 from an online fashion retailer based in Poland. The clothes arrived and do not fit. She emailed the Polish retailer within 9 days of delivery to exercise her right of withdrawal under the EU Consumer Rights Directive — the 14-day cooling-off right that applies to all distance contracts within the EU, with no obligation to give a reason. The retailer replied that "returns from Germany are not accepted" due to logistics costs, and offered a 15% store-credit voucher instead. A second email sent five days later was ignored.
**What staff/org would do:** Reiterate the no-returns-from-Germany policy. Re-send the store-credit offer. Ignore further correspondence. The retailer may be deliberately exploiting the assumption that cross-border consumers will not pursue a €340 dispute across a border.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that EU Consumer Rights Directive Article 9 (Directive 2011/83/EU) gives consumers 14 days to withdraw from distance contracts with no requirement to give a reason, and no geographic exemption within the EU. A Polish retailer selling to a German consumer has identical obligations as if the consumer were Polish. The "no returns from Germany" policy is unlawful.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false (€340 financial harm; the 14-day window is still open based on the 9-day contact — act promptly)
- Opening reply: "The retailer's no-returns-from-Germany rule contradicts EU law directly. Your 14-day right of withdrawal applies in full, and there are clear, free escalation paths designed for exactly this situation."
- Direct the consumer to: ECC Germany (ecc.de) — free mediation service specifically for cross-border EU consumer disputes, staffed with legal experts; the EU Online Dispute Resolution platform (ec.europa.eu/consumers/odr) — file a formal dispute against the Polish retailer; cite EU Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU Article 9 explicitly in any further written communication with the retailer; if the retailer continues to refuse after ODR filing, contact the consumer protection authority in Poland (UOKiK) via ECC Germany as intermediary; contact PayPal or the card issuer for a payment dispute if the retailer does not engage within 30 days of the ODR submission
- Evidence to preserve: order confirmation showing the German delivery address, delivery tracking confirmation with date of receipt, the email sent within 14 days exercising the right of withdrawal with a clear timestamp, the retailer's response refusing the return, the second unanswered email with its send timestamp
- Feedback category tag: Cross-Border Return Rights / Refund Refused / EU Consumer Law Violation / Rights Violation

### Scenario 10-10: Seven Calls, Three Disconnections, No Resolution — Elderly Consumer Trapped in Retail Call Centre Loop Over Hazardous Appliance (Pattern E)
**Real Basis:** Retail and global call centre benchmarks 2023-24: 67% of customers abandon calls before reaching a live agent (Zendesk industry data); retail sector records the highest escalation rate of any measured sector at 18-24% of all contacts (Stealth Agents / industry contact centre research); CSAT drops an average of 22 points when a ticket is escalated (SQM Group contact centre research); Salesforce "State of the AI Connected Customer" 2024 — 76% of customers more likely to switch brands after a single bad service experience; UK Consumer Detriment Survey 2024 — 48% of consumer problems left unresolved
**The Problem:** A 71-year-old retired teacher in Nairobi ordered an electric kettle from a major retail chain's online store for KES 3,200. After three uses, the kettle overheats severely and the plastic handle has begun to melt — a burn and fire hazard. Over ten days she has called the retailer's customer service line seven times. Four calls were abandoned after waiting 15 to 40 minutes without reaching anyone. On two calls she reached an agent, was placed on hold for a supervisor, and the call disconnected. On the seventh call she was transferred to a product faults queue that played automated music for 27 minutes before disconnecting automatically. She is not comfortable using online complaint forms and does not know where else to turn.
**What staff/org would do:** If eventually reached: open a new ticket, provide a reference number, tell her the escalation team will contact her within 5 to 7 business days. No one calls back. The case sits in the escalation queue because the purchase value falls below the automatic senior-agent review threshold.
**What the Owner does:** Takes full ownership immediately without transferring. Recognises the consumer is elderly, has no digital escalation path available to her, has made seven documented attempts without resolution, and is still in possession of a product that poses an active fire and burn hazard. Addresses the safety concern first, then the resolution pathway.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true (a melting handle on a heating appliance is a fire and burn hazard; the product must not be used; seven contacts and ten days without resolution for a safety-critical defect is unacceptable)
- Opening reply: "Please stop using the kettle and unplug it right away — a melting handle on a heating appliance is a real safety hazard. Seven calls and ten days without any resolution is completely unacceptable, and I am going to help you get this resolved without another transfer."
- Direct the consumer to: Stop using and unplug the appliance immediately; report the safety defect to the Kenya Bureau of Standards (KEBS) product quality complaint line and the Competition Authority of Kenya (CAK) consumer complaint portal; send a written formal complaint to the retailer by email or physical letter headed "FORMAL COMPLAINT — SAFETY DEFECT" — written complaints trigger a legal obligation to respond in most jurisdictions and cannot be closed by an automated hold; if a family member or neighbour can assist, submit the complaint through the retailer's website simultaneously to create a timestamped written record; as a last-resort escalation, contact the retailer publicly via their social media account — documented industry data shows public posts on brand accounts resolve call-centre-resistant cases significantly faster than phone queues
- Evidence to preserve: proof of purchase, photographs of the melted handle and any discolouration or burn marks on the appliance, a written log of all seven call attempts including the date, time, and duration of each attempt and any reference numbers received
- Feedback category tag: Product Safety / Defective Good / Call Centre Loop / Vulnerable Consumer / Elderly
