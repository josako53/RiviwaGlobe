### Scenario 30-1: EU User's Facebook Data Transferred to US Servers Without Legal Basis (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Irish Data Protection Commission press release, May 2023; IAPP — "Meta fined GDPR-record 1.2 billion euros in data transfer case"; TechCrunch, August 2024 — "The 10 largest GDPR fines on Big Tech"
**The Problem:** An EU consumer reads media coverage of the Irish DPC's €1.2 billion ruling against Meta and realises their Facebook profile — including private messages, friends lists, and years of behavioral data — was transferred to and stored on US servers for years without a valid legal basis. They want to know exactly what data was transferred, demand its deletion from US systems, and understand their rights. They navigate to Facebook's Help Center and find only automated FAQs about downloading their data. There is no route to object to cross-border data transfers specifically, no live agent, and no confirmation that their individual profile was involved.
**What staff/org would do:** Facebook's automated support system routes the user to a generic "Privacy Basics" page and a data download tool showing what Meta holds, but not where it has been sent. Any submitted request receives an auto-acknowledgement and goes unanswered. Meta makes no proactive outreach to EU users about the transfer or the DPC ruling, treating the regulatory matter as resolved at the corporate level while individual users remain uninformed.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the concern immediately, confirms the ruling is real and significant, explains the consumer's specific GDPR rights — right to erasure, right to object to processing, right to lodge a complaint — and directs them to the Irish DPC and digital rights organisations without minimising the structural nature of the harm.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening: "Your concern is completely valid. The Irish Data Protection Commission issued a record €1.2 billion fine against Meta in May 2023 specifically because EU user data was transferred to US servers for years without proper legal safeguards. You have enforceable rights here and a clear route to use them."
- Direct to: Irish Data Protection Commission (dataprotection.ie) to lodge a formal GDPR complaint; Meta's GDPR Data Subject Request portal (facebook.com/privacy/policy) for erasure and access requests; noyb.eu (European Centre for Digital Rights) which led the original complaint and supports collective claims; your national data protection authority if not in Ireland
- Evidence to collect: Download your Facebook data (Settings > Your Facebook Information > Download Your Information) and note what metadata is included; record the date your account was created; screenshot any prior privacy objection or data deletion requests submitted and Meta's responses; save links to the DPC ruling for your complaint file
- Feedback category: Data Privacy Violation / Unauthorized Cross-Border Data Transfer / GDPR Enforcement

### Scenario 30-2: Amazon Prime "Iliad Flow" — Call Agent Refuses to Cancel Subscription by Phone (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** FTC press release June 2023 — "FTC Takes Action Against Amazon for Enrolling Consumers in Amazon Prime Without Consent"; Amazon Prime FTC settlement September 2025, $2.5 billion ($1B penalty + $1.5B refunds to ~35 million consumers); Fortune, NPR reporting
**The Problem:** A US consumer signed up for a free Amazon Prime trial two years ago and forgot to cancel. They have been charged $139 per year — twice — without realising it. When they attempt to cancel online they are walked through a four-page, six-click process that loops through discount offers, benefit reminders, and guilt screens before any cancellation option appears. Frustrated, they call Amazon's customer service line and ask the agent to process the cancellation. The agent tells them that cancellation must be completed online through the self-service flow and will not process the request on the call. The call ends with the subscription still active and no refund initiated.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center agent reads from a script directing the consumer back to the online cancellation flow, offers a one-month "goodwill" credit as compensation for the confusion, and does not disclose that the FTC filed suit over this exact process or that a $2.5 billion settlement exists. The consumer is left with a partial credit and no cancellation.
**What the Owner does:** Takes the consumer's report seriously, explains that this deliberate cancellation obstruction — internally named "Iliad Flow" by Amazon — is the subject of a major FTC enforcement action and settlement, and tells the consumer how to claim their portion of the $1.5 billion refund pool.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening: "This is a documented consumer protection case, not just a frustrating experience. Amazon's cancellation process was formally found by the FTC to be designed to obstruct users — the agency called it the 'Iliad Flow' and Amazon settled for $2.5 billion in September 2025. You may be entitled to a refund of up to $51 and your complaint adds weight to ongoing enforcement."
- Direct to: FTC complaint portal (reportfraud.ftc.gov) to file a formal complaint; Amazon Prime FTC settlement claims administrator — check current claims eligibility at the settlement website; your state attorney general's consumer protection office for state-level action; your credit card company to dispute the unauthorized charges under the Fair Credit Billing Act (60-day window from statement date)
- Evidence to collect: Bank or credit card statements showing all Amazon Prime charge dates and amounts; screenshots of the online cancellation screens showing the multi-step obstruction flow; any email receipts for the Prime subscription; a record of the call including date, approximate time, and what the agent said; any chat transcripts from prior cancellation attempts
- Feedback category: Subscription Trap / Unauthorized Charges / Dark Pattern UX / Call Center Failure

### Scenario 30-3: Child's TikTok Account Public by Default — Unknown Adult Paired Without Parental Consent (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Irish Data Protection Commission press release, September 15, 2023 — DPC fines TikTok €345 million; Baker Botts legal analysis; TechCrunch coverage of the ruling; three separate DPC fines: €100M (public-by-default), €65M (Family Pairing failure), €180M (transparency breaches)
**The Problem:** A parent in Germany discovers that their 14-year-old's TikTok account — created when TikTok was aware of the child's age — has been set to public by default, making all videos, profile details, and interactions visible to any internet user including non-registered members of the public. More urgently, the parent finds that an unknown adult has connected to the child's account via TikTok's "Family Pairing" feature, disabling content restrictions, without the parent's knowledge or any verification that the adult is a guardian. The parent tries TikTok's in-app safety reporting and receives only automated responses and help articles about adjusting settings. There is no phone number or live escalation path for child safety concerns.
**What staff/org would do:** TikTok's automated safety bot categorises the Family Pairing concern as a routine settings change and sends a help article on how to adjust privacy controls. The unknown adult's pairing is not treated as a potential safety incident. No human review is triggered, the child's account remains public, and the unknown paired adult retains access.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an active child safety concern requiring immediate action on two fronts — securing the child's account right now and reporting both to the platform via its dedicated child safety channel and to the relevant regulator and child protection authority.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "This is a child safety concern and it needs immediate action. The Irish Data Protection Commission fined TikTok €345 million in September 2023 specifically for these two failures — children's accounts set to public by default and Family Pairing allowing unverified adults to disable content restrictions. Let's secure your child's account first, then report this properly."
- Direct to: TikTok's dedicated privacy contact (privacy@tiktok.com) with a written formal complaint including your child's account details and the unknown pairing — create a paper trail; Irish DPC (dataprotection.ie) to file a GDPR complaint on your child's behalf; in Germany: Bundeszentrale fur Kinder- und Jugendmedienschutz (bzkj.de) and Jugendschutz.net for reporting child-endangering platform behaviour; klicksafe.de for guidance on securing the account
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of the child's account privacy settings showing public visibility; screenshots of the Family Pairing screen identifying the unknown paired account; the date the TikTok account was created and what age information was provided; all automated responses received from TikTok support with timestamps; any notification emails from TikTok about the pairing event
- Feedback category: Child Safety / Data Protection by Design Failure / Unauthorized Account Access / Regulatory Violation

### Scenario 30-4: Uber Driver's Medical Records and Criminal Data Sent to US Without Consent (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens (Dutch DPA) press release, July 22, 2024 — €290 million fine on Uber; European Data Protection Board; Linklaters legal analysis; original complaints filed by 170 French Uber drivers via Ligue des droits de l'Homme; CNIL (French DPA) involvement
**The Problem:** A French Uber driver reads media coverage of the Dutch DPA's €290 million ruling and realises that their personal data — submitted during Uber's regulatory compliance checks — was transferred to US servers for over two years without any valid legal safeguard. This includes not just their taxi licence and GPS history, but potentially their identity documents, medical records, and criminal background data. The driver contacts Uber partner support to request a full list of what data was transferred and to ask for its deletion from US systems. The Uber support bot routes them to a "Partner Support" FAQ about earnings disputes and vehicle requirements. There is no pathway in the standard driver support flow for data subject rights requests.
**What staff/org would do:** Uber's partner support queue acknowledges the ticket with a two-week response target. When a response eventually arrives, it directs the driver to Uber's general privacy policy and states that data handling is "compliant with applicable law." No specific information about what data was transferred or whether it has been deleted is provided.
**What the Owner does:** Validates the driver's concern with specific reference to the Dutch DPA's ruling, explains their strong GDPR rights as a data subject — access, erasure, and complaint — and connects them to the organisations that successfully prosecuted this case.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening: "What you are describing was confirmed and penalised by the Dutch Data Protection Authority in July 2024, which fined Uber €290 million after finding that sensitive driver data — including medical records and criminal conviction data — was transferred to the US without legal cover for over two years. You have enforceable GDPR rights and a clear path to use them."
- Direct to: French CNIL (cnil.fr) to lodge a formal data subject complaint — the 170 French drivers who triggered this case filed through CNIL and Ligue des droits de l'Homme; Dutch DPA (autoriteitpersoonsgegevens.nl) as the lead authority that issued the fine; submit a formal GDPR Subject Access Request directly to Uber's EU Data Protection Officer at dpo@uber.com — Uber is legally required to respond within 30 days; noyb.eu if you want to join collective enforcement action
- Evidence to collect: All documents you submitted to Uber during onboarding and compliance checks — photos, ID documents, medical certificates, criminal background consent forms; any Uber communications referencing data handling or sharing; records of dates you worked as an Uber driver partner; any prior data access requests submitted and responses received; the Dutch DPA press release (autoriteitpersoonsgegevens.nl) as your reference document
- Feedback category: Gig Worker Data Rights / Unauthorized Cross-Border Data Transfer / GDPR Violation / Sensitive Data Mishandling

### Scenario 30-5: LastPass Vault Stolen — Cryptocurrency Wallet Drained Overnight (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** LastPass breach disclosures August and December 2022; UK ICO enforcement notice 2025 — £1.6M fine; US class action settlement 2025 — $8.2M general pool + $16.25M cryptocurrency theft pool (lastpasssettlement.com); PCWorld and BleepingComputer coverage linking millions in stolen crypto to the breach
**The Problem:** A user wakes to find their cryptocurrency wallet has been completely drained overnight. They have seen media reports linking documented cryptocurrency theft to the LastPass data breach of December 2022, in which attackers exfiltrated entire customer vaults — including unencrypted metadata and encrypted password data — and subsequently brute-forced weak master passwords offline to access the contents. The user stored their crypto wallet seed phrase in LastPass. They contact LastPass support immediately. The automated chat offers a help article titled "What data was exposed?" and opens a ticket with a 72-hour response target. No one confirms whether their specific vault was decrypted or advises them on protecting other accounts still stored in the same vault.
**What staff/org would do:** LastPass's support bot marks the cryptocurrency theft as outside its support scope since it involves a third-party financial loss. The ticket is acknowledged but not escalated to a security specialist. The user is reassured that their master password was not stolen — without acknowledging that their encrypted vault was exfiltrated and that offline brute-forcing of weak master passwords is the confirmed attack vector for the subsequent thefts.
**What the Owner does:** Treats this as an active financial emergency. Prioritises immediate triage: change all passwords still in the vault starting with email and banking accounts before the attacker can pivot. Then documents the loss for the class action settlement and reports the theft to the appropriate financial crime authorities.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "This is a financial emergency — stop what you are doing and change your most critical passwords right now before anything else. Start with your primary email account, then banking and financial accounts, then every other service in that LastPass vault. Cryptocurrency theft linked to the LastPass breach is extensively documented. I will help you with reports and claims once your remaining accounts are secured."
- Direct to: FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov) for the cryptocurrency theft report; UK Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk) if UK-based; LastPass class action settlement — general claims pool $8.2M and dedicated cryptocurrency theft pool $16.25M at lastpasssettlement.com; UK ICO (ico.org.uk) for a formal breach complaint if UK-based; your bank's fraud line immediately if banking passwords were in the vault
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of wallet transaction history showing the unauthorized drain with exact timestamps and transaction IDs; confirmation of your LastPass account email address and subscription tier; any LastPass breach notification email received; a list of services whose passwords were stored in the vault — service names only, not the passwords themselves; all communications with LastPass support including ticket numbers; any cryptocurrency exchange records showing the wallet balance before the drain
- Feedback category: Platform Security Failure / Financial Loss / Data Breach Response Failure / Inadequate Consumer Notification

### Scenario 30-6: T-Mobile Breach — 90-Minute Hold, Agent Cannot Confirm Whether SSN Was Taken (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** T-Mobile SEC 8-K breach disclosure August 2021; FCC enforcement action and $31.5M settlement September 2024; $500M class action settlement 2022; Security.org T-Mobile breach history; Cybersecurity Dive — "FCC settlement: T-Mobile to pay $31.5M for data breaches"
**The Problem:** A T-Mobile customer received a breach notification letter in August 2021 stating their data "may have been exposed" in a breach affecting 76.6 million individuals. The letter includes a dedicated support line number. The consumer calls to confirm specifically whether their Social Security number, date of birth, and driver's licence number were among the records exfiltrated — information critical for deciding whether to freeze credit, alert the IRS, and notify their bank. After a 90-minute hold, the agent confirms only that the customer "was notified" and is unable to look up which specific data fields were taken for their individual account. The consumer is transferred twice and ultimately disconnected without resolution.
**What staff/org would do:** The call center agent reads from a breach notification script, offers enrollment in a free 24-month identity monitoring service, and states they have "no further information" on what specific data fields were taken for individual customers. After two transfers and 90 minutes, the call drops. The consumer has no more information than they had from the letter.
**What the Owner does:** Gives the consumer a clear, actionable response grounded in what T-Mobile publicly disclosed at the population level — SSNs, DOBs, and driver's licence numbers were among the confirmed exfiltrated fields for current customers — and provides the specific protective steps rather than waiting for the call center to provide individual-level confirmation it cannot give.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "T-Mobile publicly confirmed in their breach disclosures that Social Security numbers, dates of birth, and driver's licence numbers were among the data exfiltrated for current customers. The call center cannot give you individual confirmation — but T-Mobile's own disclosures tell you what was taken at the population level. Act on the assumption your SSN was included. Here is exactly what to do."
- Direct to: Freeze your credit at all three bureaus immediately — Equifax (equifax.com), Experian (experian.com), TransUnion (transunion.com) — this is free by law under the Economic Growth Act; IRS Identity Protection PIN (irs.gov/identity-theft-central) to prevent fraudulent tax returns; FTC Identity Theft Report (identitytheft.gov) which creates a legal record; T-Mobile class action settlement — check current eligibility; FCC consumer complaint portal (fcc.gov/consumers/guides/filing-informal-complaint) to report the inadequate call center response
- Evidence to collect: The original T-Mobile breach notification letter with the date it was received; a written record of the call including date, total hold time, transfer count, and what each agent said; any text or email notification from T-Mobile; bank and credit card statements for suspicious activity in the period following the breach; a note of what specific data you originally provided to T-Mobile when activating your account
- Feedback category: Data Breach / Call Center Failure / Identity Theft Risk / Inadequate Breach Response

### Scenario 30-7: Google Permanently Bans 12-Year Account — Automated Appeal Rejected, No Human Review Available (Pattern D)
**Real Basis:** New York Times — Kashmir Hill investigation, August 2022 ("A Dad Took Photos of His Naked Toddler for the Doctor. Google Flagged Him as a Criminal"); Android Authority coverage; documented cases 2021–2026 showing identical pattern of permanent bans with no human escalation path
**The Problem:** A user in Canada who has held a Gmail account for twelve years wakes to find their entire Google account has been permanently disabled. Inaccessible: Gmail — including a decade of business and personal email — Google Drive documents, Google Photos, YouTube history, and all Play Store app purchases. The only notification is an automated message stating the account violated "Google's policies" with no specific violation identified. The user submits the in-app appeal form. Four days later they receive a one-line automated rejection: "We've reviewed your appeal and our decision remains the same." Google's own Help Center explicitly states that consumer accounts are not eligible for phone or chat support regarding account suspension. The Gmail address is also the recovery email for most of the user's other online accounts.
**What staff/org would do:** Google's automated system sends a second generic rejection of the appeal. The Help Center loops back to the same appeal form that already failed. There is no phone number, no email address, no chat option, and no documented route to speak with a human agent about account reinstatement for a consumer account. Google's published support structure treats this as a final automated decision.
**What the Owner does:** Acknowledges the severity — this is a digital emergency involving loss of email, irreplaceable documents, photos, and purchased content — and provides every documented avenue that has actually resulted in resolution, while being honest that the standard process has failed and escalation routes are limited but real.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening: "Losing twelve years of email, documents, and photos with no human to speak to is a serious loss and Google's automated-only process leaves almost no standard recourse. You are not alone — this pattern prompted a New York Times investigation in 2022. Here is every route that has produced results, in order of how much leverage each carries."
- Direct to: Exhaust all account recovery options first at accounts.google.com/signin/recovery — try every verification method available including phone, backup email, and security questions; if purchased content (Play Store apps) was lost, file a complaint with the Canadian Competition Bureau (competitionbureau.gc.ca) and CRTC (crtc.gc.ca) about loss of paid digital goods; consult a consumer rights lawyer about potential claims under Canadian consumer protection law for destruction of purchased digital content; the Electronic Frontier Foundation (eff.org) and Open Media (openmedia.org) for digital rights advocacy; media escalation — the Kashmir Hill NYT investigation documented that media coverage was the primary route to human review in Google account cases
- Evidence to collect: Every automated notification and rejection email from Google with full headers; a comprehensive list of all services and purchased content tied to the Google account — apps, subscriptions, storage, domains; any external backups of Gmail, Drive, or Photos content; proof of account age such as old emails on other platforms referencing the Gmail address; records of all recovery attempts with dates and outcomes
- Feedback category: Account Ban / Refusal of Service / No Escalation Path / Digital Asset Loss / Automated Decision Without Human Override

### Scenario 30-8: JustAnswer Charges AU$70/Month After AU$2 Preview Fee — Refund Refused After Cancellation (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** ACCC Federal Court proceedings VID1259/2025, filed September 23, 2025 against JustAnswer LLC; ppc.land reporting on the case; University of Melbourne Pursuit legal analysis; ACCC media release
**The Problem:** A consumer in Melbourne searched online for legal advice about a workplace dispute and clicked on a chat widget that appeared — through its language and branding — to be affiliated with the Australian Fair Work Ombudsman. The widget advertised a AU$2 joining fee to "chat with a Fair Work expert." The consumer paid AU$2, received a short response, and believed the transaction was complete. The following month they find a AU$70 charge from JustAnswer on their credit card. The month after: another AU$70. They contact JustAnswer support to cancel and request a refund of the two unauthorised monthly charges. Support confirms the cancellation going forward but denies both refunds, stating the subscription terms were visible on the payment screen and the refund window has expired.
**What staff/org would do:** The JustAnswer support agent confirms the subscription is now cancelled but refuses any refund for past charges. When the consumer raises that the widget appeared to be a government service, the agent denies any government affiliation was implied and offers a AU$10 goodwill credit. The agent does not disclose that the ACCC has filed Federal Court proceedings against JustAnswer over this exact conduct.
**What the Owner does:** Confirms that the ACCC has taken JustAnswer to Federal Court specifically over the AU$2 fee concealing a AU$50–90 monthly subscription and the false government affiliation claims, and helps the consumer access the strongest remedies available under Australian Consumer Law.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening: "What you experienced is the subject of active Federal Court proceedings in Australia. The ACCC filed suit against JustAnswer in September 2025 — case VID1259/2025 — specifically because the AU$2 fee concealed a recurring monthly subscription and the widget falsely implied affiliation with the Fair Work Ombudsman. Your grounds for a full refund are strong."
- Direct to: ACCC consumer complaint portal (accc.gov.au/consumers/consumer-rights-guarantees/make-a-consumer-complaint) — the ACCC is actively litigating this case and your complaint directly supports it; Consumer Affairs Victoria (consumer.vic.gov.au) for state-level complaint support and mediation; your credit card company to initiate a chargeback for misrepresented charges — under Australian Consumer Law the subscription was not what was advertised, which is grounds for a card dispute; Fair Trading NSW or your state equivalent if you have not already used the Victorian service
- Evidence to collect: Screenshots of the original chat widget showing the AU$2 fee prominently and any language implying Fair Work Ombudsman affiliation; bank or credit card statements showing every JustAnswer charge with exact dates and amounts; all JustAnswer email confirmations including the initial receipt; the full email thread with support showing the refund denial and the AU$10 offer; a screenshot showing how you found the widget — search engine results page if available
- Feedback category: Subscription Trap / Misleading Pricing / False Government Affiliation / Unauthorized Charges / Refund Refusal

### Scenario 30-9: Twilio IT Staff Phished — Consumer's Authy Two-Factor App Hijacked at 3AM (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Twilio breach disclosure August 2022; TechCrunch — "Twilio breach also compromised Authy two-factor accounts" August 26, 2022; Dark Reading — "Twilio Hackers Stole Okta Credentials in Sprawling Supply Chain Attack" (0ktapus investigation); BankInfoSecurity — "Twilio and Cisco Breaches Part of Massive Phishing Campaign"; 93 Authy accounts confirmed with attacker-registered devices
**The Problem:** A user receives an automated notification at 3 AM that a new device has been added to their Authy two-factor authentication account — an action they did not authorise. They rely on Authy to secure their email, online banking, and cryptocurrency accounts. After researching, they identify this as consistent with the 2022 Twilio breach, in which attackers telephoned Twilio's own IT support staff, impersonated the internal IT department, and used social-engineered credentials to access Twilio's backend systems — then registered additional devices on 93 Authy accounts. The user contacts Twilio/Authy support and is told to follow the standard account recovery flow, which requires two-factor verification — potentially now routing through the attacker's registered device.
**What staff/org would do:** Twilio's support bot provides a generic account recovery flow. The standard identity verification process requires the user to authenticate via the same Authy app that may now be compromised. The support agent, following standard protocol, cannot override the two-factor requirement without an extended identity verification process. The agent does not flag this as potentially connected to the documented 2022 breach or escalate it as a security incident.
**What the Owner does:** Recognises this as a documented supply-chain attack where Twilio's own customer-facing staff were the original entry point — making this a platform staff misconduct by vulnerability rather than intent — and guides the user through emergency steps to break the attacker's device registration before it is used to access dependent accounts.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening: "An unauthorised device registered on your Authy account at 3 AM is an active security emergency — every account you protect with Authy is potentially at risk right now. This matches the documented 2022 Twilio breach where attackers compromised Twilio's own IT staff by phone to access Authy backends. Do not use Authy to log into anything. Secure your accounts now."
- Direct to: Twilio/Authy security incident escalation — email security@twilio.com immediately with subject line "Unauthorized device registration — active incident"; call Twilio's sales/enterprise line to escalate as a security emergency rather than using the standard support bot; FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov) to file a cyber incident report; your bank's 24-hour fraud line if banking accounts use Authy 2FA — request account flags immediately; check haveibeenpwned.com to confirm exposure scope
- Evidence to collect: Screenshot of the Authy new-device notification with the exact timestamp and device details shown; a full list of every account that uses Authy for two-factor authentication — service names only; any email from Authy confirming device registration events; Twilio or Authy support ticket number from initial contact; records of any suspicious login attempts on Authy-protected accounts in the same window; your original Authy account registration email
- Feedback category: Platform Security Failure / Staff Social Engineering / Unauthorized Account Access / Supply Chain Incident / Active Security Emergency

### Scenario 30-10: Microsoft 365 Price Increase — 2.7 Million Australians Charged More Without Clear Prior Notice (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** ACCC media release 2024 — proceedings against Microsoft over subscription price increase disclosure; Wotton Kearney legal analysis — "Technology Disputes in Focus: ACCC Challenges Microsoft on AI Pricing"; SMBTech — "ACCC reports 20% jump in consumer guarantee complaints as electronics and tech sector faces scrutiny"; ACCC annual report data
**The Problem:** A small business owner in Sydney who uses Microsoft 365 Business Basic notices their monthly charge has risen from AU$9 to AU$12.50 — a 39% increase — without any prior notice they recall seeing. They call Microsoft support to dispute the charge and request a refund for the months at the higher rate. The agent confirms the price change is effective and non-negotiable, states that advance notice was sent by email, and provides a link to the Microsoft 365 pricing page. The business owner asks the agent to confirm when and to which email address the notice was sent. The agent says they cannot retrieve that information. The call ends with no refund and no evidence the notice was ever received.

The agent does not disclose that the ACCC has commenced Federal Court proceedings against Microsoft specifically over whether these price change communications were adequate for 2.7 million affected Australian consumers.
**What the Owner does:** Informs the consumer that the ACCC is actively pursuing Microsoft in court over the adequacy of these exact disclosures, which means the consumer's complaint has genuine legal weight, and identifies the specific remedies available through both the ACCC and Australian Consumer Law.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
