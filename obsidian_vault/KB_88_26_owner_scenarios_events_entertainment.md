### Scenario 26-1: Crowd Crush Medical Emergency Ignored Mid-Concert (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Astroworld Festival crowd crush, Houston, Texas, November 5, 2021 — Harris County Medical Examiner records; court filings in 2,400+ pending injury lawsuits; ABC13 Houston, PBS NewsHour; 10 killed (ages 9–27), nearly 5,000 injured; 9 of 10 wrongful death suits settled by 2024
**The Problem:** You are at a large outdoor music festival. The crowd around you has compacted so tightly you cannot raise your arms. Someone directly in front of you has gone limp and is being held up only by the surrounding bodies. You cannot reach your phone. You shout for help — people around you are also shouting — but the music continues at full volume. A security guard station is visible at the crowd's edge but nobody is moving toward you.
**What staff/org would do:** Security staff at the perimeter maintain their position unless directly radioed by a supervisor. The event continues. Stage crew and the performer are not informed of the medical situation. Medics stationed at first-aid tents are not dispatched to crowd locations without a radio dispatch code. The crush continues for several minutes before the show is stopped.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner immediately flags this as an active life-safety emergency. They document the time and location, alert venue safety staff directly rather than through a general helpline, and create a timestamped incident record — who was alerted, when, and what the response was. If the consumer can still communicate, the Owner guides them to move laterally toward the crowd's edge, not forward. They open a formal incident report that will be critical evidence in any subsequent investigation or legal claim.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "This is a life-safety emergency. I am recording everything you tell me right now with a timestamp. If you can move at all, go sideways — not forward or back — toward the nearest barrier or fence. If you cannot move, shout 'MEDICAL EMERGENCY' as loud as you can and try to raise one arm."
- Direct the consumer to: Call 911 immediately if able; alert any uniformed security officer within sight; once safe, locate the venue's Event Safety Officer and obtain an official incident report number; if injury occurred, seek medical care and preserve all treatment records — personal injury claims for concert crush incidents carry a two-year statute of limitations in most US states
- Evidence to collect: Timestamp and GPS location from their phone, video or audio recordings of the crowd conditions, names of any witnesses, medical treatment records and discharge paperwork, photos of any injuries, any communications with venue staff or security
- Feedback category tag: Safety Emergency / Crowd Crush / Life Threat / Venue Operator Failure

### Scenario 26-2: Paid Up to $12,000 for a Luxury Festival — Arrived to Disaster Relief Tents (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Fyre Festival, Bahamas, April 2017 — U.S. Department of Justice (SDNY) guilty plea and press release (March 2018); SEC enforcement action charging $27.4 million offering fraud (SEC.gov); FBI case summary; Billy McFarland sentenced to 6 years federal prison; no functioning customer service existed at any point
**The Problem:** You paid between $1,500 and $12,000 for a luxury music festival package in the Bahamas, booked months in advance after seeing paid promotions from major Instagram influencers. You have just landed on the island. There are no artist stages. There are no catered meals. The accommodation consists of disaster-relief tents. Water supplies are inadequate and transport back to the airport is unavailable for days. The customer service phone numbers in your booking confirmation go to voicemail. The festival's Instagram and website have gone dark. You are stranded in a foreign country with no functioning mechanism to reach anyone responsible.
**What staff/org would do:** No functioning staff or support infrastructure exists. Phone lines ring out or go to voicemail. The festival's social media accounts are abandoned. No one answers emails. Fyre Media has no operational customer service at all — this was by design. The company never intended to deliver the promised event.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner documents all proof of purchase, booking confirmations, wire transfer records, and influencer-promotion screenshots immediately. They help the consumer contact their bank or credit card provider to initiate a chargeback under fraud grounds. They note that the US Embassy in Nassau can assist stranded American citizens, including emergency repatriation. They capture the full timeline of communications for any future legal or regulatory claim.

### Scenario 26-3: Ticketmaster Website Crashed Mid-Queue — 90 Minutes on Hold, Transferred and Disconnected (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** Taylor Swift Eras Tour / Ticketmaster Verified Fan presale collapse, November 15, 2022 — US Senate Judiciary Committee hearing record (January 24, 2023); class-action lawsuit with approximately 400 plaintiffs (Clarkson Law Firm); NPR; Wikipedia (Taylor Swift–Ticketmaster controversy); Live Nation president Joe Berchtold testified before Senate on consumer harm from the failure
**The Problem:** You registered months ago for Ticketmaster's Verified Fan presale for a major stadium concert. You entered the queue when the sale opened and received a confirmed queue position. After waiting two hours, you reached the checkout — and the website crashed, logging you out and erasing your queue position. When you try to rejoin, the sale is shown as closed. You call Ticketmaster customer service at 800-653-8000. After 90 minutes on hold, a representative tells you nothing can be done and there will be no second-chance presale. You ask to speak to a supervisor. You are transferred and then disconnected. You call back. The same scripted response. No written acknowledgment of the failure has been sent to you.
**What staff/org would do:** The representative reads from a script: "We apologize for any inconvenience. Ticket sales are final and queue positions cannot be reinstated. There are no further options available." When escalation is requested, the call is transferred to a queue that disconnects after 12 minutes. A follow-up call produces identical results. No supervisor callback is offered and no written complaint record is opened.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner acknowledges what happened without minimizing the consumer's frustration. They document the queue entry, the crash event, and every customer service contact with timestamps and any representative names obtained. They inform the consumer of their legal rights and the regulatory action this precise event triggered at the highest levels of US government.

### Scenario 26-4: Crowd Crush at Public Halloween Event — Emergency Line Unreachable (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Seoul Halloween crowd crush, Itaewon district, Seoul, South Korea, October 29, 2022 — South Korean government special police investigation (January 13, 2023); Al Jazeera, ABC News, NPR; 159 killed, 196 injured, 27 foreign nationals among the dead; witnesses documented repeated failed calls to the 119 emergency line; government investigation found inadequate preparation despite prior warnings
**The Problem:** You are attending a public outdoor Halloween gathering in a city entertainment district. The narrow street — approximately four meters wide — has become catastrophically overcrowded. People around you are being pushed and cannot breathe. You have reached a shop doorway but the crowd is pressing against it. You call the national emergency line. The call rings and disconnects. You try again — same result. No uniformed officers are visible. No crowd management barriers were placed in this street. The pressure of the crowd is still building.
**What staff/org would do:** There are no event staff — this is an unmanaged public gathering. The emergency line is overwhelmed by simultaneous calls across the district. Police units are deployed elsewhere and are not receiving accurate information about the scale of the event. Local government has not activated any crowd management protocol despite documented prior warnings about this location on this date.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner treats this as an active life-safety emergency. They help the consumer get to elevated ground — a raised step, a counter, a ledge — to get above the worst crowd pressure. They advise continuous redialing of 119 and, if an international line is accessible, direct calling of the Seoul Metropolitan Police. They document the location, time, and the specific failure of emergency services to respond — evidence that was central to the subsequent government investigation.

- Direct the consumer to: Seoul Metropolitan Police Agency: +82-2-182; if a foreign national, contact your country's embassy emergency line immediately — do not wait; once safe, report to the National Police Agency's post-incident investigation unit; families seeking information about victims should contact the Seoul government victim support hotline established in November 2022

### Scenario 26-5: Concert Cancelled Due to COVID — Company Insisting on Voucher, Refusing Cash Refund (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** UK COVID-19 event cancellation crisis, 2020–2021 — UK Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) COVID-19 Taskforce (~2,000 event-specific complaints received); London Trading Standards official advisory documenting 218% increase in entertainment complaint volume; Citizens Advice consumer helpline data; Which? investigation finding 20 of 25 surveyed event venues refused or obstructed cash refunds
**The Problem:** You paid £340 for concert tickets. The concert was cancelled due to COVID-19 government restrictions. The promoter's customer service team has told you by phone and by email that your only option is a credit voucher valid for 18 months. You do not want a voucher — you want a cash refund, which you are entitled to under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 because the service was not delivered. The company's phone line put you on hold for over 40 minutes and told you the same thing. When you cited your legal rights in the live chat, the agent terminated the session.
**What staff/org would do:** The customer service agent reads from a policy script: "Due to extraordinary circumstances, we are unable to process cash refunds at this time. A credit voucher for the full value of your booking is available and will be sent to your registered email within 14 days." When you ask about statutory rights under the Consumer Rights Act, the agent says "that is our policy" and repeats the script. The chat session is closed by the agent.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner confirms clearly that the consumer's legal position is correct and the company's position is not. A cancelled event triggers a full cash refund right under UK consumer law regardless of the company's internal policy. The Consumer Rights Act 2015 cannot be contracted out of. The Owner helps the consumer escalate through the correct channels and documents the company's non-compliance.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "You are right and the company is wrong. Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, a cancelled service entitles you to a full cash refund — the company cannot override this with its own policy, and the agent who told you otherwise gave you factually incorrect information. Let me help you escalate this to the people who can fix it."
- Direct the consumer to: Citizens Advice consumer helpline: 0808 223 1133 (free, Monday–Friday 9am–5pm); report to Trading Standards via the Citizens Advice referral or directly at tradingstandards.uk; file a complaint with the CMA at gov.uk/competition-and-markets-authority; if the company is a Society of Ticket Agents and Retailers (STAR) member, use STAR's alternative dispute resolution scheme; initiate a Section 75 claim (credit card) or chargeback (debit card) with your bank citing the service as not provided
- Evidence to collect: Original ticket booking confirmation and payment receipt, screenshots of all email communications from the company offering vouchers only, the live chat transcript showing the session was terminated, call log entries showing date, time, and hold duration, screenshots of any voucher-only policy published on the company's website
- Feedback category tag: Refund Refused / Consumer Rights Violation / Cancelled Event / Illegal Policy

### Scenario 26-6: Event Company Charged a £75 "Admin Fee" to Process a Legally Owed Refund (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** UK Trading Standards COVID-19 event enforcement, 2020–2021 — London Trading Standards official advisory document explicitly identifying the practice of charging consumers approximately £75 per person to process legally owed refunds as an unlawful unfair term under the Consumer Rights Act 2015; CMA COVID-19 Taskforce enforcement documentation
**The Problem:** After your festival was cancelled due to COVID-19 restrictions, you submitted a refund request. The company replied that it would issue your £180 refund but would deduct a £75 "administration and processing fee." They pointed to a clause in their terms and conditions. You have now received only £105. When you call to dispute the deduction, the agent confirms it is company policy, says the fee "covers the cost of processing refund transactions," and tells you there is no further escalation path. The chat helpline says the same. No supervisor is available.
**What staff/org would do:** The customer service agent confirms the deduction, reads the T&C clause, and says the fee was agreed to at the point of purchase. When the consumer points out the legal problem, the agent says they are not able to override policy and there is no complaints manager available. The call ends with no commitment to review the charge.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner documents this as a specific practice that UK Trading Standards has explicitly identified as unlawful. Charging a fee to process a legally owed refund is an unfair contract term under Schedule 2 of the Consumer Rights Act 2015 and is unenforceable. The company's own terms cannot create a fee that penalises consumers for exercising a statutory right. The Owner helps the consumer recover the full amount through regulatory and legal channels.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "What this company did is specifically illegal — Trading Standards has flagged exactly this practice. Charging you a fee to receive money you are owed by law is an unfair term and it is not enforceable. You are entitled to the full £180 and I want to help you get every penny of it back."
- Direct the consumer to: Report the company and the specific fee practice to Trading Standards via tradingstandards.uk or through Citizens Advice (0808 223 1133); contact your bank's disputes team to initiate a Section 75 claim or chargeback for the £75 deducted amount, noting it was an unlawful charge; if the company is STAR-registered, raise through STAR's dispute resolution; as a last resort, file a claim in the County Court Small Claims track — the fee is £35 to file for claims under £300 and the company's defence will be legally very weak
- Evidence to collect: Original payment receipt and invoice showing £180 paid, the refund confirmation email showing the £75 deduction, the specific T&C clause the company cited (screenshot or download), all customer service call logs and chat transcripts, any written statement from the company confirming no further escalation is available
- Feedback category tag: Unlawful Fee / Staff Misconduct / Refund Deduction / Consumer Rights Violation / Corruption

### Scenario 26-7: Accepted a Voucher in 2020 — The Rescheduled Event Cancelled Again in 2022, Voucher Now Worthless (Pattern A)
**Real Basis:** ACCC (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission) COVID-19 event enforcement, 2020–2022 — ACCC enforcement and compliance policy address (2022–23); ACCC directly engaged 100+ companies to compel refunds; Lexology and Mondaq legal analyses of ACCC enforcement; specific "double cancellation" voucher harm documented — consumers who accepted 2020 vouchers lost value when 2021–2022 rescheduled events were also cancelled
**The Problem:** When your concert was cancelled in 2020, you accepted the promoter's credit voucher instead of a cash refund after being told the event would be rescheduled "within 12 to 18 months." The rescheduled event was cancelled again in early 2022 due to the Omicron wave. You have contacted the company to redeem or refund the voucher and been told the event is "postponed indefinitely" — not cancelled — and that your voucher "remains valid" for a future event with no confirmed date. You have been in this loop for over two years. Customer service tells you there is nothing more they can do and the policy is final.
**What staff/org would do:** The customer service representative confirms the voucher "remains valid" and that because the event is listed as "postponed" rather than "cancelled," no refund is owed under their policy. When asked when the event will be rescheduled, the representative says they cannot provide that information. No escalation path is offered and no timeframe is given.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner identifies this as the specific "double cancellation" pattern the ACCC made a priority enforcement issue in 2022–23. Two successive cancellations with no confirmed future date constitutes an effectively cancelled event in substance regardless of the company's chosen terminology. The Owner helps the consumer document the full two-year timeline and escalate to the ACCC and their state consumer protection agency.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "Two years of waiting with no confirmed event and no refund is not an acceptable outcome, and Australia's consumer regulator has specifically called this pattern out as a priority enforcement concern. You have real rights here. Let me help you escalate this to the people who can actually resolve it."
- Direct the consumer to: ACCC complaint portal: accc.gov.au/consumers/consumer-rights-and-warranties/make-a-complaint; state consumer protection agencies — NSW Fair Trading: 13 32 20; Consumer Affairs Victoria: 1300 558 181; QLD Office of Fair Trading: 13 74 68; WA Consumer Protection: 1300 304 054; if the original purchase was by credit card, attempt a chargeback citing the event as effectively cancelled (document that no confirmed future date exists — a screenshot of the event page showing "TBC" or "postponed indefinitely" is key evidence)
- Evidence to collect: Original ticket purchase receipt from 2020, the voucher document and its stated terms and expiry, all company communications about rescheduling (every email and letter), evidence that no confirmed replacement date currently exists (event website screenshot), records of all calls and messages to customer service with dates and durations, any written statement from the company confirming no refund is available
- Feedback category tag: Double Cancellation / Voucher Fraud / ACCC / Wait / No Resolution

### Scenario 26-8: QR Codes Rejected at the Venue Door — Tickets Were Fraudulent (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** ACCC consumer guidance on buying tickets to events (accc.gov.au); BBB Scam Tracker data (2022–2026): 21,000+ complaints processed, 38% of ticket scams through fake websites, average consumer loss AUD-equivalent $303–$606; BBB-documented pattern of consumers refused entry with fraudulent QR codes and receiving no response from the seller site
**The Problem:** You paid AUD $420 for two concert tickets through a website that appeared in a sponsored Google search result and used professional branding, an "Official Resale Partner" badge, and a "100% money-back guarantee." At the venue door, you present the QR codes — both return an error and venue staff tell you the codes do not correspond to any ticket issued for this event. You are refused entry. You call the number on the ticket website immediately — no answer. The site's live chat shows "agent unavailable." The concert starts in 15 minutes.
**What staff/org would do:** Venue door staff cannot issue replacement tickets and are not in a position to investigate the fraud. They direct you to contact the company you purchased from. When you explain the site is unresponsive, they say there is nothing they can do. You are refused entry. The legitimate ticketing platform says it cannot be responsible for purchases made outside its platform. Each party points to another.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner immediately documents the fraudulent QR codes, the full website URL, the transaction record, and the time and location of the refusal. They prioritise initiating a chargeback claim that same evening while the transaction window is fresh. They direct the consumer to Scamwatch and the state police cybercrime unit so the site can be investigated and potentially taken down before it claims more victims.

- is_urgent=false
- Opening reply: "I am really sorry this is happening on the night — this is ticket fraud, and it is not your fault. Let me help you protect what you can financially right now, and make sure this site gets reported so it cannot do this to anyone else."
- Direct the consumer to: Report to Scamwatch immediately at scamwatch.gov.au (run by the ACCC) — include the full URL so it can be actioned; contact your bank or card issuer's fraud department tonight and request a chargeback, using the word "fraud" and noting the merchant is unresponsive; report the website URL to the ACCC for investigation; report to state police cybercrime unit — NSW: cybercrime.police.nsw.gov.au; VIC: cybercrimeunit.police.vic.gov.au; QLD: police.qld.gov.au/programs/csoc

### Scenario 26-9: Festival Crowd Crush — No Warning Given, No Barriers in Place, No Communication (Pattern B)
**Real Basis:** Roskilde Festival Pearl Jam crowd crush, Denmark, June 30, 2000 — Rolling Stone (contemporaneous news report); Euronews 25th anniversary investigation (June 2025); Ultimate Classic Rock retrospective; 9 killed, 26 injured; pre-smartphone era, no crowd density monitoring or early warning system in operation; incident directly caused permanent changes to European festival crowd management standards including barrier configuration, capacity modeling, and real-time density monitoring
**The Problem:** You are at a large outdoor music festival. The area in front of the main stage has been filling for hours. You are now in a section so tightly packed you cannot move. No barriers separate crowd sections. No announcements have been made about crowd density, capacity limits, or safe viewing areas in this section. You cannot see any stewarding staff operating inside the crowd. The headliner has just taken the stage and the surge forward has intensified. People around you are being knocked off their feet and there is no visible response from the stage or from security.
**What staff/org would do:** Stage managers and performer management are not monitoring real-time crowd density because no monitoring system is in place. Security is stationed at the perimeter and at the front stage barrier, not deployed in the crowd interior. No capacity or density announcements are made at any point. The performance continues.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner helps the consumer with immediate physical safety actions — lateral movement toward the crowd edge — and documents the specific systemic failures: no crowd density monitoring, no internal section barriers, no communication to attendees. These are the exact failings the Roskilde investigation identified and which now underpin European festival safety standards. This documentation matters for any post-incident regulatory or legal action.
**Riviwa AI instruction:**
- is_urgent=true
- Opening reply: "If you are in an overcrowded section right now with no way to move, this is dangerous. Move sideways — not forward — toward the nearest barrier, fence, or edge of the crowd. Never push forward into a surge. I am recording what you are telling me."
- Direct the consumer to: Alert any steward or uniformed staff at the crowd perimeter immediately; if in Denmark, emergency services: 112 (EU-wide emergency number); after reaching safety, file a formal complaint with the festival's event safety officer and request a copy of their crowd safety plan; report to the Danish Safety Technology Authority (Sikkerhedsstyrelsen) if safety standards were not met; if injured, seek medical care immediately and preserve all treatment records
- Evidence to collect: Video or photos showing crowd density and the absence of internal section barriers, evidence that no crowd safety announcements were made (or any recordings of what was announced), names and contact details of any witnesses, medical treatment records for any injury, the festival's published safety and site map materials
- Feedback category tag: Crowd Safety / Information Failure / No Warning / Venue Safety / Transparency

### Scenario 26-10: Paid CAD $650 via E-Transfer for Taylor Swift Tickets Through Instagram — Seller Vanished (Pattern C)
**Real Basis:** Toronto-area Eras Tour ticket scam, Canada, 2023 — The Globe and Mail; approximately CAD $300,000 collectively lost by Toronto-area fans to a coordinated ticket scam; BBB Scam Tracker data (2022–2026): 28% of ticket scams via Facebook or social media; average consumer loss $606 (2024); BBB pattern documented — e-transfer payments sent, no tickets delivered, seller accounts deactivated
**The Problem:** You are 19 years old and saved up to buy two floor tickets for a major stadium concert. You paid CAD $650 via Interac e-transfer to a seller on Instagram who appeared credible — hundreds of followers, concert photos, and an active post history. After you sent the money, the seller stopped responding. Within 24 hours the account was deactivated. No tickets were ever delivered. Your bank has told you that e-transfer payments are very difficult to reverse once the recipient withdraws the funds. Ticketmaster says it cannot be responsible for purchases made outside its platform. Instagram says it cannot give you information about the deactivated account without a law enforcement request. Every door you try leads to another door.
**What staff/org would do:** The bank directs you to file a police report and contact the merchant — who no longer exists. The police non-emergency line says to report it online. Ticketmaster says it is not responsible. Instagram says it cannot help without law enforcement involvement. Each institution points to another. The runaround continues until you give up or someone intervenes.
**What the Owner does:** The Owner acknowledges both the financial loss and the emotional weight of what happened — particularly for a young consumer who saved specifically for this. They do not minimise the harm or suggest the consumer was careless. They help the consumer file reports with every realistic recovery and fraud-tracking channel, including those specific to e-transfer fraud in Canada, where a coordinated reporting effort can sometimes trigger bank recovery action.

- Direct the consumer to: Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre (CAFC): 1-888-495-8501 or antifraudcentre.ca — this is the national body for e-transfer fraud and the report number they give you is essential for the next steps; file a police report with your local police service (required before your bank will escalate internally); contact your bank's fraud department specifically — not general customer service — and provide the CAFC report number when requesting escalation to the e-transfer fraud team; report the Instagram seller account to Meta via in-app tools and also at facebook.com/help/contact/; file a BBB report at bbb.org/ca to add to the documented national pattern
